Bonds Can Be Boring

Bond market swings aren’t front-page news. Phew: That’s because they’re nothing to write home about.

The 10-year Treasury yield is on track for its narrowest one-week trading range (7.5 basis points) in six months.

Based on Bank of America’s MOVE Index of Treasury volatility, this week is poised to deliver the biggest one-week reduction in anticipated bond market swings since the second week of 2019.

Last week, we commented on how anticipated short-rate swings were normalizing relative to longer term swings. This time around, let’s focus in on how implied long-rate swings over the short term (3m10y swaptions) are coming in much faster than expected long-rate swings over the medium term (1y10y swaptions). The term structure of Treasury volatility is becoming more “normal” in multiple ways: the uncertainty around the extent of a Fed easing cycle is ebbing, as are expectations that longer-term Treasuries will move more over the shorter-term relative to the longer-term.

“Since the markets remain relatively stable, it’s likely a return of the pre-summer 2019 regime of implied volatility could emerge,” writes Bloomberg’s Ira Jersey.

The story of the year has been the bond market’s proactiveness in pricing in potentially dire economic outcomes. In Treasuries, the recent price action has been more a story of the bond market right-sizing economic expectations. That is, not any negative indicator is perceived as a portent of imminent doom that fosters a flight to safety and lurch downwards in yields – whether that be instances of soft U.S. economic data or earnings reports (like Texas Instruments or Caterpillar) that sounded a downbeat note on the outlook for global activity.

On the one hand, PMIs appear to be bottoming, suggesting the manufacturing recession’s biggest drag on activity may be nearing the rear-view mirror. On the other hand, China’s “iron rooster” stimulus, to borrow a moniker from Bloomberg’s Tom Orlik, is proving insufficient to serve as a major global reflationary impulse. It’s difficult to say the economic backdrop has changed materially over the past couple months – aside from an apparent fading of trade tensions. The removal of that tail risk seemingly has market participants growing more comfortable settling into a higher range for U.S. Treasury yields – but with tempered optimism. To rates strategists, that’s a recipe for the bond market staying relatively contained, for now.

“The rates market has traded within a 1.45-1.9% range over the past few weeks and appears to be searching for the next breakout catalyst,” writes Bank of America’s Mark Cabana. “We continue to recommend trading the recent range with a long duration bias given our expectation for sub-trend U.S. growth through Q2 '20 driven by persistent geopolitical uncertainty.”

Of course, this calm has coincided with the Federal Reserve’s blackout period – and could be shattered at next week’s policy meeting.

The Black Sheep of Credit

There’s been a ton of ink spilled lately – and deservedly so – on the travails of the loan market and its associated products.

These products just aren’t as attractive as they were in a rising rate environment. There are worries about concentrated ownership (and the potential for a gap in demand on downgrades that would turn such owners off. Simply, in exceedingly more cases, these products are not that attractive credits.

However, the broad credit market is not sending the same signal as the loan market. The total return index for high-yield debt is at all-time highs.

And Bank of America’s Hans Mikkelsen is making the case that “what happens in loans stays in loans.”

His thinking: The upward migration in credit ratings – so called “rising stars” from junk to investment grade outnumbering “fallen angels” heading in the opposite direction – “is inconsistent with recession risk.”

This most recent spate of concern about the loan market contrasts with what we’ve seen in corporate debt, which is enjoying perhaps its most starkly risk-on move of the year. Over the past couple weeks, the performance in credit has been inversely correlated to quality.

And to make a broader note about corporate bond performance: in 2019, duration risk has been a bigger “swing factor” in returns than credit risk. For example, AAA debt (the best investment grade) has suffered a bigger drawdown this year than BB (the best junk debt).

And even so, AAA debt has outperformed, a sign of how key the strength in Treasuries has been to overall risk asset returns this year. But if recession risk were at the forefront of investors’ minds, the composition of this dynamic is not what you would expect to see.

Mario (Farewell) Party

This week’s European Central Bank decision would have been a total non-event if not for the fact that it was Mario Draghi’s final one as president. He acknowledged the risks facing the euro area economy, while reaffirming the ample room to deliver easing through asset purchases.

Amid numerous questions from reporters as to his perception of his legacy, he was even able to enjoy a stroll down memory lane. Banque Pictet’s Frederik Ducrozet provides a better chronicling of Super Mario’s body of work than we could hope to.

In many respects, Draghi’s an excellent avatar for central banking in the post crisis era. Counterfactuals could be drawn to show his actions helped avoid much worse outcomes (like the dissolution of the euro or continental deflationary spiral), and yet the macroeconomic results during his tenure leave much to be desired. “Whatever it takes” might be enough to avoid tail risks, but not to produce more optimal outcomes.

Of course, that cannot be laid at the outgoing central banker’s feet: as Draghi began to emphasize more and more as his time at the ECB came to an end, a more active role for fiscal policy would be preferable to the addition of unconventional monetary stimulus.

Since there are doubts as to whether his successor, Christine Lagarde, will be to boost stimulus further from this point given immense opposition from within the institution, an optimistic arc of history might imply that she completes the bridge Draghi laid the foundation for in establishing fiscal policy as a potentially superior aggregate demand stabilizer to monetary policy. Particularly when the latter’s efforts have gone so far in one direction that critics contend they restrict, rather than stimulate, economic activity.

Stocks and Bonds

Inasmuch as there’s fear in the stock market – and it’s hard to say that’s the case, with the VIX Index ending Thursday at a 13 handle – it’s related to how the bond market will dictate winners and losers in the equity complex.

Looking at options skew (relative demand for 10% out of the money puts versus 5% out of the money calls), utilities, consumer staples, and homebuilders stand out as signaling a relatively elevated degree of fear compared to their two-year histories.

These are, of course, some of the more rate-sensitive areas of the market that tend to outperform when bond yields head lower. Mortgage rates, for one, have risen to their highest levels since August. Yet, all these parts of the market are close to or at all-time highs despite the recent back-up in bond yields.

“A move higher in Treasury yields may in fact be broadly risk supportive, but as we have highlighted in numerous reports recently, the amount of rate torque embedded in the sectors is enormous,” writes Michael Purves, CEO of Tallbacken Capital. “A Treasury sell-off would likely engender a big jump in Russell 2000 outperformance, and more value rotation.”

That’s why bank CEOs are pleased that the Federal Reserve isn’t following in the footsteps of the Bank of Japan and the European Central Bank in moving towards negative rates.
