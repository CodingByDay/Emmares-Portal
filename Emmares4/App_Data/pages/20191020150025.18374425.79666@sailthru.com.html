Earlier this year, Samantha Cooper, the senior photo editor for platforms at WIRED, met photographer Kiliii Yüyan at a New York Times Portfolio review. She knew, she says, that she wanted to collaborate with him; it was just a question of finding the right project. Yüyan primarily covers stories related to indigenous communities in the Arctic, but for WIRED he eventually traveled to Northern California to shoot a stunning photo essay about the Yurok Nation and something known as indigenous cultural fire management.

Cultural burns are low-intensity, carefully controlled fires that clear out the forest floor, thus preventing catastrophic burns down the line. But the thing that distinguishes them from standard controlled burns (which agencies should do more of) is the fact that native communities use them (and have used them for more than 10,000 years) to preferentially cultivate certain species of plants, create open grassland, and even facilitate acorn harvests. Indeed, Yüyan writes, “some of the California landscapes that look like pristine wilderness to the nonindigenous are actually human-modified ecosystems.” The resulting photographs are gorgeous and smart, and they show how humans can treat fire as a potent technology, not a catastrophe to be avoided. And the landscape, though perhaps not as pristine as we might think, is beautiful too: “It’s hard to walk around inside a Yurok-burned forest without a sense of awe at the renewal of life and the ingenuity of its indigenous caretakers.”

Sarah Fallon | Deputy Web Editor, WIRED
