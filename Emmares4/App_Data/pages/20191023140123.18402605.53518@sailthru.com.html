As we all ooh and ahh over new devices and software this fall, have you done a quick threat assessment of your technology and connectivity as of late? Because, phew, it's been quite a month in the security world. 

 

Forget the US government continuing to (bizarrely) push tech companies for backdoors into encrypted offerings, or yet another foreign actor trying to hack into a presidential campaign—everyday users had plenty of security happenings to monitor. We had yet another reminder of how bad two-factor authentication is at the start of the month thanks to Twitter, which used the service to target ads to users (rather than letting 2FA simply offer below-average security). Then the Buffer Overflow struck again when Linux users received a bit of bad news. Researchers discovered a potentially serious vulnerability that may make it possible for nearby devices to use Wi-Fi signals to crash or fully compromise vulnerable machines. And hopefully all those Unix pioneers have been updating their passwords regularly.

 

So for this week's Orbital Transmission, we've rounded up the security happenings that may most impact your day-to-day. When even the newest devices (like the ones coming out last week) can have pretty major security flaws with fixes "months" away, these are things readers should definitely be aware of.

Want to understand digital misinformation campaigns? There's a game for that

Just two weeks ago in Austin, Texas, we saw multiple Democratic presidential candidates state digital influence campaigns might be *the* most important tech issue of the 2020 election. And the fact these things exist was made quite clear when the Mueller report led to charges against several Russian intelligence officers. But the idea of a disinformation campaign itself can feel very nebulous or ethereal—especially with how casually folks like Mark Zuckerberg dismiss the concept as under control—so the folks at the Finnish public broadcaster Yle wanted to ground things a bit. "Troll Factory" is the result, a game putting players in the role of a disinformation operative working for an Internet Research Agency-like organization with an anti-immigrant agenda.

 

Farewell, official US military floppy disks

One of the most important parts of healthy opsec is engaging in regular updates, thus patching old vulnerabilities and ensuring the most modern features are enabled. This week, the US Air Force updated in a *big* way. As recently as five years ago, eight-inch floppy disks were still used to store data critical to operating the Air Force's intercontinental ballistic missile command, control, and communications network. That system relied on IBM Series/1 computers installed by the Air Force at Minuteman II missile sites in the 1960s and 1970s. The Air Force maintains this old hardware because it says it's actually so analog it provides a security advantage, but this week we learned floppies have at least been retired. (Those IBM Series/1 computers, however, continue on in service.)

 

Of course, someone abused Alexa and Google Home to eavesdrop and phish passwords

Here's an understatement: "It was always clear that those voice assistants have privacy implications—with Google and Amazon receiving your speech, and this possibly being triggered on accident sometimes." Fabian Bräunlein, senior security consultant at SRLabs, told Ars that recently... because his team discovered malicious apps developed by third parties and hosted by Amazon or Google for their speakers. "We now show that, not only the manufacturers, but... also hackers can abuse those voice assistants to intrude on someone's privacy." The four malicious Alexa "skills" and four Google Home "actions" all passed Amazon's or Google's security-vetting processes, and they posed as simple apps for checking horoscopes. These "smart spies," as the researchers call them, surreptitiously eavesdropped on users and phished for their passwords.

 

Hackers steal secret crypto keys for NordVPN. Here’s what we know so far

We've said it before—recommending VPNs is a tough game because it requires implicit trust in the VPN company. You may recognize NordVPN because their ads are seemingly inescapable in certain corners of the Internet, but the company may soon need a larger marketing campaign. Some 19 months ago, hackers breached the company and stole secret crypto keys. The popular VPN service is only disclosing it now. Until more information is available, it's hard to say precisely how people who use NordVPN should respond. At a minimum, users should press NordVPN to provide many more details about the breach and the keys and any other data that were leaked
