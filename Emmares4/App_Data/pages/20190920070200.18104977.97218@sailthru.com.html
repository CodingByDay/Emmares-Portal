Hi everyone, it’s Lulu. I’ve written a few #MeToo stories over the past year and, sadly, the feedback I often get from people in China is: Who cares?

Some even joke it’s a career boost. We reported last year on how venture capital house IDG fired senior executive Doug Jiang after investigating allegations on social media that he’d made sexual advances toward women in the course of his work. Soon after, I bumped into him at a social gathering in Hong Kong.

As guests washed down smoked salmon, tomato and mozzarella bites with champagne, I watched Jiang converse with managers at top Bitcoin miner Bitmain and executives from some of the world’s largest crypto-exchanges. He introduced contacts to a former male colleague at IDG. The venture capitalist’s LinkedIn page shows he joined Bitmain as an external senior adviser the same month he got canned. (Bitmain confirmed his role but declined to comment further.) Jiang remains a senior adviser as of today, according to his LinkedIn profile.

In other words, Jiang -- who once posted the message “objectifying women to the end” on his WeChat profile, according to screen shots I saw -- landed on his feet. The executive didn’t respond to repeated requests for comment sent to his Line and LinkedIn accounts. An IDG representative declined to comment.

In the U.S., sexual harassment offenders do of course make comebacks but the #MeToo movement has shaken the establishment and made waves in Silicon Valley. My colleague Emily Chang’s book, Brotopia, calls attention to the issue in tech circles.

In China, where entrenched patriarchy persisted during the race to build an industrial economy, those accused can encounter a forgiving public. JD.com founder Richard Liu Qiangdong, who was accused of rape in the U.S. but denied the allegations and was never charged, enjoyed a wave of support on social media. The company again declined to comment.

Scroll through Weibo and you’ll find posts like “We believe in JD and boss Liu” or “Brother Dong made a mistake that all men in the world will make, understand and support!” The young woman who accused him of rape is now suing Liu in civil court. Fellow entrepreneur Li Guoqing, founder of online bookstore Dangdang, posted that Liu’s behavior caused no real harm to staff and shareholders, adding that it was “just extramarital sex, not an affair, so it caused low harm to the wife.” He later apologized, but his previous comments received more than 11,000 likes on Weibo.

At the same time, the #MeToo movement has been suppressed. Posts on this topic have been scrubbed from social media, and protesting voices have been quelled. Some women, including journalist Xiao Muyi, have chosen to come forward to talk about their experiences. But those instances are exceedingly rare -- in 2018, a nascent movement by college students was rapidly squelched.

“I don’t see girls talking to each other about this topic or bonding together as much in China,” said Rui Ma, a former 500 Startups manager and angel investor who’s spent time in both countries. “There’s real repercussions in China if you speak up about this. In the U.S. there’s that too, but it’s less obvious and overt.”

There’s no template for how companies -- in China or elsewhere -- should deal with men accused of sexual harassment. Even in the U.S., there’s an unprecedented wave of people just trying to move on. But one thing’s for sure: companies on either side of the Pacific need to be sensitive to a younger workforce more attuned to corporate values. Consumers, especially the millennial and generation Z crowd, could revolt. And that’s something corporations in general can understand.
