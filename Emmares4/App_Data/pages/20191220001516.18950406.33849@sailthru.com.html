Ask a Chinese official to describe the state of ties with the U.S. and this is the euphemism you’ll most likely hear: it’s complicated.

That it is, and by and large because there are so many moving parts that appear to be gyrating with no discernible correlation. Take for example trade and technology, two crucial aspects of the U.S.-China relationship that seem to be traveling in different directions.

The trade war looks like it’s taken a dovish turn. An announcement late last week that Beijing and Washington had struck a phase-one deal was followed this week by a slow drip of details about that agreement.

The tech war, by contrast, appears to be intensifying. And nowhere has that been more apparent than when it comes to the question of who does and doesn’t work with Huawei.

In Berlin, the Chinese ambassador threatened retaliation if Germany took actions that excluded Huawei from Europe's largest economy. He also noted that German companies sell a great number of cars in China, and if Beijing were to block them from doing so by declaring them unsafe that that would be protectionism.

In addition to pushing allies like Germany to not use Huawei gear, Washington has also taken steps to limit which American companies can supply the Chinese tech giant. That effort could soon be escalating. It was revealed this week that the U.S. is mulling rule tweaks to close loopholes some have leveraged to continue doing business with Huawei.

Does the bubbling tech war diminish the importance of a phase-one deal on trade? It shouldn’t.

The pact de-escalates tensions and begins to build a floor for the relationship, even with no resolution in sight for how to treat Huawei. But it is probably another reason for why a phase-two deal will be magnitudes more difficult to pull together.

Hong Kong Calm

In the four weeks since pro-democracy candidates won an overwhelming victory in Hong Kong’s district-level elections, tensions have noticeably eased. There have been a few clashes between protesters and police but nothing approaching what we saw during the peak of the demonstrations. That said, the city is still far from recovery. Take a look at business. Hong Kong Airlines had seven planes seized because of missed payments, the city’s largest TV broadcaster laid off more than 300 people and a scion off Hong Kong’s wealthiest clan called on the city to help its hard-hit hotel industry. And with no signs of a political solution on the horizon, it remains very hard to see when a recovery might take hold.

China's Newest Warship

President Xi Jinping, on his way to celebrate the 20th anniversary of Macau’s return to Chinese rule, stopped over in the city of Sanya this week to officially commission the country’s first homemade aircraft carrier. By basing the new warship in China’s southern-most province of Hainan, Beijing will be able to project greater military power into the South China Sea, where it has territorial disputes with a number of countries. Steps China has taken to deploy weapons in the region have also spurred tensions with Washington. Adding an aircraft carrier seems unlikely to sooth those anxieties.

Trade War Winner

It took almost thirty years but Taiwan's stock market this week climbed back to the 12,000-point mark, a height it hasn't reached since 1990, thanks in large part to the trade war. The imposition of American tariffs has prompted many Taiwanese companies to divert manufacturing that had been done in China back to the island. Steps Washington has taken to limit the sale of U.S. technology to China has also buoyed Chinese demand for Taiwanese semiconductors. That combination has translated into a stronger-than-expected economy, with Taiwan last month raising its official outlook for growth in 2020 to 2.72% from 2.58%. Another notable beneficiary is President Tsai Ing-wen, who'll be seeking re-election in a January vote with a strong economy behind her.

Property Conundrum

Surging home prices are a long-running headache for China because they exacerbate inequality and inflate ominous bubbles. In response, Beijing has employed tactics including caps on how many homes one can own, large down payments and limits on financing for developers. Data released this week showed that effort is not only reining in the market, it's depressing it. Twenty-one cities reported a drop in home prices, the most in four years. Could that compel Beijing to support a soft market by relaxing its restrictions? It has in the past, acting on concerns that falling home prices could derail economic growth and fuel unrest. But that loosening and the buying it encourages are also what often sparks the next wave of price gains. What happens next will say much about the government's shifting priorities.
