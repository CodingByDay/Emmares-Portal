There’s more good news than bad about COVID-19 at the moment: 51% of all adults in the U.S. have received at least one dose of one of the vaccines, which is likely helping to arrest what had recently looked like an upward trend in infections. But there’s still a lot that scientists don’t know about the virus that they will be trying to answer in the coming months.

One of those questions: Why does COVID-19 have such widely varying effects on people? It’s likely that other health issues play a role, but no firm rules have been found so far.

How long will immunity last, and will booster shots be needed?

It is known that people can be reinfected, but how many will be? Will reinfections always be mild?

Will COVID variants be a major problem or a minor one? It appears that existing vaccines are still effective against the variants, even if less so than against the first strain. But scientists will be kept busy for a long time sequencing COVID-19 to try to find new variants.

Most people who get infected get over the symptoms in a week or less. Why do a few experience symptoms for months? So far, there does not seem to be any correlation to age in these cases.

How does the virus affect kids? Can kids infect others even if they do not have any symptoms? Can vaccines be given to the very young (under 12)?

Why do certain people spread the virus widely, while 80% of those infected don’t infect anyone else?

Will herd immunity actually be achieved? That’s the point where enough members of a population are resistant to the virus that it stops spreading because it can’t find new hosts.
