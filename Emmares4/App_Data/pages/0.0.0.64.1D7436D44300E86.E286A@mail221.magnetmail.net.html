Don’t be disappointed by April’s jobs report, which showed “only” 266,000 net new jobs created in the month, versus Wall Street expectations of close to a million. But there was still plenty of good news contained within the report. Hiring in service industries like dining, lodging and recreation grew strongly, as more Americans get vaccinated and resume things like going out to eat and vacationing. Also, more people who hadn’t been working or seeking a job started looking for one in April, which bodes well for future hiring. Employers have been struggling to get enough workers as the economy picks up steam, so it’s a good sign that the ranks of jobseekers are growing. And more part-time workers got full-time work in April. But there are also some worrying signs, such as the decline in manufacturing jobs. That may be the result of auto companies and others slowing production because of logistical problems like the shortage of semiconductors. Those constraints could keep the jobs market from hitting on all cylinders in coming months.

On the public health front, a third shot of Moderna's (MRNA) vaccine increased effectiveness against the major COVID-19 variants, according to a study. This result is also likely to be true of the Pfizer (PFE) vaccine, which uses the same technology. This means that booster shots could be used to address any future infection spikes due to variants.
 
“DIY” investing is still in style. BofA Global Research’s sixth annual Millennial Home Improvement survey will tickle the likes of Home Depot (HD) and Lowe’s (LOW) shareholders. Eighty-two percent of respondents in the survey, which polled more than 1,100 men and women in their 20s and 30s, said that “in order to save money, they are likely to buy an older home and renovate it rather than purchase a newly built home.” That’s not all talk, either. Fifty-two percent of those surveyed had already started home improvement projects within six months of their purchase. And “given that affordability was cited as a top reason for not buying a home … we view Millennials’ willingness to renovate older homes as a bullish indicator for future home improvement demand.” Unsurprisingly, that bodes well for outperform-rated Home Depot and Lowe’s, which were Nos. 1 and 3, respectively, when respondents were asked where they were most likely to do home improvement shopping (Amazon.com was No. 2.) But BofA sees several other retailers benefiting from this trend as well, including Bed Bath & Beyond (BBBY), Floor and Decor Holdings (FND) and RH (RH).
