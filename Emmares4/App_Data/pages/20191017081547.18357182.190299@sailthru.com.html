THE BIG STORY

What to expect when you’re impeaching: Republicans lay out what Trump’s Senate trial will look like

 

Before diving in to this story, some news this morning: Rep. Elijah Cummings, one of the most powerful Democrats in Congress, has died of longtime health challenges. He was chair of the House Oversight Committee that’s involved in the Trump impeachment inquiry.


Background about today’s story: if the House votes to impeach President Donald Trump, the next step in the process is for the Senate to hold a full trial.


Among the questions floating around about impeachment, this was a big one: could Senate Republicans use their majority to dismiss the case, instead of subjecting Trump to a high-profile trial?


That speculation was laid to rest this week. Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell presented the plan: the Senate trial would begin every day at around 12:30 p.m. and run six days per week with only Sundays off. All senators must be present and, in a break from norms and nature, they must remain silent.


House lawyers would serve the role of prosecutors, while White House lawyers would be defense counsel. Supreme Court Chief Justice John Roberts would act as judge. Senators would serve as the jury.

STAYING ON TOP OF THIS

 

Trump rambled his way through a defense of his Syria decision that made no sense

 

Trump defended his decision to move US troops out of the way of a Turkish invasion of Syria in a press conference that was freewheeling and unusual, even by the president’s standards.


At one point, the president said that the US no longer needs to care about the Middle East, because “it's 7,000 miles away.”


Trump rejected the notion that the US enabled Turkey’s military action, and said he wrote a letter to Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan. 


In the letter, which was first obtained by Fox Business, Trump tells Erdogan not to be a “tough guy.” The letter — which opens with the line “let’s work out a great deal!” — was so bizarre, many people thought it was a hoax.


👉 Lost in all this mess is an important origin story: The Kurds, who now find themselves facing an assault from Turkish forces, saved the US from ISIS. That’s how all of this began.

THE OTHER BIG STORY

 

Boris Johnson has agreed a new Brexit deal with the EU

 

The British Prime Minister locked down an agreement with the European Union at the 11th hour before a key summit in Brussels. Johnson now faces the casual and not all daunting task of convincing the House of Commons to back his deal.


After days of intense negotiations, the UK and the EU announced that they reached an agreement over the key Irish border issue.


Under the agreement, Northern Ireland would be part of the UK's customs territory, however it would follow EU customs rules and law, avoiding a customs border with Ireland.


Johnson will try to get the deal passed in Parliament at a special sitting on Saturday.

TRAP KING

 

How a massive Facebook scam siphoned millions of dollars from unsuspecting boomers

Online scams come in varying degrees of sophistication. In his latest story, Craig Silverman details how Asher Burke built his company, Ads Inc, on the back of one of the internet’s most persistent, lucrative, and sophisticated scams — the subscription trap. And then it all fell apart.


The subscription trap is this: it works by tricking people into buying what they think is a free trial of a celebrity-endorsed product. The celebrity has nothing to do with the offer, but in purchasing the free trial, the customer unwittingly commits to a pricey monthly subscription designed to be hard to cancel.


Classic, right? Except Burke fused the trap with an operation that relied on convincing thousands of average people to rent their personal Facebook accounts to the company, which Ads Inc. then used to place ads for its deceptive free trial offers.
