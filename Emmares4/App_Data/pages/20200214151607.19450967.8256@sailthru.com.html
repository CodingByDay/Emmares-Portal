With the count of coronavirus cases passing 60,000 amid fresh concerns over the adequacy of diagnostic testing tools, airlines and travelers aren’t taking any chances. For some passengers, that’s meant sporting face masks, or, in at least once case, a wearable plastic tent. For many others, that’s meant staying home.  

China was operating at a third of its typical international aviation capacity as of last week, OAG Aviation Worldwide analyst John Grant wrote in a Feb. 10 note. The 1.4 million decline in international seats is roughly equivalent to all of the flights scheduled to go in and out of France last week, Grant said. Some 718,000 seats were still scheduled to operate out of China at that point in time, suggesting the total number of cancellations may have grown further. American Airlines Group Inc. said this week that it would extend the suspension of Dallas and Los Angeles flights to China and Hong Kong through late April, citing a “reduction in demand.” The International Civil Aviation Organization projected as much as a 41% decline in China-related flights over the course of the first three months of the year, compared with pre-virus expectations for a 9% increase in capacity. That will cost airlines as much as $5 billion in gross operating revenue.

It’s not just China, though: the Singapore Air Show was all but deserted this week as many companies stayed away. The Mobile World Congress — one of the largest smartphone events, which was set to begin Feb. 24 in Barcelona — was canceled, prompting United Airlines Holdings Inc. to scale back flights. The London International Petroleum Week gathering of the world’s top oil traders the same week is expected to be lightly attended. Grant of OAG even questioned whether the coronavirus may impact attendance at the summer Olympics in Japan, which begin on July 24.

All of this threatens to cast a cloud on aerospace, which has remained a relative bright spot among industrial companies throughout the 2015-2016 oil price slump and recent trade-war weakness. While it’s a manufacturing-based sector, its prospects are tied more readily to consumer demand for travel. The continuation of robust growth on that front looks more uncertain than it has in a long while. Even without factoring in the impact of the coronavirus, the International Air Transport Association was forecasting 4.1% passenger traffic growth in 2020, slightly below a 2019 pace that was the weakest since the financial crisis a decade ago. Boeing Co. and its supply chain are grappling with the production halt for the grounded 737 Max that took effect in January, as well as lackluster demand for the biggest jets amid a softer global economy and the Trump administration’s trade wars. A flood of grounded Max jets set to enter the market later this year will likely pressure U.S. airlines’ ability to raise fares, and projections for modest U.S. economic growth may test their willingness to follow through with expansion plans. 

Could the virus “be the proverbial straw that breaks the camel’s back?,” Vertical Research Partners analyst Rob Stallard wrote in a Feb. 10 report. “It’s too early to say, and unfortunately, aerospace companies are normally the last to get the memo.” Indeed, Boeing Chief Financial Officer Greg Smith insisted this week that the underlying demand outlook sustaining the aerospace industry really hasn’t changed. That’s supported by his estimate that the Max grounding wiped as much as 1.5% off of passenger traffic last year, suggesting that without that, there wouldn't have been as big of a downshift from the 5.5% longer-term trend documented by IATA. But he acknowledged that the coronavirus drag on air traffic is likely to impact first-quarter deliveries and is a “watch item.” 

Boeing hasn’t had commercial orders from China in more than two years, Smith said, and it’s possible that the U.S.-China trade deal will change that. But Bloomberg News has reported that China is hoping for flexibility on purchasing commitments agreed to as part of that deal because of the hit to its economy from the coronavirus. Meanwhile, Randy Tinseth, vice president of marketing in Boeing’s commercial aircraft unit and one of the few executives to actually attend the Singapore Air Show, told reporters there this week that the coronavirus makes it unlikely global air cargo traffic will grow this year. Boeing had previously forecast 1% to 2% growth, compared with a 3.3% decline in freight tonne kilometers in 2019 as measured by IATA.

The question is whether this is all just a blip or the beginning of a bigger sea change. Analysts and investors have tried to benchmark the potential financial fallout of the coronavirus to the 2003 SARS outbreak, which Cowen & Co. analyst Cai von Rumohr notes caused a six-month decline in international air traffic. That comparison is problematic on several fronts, with notable differences in the way the two diseases are spread and a significant increase in China’s share of the global economy since the SARS outbreak. Perhaps underappreciated is the rise in the pervasiveness of social media over that time period as well. Panic can be a very powerful thing, and consumer concerns about the safety of Boeing’s Max amid a prolonged grounding are set to collide online with fears of contracting the coronavirus while flying. Not to mention flight-shaming, which is increasingly in vogue amid rising concerns about global warming, with JetBlue Airways Corp. CEO Robin Hayes warning last month of the need to stay on top of the "clear and present danger" to airlines' profits and Delta Air Lines Inc. CEO Ed Bastian committing to spend $1 billion over the next decade to mitigate emissions from its business. Aerospace investors may need some of those wearable plastic tents for the aftermath.

WHERE DID ALL THE FILES GO?
There was a smattering of developments in the area of industrial software this week, a good reminder that many companies remain focused on expanding digital applications for manufacturing and other processes, even if some of the initial hype has died down. Aker BP ASA rolled out a robot (named Spot for its doglike appearance) that will patrol its oil-and-gas vessel at the Skarv field in the Norwegian Sea. Atlas Copco AB agreed to buy Isra Vision AG for about 1.1 billion euros ($1.2 billion), adding robot-vision and surface-inspection tools. And lastly, Schneider Electric SE agreed to buy RIB Software SE, a maker of construction-management software, for 1.4 billion euros ($1.5 billion). Schneider is no stranger to software, having partnered with Aveva Group Plc in 2018 for digital tools that make power plants and factories run more smoothly. But the idea behind the RIB purchase is to involve the company in the design and construction phase of all-digital and all-electric buildings that Schneider can then monitor and maintain through its own connected product offerings, which is an interesting strategy.

Neither the Isra Vision deal nor the RIB Software purchase came cheap, with both acquirers paying a more than 40% premium to the targets’ unaffected price. But notably, both Atlas Copco and Schneider rose modestly on news of their respective deals. The jury is still out on the potential returns from these investments in terms of new revenue lines, but there are clear internal margin and safety benefits as well as customer retention advantages. Investors seem willing to accept these investments as strategic steps forward — as long as the sales pitch is grounded in reality.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Emerson Electric Co.  isn’t going to break itself up right now, rebuffing calls from activist investor D.E. Shaw to separate a hodgepodge of climate, tools and home-product businesses from its automation unit. Instead, Emerson will spend $4 billion on M&A over the next few years and only split itself up if there’s a “major strategic acquisition catalyst” for doing so. Emerson’s goal of generating $5 in earnings per share by 2023 depends on that M&A push delivering a 15 cent boost and $1.6 billion of incremental revenue. It was debatable how much value would have been created by breaking Emerson up, with most analysts’ estimates implying only modest upside. But the company’s track record on M&A – which includes a $508 million writedown related to the $1.5 billion acquisition of Chloride Group Plc and a failed, extremely pricey pursuit of Rockwell Automation Inc. – may help explain why the stock fell more than 3% on the day. But Emerson does appear to be taking D.E. Shaw’s cost-cutting message to heart. The company is planning to step up its restructuring efforts to offset a still-sluggish growth environment, with a goal of reaping $450 million of savings by 2023. Separately, the company signaled long-time CEO Dave Farr would retire in fiscal 2021, along with CFO Frank Dellaquila, with “major” leadership changes set to be announced later this year. 

Bombardier Inc.’s great unwinding advanced a few steps this week, with the Canadian company agreeing to officially exit the C-series jet program whose escalating development costs left it saddled with debt. Airbus SE will pay $591 million to raise its stake in the regional aircraft business – now dubbed the A220 – to 75%. The Quebec government, which was forced to step in and backstop the Bombardier jet in 2016, will see its share of the venture increase to 25%. Bombardier is no longer on the hook for funding the A220’s production and that’s what’s most important to the cash-strapped company right now, but it’s incredibly sad that someone else will capitalize on burgeoning demand for the jet’s promising features. Bombardier’s withdrawal from commercial aerospace is an “acknowledgement of how hard it is to make money selling big aircraft,” even for heavyweights Boeing and Airbus, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Chris Bryant. What’s even worse is this doesn’t ease Bombardier’s debt burden by all that much. The company is also reportedly in advanced talks to sell its rail business to Alstom SA, with Handelsblatt reporting a potential purchase price of about 7 billion euros ($7.6 billion).

Honeywell International Inc. is reportedly exploring a sale of its personal protective-equipment unit. The business is part of the safety and productivity division that’s been a drag on Honeywell’s growth of late amid a broader industrial slowdown. People familiar with the matter told Bloomberg News the protective-gear business could fetch as much as $2 billion. Things like work boots and gloves are more commoditized and may not fit with the company’s pivot toward industrial software, notes Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Joel Levington. 3M Co., by contrast, has invested significantly in protective gear. The company bought Scott Safety – which made respiratory-protection products and thermal-imaging devices – for $2 billion in 2017 and acquired Capital Safety, which made harnesses and safety nets, for $2.5 billion in 2015 from KKR & Co.  

Thyssenkrupp AG asked for final offers for its elevator business by Feb. 11. There’s no official decision yet, but it’s not looking good for rival Kone Oyj’s proposal. Even with the company offering a practically unheard-of non-refundable down payment on a takeover and lining up a deal to sell Thyssenkrupp’s European operations to private equity firm CVC Capital Partners, key parties on the sellers’ side still have concerns about the antitrust process. Thyssenkrupp is understandably gun-shy after a previous deal to merge its steel operations with Tata Steel Ltd. was derailed by European regulators. Even if a deal does eventually pass muster, the review process likely would take about a year, much longer than any deal with one of the private equity suitors still circling Thyssenkrupp’s elevator unit. And one thing that Thyssenkrupp doesn't have is time: fiscal first quarter results released this week showed debt climbing and losses deepening. Kone reportedly increased its bid to more than 17 billion euros in the latest round of bidding, with the private equity firms offering close to 16 billion euros. A consortium led by Blackstone Group Inc. had the highest offer among the financial bidders.
