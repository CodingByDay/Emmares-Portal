Matt Levine

WeContinue

I promise you that no one is more excited than I am about the day I get to stop writing about WeWork forever, but we are not quite there yet. We stuff just keeps happening. Here, for instance, is a story about how WeWork’s employees are mad that their options are all underwater, which is both reasonable (they were induced to effectively invest in a fast-growing startup that ended up exploding) and kind of not (they worked at a money furnace that lost almost $2 billion a year and destroyed $40 billion of market value in a few months; why should they get equity upside?).

They’re particularly mad that, while their equity value has evaporated, founder Adam Neumann will walk away with over a billion dollars in payment for his stock and also a big consulting contract:

At a meeting with employees Wednesday morning, Marcelo Claure, a top SoftBank official and WeWork’s new executive chairman, said that some of the payout to Mr. Neumann was necessary to persuade him to give up his substantial control over the company, according to a person who heard the remarks.

Yeah no that doesn’t make it better? “We had to give him a billion dollars to go away because we couldn’t afford to have him stick around,” is the basic message here; his value to the company was negative a billion dollars.[1] As a decision of corporate strategy that is perfectly defensible, but as consolation to employees it is pretty cold. “We can’t pay you for your good work because it was more urgent to pay your boss a billion dollars to stop doing his bad work.” It probably was! But it raises uncomfortable questions about why he was the boss and they weren’t. “WeWork HR Invites Employees To Sign Goodbye Checks For Departing CEO,” says the Onion.

Also I am old enough to remember last month, when the prospectus for WeWork’s failed initial public offering said that the company’s “future success depends in large part on the continued service of Adam Neumann, our Co-Founder and Chief Executive Officer, which cannot be ensured or guaranteed.” Six weeks ago he was essential to the company’s success, and the possibility that he might quit was a material risk that shareholders needed to be warned about. Now they are building a giant wall of reinforced money just to keep him out.

Speaking of material risks, here’s a funny story about how “Japanese conglomerate SoftBank Group Corp. plans to seek approval of its WeWork bailout package with a U.S. government committee that reviews corporate deals for national security risks.” Is there a chance that the Committee on Foreign Investment in the U.S. will say no? Is WeWork a strategic asset? Are there CIA teams working out of its shared offices? Or is the ability to vaporize money on this scale so powerful that we can’t run the risk of it falling into hostile hands? 

Elsewhere, “WeWork will spend the next month putting together a plan that will prioritize profit over growth, executives said in a meeting with employees Wednesday.” Seems reasonable? “The goal is to narrow the unprofitable company’s focus to its original business of renting and renovating office spaces.” They’re going to try the IPO again in a year, aren’t they? Maybe it’ll be straightforward and boring, a profitable slow-growing company going public at a valuation in line with its peers, and I can just sit it out.

Global insider trading ring

Doesn’t it sound like a “global insider trading ring” should be a powerful shadowy network of senior executives at big investment banks and other advisory firms who feed information to leading hedge fund managers? There are occasional glimpses of that world—the head of McKinsey & Co. once fed secret information from a Goldman Sachs Group Inc. board meeting to the head of a multibillion-dollar hedge fund!—and it is fun to imagine that those are the tip of the iceberg, that there is a secret Illuminati of illegal trading at the heart of the capital markets.

But the actual alleged global insider trading ring that U.S. prosecutors and the Securities and Exchange Commission busted this week is, like, eh, I don’t know, it’s fine:

U.S. prosecutors announced criminal charges against six people in what they described as a global insider-trading ring that generated tens of millions of dollars in profits.

The charges announced Tuesday include Joseph El-Khouri, 52, a securities trader who lives in London and Monaco. Bloomberg News has previously reported on the cases against the other five people charged, including an investment banker at Goldman Sachs Group Inc., Bryan Cohen, accused of passing confidential information to a securities trader in Switzerland. … Among those accused are Benjamin Taylor, 35, who previously worked at Moelis & Co., and Darina Windsor, 32, who had worked at Centerview Partners LLC in London. They’re charged with passing inside information on 22 companies.

Goldman and Moelis and Centerview are big names, but these particular people were pretty junior. It is unclear from the complaints how they all got involved with each other, and frankly whether they were all involved with each other; this seems to have been less a “ring” of criminals with gang signs and a joint esprit de corps, and more a loosely overlapping network of people who got tips from some of the other people. I hope someone put out an ad for, like, “international bankers wanted for secret insider trading ring,” and these were the random people who showed up.

Still, some of them showed some spirit. Taylor and Windsor, two of the alleged tipsters, were in a romantic relationship with each other, and while they “used encrypted messaging applications and unregistered ‘burner’ cellphones to communicate with each other and arrange in-person meetings to discuss their scheme,” they also allegedly did this:

On or about October 10, 2012, WINDSOR sent an email to TAYLOR titled “Once upon a time, there was a Pops searching for Truffles in the Forest ...” “Pops” and “Popsy” were names that TAYLOR and WINDSOR used to refer to each other. Attached to the email was confidential information relating to Onyx, one of Investment Bank B’s [i.e. Centerview’s] clients, which WINDSOR had obtained from Investment Bank B’s computer systems.

Yeah look I have a lot of objections here, let’s just list them:

  1. If you are going to use a system of codewords to refer to illegal inside information, don’t use “truffles,” it is too on-the-nose.
  2. Also don’t attach the information, that really undermines the secrecy.
  3. If you are going to use burner cellphones, encrypted messaging and in-person meetings to avoid creating a record of your illegal activities, you gotta do that consistently.
  4. Also maybe save the cutesy pet names for those encrypted channels, because otherwise your indictment will definitely mention them.

Also!

Taylor and Windsor participated in the scheme because they expected to receive a share of the profits. Indeed, in February or March 2013, shortly after the scheme began, Windsor created an excel file, entitled Popsy (her pet name for Taylor), that detailed how the profit would be split from an expected investment. …

Among other things, the spreadsheet calculates the amount of specific US and British currency, in denominations of hundred dollar bills and 50 pound notes respectively, required to make the requisite cash payment of Windsor’s share of the trading proceeds.

I have omitted the actual spreadsheet, but as Sujeet Indap pointed out on Twitter it follows the investment banking convention of coloring input numbers in blue. After four years in investment banking I also do this instinctively, and am not capable of making a spreadsheet without blue inputs, so I can understand how it happened in their alleged crime spreadsheet. On the other hand calculating the number of $100 bills required for a transaction is absolutely not standard practice in investment banking. There’s not generally a line in the merger spreadsheet calculating the purchase price in briefcases full of cash. 

Disappointingly Windsor and Taylor were not indicted as “DARINA WINDSOR, a/k/a POPS,” etc.; the securities prosecutors still have a lot to learn from the mob prosecutors. Also they did get one piece of tradecraft very right: They “aren’t in custody, with Taylor now in France and Windsor in Thailand.” 

Manipulation

The thing to realize is that, on the face of it, this makes no sense:

The SEC alleges that the traders, who are primarily based in China, manipulated the prices of thousands of thinly traded securities by creating the false appearance of trading interest and activity in those stocks, thereby enabling them to reap illicit profits by artificially boosting or depressing stock prices. For example, according to the SEC's complaint, the traders used multiple accounts to place several small sell orders to drive down a stock’s price before using a different set of accounts to buy larger amounts of the stock at the artificially low prices. After accumulating their position, the traders then flipped the script and placed several small buy orders to push up prices so they could then sell their stock at artificially high prices.

That’s from the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission’s announcement last week of charges against 18 traders, “who are primarily based in China,” for an alleged “scheme to manipulate more than 3,000 U.S.-listed securities for over $31 million in illicit profits.” It’s a pretty standard description of stock manipulation: First (1) you sell a little stock to drive down the price, then (2) you buy a lot of the stock at the new artificially depressed price, then (3) you buy a little more stock to drive up the price, then (4) you sell a lot of stock at the new artificially inflated price, etc.; it is a financial perpetual motion machine, but why? If selling a little stock drives down the price (step 1), and buying a little stock drives it up (step 3), then why wouldn’t buying a lot of stock (in step 2) drive up the price more and eat up all of your profits? How do you do some (“manipulative”) buying and selling noisily, in a way that moves the price in your favor, and other (“real”) buying and selling silently, in a way that doesn’t move the price against you? You need some sort of specific market-microstructure explanation of how that would work, some reason why markets would sometimes respond to supply and demand by moving dramatically and other times not respond at all.

And the SEC has a good one. From its complaint:

One or more helper accounts placed orders on stock exchanges to sell a thinly traded security at prices below the prevailing national best bid or offer (“NBBO”). These orders had two related functions: (1) sell orders placed at prices at or below the prevailing national best bid (“NBB”) were used to lower the NBB by removing buying interest at that price level, and (2) sell orders placed at prices above the prevailing NBB and below the prevailing national best offer (“NBO”) were used to set new, lower NBOs.

After the NBBO had been artificially depressed, one or more winner accounts placed large buy orders on non-exchange venues. The operator(s) of the winner accounts typically placed the orders from accounts at different brokerage firms and/or in different names than the helper accounts. These winner account orders were often placed while there were outstanding sell orders from helper accounts that should have at least partially filled them. However, because the operator(s) of the winner accounts caused their orders to be sent to non-exchange venues, the winner and helper account orders rarely crossed. Instead, the winner account orders were usually filled at artificially high or low prices within the manipulated NBBO at non-exchange venues.

The trick here is a sort of arbitrage between stock exchanges, where prices are displayed publicly, and “non-exchange venues” where they aren’t. “Non-exchange venues” include dark pools (venues that banks and brokers operate that allow institutions and high-frequency traders to trade with each other without publicly displaying prices), internalizers (brokers filling orders from their own inventory) and wholesalers (high-frequency traders who get orders from retail brokers, fill them from inventory, and pay the retail brokers for the orders). Orders on stock exchanges are “lit”: You can see the highest bid price that someone is willing to pay for a stock (and how much they’re willing to buy), and the lowest offer price that someone is willing to accept for it (and how much they’re willing to sell), and if you want to sell quickly you can hit the bid and sell immediately at that price. Orders on other venues generally aren’t lit, but they price based on the lit prices on the exchange: A dark pool or wholesaler can’t buy stock from you for less than the national best bid on a lit stock exchange, and in fact will often give you “price improvement,” paying you a bit more for your stock than you’d get on an exchange.

All of this stuff is super controversial in various directions, and we talk sometimes about how angry people get at wholesalers paying retail brokers for order flow, but the point here is that it opens up a nice little trade for manipulators. You find a small stock that doesn’t trade that much. Electronic market makers will quote it on stock exchanges, but their quotes will be small (for not that many shares) and wide (the bid price they’re willing to pay for the stock is much lower than the offer price they’re willing to accept for it). Perhaps the quote will be $9.12 bid / $9.43 offered.[2] You send orders to the stock exchanges to sell small lots (1 to 100 shares) “at progressively lower prices from $9.41 to $9.12 per share,” which have the effect of (1) lowering the best offer (because now you are offering shares at lower prices than $9.43) and (2) actually selling some shares and “taking out” the best bid (because whoever was bidding $9.12 will buy your shares at $9.12). You won’t sell that many shares, but you’ll sell some, and the market will reprice to, say, $9.05 bid / $9.14 offered.

If you then just sent buy orders to the stock exchange, the stock would reprice right back up and you’d lose money, but you don’t. Instead you do a big off-exchange trade:

Xiaosong Wang placed, directly or indirectly, an order to buy 6,000 shares of CSSE with a limit price of $9.20 per share through an account in his own name. The 6,000 share order was immediately filled by another market participant at a price of $9.127 per share.

Why would that “other market participant”—a wholesaler or internalizer, or a market maker in a dark pool—do that trade with you? Well the very rough theory is something like (1) the lit market price—the national best bid and offer—is “right,” so if you do a trade at a price between the bid and offer then you are getting a fair price, and (2) trades on non-exchange venues are not supposed to move the price: If you trade 6,000 shares in a dark pool, that is all the stock you want to buy and you will not go out to public markets and push up the price; if you buy 6,000 shares from a wholesaler, you are a retail customer and, again, you will not go out and push up the price. And so the wholesaler (or whoever) is happy to sell you stock at $9.127, a little bit lower than the best public-market price, because the point of the off-exchange venue is to cherry-pick “good” orders that will not move the price as much as orders in the public markets. Or that is the idea.

But in fact you are a very “bad” order, and after you buy a lot of stock off-exchange at the new lower price, you go back and buy small lots of stock on the exchange, pushing the price right back up. And then when it’s high again you unload your stock, at the new high price, off the exchange.

A recurring theme around here is that it is weirdly hard to define “market manipulation.” On the one hand this is the absolutely most classic stereotype of market manipulation: selling stock to push down the price to artificial levels so you can buy, then buying stock to push up the price so you can sell. On the other hand it is a little hard to spot the fraud here: Unlike pump-and-dump schemes, these traders weren’t publishing false rumors about the stock; unlike in spoofing, they were putting in many “manipulative” orders that were intended to (and did) execute[3]; unlike in wash trading, they weren’t just trading back and forth with themselves to create a fake price. They were creating real prices by really selling stock to willing buyers on the stock exchange. And then they were trading at those real prices in other venues. The bad part is that they took advantage of a quirk in market structure, one that makes it possible to do a manipulation that otherwise shouldn’t work.

Work stuff

I often read stories about new experiments in workplace organization, and a lot of them are horrifying (desks that narc on slackers), and some are okay-sure-but-I-have-concerns (banning after-hours emails), and some are horrifying-yet-intriguing (everything about Bridgewater), but this might be the first purely good business story I have ever read:

At the firm he renamed Rheingans Digital Enabler, the 16 employees start work at 8 a.m. and may leave at 1 p.m.  Mr. Rheingans, the firm's managing director, says employees can deliver the same output during a focused 25-hour week as in 40 hours interrupted with distractions.

“We have all experienced that: We sit in the office, out of energy, reading newspapers online or Facebook, just in need of the little pauses to recharge, but you don’t really recharge,” he says. “My idea is focusing on the first five hours and then just leave, and have a proper break.”

To accomplish that, small talk during work hours is discouraged. Social media is banned. Phones are kept in backpacks. Company email accounts are checked just twice a day. Most meetings are scheduled to last no more than 15 minutes.

As a result, the company produces the same level of output for clients despite shorter days, says Mr. Rheingans. He says the company, which develops websites, apps and e-commerce platforms, was profitable in 2018, the first full year he owned it. He says happier employees deliver better work for clients, and the shorter workday is a draw, boosting recruitment in Germany’s tight labor market. 

And!

At Digital Enabler, a monitor displays the hours, minutes and seconds remaining in the workday. At 1 p.m., the display changed to “#high5, #feierabend”—or closing time in German.

It is not perfect, some days they work later to meet client deadlines, but, you know, so do you, and you don’t leave at 1 on the other days. It is as perfect as professional work is going to get. Read the whole thing, every sentence makes me happy, particularly this spectacular bit of negotiating strategy:

At a previous firm, Mr. Rheingans took a salary cut so he could spend two afternoons a week with his children. A few months later he asked for his salary to be reinstated because he was producing as much work as before. His partners agreed, though, he says, they were rankled by the arrangement. 

Try that at your investment banking job!
