This week perhaps more than ever, politics felt inescapable. A certain memo kept coming up everywhere (even if the IT facepalm it included didn't). October 1, by the way, officially marks... 13 more months until the 2020 elections.

 

But long before this latest news surfaced, Ars had already planned on spending a quiet, intellectually stimulating weekend at the 2019 Texas Tribune Festival in Austin, Texas. Sponsored by a nonprofit news organization of the same name, this event has quietly become one of the premier political gatherings in the country where ordinary people can attend and hear directly from those making decisions at various capitols. Jeh Johnson was on hand talking election security. The honorable John Bates, who once served as the presiding judge of the US Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (aka FISA) in the post-Snowden world, would be reflecting on his career. Speaker of the House Nancy Pelosi was long scheduled to deliver the closing keynote, where she was presumably going to talk about something else until, well, you know.

 

It's still a bit early for Ars to dig-in fully to all the various ways the 2020 campaign will influence the scientific and technical world, but a weekend at TribFest certainly gave us a small taste of what may be on the horizon—which we'll share in this week's Orbital Transmission. Everyone knows health care and climate change will factor in prominently (perhaps the best one-liner, from candidate Pete Buttigieg: "When I say we need a carbon neutral policy by 2020... a middle-aged Greta Thunberg can approach me in 2050, then a retired ex-president, and I can know I worked towards a real solution"), but hopefully broadband connectivity, social media influence campaigns, Big Tech's effect on competition, and more will get some air time soon, too.

If Big Tech could take a straw poll...

TribFest, like all self-respecting political events, had a straw poll. And if those numbers came to fruition, evidently Big Tech would be displeased. Elizabeth Warren led the way among TribFest attendees, and just days later an audio recording leaked from Facebook revealed CEO Mark Zuckerberg thinks that wouldn't be great for business. "If [Warren] gets elected president, then I would bet that we will have a legal challenge, and I would bet that we will win the legal challenge," he prophesied. "And does that still suck for us? Yeah..." Warren made her plan to breakup Big Tech companies public way back ahead of SXSW, and many, many federal agencies have since started their own antitrust probes into the likes of Google, Facebook, and Amazon.

 

Can we get a little campaign time for connectivity?

Some of the topics most dear to Ars—climate change, health—are certainly getting their share of airtime in the early goings of election 2020. But when it comes to perhaps the core IT concerns of Americans—net neutrality and the broadband divide—those topics have remained conspicuously absent. Hopefully as things progress and the field narrows, the range of topics can widen to include those. But it gave us a little smirk when at least one candidate—Minnesota Senator Amy Klobuchar—made sure to mention this as an imperative. "We can hook up every county and rural area to broadband by 2022," she told NBC's Steve Kornacki on-stage. "If they can do it around volcanoes in Iceland, we can do it in Texas." (A recent study noted it'd take $70 billion to get 90% of US households to fiber broadband by 2025; meanwhile the FCC still doesn't really know how many homes lack broadband today.)

 

This politician thinks tech can solve major policy issues—but it'll happen from the private sector

Texas Congressman Will Hurd (R) turned heads earlier this year when he announced he wouldn't be seeking re-election. Hurd happens to be the only Black Republican in Congress, which makes his absence somewhat devastating. Also bad, Hurd happens to be one of the few politicians with genuine tech bonafides—he studied computer science at Texas A&M and went on to a career in intelligence with the CIA before his political start. (He was set to keynote Black Hat 2019 until protests over Hurd's policies towards women's rights nudged him to withdraw.) Ostensibly, Hurd said he was leaving office to have a greater impact on tech issues that impact the nation. So at TribFest when asked about what's pressing on the tech front, he said, well, everything—tech can have an impact on all kinds of policy issues in Hurd's eyes. "Take gun violence—since 2017, there have been more deaths in Texas from gun violence than there are deaths in Afghanistan through the global war on terrorism. And in talking to my friends in the FBI, they’re overwhelmed with social media leads," Hurd said. "But right now, if the special agent in charge, the Senior FBI officer responsible for Austin, wanted to go to twitter.com and search for 'Attack, Paramount, Austin, Today’s Date'—they can’t do that. The DOJ has issued a policy saying that may be an infringement on privacy, but that’s publicly available information. Why don’t we have algorithms sitting on the social media side that are helping tip through these leads so the FBI can focus on the most important things? Ultimately, the way we’re going to solve a lot of these problems is with the private and public sector actively working together on this." (Photo: Texas Tribune / Bob Daemmrich)

 

The real tech issue of 2020 is likely the same as 2016

The most discussed tech topic of the entire TribFest was undoubtedly the biggest tech topic of 2016: social media influence. As articulated by South Bend, Indiana Mayor Pete Buttigieg, sometimes this was intentional. "Democrats like to think of themselves as digitally sophisticated, but recently we’ve been caught a little behind. We were very good at the first wave of digital organizing and at digital fundraising, but digital persuasion of the kind [Donald Trump's] campaign did in 2016, they were on the cutting edge." At other times, of course, it was social media influence coming from folks far outside the campaigns. (Russia tried in 2018, too, and US Cyber Command luckily responded.) "$3-4 billion is forecast to be spent on social media ads, it was $1.4 billion in 2016," Senator Klobuchar said, advocating for social media campaign ads transparency legislation. "There are no rules of the road. A Texas radio station has to say who sponsored an ad, but these companies under oath tell me that can't figure it out? In 2016, there was an ad of a black woman telling people to text their vote—it was paid for in rubles." (Photo: Texas Tribune / Bob Daemmrich)
