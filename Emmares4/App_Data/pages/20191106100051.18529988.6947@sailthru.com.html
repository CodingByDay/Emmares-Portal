Time (Mc)flies, eh? Thirty years ago this month, everyone's favorite future-looking sci-fi flick, the iconic Back To The Future II, hit theaters. Arriving just before Thanksgiving that year, it had the best holiday box office weekend of all-time at that point (eclipsing Rocky IV, eat that Drago).

 

The film famously depicted the year 2015, but this 2015 was filled with self-lacing shoes in every mud room and hydrating ovens in every kitchen. Some of the film's depictions clearly turned true in the subsequent thirty years—self-lacing shoes can even be bricked in 2019—but sadly that can't be said for every futuristic flourish Spielberg and Zemeckis gave to the beloved Marty McFly.

 

With Halloween just passed and another year of McFly impersonators fresh on our minds (to say nothing of a different '80s franchise back in theaters this week), we wanted to dedicate the latest Orbital HQ to how Hill Valley, California's greatest adventure has impacted our world today. Maybe the Cubs took one year too many to finally win that World Series, but no one made Back To The Future II for gambling—they made this film to travel through time.

 

—@NathanMattise

Orbital Transmission 11.06.2019
 

All good sci-fi uses fancy VR/AR glasses, BTTF II included

Fancy future glasses are a sci-fi trope almost as old as aliens, Back to The Future II included. Marty's future kitchen, for instance, features an AR headset kids can use to order food or answer the phone. (It even kinda, sorta looked like the original HoloLens.) Today's best VR tech is likely the Oculus Quest due to its combination of game offerings, affordability, and wireless usage, but AR-leaning headsets like the HoloLens aren't dead yet. In fact, Microsoft recently shared a research initiative for a headset with fully immersive VR capabilities meant to interact with the real world, all called DreamWalker VR. Evidently, your daily bodega walk may soon become a customizable experience.

 

Somehow, BTTF II's most retro tech is quietly making a comeback

That iconic DeLorean may have felt dated even back in 1989. But in recent years, the Texas-based DeLorean Motor Company quietly announced it's aiming to regularly produce a small number of new retro DeLoreans for sale (Marty's is sitting in a museum, after all). As of January 2019, DeLorean had just doubled its East Texas facilities with the hopes of getting production started for 2020, but updates have been mum since then. At worst, we can thank the classic sci-fi ride for (maybe) partially inspiring Tesla's Falcon wings and admire the fact researchers have made it autonomously drift.

 

Biometric actions are here, though not always perfect

In Marty's world, we've already evolved to the point where we can skip the phone entirely—Biff Tannen pays for a cab ride with a scan of his thumb; a fundraiser asks Marty to help save the clock tower with, “Hey kid, thumb a hundred bucks will ya, help save the clock tower.” We're not quite there yet in 2019, but our tech has increasingly come to rely upon biometric authentication (stuff that uses your thumbprint or facial scans and other unique identifiers). Unfortunately, even that level of ease isn't always perfect—last month, the new Galaxy S10 was having thumbprints spoofed via clear phone cases, and Google's new Pixel 4 could be unlocked with facial recognition even if a user kept their eyes closed.

 

30 years later, the world still waits on genuine hoverboards

The most famous bit of Back to the Future tech, of course, is that super cool hoverboard Marty uses to escape some local bullies. And despite products calling themselves "hoverboards" being readily available, none are really the same kind of personal aviation machine this film depicted. That hasn't stopped folks from trying, however, despite the results being kinda proof-of-concept, unbelievably expensive, and not entirely feasible. Oh well... at least tech today is good enough to *teach* us how to fly more traditional contraptions.
