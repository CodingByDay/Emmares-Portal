Transporting parts from country A to country B is an obstacle course that seems to only get more complicated. If the trade war showed the risks of globalized supply-chains, the coronavirus may solidify that business model as a liability. 

While some factories have started to come back on line, many remain closed as China tries to contain a respiratory illness that has already killed more than 2,000 people. Deere & Co. said Friday that it would spend an extra $40 million on expedited freight to help ensure the availability of parts for its international operations as the virus threatens its supply chain. Meanwhile, Nissan Motor Co. is bracing for disruptions and parts shortages at plants as far away as the U.S. should facilities in the outbreak epicenter of Hubei — which are responsible for some 800 types of vehicle parts — stay idled much longer. Three General Electric Co. sites in Hubei are still closed, and others elsewhere in China aren’t at full staffing levels, Chief Executive Officer Larry Culp said at a Citigroup Inc. conference this week. While there’s sufficient buffer inventory right now, Culp said he anticipates any disruption to the supply chain to show up later in the spring. 

General consensus among market analysts had been that the outbreak would be a passing blip from which the economy should recover quickly, but growing signs that both the virus and the business paralysis is beginning to spread beyond mainland China make that far from a guaranteed outcome. A notable driver of the better-than-expected preliminary IHS Markit read on German factory activity for February was a lengthening of delivery times. While that’s typically a reflection of such strong demand that companies are struggling to keep up, in this case it most likely reflects a breakdown in the supply chain from the coronavirus-linked shutdowns in Asia.  Even the comparatively robust U.S. economy felt the sting of the coronavirus, with a preliminary read from from IHS Markit pointing to the first contraction in business activity since 2013.

Whether the outbreak derails global growth or not, it’s likely to only speed a deeper reexamination of manufacturing supply chains and accelerate the unwinding of far-flung parts networks. Interestingly, the virus-driven disruption may reinforce the appeal of industrial-software offerings to guard against unplanned downtime. When a piece of equipment breaks, it’s not tenable for companies to wait days or weeks to obtain the spare part they need, and the virus makes that scenario all the more real, Craig Hayman, CEO of Aveva Group Plc, said in an interview at Bloomberg headquarters this week. The benefit of predictive maintenance analytics is that companies can foresee when their machinery will fail and plan accordingly. The goal is to drive efficiency whatever the conditions, whether that’s a slump in oil prices or a virus cutting off the flow of parts, Hayman said.
 

Many industrial companies like to talk about localized manufacturing strategies, but the tens of millions of dollars — or sometimes hundreds of millions — in increased costs that stemmed from Trump’s trade war with China suggests that circle wasn’t as fully closed as they might have wanted. This week brought another reminder of just how tenuous existing export policies are. The Wall Street Journal reported on efforts by some in the White House and Commerce Department to block GE from shipping Leap 1C engines for the development of Commercial Aircraft Corp. of China’s C919 jet. President Donald Trump later rebuffed the idea on Twitter, lamenting the national security “excuse” that he has had no problem invoking with regard to steel from Canada or cars from Germany. Is there a flop looming for this flip? Time will tell.

GE jet engines make for a odd trade-war pawn, not least of all because the technology is difficult to reverse engineer and any copying has probably already taken place. GE and its joint venture partner Safran SA have been exporting the Leap 1C engine to Comac for years, and the 1B and 1A variants are on Boeing Co.’s 737 Max and Airbus SE’s A320neo jets, which have already been delivered to local airlines. Comac’s C919 has been significantly delayed and is expected at best to be the technological equivalent of Boeing and Airbus jets that already have a large lead on orders. It’s worth reinforcing the point that neither Airbus nor Boeing make their own jet engines. While there’s always a risk of China developing a homegrown competitor in any industry, it’s also possible that Comac continues to mimic the business models of the incumbents and rely on the best and brightest technology providers. Cutting off the flow of GE engines would undoubtedly stymie Comac’s aerospace ambitions in the short term, but it may have the longer-term undesirable effect of driving the company to look further inward for its supply chain.

The message conveyed by these kinds of bizarre interludes for GE and other companies that do significant business with China is that perhaps it’s worth rethinking supply chains to avoid being dependent on export licenses from an administration that seems to change its mind about international trade every few minutes. Companies including Intel Corp. and Micron Technology Inc. have reportedly found ways to avoid labeling goods produced overseas as American-made, a loophole to the Trump administration’s crackdown on the flow of U.S. products to Huawei Technologies Co. A proposal to close those loopholes by requiring manufacturers to obtain an export license to use U.S. equipment when making products for Huawei was meant to be considered alongside the jet-engine crackdown at a Cabinet meeting that has since been canceled, according to the Wall Street Journal. But the smart companies aren’t likely to wait around to see how long Trump’s new-found commitment to easing the flow of goods will last. 

FLYING NOWHERE FAST
We talked last week about whether the coronavirus may be what finally grounds the aerospace boom, and things are certainly not looking good. Both Air Lease Corp. CEO John Plueger and the International Air Transport Association warned that global traffic growth may turn negative for the first time since the financial crisis amid a growing list of scuppered flights and events. IATA estimates a $30 billion drag on global airline revenue this year from the coronavirus but these estimates assume most of the pain is confined to markets linked to China. IATA had previously estimated a 4.1% increase in passenger traffic this year. Even the Olympics in Japan five months from now are increasingly subject to cancellation speculation, particularly after the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention issued a travel advisory for Japan and the Tokyo Marathon Foundation announced the March 1 race would be canceled for amateur runners.

Meanwhile, there’s no update to Boeing’s estimate for a mid-2020 return of its grounded 737 Max, but the hits keep on coming for the company. The latest slip-up involves debris such as tools or rags left in the fuel tanks of stored Max jets, which creates a safety hazard. Boeing CEO David Calhoun told Seattle’s King 5 News that the fact that the company disclosed this problem with the Max is a testament to its commitment to being “the most open book the world’s ever seen on this subject,” which I suppose is one generous way to look at it. Boeing also said it supports legislation introduced in Washington state this week to suspend tax breaks ruled illegal by the World Trade Organization in a long-standing dispute between the U.S. and the European Union over government aid for aircraft development. Rival Airbus is already subject to U.S. tariffs after a similar WTO ruling, and this move may help Boeing avoid the same fate. That’s a fair trade for a tax benefit that amounted to about $100 million in the most recent annual accounting at Boeing. But the proposed legislation will mean all aerospace companies will no longer be able to take advantage of the industry exception to the state’s business tax rate, according to the Seattle Times. Separately, in an acknowledgment that GE may have bet too big on the wrong horse with Boeing amid the Max production halt and a cut to 787 output, the company is reportedly in talks with Airbus about developing engines for the A330, having previouslyheld discussions on an alternative for the A350. 

Deals, Activists and Corporate Governance:
Guess who’s back, back again. 
While rapper Eminem did have a moment at the Oscars this year, I’m actually talking about Ed Breen, the man who salvaged Tyco International from an accounting scandal and orchestrated the merger and subsequent three-way breakup of Dow and DuPont. Breen had remained executive chairman of DuPont but handed over the CEO reins to Marc Doyle once the spinoffs were completed last year. Doyle’s brief tenure was marred by a sluggish industrial economy and muted payoff from the cascade of financial engineering, so Breen will become CEO again. CFO Jeanmarie Desmond is also out. Breen’s official return as CEO will most likely herald a step-up in cost-cutting and integration efforts and additional asset sales. The chemicals giant agreed in December to sell its nutrition and biosciences division to International Flavors & Fragrances Inc. through a tax-friendly Reverse Morris Trust transaction that values the business at about $26 billion. The company has also recently explored divesting its electronics unit, or the transportation and industrial business, people familiar with the matter have told Bloomberg News. 

Bombardier Inc. agreed to sell its rail unit to Alstom SA for as much as 6.2 billion euros ($6.7 billion). The takeover will help Alstom challenge the inroads made by China’s CRRC Corp. overseas, but the true appeal is the ability to repair Bombardier’s dismal margins, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Chris Bryant. While the rail tie-up may seem jarring so soon after regulators rejected Alstom’s combination with Siemens AG, CRRC has continued to expand its European presence and there’s less problematic overlap between Bombardier and Alstom. The companies have pledged to address whatever common ground there is with divestitures. For Bombardier, this was another asset sale that had to happen, but it means the onetime conglomerate is now a one-trick pony focused on private jets. There’s a reason its main rivals are part of larger, more diverse companies that can better absorb the volatility from this notoriously economically sensitive industry. So while Bombardier CEO Alain Bellemare has indicated the yard sale is over, it wouldn’t be surprising to see a bid emerge for the business-jet operations. Textron Inc. was reportedly in talks to buy the unit before the Alstom deal was sealed. Other possible buyers include Lockheed Martin Corp., which has signaled an interest in M&A recently.

Kone Oyj withdrew from competition to acquire Thyssenkrupp AG’s elevator unit, with CEO Henrik Ehrnrooth telling Finnish newspaper Kauppalehti that the risks were just too big. This included an unusual upfront down payment that would have been nonrefundable if antitrust regulators blocked a deal, or, as Ehrnrooth suggested, Thyssenkrupp went bankrupt. It’s hard to argue with him that this would have been a gamble, although one might have thought this would have been obvious earlier. Kone shares dropped on the loss of what Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Mustafa Okur called a “once-in-a-lifetime growth opportunity.” That leaves Thyssenkrupp with two private-equity consortium bidders and a reported target for signing a deal by the end of the month. Thyssenkrupp is leaning toward a full sale given its cash needs, according to a Reuters report.

A.P. Moller-Maersk A/S agreed to buy U.S. warehousing and distribution company Performance Team for an enterprise value of $545 million. The deal is part of Maersk’s efforts to expand into land-based logistics as its main container-shipping business grapples with the fallout from the U.S.-China trade war, new low-sulfur fuel standards and now the coronavirus. Maersk’s 2020 guidance lagged analysts’ estimates, but it wasn’t clear how much of an impact from the coronavirus was baked in, and analysts suggested the company may be conservative. Speaking on Bloomberg Television, Maersk CEO Soren Skou predicted a sharp rebound in container-shipping demand come April, having canceled more than 50 departures from China in the past two weeks. The roughly 1 times sales multiple that Maersk paid for Performance Team is quite reasonable, but I will just note that warehousing and distribution logistics is becoming an incredibly crowded field after a spate of private-equity deals.
