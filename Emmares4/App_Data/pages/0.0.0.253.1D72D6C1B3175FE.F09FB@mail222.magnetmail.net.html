What part should bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies play in your portfolio? Kiplinger has frequently asked experts whether people should invest in bitcoin and digital currencies, and the answer typically hinges on risk tolerance (in short, you need to have plenty of stomach for it). But should you decide to take the dive, what’s the proper approach? While opinions vary widely, numerous investment managers treat digital currencies like commodities and other alternatives, from an allocation standpoint. “Investors able to accept the wild swings in bitcoin (BTC) might do well with a small amount of exposure to the cryptocurrency asset class. We would suggest 1% to 3% of total liquid assets,” says Rick Lear, founder and managing partner of Dallas-based Lear Investment Management, with $500 million in assets under management. While he touts crypto’s lack of correlation to other asset classes, he also points out several risks, chief among them its volatility, as well as the high risk that “regulation makes investing in (bitcoin) more complicated and less profitable.” If you’d like to learn more about bitcoin and digital assets, check out our most recent Your Money’s Worth podcast, where we discuss the topic with Tyrone Ross, CEO and co-founder of next-gen investment platform Onramp Invest.

Recent jobs reports and other data suggest that the U.S. economy is ramping up, now that enough people are getting vaccinated to start controlling the COVID-19 pandemic. But it’s not just the U.S. economy that is hitting its stride. Worldwide economic growth looks poised for its best year in decades, according to this week’s forthcoming issue of The Kiplinger Letter. The Letter is predicting global GDP to expand by 6%, after last year’s brutal 3.3% contraction. The U.S. and China are leading the way and helping to power smaller countries that export a lot of goods to the two behemoths. Many regions continue to struggle with viral outbreaks, which means they aren’t yet seeing the economic benefits of large-scale vaccination campaigns. But Europe in particular looks like it’ll have enough people vaccinated to end or relax its current lockdown policies by the autumn, which should enable its economies to take off, too.
