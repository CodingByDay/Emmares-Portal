Andrew Yang is nothing but a conundrum for the tech industry. He’s called an entrepreneur, but he’s not really. He’s a master of campaigning on the Internet, but he’s largely campaigning against technology. If there’s a villain in his stump speeches, it’s Amazon, not Donald Trump. He’s a profound pessimist about the effect that technology will have on our economy, and he has proposed a universal basic income to help alleviate the pain he thinks will come. But unlike many of his fellow candidates, he doesn’t call for the breakup of Big Tech.

For the last month, I have been digging into the campaign and interviewing Yang—at his headquarters in New York, at a rally, and at a foosball table in San Francisco. This is a story about Yang’s life, his policies, and his campaign. When Yang said he was running for president, one friend told me that her immediate question was “president of what?” But now he’s in the thick of it—and nowhere near the front but nowhere near the back. He’s not likely to win, yet against the odds, he’s actually helping to shape a debate.

Nicholas Thompson | Editor in Chief, WIRED
