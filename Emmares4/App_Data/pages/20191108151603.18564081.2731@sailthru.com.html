In my book, the title of best industrial stock of 2019 is going once, going twice, sold — to an auctioneer of junked cars.

Copart Inc. is a $19 billion company that I had never heard of until this week, but its story has held more allure for investors this year than the aerospace growth at Honeywell International Inc, the turnaround tale at General Electric Co. or the investment rationale of almost any other major industrial company. Copart specializes in online auctions of cars that are too damaged for insurance companies to justify paying the price of repairing them; buyers include scrap yards and companies like LKQ Corp., which dismantles old cars and reconditions their parts to be sold in collision-repair shops. The insurance companies get what’s recovered in the auction, minus fees due to Copart that in some cases will increase proportionally with the value of a vehicle. Business has been good: Copart’s revenue increased 13% in the fiscal year ended in July, while net income increased more than 40%, in part because of the U.S. tax overhaul.

A big reason for this surge is that cars are increasingly complicated and technologically advanced, says Stephens Inc. analyst Daniel Imbro. Even the parts most vulnerable to fender benders, like headlights and bumpers, carry cameras and sensors. All of that technology makes it more expensive for insurance companies to repair banged-up vehicles, so they’re more likely to declare a car totaled and put it on the block with Copart. This also means that the cars coming to auction tend to be relatively young, less damaged and more valuable than the clunkers of yesteryear. In countries with less stringent safety standards, some of these cars may even still be driveable. Copart has been more aggressive than its rival IAA Inc. in investing in international marketing, which brings in more potential buyers, says Imbro. So there’s more volume, more buyers and higher prices – a trifecta.  

One contributor to Copart’s revenue equation is the total number of miles driven in cars, which does tend to decline in a recession as people choose other means of transportation or stay at home more. The trend toward making vehicles more technologically advanced, however, is unlikely to abate, which likely makes Copart’s business model much more resilient than those of the automakers themselves. That’s particularly true if the current slump remains confined to the manufacturing sector. But Copart was also one of the best-performing industrial stocks last year, and after a 71% run in 2019, the valuation is getting a little heady. Copart currently trades for about 21 times its expected fiscal 2020 Ebitda, according to data compiled by Bloomberg; that’s more expensive than Apple Inc. There may still be a bit of gas left in the tank: Analysts on average expect the stock to rise to about $88 over the next year, compared with about $82 currently.

Another high flier this year – aerospace-parts supplier Arconic Inc. – may have a longer runway ahead of it. When I last checked in on Arconic in February, the company was moving to its fourth CEO in two years after a plan to sell itself to Apollo Global Management collapsed. So I was pleasantly surprised to see the stock was also up a whopping 71% so far in 2019. That’s to the credit of current CEO John Plant, former head of TRW Automotive Holdings Corp., says Cowen & Co. analyst Gautam Khanna. "Clearly the company was under-earning,” Khanna said. “Plant has been a lot more forceful about getting stuff done.” Khanna pointed to how Plant personally reviews any capital expenditure request over $1 million and is renegotiating contracts with customers to get better pricing. He’s also divested less profitable assets, opportunistically bought back stock and is cutting $180 million in costs. The benefits of these changes are starting to show in Arconic’s improving profitability, free cash flow, and, of course, a higher stock price.

But there’s a lot left to do, particularly on pricing, with increases already planned for 2020 and 2021 on the aerospace side of Arconic. That business – to be called Howmet Aerospace – will stand alone after a spinoff of the Arconic aluminum-sheet operations next year, providing another boost to the valuation. JPMorgan Chase & Co. analyst Seth Seifman is targeting a $30 stock price for Arconic by December 2020, but says there’s a path to about $36 if the Howmet business continues operational improvements and investors give it more credit for its position in the aerospace industry. The sheet-metal arm is obviously less recession-resistant than Copart, but the growing use of aluminum in cars to improve fuel efficiency may provide some kind of buffer, Seifman wrote in a report last month. When Elliott Management Corp. pushed for the ouster of then-CEO Klaus Kleinfeld in 2017, the activist investor argued that Arconic could achieve at least a $33 share price. For a long while, that looked hopelessly out of reach. It now looks entirely realistic. Elliott has sold some of its stake, but to its credit has largely stuck it out at Arconic. It remains the biggest shareholder, and partner Dave Miller sits on the board. Elliott may have underestimated how long an Arconic turnaround would take, but it wasn’t wrong about the opportunity.

BOEING'S GOT 99 PROBLEMS AND THE MAX IS ONLY ONE
As if troubled plane maker Boeing Co. didn’t have enough to worry about: Global airline passenger traffic eked out a 3.8% gain in September, marking the eighth straight month of sub-par growth, according to the International Air Transport Association. Faced with slowing economies and a glut of seats in Europe, airlines are rethinking how many planes they really need. Just this week, British Airways parent IAG SA said it will almost halve its capacity expansion goals.  A plan to operate with 22 fewer long-range jets through 2022 likely reflects some pushed-out orders for Boeing’s 777X. Meanwhile, Deutsche Lufthansa AG switched 14 orders for the 777X to “options”. Weaker demand will likely keep a lid on production rates for that model, 

notes Bloomberg Intelligence analyst George Ferguson. The plane’s entry into service has already been pushed to 2021 amid durability issues with a GE jet engine. It didn't help that one of the first engines bearing a fix was damaged in a freak accident when the plane ferrying the part to the factory made a hard landing. On the 737 Max front, the debate over when the plane will actually return remains a day-by-day affair. U.S. and European regulators reportedly found gaps in Boeing’s documentation for its proposed software fix and have asked that it be resubmitted, increasing the risk of additional delays. “We think there is still some work to be done,” EASA Executive Director Patrick Ky told Reuters on Monday. Ky was not amused by Rynair Holdings Plc CEO Michael O’Leary’s claim on Bloomberg TV that the regulators were “dragging their heels” on the Max. “If Mr. O’Leary doesn’t like it, that’s his problem,” Ky said in an interview with Bloomberg News. “We are doing what we think is right.”

Deals, Activists and Corporate Governance
DuPont de Nemours Inc. is considering a divestiture of its transportation and industrial unit, people familiar with the matter told Bloomberg News. This would be the latest move in a long stretch of financial engineering for DuPont, which merged with rival Dow Chemical and then broke into three parts. The company also has reportedly considered divesting its nutrition and biosciences operations, which could be worth at least $25 billion. The transportation and industrial business is slightly smaller, with about $3.8 billion of sales so far this year compared to the nutrition unit’s $4.6 billion. One potential partner is Celanese Corp., which has a complementary engineered materials segment and is in the process of its own strategic review, according to Bloomberg News. I get the logic for this; a weak macroeconomic backdrop has countered the benefits of the three-way breakup and left DuPont’s stock mired in a slump. But what happens when you run out of things to spin off or sell?

General Motors Co. agreed to sell its plant in Lordstown, Ohio to an upstart electric-truck startup formed specifically to save the factory. The United Auto Workers union sought protections for the plant as part of a nearly six-week labor strike at GM, but couldn’t convince the automaker to keep it open. While Lordstown Motors Corp. is affiliated with Workhorse Group Inc., they don’t share ownership. That means that even if Workhorse wins a lucrative contract to develop plug-in trucks for the U.S. Postal Service, the work won’t necessarily go to the Lordstown plant. Steve Burns, who used to lead Workhorse and now leads Lordstown Motors, will need to raise a significant amount of money to convert the plant into an electric-vehicle operation, with no guarantees that demand for such cars will grow as expected. So Lordstown will still have a car factory, but with some serious footnotes.

Thyssenkrupp AG set a Friday deadline for initial offers for its elevator business, according to Reuters. Rival Kone Oyj is expected to bid with private equity firm CVC, while a Carlyle Group and Blackstone Group Inc. team are also working on a proposal. Brookfield Asset Management Inc., Japan’s Hitachi Ltd. and a consortium of Advent International, Cinven and the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority were in the running as of mid-October, people familiar with the matter told Bloomberg News at the time. While activist investor Cevian has advocated for selling a majority stake in the elevator unit to raise the maximum amount of cash, management, labor leaders and the head of the Alfried Krupp von Bohlen und Halbach Foundation (Thyssenkrupp’s largest shareholder) have expressed a preference for selling a minority stake, according to Reuters. That would seem to favor the private equity bidders over a rival like Kone, which may also face antitrust challenges. Separately, BASF SE asked the remaining interested parties to submit their final bids for its 3 billion euro ($3.3 billion) construction chemicals unit by Friday as well, Reuters reported. Big day! 

Penske Corp.’s entertainment division agreed to buy the Indianapolis Motor Speedway and the IndyCar series of racing events from the Hulman family. Penske manages businesses with consolidated revenue of more than $32 billion, according to its website, with most of that coming from the publicly traded car dealership company, Penske Automotive Group Inc. Penske-backed teams have won the Indianapolis 500 hosted at the speedway 18 times, according to Autosport, and more formal backing from the billionaire Penske family may inject stability into the franchise as racing events struggle to attract viewers. No price was given for the purchase.
