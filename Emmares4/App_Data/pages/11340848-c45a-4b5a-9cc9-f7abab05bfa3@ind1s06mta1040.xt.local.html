Hi readers,

I often write for New Scientist about the “food wars”, the arguments between doctors, nutritionists and researchers about what makes a healthy diet. Often the experts disagree with each other, but one thing that gets wide approval is the idea we should be eating less junk food.

And yet… what is junk food? There’s no universally agreed definition, and we don’t even know what it is about this way of eating that’s bad for us. These are questions I have been investigating and the resultant article has the honour of being the magazine’s cover feature this week.
 
For a long time, the idea was that processed food is bad for us because it is high in the frowned-on ingredients of fat, salt and sugar. And yet, the only randomised controlled trial that has investigated this question found that is not the case. 

This trial, done by a prestigious laboratory at the US National Institutes of Health, asked volunteers to eat two different diets for two weeks each. One was mainly based on processed food, while the other was mainly wholefoods, yet they were carefully designed to have the same levels of fat, salt and sugar. The volunteers could eat as much or as little as they wanted from the buffet on offer.

When eating the processed diet, people ate an average of 500 calories a day more than when they were offered the wholefood buffet. Unsurprisingly, they put on weight too.

If it isn’t the fat, salt and sugar in processed foods that’s the culprit, then what is? One idea is that our normal gut functioning is somehow disturbed by the lack of fibre in processed food, specifically a type known as insoluble fibre, found mainly in vegetables and wholegrains.
 

Another idea is the “protein leverage hypothesis”, which says that because processed food generally contains less protein, it isn’t as satisfying and so makes us overeat to hit our required protein intake. The scientists who developed this idea wrote about it previously for New Scientist here.
 
Whatever the explanation, it is even less clear what we are supposed to do about it. There are proposals to tax processed foods more severely and limit supermarket promotional offers on them. But processed food is firmly enmeshed in our modern lifestyles, forming up to 60 per cent of the diet in countries like the UK.

The most obvious advantage of ready-to-eat food is that it frees people from the labour of cooking from scratch several times a day. Processed food can also be cheaper thanks to economies of scale, a factor that’s obviously more important for poorer households. “There are families who won't have the time, resources or the inclination to cook from scratch,” Anthony Warner, a writer and development chef for the food industry told me. 

On the other hand, just because it may be difficult to reduce our reliance on processed foods doesn’t mean we should shy away from attempting to tackle the problem. At the very least, we should be trying to carry out more trials like the one done by the National Institutes of Health, to get to the bottom of understanding the effects of modern diets on our bodies.
