China

THAT DOESN'T RULE — A federal regulation expected to ban U.S. companies from using Chinese telecom equipment has been delayed by interagency squabbling and political concerns, three people briefed on the developments told Eric. Trump's supply chain executive order directed the Commerce Department to produce a rule by Oct. 12 banning technology linked to foreign adversaries, a restriction primarily aimed at Huawei, ZTE and other Chinese firms. But two weeks later, the rule is "stuck," said one lobbyist familiar with the matter. "It is held up," said a second lobbyist. A senior administration official told our colleague Daniel Lippman only that "the rule is under review" at OMB's Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs.

At the heart of the dispute, according to one industry insider, is whether to explicitly name China as one of the foreign adversaries with which products and services cannot be associated. Those pushing for an explicit blacklist include senior National Security Council officials like deputy national security adviser Matthew Pottinger, who has advocated for integrating trade and security conversations . On the other side are those at Commerce, the Treasury Department and the Office of the U.S. Trade Representative who want flexibility in implementing the rule and to avoid disrupting trade talks with Beijing ahead of President Donald Trump's meeting with Chinese President Xi Jinping in November. (Pottinger and the agencies did not respond to requests for comment.)

The fast-moving conversations about the supply chain rule have vexed telecom experts and industry lobbyists, who are trying to prepare for what could be strict rules requiring expensive and complicated equipment replacement. The second lobbyist said the rule was "delayed because of [an] interagency squabble" but could not confirm the combatants. "It's a shit show," this person said.

STORMING YE SKIFF (OOPS, SCIF) — House Republicans sparked a security incident on Wednesday when they stormed a secure room hosting an impeachment-related deposition, many of them visibly carrying phones and in some cases, reportedly refusing to relinquish them despite protocol. Third Way's Mieke Eoyang, a former Hill staffer who worked in the Secure Compartmented Information Facility, explained the problem : Such rooms are designed to prevent electronic surveillance, and the lawmakers' devices present a threat to that not just because of the devices themselves, but also because of who was carrying them, i.e. high-value targets for foreign intelligence agencies who have a track record of occasional slipshod security.

The Republicans "endangered our national security & demonstrated they care more about a political stunt than protecting intelligence information," tweeted Eoyang, whose organization is a center-left think tank. "I cannot emphasize enough how serious this is." Their actions required another security sweep and clearance of unauthorized members and devices, spurred criticism of hypocrisy given GOP rhetoric during the 2016 and naturally delayed the deposition.

GOP reaction was all over the place: House Minority Leader Rep. Kevin McCarthy (R-Calif.) dismissed the incident as "nothing serious," some lawmakers appended tweets typed during the showdown to assure readers they were from staffers, one lawmaker who made a phone call within the SCIF said it was not from his personal phone, and some panel leaders tried to evict the phones.

House Homeland Security Chairman Bennie Thompson (D-Miss.) requested the Sergeant at Arms "take action" regarding members involved in the breach. "Such action is a blatant breach of security, violates the Oath all Members of Congress sign to gain access to classified information, and contravenes security controls established by the Director of the Central Intelligence Agency for the protection of classified information," Thompson wrote.

HOW TO PROTECT WHISTLEBLOWERS — Sen. Ron Wyden (D-Ore.) asked a group of inspectors general to examine how cybersecurity tech like SecureDrop or Tor might help protect federal whistleblowers, as POLITICO first reported Wednesday. It's a timely request, given President Donald Trump's demands to know the identity of the whistleblower who flagged his phone call with the Ukrainian president, a call that now looms in a Trump impeachment inquiry.

 

 
Markup Spotlight

LET'S MARK IT UP — The House Homeland Security Committee swiftly advanced a bill (H.R. 4237 ) on Wednesday that would codify the DHS Continuous Diagnostics and Mitigation program and expand its capabilities and tools to additional agencies at the state and local level. The measure, sponsored by Rep. John Ratcliffe (R-Texas), would direct the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency director to regularly deploy new technologies to the program and require DHS to establish a strategy to ensure the program counters evolving cyber threats. The Senate's version of the bill (S.2318 ) is still awaiting committee consideration.

The panel also approved a bill (H.R. 4782) that would establish a 12-member commission to analyze how "online platforms have been utilized in furtherance of acts of targeted violence." The measure would require the experts to submit a report to Congress on their findings and recommendations within two years of convening. The panel also approved a bill to ban DHS from buying foreign-made drones and another that would create a counterdrone coordinator position within the department.

ANOTHER ONE FOR THE GRAVEYARD — The House on Wednesday easily passed the SHIELD Act (H.R. 4617 ), which would require campaigns to notify the FBI and the FEC of "illicit offers" of election assistance from foreign governments or individuals. However, the bill looks dead in the Senate. "It's a textbook example of policy designed to reduce the amount of free speech in our country," Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.) said.

Sen. Amy Klobuchar , the ranking Democrat on the Rules Committee and a 2020 presidential contender, asked for unanimous consent to pass the legislation in the upper chamber. However, her request — and requests by Sens. Mark Warner (D-Va.) and Wyden to approve their own election bills — were blocked by Sen. Marsha Blackburn (R-Tenn.). Warner, the top Democrat on the Senate Intelligence Committee, decried the blockage of his FIRE Act ( S. 1652), which would similarly require campaigns to notify the FEC and the FBI about attempts by foreign nationals to influence an election. "The alarm bells are going off and what are we doing?" he asked on the chamber floor on the second consecutive day that Democrats tried to force Senate consideration of election security legislation.

IT'S 10, NOT 60 — Every few months, somebody in a position of power on cybersecurity (usually a member of Congress) cites an unsupported statistic that 60 percent of small businesses close within six months of a cyberattack. Often attributed to the National Cyber Security Alliance, that organization debunked the figure two years ago and said it isn't sure how it made its way into an infographic it since corrected. But on Wednesday the alliance released a Zogby Analytics survey with data indicating that of the 28 percent of businesses that suffered a data breach in the last year, 10 percent closed and 25 percent filed for bankruptcy. Other stats: 88 percent of small businesses believe they're at least "somewhat likely" to be a cyberattack target; 46 percent feel "very prepared"; and 58 percent have a response plan to act quickly.
