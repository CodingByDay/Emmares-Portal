Encryption

WE CAN’T DO IT (EXCEPT WHEN WE CAN) — Top Trump administration officials on Monday used a surprise breakthrough in the Pensacola naval base shooting investigation to repeat their familiar refrain about the dangers of end-to-end encryption. After announcing that the FBI finally bypassed the encryption on the alleged shooter’s iPhones, Attorney General William Barr told reporters, “The developments in this case demonstrate the need for a legislative solution” to the encryption debate. “The truth is that we needed luck, in addition to ingenuity, to get into the phones this time,” Barr said. “There is no guarantee that we will be successful again.”

The news conference offered the administration and its allies a chance to bash Apple for its refusal to downgrade the integrity of its encryption. Because the company lacked the means to comply with a search warrant issued after the shooting five months ago, FBI experts “had to spend all that time” devising a workaround, said FBI Director Christopher Wray. And Sen. Tom Cotton (R-Ark.) referenced Apple’s famous ad campaign in urging the company to “‘Think Different’ about siding with terrorists over law enforcement.” Barr said Apple “would not help us unlock the phones,” although Apple has said it offered what help it could. In a statement Monday, Apple blasted what it called the government’s “false claims,” adding: “It is because we take our responsibility to national security so seriously that we do not believe in the creation of a backdoor.”

Privacy and civil liberties groups criticized the government comments as posturing. "Every time there’s a traumatic event requiring investigation into digital devices, the Justice Department loudly claims that it needs backdoors to encryption, and then quietly announces it actually found a way to access information without threatening the security and privacy of the entire world,” said Brett Max Kaufman, senior staff attorney for the American Civil Liberties Union. “The boy who cried wolf has nothing on the agency that cried encryption." The Center for Democracy and Technology, Global Partners Digital and Internet Society issued a joint statement offering similar sentiments.

Despite the rhetoric, there is no sign the White House is moving closer to proposing or endorsing encryption legislation. Capitol Hill has received no briefings or legislative pitches recently, a congressional source told MC. The latest public activity was a Senate hearing in December that featured bipartisan scorn for Silicon Valley. Meanwhile, the FBI has yet to issue accurate statistics about encryption’s impact on its investigations, after admitting that the figures it repeatedly cited were wrong. Monday’s press conference also prompted comparisons between Pensacola, where the FBI claimed it was impossible to access encrypted phones and then accessed them, and San Bernardino, where a similar reversal occurred amid dubious circumstances.

In Congress

SWITCHER-RUBIO — Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.) on Monday appointed Rubio as acting chairman of the Intelligence Committee in the absence of Richard Burr (R-N.C.), who temporarily stepped down from the top spot while the FBI is investigating his stock trades. One of Rubio’s first tasks will be presiding over a vote today on the nomination of Rep. John Ratcliffe (R-Texas) for director of national intelligence, with McConnell confirming the panel’s plans on the Senate floor. It also puts Rubio on the hot seat to either stand by the committee’s guns on the final forthcoming Russia collusion report, or back down in the face of pressure from Trump.

Last week, Rubio praised Burr’s handling of the Russia investigation, by getting the committee through “what could have been a very difficult process in the committee in a way that didn't fall into some of these radical theories out there about — we looked at everything,” Rubio told Martin. “You have people out there demanding we find collusion and it wasn't there. But we learned a lot about Russian efforts and we've learned a lot about our intelligence agencies. It was a well run process in a politically charged environment.”

Rubio is one of the chief sponsors of a bill (S. 1060) to punish election interference, and warned his state's election officials in 2018 about security risks because he was aware of an intrusion in Florida that later made news. He's been hawkish about cracking down on Huawei and other Chinese tech companies. As Small Business Committee chairman, Rubio has advanced several pieces of legislation designed to aid small businesses on cybersecurity.

WHODUNIT ON BREACHES — Hacking played a much smaller role in 2019’s data breaches while human error rose as a cause, Verizon found in its Data Breach Investigations Report out today . The annual Verizon report, assembled with the aid of leading cybersecurity companies and government agencies across the globe, is perhaps the most comprehensive released each year — and while it concluded that hacking is still involved in 44 percent of breaches, error (like misdelivery or misconfiguration) is the only cause to increase every year since 2016, now standing at 17 percent.

External actors are still responsible for more attacks, but the percentage of external to internal responsibility may change in next year’s report due to Covid-19, said Bryan Sartin, executive director of global security services at the company. “Inside the next 60 days we should see a relative explosion in detection of insider threats,” Sartin told MC. Financial remains the primary motive (86 percent) and 81 percent of breaches were discovered in days or less, Verizon found. And Sartin said he was surprised to see that attacks on large organizations were starting to pull even with attacks on small and medium-size businesses.

WAR EAGLE — Auburn University’s McCrary Institute for Cyber and Critical Infrastructure Security today announced its inaugural group of senior fellows , a list of 30 names with high-level government and industry service. That includes current government officials like Cheri Caddy, senior adviser for cyber in the Energy Department's Cybersecurity, Energy Security and Emergency Response office; Christopher Cleary, the Navy’s chief information security officer; John Felker, assistant director of the Integrated Operations Division at CISA; and DHS adviser Emily Scala.

“This new cohort of senior fellows is tremendously talented and brings a wealth of national security expertise,” said Frank Cilluffo, McCrary Institute director. “Their knowledge will help power our efforts to shape policy and impact practice in the United States and build a community of experts committed to advancing our cyber and critical infrastructure security.”

REvil… DOESN’T REALLY REVEAL — The ransomware gang REvil issued an update on its supposed trove of stolen data on President Donald Trump. “Interested people contacted us and agreed to buy all the data about the US president, which we have accumulated over the entire time of our activity,” the group stated. “We are pleased with the deal and keep our word." That makes it nigh impossible to verify an already dubious proposition , unless the supposed buyers spill details. In the same message, the group said it would auction off data on Madonna, a more plausible claim given past leaks. The starting asking price is $1 million. By the way, the FBI doesn’t seem to have designated this as a terror attack, contrary to what some have said.

ANOTHER MEASURE OF MOST COMMON ATTACKS — Over half of all 2019 attacks were web-application and application-specific attacks, NTT said in its 2020 Global Threat Intelligence Report out today, a leap of 32 percent from 2018. Reconnaissance (14 percent), DoS/DDoS (14 percent) and network manipulation (5 percent) attacks rounded out the most common attack types. The tech and government sectors were the most attacked, with tech experiencing a 70 percent increase, the company said.
