Neuroscientists postulate that our brains could be making decisions before we're aware of them. Some may be instantaneous (fight or flight) while others necessitate more deliberation (Star Wars or Star Trek). Donald Trump and fellow Republicans decide to fight articles of impeachment in the U.S. House of Representatives, while U.K. voters decide whether flight from the EU is their best way forward.

Fans of intergalactic adventures face their own ticket-buying decisions when the ninth Star Wars episode debuts next week. If you've been busy pondering fight, flight or light (saber), here are some stories you may have missed this week. —Michael Tighe

Suspicious minds

A U.S. Army engineer's top-security clearance was revoked on suspicions he was spying for China. Su Wei, pictured above, was cleared, but his case exemplifies how Washington's distrust of Beijing envelops Chinese Americans. We analyzed more than 26,000 security clearance decisions.

 

No-fee MBA

It's nice to get something for nothing, especially when it's an MBA. More international corporations are offering that perk to employees, with B-schools in Europe and Asia being popular destinations. And the companies say it's not just a one-sided deal.

 

A faster, cheaper cure?

A Chinese startup is accelerating a promising gene therapy for cancer, and clinical trials are positive. Gracell Biotechnology is capitalizing on the government's push to become a science superpower, but critics warn there are hidden risks.

 

One of Wall Street's most lucrative businesses at risk

Spotify and Slack are trendy tech companies, and the way they went public is rather trendy itself. Disaffection with hefty IPO fees is prompting some startups to take a more direct route to the market, cutting out the bankers that have long dominated initial public offerings.

Fangirls clap back

Social media-savvy millennials in China are defending their nation by hopping the Great Firewall that divides mainland from the rest of the world. Online patriots dubbed "fangirls" say they're defending "Brother China" in the face of violent anti-government protests in Hong Kong.

 

Solitary man

Renowned globetrotter and Carlos Ghosn now cuts a lonely figure in Tokyo as he awaits his trial on financial misconduct charges. But just before his downfall, the auto titan and former Nissan CEO pursued an acquisition that would have reshaped the industry and capped his reputation.

 

Naming and shaming

This is the time of year when people compile lists either looking back or looking ahead. But there's one list nobody wants to be on in China, and some companies are paying $2,500 an hour to make sure they avoid it.

 

American dreaming

To make it in the car business, you have to dream big. So that's what Vietnam's richest man is doing. Billionaire Pham Nhat Vuong wants Americans driving his VinFast electric cars in 2021, which means reaching U.S. showrooms quicker than some global titans did.
