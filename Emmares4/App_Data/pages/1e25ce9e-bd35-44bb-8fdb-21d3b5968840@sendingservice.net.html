WordCamp Asia 2020 Cancellation

The first WordCamp Asia is cancelled
The first WordCamp Asia, due to be held next week, has been cancelled because of concerns about the health and safety of attendees. This is a real shame. I know a phenomenal amount of work has gone into organising the event, and being a regional WordCamp presumably the vast majority of attendees have already booked hotels, flights, and so on.

But, health and safety have to come first, and whilst the decision to cancel is disappointing, it's understandable. The specific reason for cancellation was "too many unknowns around the health issues unfolding right now in the region to explicitly encourage a large public gathering bringing together over 1,300 people from around the world". That seems very fair.
I understand attendees and organisers have been split about whether the event should go ahead or not, and that Matt Mullenweg made the decision to cancel, presumably after serious discussion with the organisers. It's obviously a very difficult decision and not one which would have been taken lightly. Last week I did see discussion on Twitter from individuals about whether it was safe to attend, and it seemed some people were making their own minds up not to go before this decision. At the time of writing as far as I can tell there are confirmed cases of coronavirus in Bangkok, where the conference was due to be held, but I understand there's no official local advice not to travel which may make travel insurance claims difficult. WordFence have very generously offered to cover some costs.
It's a great shame, but one can understand the decision. Matt does say he hopes he and other speakers can livestream their talks. That won't be the same, but it's probably the next best option. I've only organised one WordCamp and found that tough enough, so I really do empathise with the organisers. A phenomenal amount of work goes into organising these events, especially when it's the first time. And hopefully WordCamp Asia is able to come back next year. - Alex.


The Click-Away Pound Report 2019 (PDF)

Financial loss because of accessibility issues
I hadn’t heard of this report before, but apparently it’s a report which has been run previously in 2016. I’ve seen a few people mention it over the last week so I had to take a look.
The Click-Away Pound is the online shopping experience of customers with
disabilities, and the cost to business of ignoring them. Referring to the pound (£) means this report is UK centric, so I have no idea how this translates to other countries, but I imagine that you can scale the numbers based on population size and get something reasonable close to reality.
So what got me interested? Well, I often talk about accessibility, but I don’t (didn’t) have any hard numbers to show how it’s impacting businesses. This report estimates how much accessibility issues impact business earnings.
In 2019 they estimate that businesses lost a potential £17.1billion. That’s a lot of money left behind for something that could be fixed relatively easily.
A few other numbers I found interesting include the fact that 69% of users who had accessibility (a11y) or usability issues ‘clicked-away’. They left. I don’t know if they spent the money elsewhere, either on a different site, or in a real-world shop - but I do know that that’s a really high percentage. Especially since of the top million sites in the world, 97.8% were found to have a11y issues.
I also found it interesting that only 8% of the users who clicked-away told the websites about the problems. I don’t know why it’s not more - I know I’d be emailing people all the time if I had issues. But also this means that a lot of website owners probably aren’t even aware of the problems with their sites and don’t realise they are losing custom.
I also liked the addition of Recaptcha. I think people with access needs will be “pleased” to know that most people find them a pain and would rather something else was used instead.
Loads of interesting things to take away from the report and, as always, the key takeaway is to make sure your site is accessible. You could be missing out on a lot of business. - Ben.

Navigation block exclusion from WP 5.4

The navigation block is ready, but not going into 5.4
I’m really pleased the Gutenberg Team has made this decision. As the post notes, this was a priority for 2019 and "being a priority" alone has previously led to Core features being shipped before they were ready. The post also notes, though: “The Navigation block is usable right now. But we don’t think it’s useful yet – at least not until it has an intuitive place to live.” That’s a very sensible decision, and I applaud them for it! - Alex.

Syed Balkhi on Awesome Motive’s acquisition of All In One SEO

Brian Krogsgard’s interview with Syed
Awesome Motive, the company behind OptinMonster, WP Forms, WP Beginner and a lot more, has acquired All In One SEO, the other SEO plugin. Anyone who’s familiar with Awesome Motive’s CEO, Syed Balkhi, knows things are about to get really interesting. Syed is one of the most effective people in the WordPress space. The link is an interview with Brian Krogsgard on Post Status – as Brian says, when Syed says he has a plan, have no doubt it’s going to happen.
All In One SEO was the “original” WordPress SEO plugin, but in recent years the space has been dominated by Yoast. The Yoast plugin is very good, but it sounds like Syed wants to tackle an adjacent problem; Yoast will help you get your on-page optimisation for your target keyword, but how do you find that target keyword? You need an external SEO tool.
This is a huge space – I know, because Ellipsis spends a small fortune on five or six SEO software tools every month. It sounds like this is what Syed wants to tackle. Here’s his description of the “problem” to Brian on the podcast:
a small business owner, they come in and install an SEO plugin [at the moment]. Then what happens: do they chase a light, a specific color… but what is that in regards to anyways? I want to really help people discover new growth opportunities. So SEO is not just some acronym that doesn't mean anything new… [with SEO] everybody kind of has a vision [for what they should be doing], but everybody's lost. Really making that easy, making it easy to identify opportunities, measure those opportunities and you know, doing a little bit more than just setting up an optimizing [is what I want to do].

That’s a huge problem, but I don’t see why Awesome Motive can’t solve it. Most WordPress plugin niches have a lot of competition – forms is a great example of this – and there’s clearly space for lots of solutions. Competition creates more options for consumers and pushes everyone to get better. SEO has been the outlier in WordPress plugins in that Yoast has been the only real choice for a long time. There’s definitely space for multiple smart teams to work on this, and I look forward to seeing what happens next. - Alex.

Old CSS, New CSS

A trip down memory lane

For people who are coming to HTML now, or who have gone straight to Javascript (and by Javascript I mean React), this should be quite an interesting look at how the tech we use for our living has evolved in the last 20+ years.
