Hi all, Julie here. A lot of people laughed at how many things Peloton Interactive Inc. called itself in its filing to go public. Among other descriptors, it said it was an apparel, logistics, design and experience company, before concluding that, "on the most basic level, Peloton sells happiness." 

But the startup actually has a point. The $2,000 maker of web-connected exercise bikes sells a lot of different things using an unfamiliar business model, and that's making investors' jobs tricky as they try to figure out how to value Peloton in advance of its initial public offering expected this week. 

"As far as the company's peers, they’re kind of all over the place," said Michael Kawamoto, an analyst for D.A. Davidson. "You kind of have to use a hodgepodge of companies to apply to Peloton," he said. Those include Netflix Inc., Spotify Technology SA and Nautilus Inc.—all of which Kawamoto said he'll be looking at when writing research.

When John Foley co-founded Peloton in 2012, he knew he was creating something unusual. He wanted to recreate the experience of working out in a boutique fitness studio from home, starting with spin class. Working out without leaving the house wasn’t new (hello, Jane Fonda) and buying a treadmill that eventually gathers dust in a corner is a pastime for many of us. But having an instructor on your screen, with a real-time leader board and social media-style interactions with other people taking the class around the world? T hat was something different, and it helped Peloton amass one of the biggest cult followings the fitness world has ever seen.

All of those elements make it difficult to figure out how much Peloton is worth, both for the bankers on the roadshow and future investors on the public market. The company last raised private funding at a valuation of $4 billion, and has been said to be seeking a public market valuation of $8 billion to $10 billion.

To figure out whether that price is right, investors will look at Peloton's subscription model and will likely study Netflix and Spotify to get some comparable data. Then when it comes to selling bikes and treadmills, they might look at Nautilus as a point of comparison. Kawamoto says he expects some potentially big price swings on the first day as Wall Street makes its determination.

Once the analysts get a few earnings reports out of the way, and an idea of how to value various aspects of its business, it might all seem obvious. But until then, the true worth of an expensive spin bike business, plus a young subscription service with hundreds of thousands of members will be hard to determine. Investors might be in for a wild ride.  —Julie VerHage
