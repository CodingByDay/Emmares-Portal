Happy New Year

Welcome back to The Station, your hub for transportation news and analysis. I’m your host Kirsten Korosec, senior transportation reporter at TechCrunch.

A lot has happened since the last newsletter, namely that it’s a New Year and we’re entering into a new decade (unless you are in the decade-doesn’t-start-until-next-year camp).

This is a good time to talk about what my plan is for 2020. There are so many announcements, including funding rounds, partnerships, products and of course, earnings season, that it’s easy to lose sight of why I got into journalism 20 years ago. And that’s to root out the truth and provide context of why it matters. I want readers to bit smarter and have a greater understanding of this little slice of the world, not dumber, as a result of my coverage.

I’m back on the right trail. I will be more focused on investigating companies, vetting technical and business claims, and sniffing out the new trends, startups and people who will affect the industry this year. I hope you follow along. The newsletter will continue to evolve, but the analysis and original reporting in here will continue.

Micromobbin'

Did we mention that Lyft’s bikes are back after its batteries caught on fire? Just in time for Christmas, Lyft brought its pink-wheeled, pedal-assist e-bikes back to San Francisco. The plan is to roll out hundreds of bikes each week until it hits 4,000 by the end of April 2020. That comes after Lyft had to pull its e-bikes in light of battery-related fires in July.

Meanwhile, Segway-Ninebot unveiled a weird-looking transportation pod called the S-pod that travels up to 24 miles per hour and an electric kick scooter that goes a whopping 12.4 mph. Expect more details from Segway and other micromobility operators as CES kicks off next week.

Look into my CES crystal ball

It’s the beginning of a new year, that time when journalists everywhere put on their prognosticator hats (or interview those who have) and provide roundups of what’s to come. It can be a worthwhile exercise if you talk to the right folks and turn over the right stones.

In the world of transportation, one such stone is CES. Or more accurately, my CES emails. I filter them as to not muck up the works of my regular inbox. I received 900 of them, and about one-third were irrelevant and hit the trash can. What remained provides a few clues that I intend to follow once I arrive in Vegas sometime Saturday evening.

Here are the big three: ADAS, lidar and digital cockpit systems with an emphasis on voice.

I’ll dig into that this week, but I want to briefly make a mention of lidar, or light detection and ranging radar, that is viewed by many as the necessary third sensor to allow for the commercial deployment of autonomous vehicles. In 2017, I thought that lidar had hit its peak. By 2019, I was certain that the lidar party would soon come to an end. At CES last year, I was told by one founder that there were about 70 lidar companies floating around in the world. How could that many lidar companies survive? They cannot.

But now it’s 2020 and there are more. I have received more pitches from lidar sensor companies than any previous CES. What has changed? So far, I can only point to one noticeable shift and that’s the renewed interest in advanced driver assistance systems in passenger cars, trucks and SUVs and a push to include lidar on these vehicles.

Is vision zero possible?

Oslo, Norway’s capital city, has achieved what many have said only autonomous vehicles can accomplish: almost reaching a goal of zero traffic fatalities. The city recorded only one traffic fatality in 2019 after a driver hit a fence, the Norwegian newspaper Aftenposten reported.

Anders Hartmann, who works on policy focused on road safety, tweeted there were no pedestrian or cyclist fatalities either.

Vision zero has a cost however like stricter traffic enforcement and the city center banning vehicles altogether. I expect other European cities to follow Oslo’s move. Do you think a U.S. city also take this approach?
