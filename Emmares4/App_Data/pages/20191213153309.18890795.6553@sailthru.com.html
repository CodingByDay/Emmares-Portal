Not even a trade truce can salvage Boeing Co.’s rough week.

The U.S. and China agreed on the text of an initial trade accord would avert tariffs on $160 billion of consumer products previously set to take effect Sunday. China has agreed to step up agricultural purchases, although the exact commitments remain unknown and seemingly nebulous. The 25% tariffs that Trump applied to the initial swath of $250 billion of goods (mostly industrial materials) will stay in place, but the U.S. will cut in half the 15% levies that it slapped on about $120 billion of additional products in September. This is more of a ceasefire than an actual trade deal, and among the many issues left unaddressed by the sporadic comments from both sides is whether China’s purchasing commitments include large orders of Boeing planes. Trump has repeatedly indicated that would be part of any agreement and referred vaguely on Friday to purchases of manufactured goods, but any conversation of a trade-linked Boeing order from China has largely faded from the headlines as negotiators focus on agricultural purchases.

Boeing could have really used the boost. Its missteps on the 737 Max were on prominent display this week, sending the shares toward a more than 3% decline in one of the worst performances on the S&P 500 Industrial index.

The Federal Aviation Administration made abundantly clear that Boeing won’t have its blessing to put Max jets back in the sky until 2020. The plane has been grounded for nine months following two fatal crashes linked in part to a flight-control software system that Boeing added to balance out the aerodynamic impact of larger, more fuel-efficient engines. Boeing’s last timing update in November called for the FAA to sign off on proposed software fixes by December and allow the airplane maker to start clearing a glut of undelivered jets, even as the regulator continued to work on updated pilot training requirements. It now appears that the FAA wasn’t on board with that timeline at all and is quite irked with Boeing for putting that schedule out there. So irked, in fact, that Boeing CEO Dennis Muilenburg and commercial-airplanes division head Stan Deal got dragged into the FAA-equivalent of the principal’s office.

FAA Administrator Steve Dickson called a meeting with Muilenburg and Deal this week to address what it views as an unrealistic return-to-service schedule and concerns that the company’s public statements were designed to force the regulator into quicker action. “The administrator recommended to Mr. Muilenburg that Boeing’s focus should be on the quality and timeliness of data submittals for FAA review,” the agency said in an email to lawmakers after the meeting. “He made clear that FAA’s certification requirements must be 100% complete before return to service.” It’s worth remembering that the Wall Street Journal reported way back in July that regulators believed a 2020 return was more realistic, even as Boeing pushed the narrative of an early fourth-quarter ungrounding. You really have to wonder what Boeing was thinking. Throughout the Max crisis, it’s been on the wrong side of conservatism, as I’ve written time and again. Is the company really that tone deaf, or have the obvious lessons in humility from the Max crisis just failed to sink in? At this point, neither answer is acceptable.

But if Boeing isn’t going to self-correct, then perhaps the FAA and its newfound sense of gumption will make it. In testimony to Congress this week, Dickson left open the possibility of further enforcement action. People familiar with the boxes Boeing still has to check don’t believe the Max certification process will be completed until at least February, Bloomberg News reported. The practical implications of that mean Boeing may still be clearing out its inventory of undelivered Max jets through 2021 and won’t be able to ramp up production to 57 jets a month by the end of next year as planned. It also means a further delay for the airlines. American Airlines Group Inc. said Thursday it had pulled the Max from flight schedules through early April, while Southwest Airlines Co. CEO Gary Kelly warned in an interview that its current call for an early March return would likely need to be adjusted. Southwest and Boeing reached a settlement this week over compensation for the $830 million hit to the airline’s operating income from the grounding, but the agreement only covers 2019; Kelly said he expected the impact in 2020 to be as big or bigger, given the drag on capacity growth. That reinforces the likelihood that Boeing’s estimate of $5.6 billion in customer compensation is only a starting point.

And just to circle back on trade, the Max is certainly the biggest issue for Boeing, but it’s but far from the only one. Boeing in October announced it would cut production of its 787 Dreamliner wide-body jet as orders from China that it had been counting on to sustain capacity failed to firm up. That looks unlikely to change. China carriers have significantly more orders for Airbus SE’s A350 than Boeing’s 787 or 777, Bloomberg Intelligence analyst George Ferguson wrote in a note. Continuing weak demand for long-haul flights and slowing GDP growth globally could keep a lid on wide-body order in 2020 and potentially force more production cuts, The International Air Transport Association this week estimated passenger traffic would grow 4.1% in 2020, roughly in line with 2019 but down from years past. It’s hard to imagine 2020 could be worse for Boeing than 2019 was, but it’s shaping up for an inauspicious start.

 EXTREMELY LOUD AND INCREDIBLY CONFUSING
As far as what this trade “deal” actually means, it’s anyone’s guess. The commitments from China on agricultural orders look fluffy at best. Officials in a news conference on Friday declined to detail specific volumes, insisting purchasing increases will be based on market conditions, and that they’ll comply with World Trade Organization rules and involve other countries. Industrial companies who bore the brunt of the initial U.S. tariffs targeting $250 billion of Chinese goods will still have to pay the levies. To the extent that tensions between the two countries have been diffused, that’s clearly helpful, but there are still plenty of ways for this deal to fall apart and enough lingering uncertainty to convince companies to continue to delay investment decisions. Still to be negotiated are some of the thorniest issues around industrial subsidies, technology transfers and foreign access – which happen to be problems that companies as a whole care a lot more about than extra orders of soybeans. As my colleague David Fickling wrote, “There is nothing in this tentative deal that wouldn’t have existed in the absence of the past two years of wrangling.” The latest tariff adjustments aren’t large enough on their own to impact Bloomberg Economics’ headline U.S. growth forecast for 2020, which remains at 2%. So any boost will have to come from an unleashing of pent-up demand. For some sectors such as agriculture, where farmers are stretching the lives of aging equipment, there is clearly a need for replenishment. But while trade has been the loudest issue in the room, concerns about cyclical peaks in some businesses actually predate the worst of the U.S.-China spat and a truce could just refocus those concerns. In others, the sales slowdown has been relatively mild, which may limit the trajectory of earnings growth from here. Rather than a sharp recovery, manufacturers may be looking at a lingering downshift to slower growth.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND  CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Nothing says festive like executive roulette.  This was apparently the week for industrial companies to announce the management teams for their soon-to-be separated assets. Ingersoll-Rand Plc CFO Sue Carter will retire after the company completes the reverse Morris trust combination of its industrial-products division with Gardner Denver Holdings Inc. in early 2020. CEO and Chairman Mike Lamach will stay in those roles at the remaining Trane Technologies Plc climate operations, while Christopher Kuehn will become the CFO and Dave Regnery will be chief operating officer, having served in a similar capacity at Ingersoll-Rand since 2017. Also this week, Fortive Corp. appointed the executives for the yet-to-be named industrial company it plans to spin off next year. Mark Morelli, currently CEO of motion-control technology company Columbus McKinnon Corp., will lead the business, while David Naemura will leave his post as CFO of Gates Industrial Corp. to assume the same role at the Fortive spinoff. Naemura’s departure is a loss for Gates, says RBC analyst Deane Dray, but the jump makes sense given that he previously spent years working for former Fortive parent Danaher Corp. That should also give investors some confidence that the much-vaunted Danaher business model will live on in this latest spinoff. Perhaps following the blueprint of Fortive, which separates its CEO and chairman roles, Karen Francis will serve as chairman of the new company.

United Technologies Corp., having already announced the executives for its Otis elevator and Carrier building-controls spinoffs, named the board members on Wednesday. In both cases, the CEO and chairman roles will be split. Otis CEO Judy Marks will serve on the board but the chairman title will go to Christopher Kearney, a current United Technologies director and the former CEO of SPX Corp. Likewise for Carrier CEO David Gitlin, who will share oversight with executive chairman John Faraci, a United Technologies director and former CEO of International Paper Co. It’s unclear if this dynamic is meant to be an interim or lasting arrangement. But if more oversight is in fact a trend, I’m all for it.  

Dave Cote is back. The former Honeywell International Inc. CEO announced the first takeover target for an acquisition vehicle he formed with Goldman Sachs Group Inc. following his 2018 retirement. Interestingly enough, it’s essentially the network power business that was cobbled together by one of his peers, Emerson Electric Co. CEO Dave Farr. Farr, who had to take a writedown on a key acquisition for the business and a concurrent hit to his bonus, memorably said that he wouldn’t put the network power unit “on my tombstone to say that was a strong accomplishment.” Emerson sold it in 2016 to private equity firm Platinum Equity for $4 billion, and Platinum is now selling to the Goldman investment vehicle, which values the business at $5.3 billion including debt. The deal is expected to close in the first quarter of 2020 and the company – now called Vertiv – will be listed on the New York Stock Exchange (Platinum will retain a stake). Cote told Bloomberg News’s Thomas Black that the business is a “hidden gem” and about on par with where Honeywell was three years into his remarkable turnaround effort there. It remains to be seen if Cote can succeed where Farr failed, but Cote is betting on himself by putting $20 million of his own money into the deal.  

3M Co. agreed to sell most of its drug-delivery systems business to an affiliate of Altaris Capital Partners LLC for about $650 million. It will retain a transdermal components business and a 17% stake in the divested operations. The implied multiple of about 2 times the divested assets’ $380 million in annual sales isn’t anything to write home about, but it seems fine and the deal will help rid 3M of a volatile business that’s recently been a drag on its health-care unit. 3M will likely direct the proceeds to paying down debt from the $6.7 billion acquisition of Acelity Inc. or to accelerate restructuring after a string of earnings disappointments.

Tenneco Inc.’s power train division has reportedly drawn interest from Apollo Global Management Inc. The private equity firm bid $4.3 billion for the business, according to the Wall Street Journal. Tenneco paid $5.4 billion in 2018 to acquire Carl Icahn-owned auto-supplier Federal-Mogul and announced plans to separate its power train operations from other auto-part businesses that are more geared toward maintenance and replacement. It’s struggled to complete the split amid a global slowdown in auto sales. One big puzzle it has to work out is how to allocate its heavy debt load between the two businesses, so cash from a buyout firm would theoretically make that equation easier and reduce risks. But the WSJ reports there are several asterisks to the Apollo proposal, including the fact that the private equity firm isn’t offering to assume pension or other liabilities linked to the division. For that reason, Tenneco is likely to reject the bid, the WSJ said.   

Thyssenkrupp AG is going through the motions of setting its elevator unit up for a public offering, even as it debates the merits of takeover bids from a range of suitors. The company hosted a capital markets day in London this week and laid out strategy targets for the elevator unit, including increasing its adjusted Ebit margin to a range of 11.5% to 13% by fiscal 2021. Jefferies analysts still think a sale is most likely, but there are really no great options. Moody’s Investors Service lowered its outlook on Thyssenkrupp’s credit rating to negative this week, citing expectations for depressed cash flow under all possible scenarios. A full elevator sale would help Thyssenkrupp pay down debt, but leave it with a pile of volatile and mostly cash-burning assets, Moody’s said. A partial sale could lead to cash leakage through minority dividends, but retaining a majority stake with continued access to its stable earnings and cash flows would help protect the business, the firm said.
