RUNS IN BEHIND
 
Running in behind of the opposition defence

If your team is struggling to break down opposition defences then often a lack of creative runs in behind can be to blame. This is because sometimes in order to beat the block and undo the defensive structure of the opposition, your players need to utilise the space behind the back line.

Runs in behind are incredibly efficient as an attacking tool due to their ability to create scoring chances out of nothing. In order to ensure your team’s runs in behind are effective  your players need to be alert, intelligent and fast.  These drills will help to portray the importance of making deep runs in games, helping to incorporate it into your players' game plan.

ALEX MCLEISH
 
RUNNING IN BEHIND
 
This exercise is a shaping session for the tactic of making runs in behind the defence, which is such a vital way of creating goal scoring opportunities. These tactics are used against a high line of defence and even on the edge of the box. Therefore, when planning tactics to eliminate the opposition back line, this type of exercise encourages forward players to break the last line of defence.

PHIL PARKINSON
 
RUNNING WITHOUT THE BALL
 
We will practice this session at least once a fortnight. Running in behind without the ball is extremely valuable because it steers players into dangerous areas of the pitch. Constantly offering different threats to the opposition defence is important, but this move also helps us retain possession, which is vital.

BRENDAN RODGERS
 
PENETRATION IN BEHIND
 
For any attacking team, exploiting space behind defenders is vital. This session is focused on developing players and teams to arrive in behind the opponents’ defence, either with or without the ball using the different ways you can do that, either by combination play or individual skill or running to receive a pass. The idea is to improve the patterns in order to go beyond the oppositions’ defence.

KOFI HAGAN
 
QUICK PLAY THROUGH CENTRAL MIDFIELD
 
This is a small unit session that works on passing aspects of a midfielder’s play in the central area of the pitch, including changing angles to pass and receive and developing final pass options. This session allows the coach to look at players individually and focus on their strengths and weaknesses, which may be difficult to highlight when working in larger groups.
