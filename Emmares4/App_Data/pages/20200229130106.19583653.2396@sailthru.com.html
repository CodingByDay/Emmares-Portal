Micromobbin'

T’was a big(ish) week in the micromobility world. Let’s jump right in.

Bird expanded into the payments business with the launch of Bird Pay, which allows riders to pay for certain products using the balance in their Bird account. Initially, Bird Pay is being tested in Los Angeles and Santa Monica.

Next up was Spin, which announced plans to launch electric scooters in Germany. This spring, the plan is to launch in Cologne, Germany, followed by other German cities. Spin also plans to apply for the e-scooter permit in France next month, as well as explore opportunities in the U.K. for electric scooter sharing. Also in Europe, Tier acquired moped assets from Coup to become more than just a kick scooter operator.

AV data dump

The California Department of Motor Vehicles released the “disengagement” reports of every company testing autonomous vehicle technology on public roads. Companies provided total autonomous miles driven, fleet size and “disengagements,” a term that describes each time a self-driving vehicle disengages out of autonomous mode either because its technology failed or a human safety driver took manual control for safety reasons.

A growing number of companies including Waymo and Cruise are criticizing the reports, arguing that they fail to gauge the competency or readiness of the tech. Much of the coverage of these disengagement reports focused on this rising tide of complaints.

Let’s ignore this airing of grievances for a moment. It’s true that these reports should not be used to compare one company to another for a number of reasons, including the fact that testing on public roads is conducted in areas with varying degrees of complexity. However, these reports have value. One can, in fact, learn a thing or two by reading them.

For instance, Lyft’s self-driving car fleet went from a big fat zero to 19 autonomous vehicles in 2019. Apple, meanwhile, greatly reduced testing on public roads and nearly half of the registered vehicles in its fleet didn’t drive a single mile. That could mean Apple is ramping up testing on closed courses or is testing these vehicles on public roads outside of California. Meanwhile, some companies have faded into vapor. You can learn this by diving into the data.

There are other ways to understand how and what AV developers are up to — at least in the state of California. Try checking out the collision reports and then compare them to these disengagements.

Speaking of autonomous vehicles in California, one more note. Cruise received a permit to transport passengers in its autonomous vehicles in California. Granted by the California Public Utilities Commission, the permit is part of the state’s Autonomous Vehicle Passenger Service pilot.

Deal of the week

 

The autonomous vehicle technology industry is beginning to see some consolidation and a tightening of investor dollars. This broader trend isn’t affecting every company equally.

Pony.ai, an autonomous vehicle technology company based in Fremont, California and Guangzhou, China, is one such example. The three-year-old startup raised $400 million from Toyota Motor Corporation, bringing its total raise to $462 million and a valuation of “just over” $3 billion.

I spoke to Pony.ai founder and CEO James Peng after the announcement to get a sense of where the startup was headed and how the deal with Toyota came together.

Peng didn’t provide too many specifics on how Toyota plans to use Pony.ai’s tech. But it’s clear Peng intends to expand his company in every way one might imagine — meaning people, test vehicles and robotaxi pilots. Pony.ai has been testing its robotaxi pilot service called PonyPilot since late 2018 in Guangzhou. Since then, Pony has scaled up its fleets, launched joint autonomous driving pilots with Toyota in China, and started testing a robotaxi service in Irvine, Calif.

Peng plans to expand its existing test fleets as well as its robotaxi service in Irvine. He said Pony will likely expand to San Francisco next. Today, the company has about 80 autonomous vehicles, with more than 20 in California.

Peng took his first trip out to Tokyo to meet with Toyota about 14 months ago. Toyota was looking for companies to run pilot programs with, which Peng speculated might have been a gateway for the automaker to find investment opportunities. Pony.air met with other OEMs, and while Peng wouldn’t name them, he did say there was interest in investing in the startup. So beyond the obvious benefits of resources, reach and capital, why did Pony.ai go with Toyota?

For Peng, it was Toyota’s patience, commitment and its tendency to under promise and over deliver. He shared an anecdote of his visit, which included a tour of the Toyota museum where he saw the various generations of vehicles.

“I was quite impressed with the hydrogen vehicles; they started working on that 20 years ago,” Peng told me. “And while it’s not in the mainstream yet, they’re still continuing to put a lot of money and support into the development of hydrogen. That the kind of commitment and that kind of professionalism is exactly what is needed for technology like autonomous driving.”

The next new job: Chief mobility officer

While U.S. Congress bats around federal legislation on autonomous vehicles like a cat with a yarn ball, states are pushing forward. The effort by states is as much as about economic opportunity as regulation and safety.

The latest example is in Michigan. This week, Gov. Gretchen Whitmer signed an executive directive to create a new job and office centered on mobility. The Michigan Office of Future Mobility and Electrification will be housed within the Department of Labor and Economic Opportunity. The yet-to-be-named “chief mobility officer” will coordinate initiatives on autonomous vehicles as well as shared mobility, electrification and connected cars.
