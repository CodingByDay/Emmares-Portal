The Lucky Number May Not Be the NFP

As I write, the world waits for the latest nonfarm payrolls number from the U.S. By tradition, or habit, U.S. unemployment is the most closely watched macroeconomic indicator anywhere, even though it is a noisy series that is very subject to revisions.

The U.S. economy is central to hopes for the global economy at present. Where once all hopes rested on China and its manufacturing sector, now the baton has been passed to the insatiable appetite of the American consumer. But the world’s major economies are out of sync at present, while the effect of the U.S.-China trade conflict makes clear that the links between them are as close as ever. So as we get ready for the monthly prognostications over the U.S. employment numbers, let me offer some numbers that are probably at least as important, and won’t get the same attention.

First, the story of the moment for markets has been the U-turn by central banks in the developed world, who are now pumping out liquidity, even as emerging markets see liquidity tightening. In practice, a look at some of the other lucky numbers shows this remains important. If worried about trade, few countries are more trade-dependent than the great exporter South Korea. Its export growth continues to be negative, although it still looks no worse than the last slowdown in 2015 and early 2016. So this isn’t good, but still consistent with another pick-up and another mini-cycle, as we saw in 2016:

Meanwhile, the sick man of the world economy, and arguably the pivot for global growth, continues to be Germany, which is stricken by its dependence on exports, particularly of autos. And again the news here isn’t great, as manufacturing orders continue to decline. If we want Germany to lift us into a new leg of expansion, it isn’t ready to do so just yet.

But there is more to the eurozone than Germany, and there are signs of recovery. Looking at Markit’s surveys, we see a big disjunction between services, which have continued serenely, and manufacturing, which has run into serious trouble. This might well be because the big doses of liquidity from the European Central Bank this year would flow through into the services sector, and consumer demand, more swiftly than into the manufacturing sector — which also has to contend with the trade situation. But it does look as through services are continuing above the level of 50 that would indicate recession, while manufacturing looks as though it is rising, having hit bottom.

Where the PMI surveys come in very useful is gauging the next stage of the cycle. With separate measures for inventories and new orders, we can get an idea of whether companies may need to resort to a restocking boom, or whether excessive stockpiles will lead to reduced output. The onset of the trade war led to a reduction in new orders across the world last year. The uncertainty hasn’t gone away, but inventories have reduced and business needs to keep ticking over. As a result, this useful chart from the International Institute of Finance shows that new orders are rising relative to inventories across the world. This is most notable in France, whose economy looks relatively healthy despite the social discontent.

Now, let’s add liquidity to the picture. International investors have started to put money into European stocks again, as this Variant Perception chart shows. That is a good sign:

Meanwhile, the extra liquidity highlighted at the top is yet to hit. It tends to have a lagged impact on manufacturing. The following Variant Perception chart shows that German manufacturing orders follow rises and falls in global liquidity with about a 12-month lag. That again suggests that Germany should be putting in place a manageable bottom now (although the long-term future for its competitiveness remains very troubled):

Meanwhile, the same calculations suggest that global liquidity boosts China’s leading economic indicators more swiftly, with only a six-month lag. So the resumed gush from central banks may have been enough to rescue another of the world’s economic engines (progress on trade talks permitting):

Where does that leave us? There are signs globally that we are nearing the end of a slowdown in the manufacturing cycle and should soon see a re-acceleration. They are clearest in the eurozone. The money emanating from central banks strongly helps the chances of a re-acceleration. But the weakness of trade and the continued poor manufacturing numbers suggest we cannot afford a prolonged or escalated trade conflict.

 

Fiscal Policy: Big in Japan

Meanwhile, there has been one story this week with an unfamiliar ring to it. One of the world’s biggest economies has decided on a big and aggressive new fiscal stimulus. This has attracted relatively little attention, because the country in question is Japan.

Investors are used to ignoring events there. When it comes to monetary expansion, Japan leaves them all behind. This is what has happened to central bank balance sheets in different countries compared to GDP:

The 26 trillion yen ($239 billion) in headline extra budget (equivalent to almost 5% of GDP) is intended to boost public infrastructure, in part through reconstruction efforts after the typhoon season, and in part to counter the likely downturn after next summer’s Olympics. But it is obviously tiny in comparison to what the BOJ has already done, and investors are perhaps understandably jaded after seven years of “Abenomics,” the program of Prime Minister Shinzo Abe that was supposed to jolt the economy back into life. Somehow, this has seen the yield on 10-year Japanese government bonds drop into negative territory (with some deliberate help from the BOJ at times). The response to the stimulus news in the JGB market was minimal:

The fiscal moves are nothing if not ambitious, even if they are being widely regarded as a necessary step now that the BOJ has run out of room to expand further. The assessment of the veteran Japan investor Jesper Koll, who now heads the operations of WisdomTree Investments Inc. in the country, is that Abe’s is “the only government capable and willing to enact rational, pragmatic and decisive counter-cyclical fiscal policies.” That in turn is because Japan (and, in a different way, China) still has some political cohesion, of a kind that the West’s biggest economies have lost:

China’s Communist Party is also far too pragmatic and rational to entrust the nation’s future to the “cut rates, buy assets, and good things will happen” mantra. What do Japan and China have that “the west” has not? Functioning government, one by top-down dictate, the other by a de-facto supermajority in a democratically elected Parliament — Those who can, do ; those who cannot, tweet.

That isn’t the way most people look at Japan. But this stimulus could yet prove to be significant. Japan led the world into QE asset purchases — will it now lead the world into expansive fiscal policy?

 

America’s Aging Home Buyers

To finish, one remarkable chart from Deutsche Bank AG’s chief economist, Torsten Slok. Americans are having to wait much later until they buy a house. And in the decade since the financial crisis hit, the average age of home buyers has increased by eight years — which means that a generation that thought they were about to own their own home as the crisis hit in 2008 have only recently managed to do so.
