Cold daze ahead

Welcome back to The Station, your hub for transportation news and analysis. I’m your host Kirsten Korosec, senior transportation reporter at TechCrunch.

This week’s newsletter might look a bit abbreviated and that’s because it is. I escaped CES without contracting a cold. And then one caught up to me this week.

On one particularly hazy day — when the cold meds provided a layer of disconnectedness to the very real deadlines that hung just moments away — I got to daydreaming.

I thought about how lucky I was to have other reporters with expertise in motorcycles and micromobility who would help carry the load this week. And naturally, I started to think about talent and recruitment, and the knife fight that is happening right now in the autonomous vehicle technology industry.

In short: it’s getting rough (and expensive) out there. It’s an area I’m digging into; if you have any insights or anecdotes, please reach out.

Micromobbin'

Micromobility was hot this week, mostly thanks to Bird. To kick off the week, Bird acquired European rival Circ and simultaneously announced a $75 million extension of its Series D round. That total round size is now $350 million.

Later in the week, Bird began deploying 1,000 of its Bird Two electric scooters in San Francisco via the Scoot brand. What’s unique about the Bird Two is its self-reporting damage sensors that notify the company of any vehicle issues.

Meanwhile, TechCrunch took a look inside scooter company Spin since its sale to Ford. Spin co-founder Euwyn Poon told us:

“One of the things that struck me that has made this whole acquisition work really well in my view, was the fact that Ford, while still in this space of mobility, wasn’t in the business of software or micromobility. In the same way that if Google or Facebook acquired an app, they’re sort of like in the same business.”

Tapping into the Tesla ethos

Tesla and its CEO Elon Musk have created a consumer that other companies are trying to reach, including Damon Motors, a Vancouver-based electric motorcycle startup.

TechCrunch caught up with Damon CEO Jay Giraud at CES last month. He told TechCrunch that he doesn’t want to be the Tesla of e-moto companies. But he does believe its EV two-wheeler is the first to capture the ethos of Tesla owners.

Giraud sees Tesla owners as the primary market segment for its new Hypersport,  a $24,995 electric two-wheeler that debuted in January. The moto, which can travel 200 miles on a single charge, has the specs to compete with this increasingly crowded market.

But it’s the advanced driver assistance tech that Giraud thinks will be the final piece to tap into the Tesla owner ethos. The seed-stage company has wrapped its e-moto with proprietary digital safety technology, such as its CoPilot system, which uses sensors, radar and cameras to detect and track moving objects around the motorcycle — including blind spots — and alert riders to danger.

Damon has also addressed the one-size-fits-all problem in motorcycle design, integrating a system on its Hypersport for adjustable ergonomics to shift the motorcycle’s windscreen, seat, foot-pegs and handlebars to accommodate different positions and conditions.

A coming storm?

Earlier this week, I reported that Techstars Detroit, the accelerator that has funded 54 startups in the past five years, is shutting down after failing to secure enough funding for 2020.

In an email, Techstars Detroit managing director Ted Serbinski wrote to supporters that ‘it’s clear the entire automotive mobility industry is tightening as sales slump and we hit the trough of disillusionment with autonomy.” (TechCrunch viewed the email)

It should be noted that Techstars isn’t disappearing from Detroit altogether. The company has a presence through events like Startup Week and Startup Weekends. Serbinski will continue to support the 54 startups that have come out of the program.

I reached out to Serbinski and we talked about the program, the broader industry and what the heck happened. He’s confident that the portfolio will continue to do well because of his underlying thesis to focus on what would work “today and tomorrow.” In other words, he didn’t heavily weight the portfolio with startups focused on autonomous vehicle technology, which could take 10 to 15 years to turn into a product.

We also talked about whether there was a general tightening of capital from corporate investors and if automakers were starting to pull back on “innovation” plays to focus on their core businesses.

There is some evidence of that, as I written before. But some argue that it’s not so cut and dry.

Jeff Peters, a principal at Autotech Ventures, a ground transportation-focused venture capital firm, noted in a thread on Twitter that his firm has seen “plenty of interest from transport corporations in engaging meaningfully with startups as evidenced by our ~30 corp partners.  I do think we are seeing a pull back from “innovation for innovation sake”. Maybe both reduction and redirection.”

However, he also noted and agreed that “many generalist investors (and corps) “turned the fire hose” to a hot sector without appreciating the challenges of mobility.” He went on: “As a VC it is now no harder or easier. For startups, the pool of investors is shrinking.”
