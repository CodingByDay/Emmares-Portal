Hey y’all, it’s Austin. I’ll admit it: I am a sucker for clickbait. No matter how highbrow my web surfing gets, I can’t help but fall down a rabbit hole of end-of-article links to childish quizzes and tabloid headlines, such as:

“Your IQ is 140 If You Get 10/12 On This Quiz”

“Here’s Why Guys Are Obsessed With this Underwear…”

“Robert Redford's Daughter Is Probably The Prettiest Woman Who Ever Existed”

“The Genius Hack Every Home Depot Shopper Should Know”

The content recommendations come courtesy of Outbrain and Taboola, two of the biggest digital advertising networks on the planet, which last week announced a multi-hundred-million-dollar merger. Combined, the companies claim they’ll reach nearly 2 billion eyeballs a month and become a formidable competitor in a landscape where tech giants Amazon.com Inc., Facebook Inc. and Alphabet Inc.’s Google are on track to command more than 70% of digital ad spending. But are Outbrain and Taboola, long synonymous with low-quality ads, really the best hope of toppling Silicon Valley’s ad industry hegemony? Or does their merger merely mark the creation of a clickbait monster brand?

It’s likely inevitable you’ve encountered Taboola’s and Outbrain’s ads. They’re featured prominently on many publishers’ websites, often in the form of a content grid hugging the bottom of their articles, with eye-catching links to celebrity gossip, shopping "hacks” and beauty tips "plastic surgeons don't want you to know." USA Today, Fox News, CNN and thousands of other companies rely on their ad products to generate tons of digital clicks, which in turn, can generate tons of digital dollars.

But Taboola and Outbrain have also come under scrutiny for both the ads they post and the content they link to. A 2016 study of the most popular content-recommendation tools, which included a survey of ads delivered by Taboola and Outbrain, found that fewer than half of such ads linked to legitimate advertisers. About a quarter led to “clickbait” sites of questionable provenance, usually filled with more questionable ads. Some publishers, such as Slate and the New Yorker, have removed these types of links altogether, and both Taboola and Outbrain have since been working to offer more “premium” ad services.

An even bigger challenge for these companies is data. The more consumer data a company has, the more effective ads it can build—one reason Facebook and Google are the powerhouses in this market. But though you may not realize it, Taboola and Outbrain have also built a profile of you based on your clicks. My Outbrain profile, for example, shows that 18.3% of my viewing habits are centered on business and finance interests (with that category broken down into subset of interests like credit cards, real estate, and investing). And 7.9% are focused on sports news: To my surprise, they apparently believe I read a lot about martial arts and basketball (I don’t).

This dataset pales in comparison to the granularity I can see on, say, my Instagram account, where its parent company Facebook has determined, based on endless more clicks, that I am into the New England Patriots and specific types of climbing, which is remarkably accurate. By coming together, Outbrain and Taboola can better compete on this front. In a press release, the companies highlighted their “strongest data science” and “largest dataset of content consumption patterns on the open web.”

All of this raises the question: Are we comfortable with tech giants mining more of our data to serve us advertising for products we actually might want to buy? Or do we prefer the cruder brand of persuasion practiced by Outbrain and Taboola, whose ads are unpolished, but also less likely to induce Allbirds purchases? One is masterfully streamlined, and maximally manipulative. The other is messy, even as the companies race to improve targeting. Which version of the internet are we going to start seeing more of? It might depend on whether you clicked on those shopping hack links above. —Austin Carr
