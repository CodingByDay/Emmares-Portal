Nearly seven years ago, I wrote a story for TheWrap about the ascension of a new class of executives with the following headline:

Hollywood Studios’ Message to Executives: Embrace Digital or Die

It was hyperbolic. While a few companies were promoting their "digital" executives, Hollywood was still in denial about the slow-motion earthquake upending the foundations of its business model. Movie studios insisted you go to the theater. TV networks insisted you pay for cable. And record labels still weren't sure about this whole streaming thing.

What a difference a few years makes! This week, AT&T hired Jason Kilar to run WarnerMedia. This is a huge job. The chief executive officer of WarnerMedia is in charge of:

  • HBO, the most popular premium cable network in the U.S.
  • Warner Bros., the most productive independent TV studio in the world (and one of the three largest movie studios).
  • CNN, a sprawling, global news operation with about 3000 employees.
  • TNT, TBS, TruTV, Cartoon Network, Adult Swim.
  • Oh, and HBO Max, a new streaming service that is WarnerMedia's single biggest strategic priority.

Kilar is a bold choice. AT&T could have shifted someone from another division of its company to do the job, as it did at first with John Stankey. It could have promoted an executive from within WarnerMedia, or one of many unemployed Hollywood veterans.

Instead, the phone company hired someone with little experience programming a network, even less experience running a studio and no background in news. He is a Silicon Valley guy, perhaps the first Silicon Valley guy to run one of the world's old school media companies. (Fact checkers, please bring me other examples!)

Photographer: Andrew Harrer/Bloomberg

You can criticize AT&T for a lot of its actions over the past few years, not least of which was buying DirecTV, a Hindenburg in disguise. But you can't criticize it for being timid. HBO Max is a very big swing, and Kilar is too.

While Kilar doesn't have a ton of experience in traditional media, there are few people with more experience in online video. He was Netflix's first real rival when he helped create and ran Hulu, and played Reed Hastings & co. even for a while. He talked about taking Hulu overseas as early as 2008, but was often impeded by owners not as ready to accept the future.

His next venture, Vessel, didn't go so well; that's what happens when you try to take on YouTube. Speaking of that...  – Lucas Shaw

Quibi vs. the quarantine

Photographer: Martina Albertazzi/Bloomberg

Bloomberg

Tomorrow is the big day! Quibi will be available to the public for the first time. 

For those unfamiliar, Quibi is a short-form video app that will cost $5 a month with ads, or $8 a month without them. The name is a portmanteau of "Quick Bites," which checks the box of quirky name that also explains the app's rasion d'etre. (See: Netflix, YouTube.) You are supposed to Quibi when you are in the dentist's waiting room, sitting on the bus or bored at home. 

Before the coronavirus, "What do you think of Quibi?" was one of the most common questions I received, and it remains a popular one from readers like David Wilson in Toronto.

The curiosity is easy to understand. The company is founded by Jeffrey Katzenberg, one of the most intelligent and aggressive Hollywood executives of the last half century, and its CEO is Meg Whitman, the former CEO of eBay and a former candidate for governor in California. Executive star power, check.

Katzenberg and Whitman have raised closed to $2 billion, and hired an executive team that at one point or another has included some of the most successful and respected media executives in the world. I say 'at one point or another' because a few of those flashy executives left before the product was out the door.

Those executives have commissioned an extensive roster of shows featuring A-list celebrities Chrissy Teigen, Jennifer Lopez and Chance the Rapper.

These are all the reasons to believe it will work: Smart executives, big-name talent and a clear proposition (mobile video!). 

And yet, almost no one believes in it. It's spending a fortune on programs it doesn't own, and is making shows for people to watch in their doctor's office. Most people already have plenty to do when they are idle (YouTube, Instagram, SnapChat, TikTok, Twitter, texting, email, games), and almost all of it is free-99.

It doesn't help their case that Whitman compared reporters to sexual predators. Katzenberg addressed all his critics in an interview with Kelly Gilblom in which he says he has rhino skin. I urge you to read it.

To get a sense of how other people view Quibi, I asked a couple of media experts to give their thoughts. Here's what they had to say:

  • Alison Herman, The Ringer

I’m a bit of a contrarian when it comes to Quibi. Yes, the name sounds a bit silly; yes, all the shows sound like "30 Rock" jokes rejected for being too on-the-nose; yes, an investment of this size and scale has “boondoggle” written all over it.

But writing the service off entirely seems a bit too easy. Instead, I’ve been thinking about what Quibi might be doing right. That includes catering to the viewing habits of a generation whose focus has been eroded by a steady drumbeat of Instagram stories and Snapchats. It also means trying to do something meaningfully different from more established streamers like Hulu, Netflix, and Amazon Prime.

And, not trivially, offering a 90-day free trial — just long enough for dilettantes to forget there’s a bill in the mail. I get that $7.99 is an awful lot to shell out for an app without a reliable back catalog to draw viewers in. (That’s where Apple TV+ has really struggled, and why I’m pretty optimistic about HBO Max. Building things from scratch is hard!) Still, I admire Quibi’s attempt to separate itself from the competition with a clearly legible focus on short-form series and “movies in parts.”

  • Tom Dotan, The Information

This could hardly be what Katzenberg had in mind four years ago when he first started pitching people on the short, premium video idea. Put aside the coronavirus for a moment—back then almost none of the major tech and media companies had bet bigly on subscription streaming services. He may argue that Quibi is something different, but there's no denying that the streaming video landscape is just way more competitive now than in 2016.

But even skeptics have to concede that on key aspects of his business plan, Katzenberg has absolutely delivered. Namely he's hauled in a ton of money and big stars. So if there's anything to the shot-form premium video thing, Quibi is by far the best attempt to prove that out.

Yes, it's launching during a public health crisis that has shut down the economy. And a housebound nation really undercuts Quibi's entertainment-on-the-go use case (unless you consider walking between your bedroom and the kitchen sink to drop off more dishes an "on-the-go opportunity"). Despite expensive ad campaigns, public awareness for Quibi has been tracking low, as Meg has said to the team. The silver lining of the pandemic is if Quibi does have a poor launch, the company can simply retcon it into a "soft launch." 

Reader mailbag: remember radio?

Photographer: Sean Drakes/Getty Images South America

Photographer: Sean Drakes/Getty Images South America

"What's actually happening to radio? The state of the often-forgotten reach medium right now would probably shine some light on if a digital transformation is actually possible for that industry." -- Devin Emery

I thought radio usage would fall off a cliff. People listen to radio in their car, and they are no longer in their car. Early day suggests I was very, very wrong. Some 80% of people are listening to as much or more radio, according to this Nielsen study.

Important caveat: This is an online survey that doesn't actually measure the amount of time people spend watching radio! 

Even if more people are listening to radio, radio stations are making less money. Lots of advertisers have stopped spending, and live sports is on hiatus. That's why Entercom, one of the largest radio station chains in the U.S, is firing staff.

"I was wondering if you've seen any preliminary info on cord cutting? ... Flip side of that could be an increase in subscribers for streaming platforms. Any preliminary information on this?" -- Norman Cohen

Early research indicates all the big streaming services are gaining subscribers at a faster clip than normal. Matthew Ball breaks this down, using data from Antenna.

Disney+ appears to be seeing the biggest boost, followed by HBO and Showtime. Netflix is experiencing a smaller surge, but, remember, it's from a much bigger base. 

Why are Disney and Netflix etc not being more aggressive on early release of films and shows given we are all at home? -- Ed Ludlow, San Francisco

I have tackled this a couple times, bit it is worth repeating: these companies aren't sitting on endless stockpiles of shows, and they need to prepare for the possibility that they can't make anything for the rest of the year. 

That being said, Ed, check out your Netflix! There is new programming almost every day. Just this week, Netflix released season four of "Money Heist," a comedy series starring Ed Helms, the anime movie "Violet Evergarden" and kids' series "Spirit Riding Free" and "StarBeam." It also added movies including "The Death of Stalin" and "The Good, The Bad and the Ugly."
