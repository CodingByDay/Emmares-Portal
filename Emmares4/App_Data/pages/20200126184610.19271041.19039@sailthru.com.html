Good afternoon from the bowels of the Staples Center in Los Angeles, wherever you may be.

A somber fog rests across the city today. Kobe Bryant, one of the greatest basketball players of all-time and an icon in this city, died in a helicopter crash Sunday.

Fellow players, friends, family and journalists will offer stories about Kobe in the coming days. He was the most popular Laker of my lifetime, and the most beloved athlete in Los Angeles this century.

For all his flaws and alleged indiscretions, as a player he brought the one characteristic that mattered most: he cared. No one ever doubted how hard he was trying, or how much he wanted to win. The stories of his devotion to his craft and his obsession with perfection are legion. 

The fact that he so often fell short made him all the more lovable. His hubris, stubbornness and bravado made him the most frustrating player I’ve ever loved. They also made him human.

For many of my friends, there was no more inspirational athlete. What a sad day for this city, for basketball fans and for his family.  -- Lucas Shaw

Has Netflix peaked?

Netflix company reported a slowdown in annual subscriber additions this past week, the first time that’s happened since Netflix split off its streaming service from its DVD business. After a week of sitting with the numbers, that’s the big takeaway.

All of the reports about Netflix’s earnings focused on different data points to draw different conclusions. Optimists will note Netflix added 8.8 million customers, surpassing its own forecasts and Wall Street estimates. 

Pessimists will point out Netflix added just 550,000 customers in the U.S. and Canada to close the year. They will also point out that Netflix’s forecast for the first quarter of 2020 (7 million) is way down from 2019 (9.6 million).

These data points paint a muddled picture, which was reflected in the investor response to the earnings. The stock went up in after-market trading, went down the next day and then went up as soon as Comcast reported another drop in subscribers. (What's bad for pay-TV is seen as good for Netflix.)

But if you take a step back and think about Netflix in the big picture, here is the fundamental question: Do you think Netflix will be able to grow at the same rate it has been, or is its growth going to slow? Put another way, has Netflix peaked?

Let's look at the paid additions by year:

2013: 11.1 million

2014: 13.0 million

2015: 16.4 million

2016: 18.3 million

2017: 21.6 million

2018: 28.6 million

2019: 27.8 million

Netflix believes it had a great year. It grew by almost 28 million customers in 2019, the second-most ever. It got more Oscar nominations than any movie studio. It got the second most Emmy nominations of any studio. 

But investors are going to judge Netflix against past results, and the company has set a high bar for itself. Netflix already has more than 60 million customers at home,  and now faces more competition than ever before. For 2019, it added fewer than 3 million customers in the U.S., its worst performance since it split streaming from its DVD business.

Netflix acknowledged this. It blamed the sluggish growth and the slight forecast on a few factors, one of which was competition from new services, namely Disney+. That’s the first time Netflix acknowledged competition might slow growth, as Peter Kafka noted.

The only way to match or surpass the numbers, barring a major acquisition or a move into advertising, both of which Netflix says will not happen, is growth overseas.

Netflix already has almost 100 million customers, but has a lot more room to grow. Europe is its saving grace right now. Netflix added 13.4 million customers in EMEA (Europe, Middle East and Africa) this past year, a pretty staggering number.

The Grammys are a disaster.

NEW YORK, NEW YORK - NOVEMBER 20: Recording Academy president and CEO Deborah Dugan speaks at the 62nd Grammy Awards Nominations at CBS Broadcast Center on November 20, 2019 in New York City. (Photo by Jamie McCarthy/Getty Images)

Photographer: Jamie McCarthy/Getty Images

This year’s Grammy Awards were supposed to be a celebration of the next generation of pop stars: a genre-busting female singer, a gay black rapper and an 18-year-old sensation. After years of nominating too many men and not enough people of color, the Recording Academy wanted to prove it could change.

Nope. A shake-up at the top of the Academy, which hands out the biggest awards in music, has brought fresh claims of sexism and harassment -- and reminded everyone that most of the record industry is still ruled by older white men.

The music industry has been waiting for its #MeToo moment. While claims of sexual harassment have forced TV moguls and movie producers to resign, the recording industry has been relatively unscathed.

Deobrah Dugan was supposed to be a change agent. The Recording Academy created a task force on diversity and inclusion, which spent a year and a half figuring out how to improve the organization. It issued a report in December recommending changes, including ranked-choice voting and reforming the board of trustees election system.

But as James Baldwin told the New York Times in 1977, people can cry much easier than they can change.

Sundance kicks off

The Sundance film festival began this weekend in Park City, Utah. Most experts expect a slower sales market than a year ago, when Amazon spent almost $50 million on 3 films, none of which made much of a splash.

Fox News leads impeachment ratings

Fox News drew the biggest ratings of any network during the first two days of the Senate impeachment trial of President Donald Trump. The network, of which Trump is an avid viewer, averaged 2.2 million viewers during its coverage Tuesday and Wednesday.

No network commanded a majority of the viewership. About 10 million people watched the Senate impeachment trial of President Donald Trump across six networks in the first two days.

MSNBC drew the second-largest audience, followed by CBS. CNN and NBC drew the least.

Perhaps Fox News drew the most because it is the most-watched cable news network. But the numbers are still a bit of a surprise. Time was CNN would command breaking news events. Instead, the two more partisan cable news networks out drew it. That suggests the coverage didn’t change the minds of many people. Instead, it reinforced their biases.

And what about the broadcast networks? How are are CBS, ABC and NBC trailing cable on this?

  • The No. 1 album in the U.S. is Selena Gomez's "Rare." This is her third chart topper.
