Welcome to the Weekly Fix, the newsletter that's pondering if we should have just listened to the bond bulls all along. --Christopher Anstey and Adam Haigh, Cross Asset editors. 

Historic Week 

Treasuries kicked off the week with a dramatic rally that pushed yields to lower than many market participants probably thought it was possible a year ago. The yield on 10-year notes hit just 0.31%, a figure that was a fraction of where it began the year, just shy of 2%. The bond bulls, of course, are having their moment. 

Although the coronavirus pandemic couldn't have been a component of anyone's forecasts, there's long been a swath of prognosticators expecting American bond yields to head toward zero. Albert Edwards, a strategist in London at Societe Generale, as recently as last month reminded readers of his ``Ice Age'' thesis and his argument that U.S. yields would eventually succumb to the gravity that dragged down European and Japanese yields into negative territory.

Indeed the entire Treasuries curve on Monday dropped below 1% as investors weighed the likely need for further Federal Reserve interest-rate cuts and the rapidly deteriorating outlook for economic growth over the next few months. Economists by mid-week were scrambling to redo their forecasts for next week's Fed meeting -- with many now seeing a full-bore cut to the zero lower bound within days for the policy rate. Given how financial markets reacted during the last Fed tightening cycle, with a dramatic sell-off in late 2018, the likelihood is that, whatever happens in the next quarter or two, the U.S. central bank will need to keep its benchmark quite a bit lower for quite a bit longer. And that means lower yields for the long haul, beyond any day-to-day panic buying.

Even the concept of the world's largest bond market tipping into negative territory on yields now seems not so far fetched. While some Treasury bills saw sub-zero rates back back during the crisis, no coupon securities have ever seen that mark.

That makes it interesting that Steve Major, a renowned bond bull at HSBC Holdings Inc., was non-committal on that point.

"In the U.S. it looks like there’s a strong resistance to negative policy rates and negative yields," both among policy makers and in the financial industry, he says. "Never say never," but negative yields on Treasuries may be something more for the next cycle, Major says.

The biggest thing that could stop that move from happening in coming days could be a preference for cash. Longer-dated Treasuries, which have seen a powerful rally since the coronavirus started spreading outside of China in February lost their bid during stretches of trading this week. Monday's plunge in 10-year Treasury yields came alongside a rout of more than 7% in the S&P 500 Index. Both stocks and yields bounced back some on Tuesday. So far, so correlated. But on Wednesday, yields rose even while the S&P 500 tumbled almost 5%. And the seven basis point drop on Thursday seems quite anomalous against the near 10% crash on Wall Street -- the worst since Black Monday in 1987. 

The explanation is the same reason why the yen also failed to catch a mid-week haven bid (it ended little changed Thursday, after falling on Wednesday despite the risk-off moves that day). And take a look at gold: its declines Tuesday through Thursday also speak to a powerful cross-asset washout.

"Overnight action was very instructive,'' Michael McCarthy, chief market strategist at CMC Markets in Sydney, said in reviewing Thursday's session in the U.S. The drop in longer-dated Treasuries suggested investors "don't even want duration in their portfolios -- they'e going to cash." The bottom line is "we're nowhere near a bottom for share markets as yet."

It's all reminiscent of a quip from former Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas President Richard Fisher during the financial crisis in 2008: in desperate times,  the position to be in is "somewhere between cash and fetal.”

Meantime, JPMorgan Chase analysts highlighted the danger of disruptions to trading from the increasingly ubiquitous "work from home" practice, which has forced the big dealing banks to split their operations to ensure continuity. 

“Liquidity overall could also suffer, with some signs of emergent stress already apparent and transaction costs in even benchmark Treasuries significantly higher than even prior episodes of stress,” JPMorgan's Josh Younger warned this week. 

Panic Buyers 

In a parallel to the shoppers around the world rushing to nab toilet-paper rolls off supermarket shelves amid the coronavirus panic, Japanese investors are scrambling to grab overseas bonds while they still have some yield. They placed overs for 4.24 trillion yen ($41 billion) of debt last week, the most in records dating back to 2005. 

For now, the massive financial institutions of Japan and Taiwan that have flocked to American fixed income for years as a refuge from rock-bottom yields at home are sitting on big mark-to-market gains thanks to the latest rally. But in the longer term, this trenchant decline in yields poses a disaster as the likes of Japan Post Bank, with some $1.8 trillion of deposits to invest, have to figure out what to do with the cash they get from maturing securities. Many funds might need to simply cut their return targets, or even cut back on the deposits they take or insurance products they sell. 

Major at HSBC made the argument that interest rates cannot rise because the collective public and private sector debt load is simply too large for borrowers to survive an increase. Looking at the past two decades, and how benchmark U.S. yields never go back to where they were before the Fed launched a rate-cutting cycle, is a sobering exercise for institutional savers that rely on a positive risk-free return. 

 

Credit Crash?

It's not just investors that have been dashing for cash, but companies too. From Blackstone and Carlyle telling their portfolio companies to draw down credit lines to Japanese companies issuing a record amount of commercial paper, businesses around the world are moving to stave off any liquidity shortfalls. The proximate concern was highlighted earlier this month by Zoltan Pozsar and James Sweeney at Credit Suisse: given that the global supply chain has been severely disrupted by the epidemic shutting down factories, it was logical to assume that there would similarly be a global payment-chain disruption. When companies can't move the goods they've already paid for, or continue to pay employees who aren't able to produce because -- again -- the factories are shut, it quickly becomes a liquidity issue.

 

Layer on top of that the increasingly severe blow to global travel, and the crippling effect for the energy industry of the all-out price war led by Saudi Arabia and Russia, and vast swathes of the corporate world are finding incoming cash flows drying up very quickly. 

Premiums on corporate debt have blown out at a breathtaking pace, unseen since back in that crisis. But both investment-grade and high-yield corporate debt spreads remain for the moment below levels reached in 2015-16, when the global economy was coping with China hard-landing fears and a slide in oil prices that hobbled capital spending for the energy industry. Junk yields have soared more than 300 basis points, but thanks to the rally in Treasuries they're still only around where they reached in late 2018.
 

A more dramatic picture is found in the FRA-OIS spread, a gauge of short-term funding costs that's captured everyone's attention this month and has soared to the highest since 2009. 

Taken on the face of it, the charts suggest a liquidity issue but not a solvency one -- at least for corporate America as a whole. Defaults are bound to rise in the energy industry assuming a prolonged, lower price of oil. But assuming central bankers do what they are charged with doing, and if the healthcare industry and its authorities finally do get a grip on the coronavirus, there's still a chance at avoiding a full-blown credit crisis. Monetary policy makers, better late than never, have started moving decisively to address the liquidity problems, with the Fed mounting bigger and bigger repo injections -- add it all up and throw in the shorter repo maturities the Fed has scheduled, and the sum will exceed $5 trillion over the next month -- and others stepping up. Targeted credit programs may be needed in addition, and officials may yet again need to get creative. Their lack of coordination hasn't inspired investor confidence so far, and they haven't had a whole lot of support from fiscal policy makers, but that could all change. When Bundesbank President Jens Weidmann’s starts saying it's "no time to be dogmatic" on a balanced budget, that's a major sign of hope for stimulus.
