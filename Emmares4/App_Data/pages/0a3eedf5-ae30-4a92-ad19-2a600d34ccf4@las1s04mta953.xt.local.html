Driving the Day

TECH CHECK FOR THE VA — How the Department of Veterans Affairs handles information and network security will get a House committee checkup today. The inspector general's office will tell the House VA panel that IT security remains a top challenge at the department, and that financial auditors this year will again identify IT security as a significant weakness, as they have for more than a decade. "Until proven processes are in place to ensure adequate controls across the enterprise, the IT material weakness will persist — putting VA's mission-critical systems and sensitive veterans' data at risk," Nick Dahl, deputy assistant inspector general for audits and investigations, will testify.

Many key department systems have been obsolete for years, according to Dahl's testimony, which will also cover recent IG studies involving mishandling or inadequate protection of sensitive veteran data. The department requested $362 million for IT security in fiscal 2020. It has a "complex cybersecurity environment" to deal with, according to written testimony from the department's chief information security officer, Paul Cunningham, with 1.6 million connected devices spread across 2,500 facilities. But Cunningham will testify that the department is modernizing its cybersecurity strategy and has "risen to the challenge" of simultaneously improving cybersecurity as well as access and services.

THAT ACRONYM IS NOT A STRETCH AT ALL — The House Energy and Commerce Subcommittee on Consumer Protection and Commerce will mark up legislation today that would extend a law on cross-border spyware, spam and online fraud. The bill (H.R. 4779 ) would extend for seven years the Undertaking Spam, Spyware, And Fraud Enforcement With Enforcers beyond Borders (U.S. SAFE WEB) Act that expires next September. The FTC got several powers under that 2006 law, such as the authority to share information and cooperate on investigations with foreign governments. And don't forget: The House Science Committee is marking up some cyber legislation today, too.

INSECURE WORLD — Check Point researchers found a hole in what has been dubbed the safest compartment in our mobile phones, the company disclosed today. The Qualcomm "secure world" houses credit card and other sensitive data, and nearly half of mobile phones entrust Qualcomm, according to Check Point. Over four months, Check Point found the hole by "inputting massive amounts of random data, called fuzz, to the test subject in an attempt to make it crash," according to a summary of the research. In response to Check Point's findings, Samsung and LG issued patches, but the cyber firm said it had no confirmation that Motorola and Qualcomm would act on their word to do so.

In Congress

ZOMBIE CONSPIRACY THEORIES — As predicted in Monday's newsletter, the conspiracy about cybersecurity firm CrowdStrike and a server in Ukraine was mentioned during Wednesday's impeachment hearing. President Donald Trump referenced it during the July 25 phone call with Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky. Asked if he knew why the topic, which was included in the White House's "transcript" of the conversation, came up, George Kent, the deputy assistant secretary of state for European and Eurasian affairs, pleaded ignorance. "To be honest I had not heard of CrowdStrike until I read this transcript on Sept. 25," he said, adding he was "not aware of any Ukrainian connection to the company."

Rep. Devin Nunes (Calif.), the top Republican on the House Intelligence Committee, brought up alleged Ukrainian meddling in the 2016 election. "After all if there actually were indications of Ukraine election meddling and foreign election meddling is a dire threat, then President Trump would have a perfectly good reason for wanting to find out what happened," he said. Nunes mentioned the allegation even though Kent already told Democratic counsel Daniel Goldman that there is "no factual basis" to support the theory.

MOVING TO THE FLOOR — The Senate Commerce, Science and Transportation Committee advanced a bill (S. 2775) on Wednesday that would direct the NIST director to develop "reliable metrics" for evaluating federally funded cybersecurity workforce programs and initiatives. The measure, which advanced by voice vote, would also direct NIST to create standards and guidelines for beefing up cybersecurity awareness of federal employees and contractors.

The panel also adopted an amendment by voice vote from John Thune (R-S.D.) and Amy Klobuchar (D-Minn.) that would add a new provision directing NIST and OPM directors to create a voluntary program for employees working in certain cybersecurity positions to rotate between roles at NIST and private-sector institutions. It's unclear when the full Senate is expected to take up the bill.

YES, LET'S — A group of researchers has published a paper on how the Let's Encrypt project fueled adoption of HTTPS encryption, contributing to a boost in HTTPS protections from 40 percent in 2016 to 80 percent 2019. Why publish it now, though? "Because creating a new kind of certificate authority that gives out free certificates was a crazy idea" when it debuted, University of Michigan computer science professor J. Alex Halderman said in a faculty Q&A. "If we had written the paper before we built it, it wouldn't have gotten published. We had to prove that the economics would work, and there was no way to do that except to just build it." The free, automated certificate authority made the process less cumbersome, Halderman said.

The paper will be presented today at the ACM Conference on Computer and Communications Security in London. "I hope this paper, which looks back at how we built it and measures its impact on the web, can help spread some of the lessons we've learned to help other parts of the internet infrastructure work better," Halderman said.

YOU CAN TRUST US, WE PROMISE — Kaspersky on Wednesday continued its efforts to defuse tensions with distrustful Western governments, saying it would use a new Swiss facility to securely process telemetry data shared by customers in the U.S. and Canada, in addition to the European customers whose data it previously planned to store there. Kaspersky also said it would open a fourth "transparency center" where "trusted partners" could review its source code to satisfy themselves that it was safe. The new center, to be located in Latin America, will join ones established in Zurich, Madrid and Malaysia.

The moves on code transparency and data storage are part of an effort to undermine the U.S. intelligence community's contention that Kaspersky could, either voluntarily or under duress, help the Russian government spy on and hack its customers . But regardless of where data is stored or how often code is checked, the Kremlin could still enlist the aid of the Moscow-based firm, because it is subject to Russian laws.

U.S., EU, JAPAN PAN CHINA CYBERSECURITY MEASURES — From our friends at Morning Trade: The United States on Wednesday again raised concerns at the World Trade Organization over China's alleged cybersecurity local content requirements that restrict the ability of Chinese companies to buy technology from other countries and reduces market access for foreign investors, a Geneva trade official said.

The EU also said it believed some of the measures could discriminate against foreign firms, including a new cybersecurity protection scheme that classifies information systems in China according to their relative impact on national security, social order and economic interests. Japan also expressed concern the scheme could give domestic producers preferential treatment.

China said neither its cybersecurity law nor other relevant measures contain any local content requirements, adding it would provide more information given other countries' concerns. Systems are subject to a security review in accordance with the potential impact on national security, but both domestic and foreign entities will be treated equally, China said.
