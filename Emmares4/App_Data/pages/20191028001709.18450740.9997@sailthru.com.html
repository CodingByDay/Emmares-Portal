A Week to Break the Buck?

Amid the noise and division of 2019, arguably the world’s single most important financial measure remains as stable as can be. The U.S. dollar, linchpin of the financial system and for almost 50 years now unattached to gold, is startlingly calm. Using Bloomberg’s broad measure of the dollar against its major developed and emerging trading partners, it’s exactly where it was when Donald Trump was elected president. It’s also sitting exactly on its average price for the last 200 days. The hurly-burly of the Trump years have left it unchanged, while there’s no clarity as to whether the trend is up or down:

Where it goes next will matter a lot, with many rooting for a lower dollar. If the currency is cheaper, the U.S. would benefit from greater export competitiveness, and many countries would find their surfeit of dollar-denominated debt easier to finance. But this is only partly because of the effects of a changed dollar. The causes also matter greatly. The dollar has become a gauge of perceived risk. As such, its recent pullback is like the recent rise in bond yields — it shows less negativity.

If people are more worried about global economic risks, they buy the dollar — even if the risks they’re worried about emanate from the U.S. itself. Back in 2011, the historic decision by Standard & Poor’s to downgrade the U.S. sovereign credit rating from AAA prompted a flood of money to head for safety. In the U.S.

So a weaker dollar would imply that recession fears were being put back in the bag. Beyond that, it’s a specific indicator of trade tensions. The more investors expect the U.S. to impose tariffs, the higher they will bid the dollar to counteract those tariffs.

There’s help for dollar bears from valuation. In the short term, valuation seldom matters; an overpriced asset can always become even more overpriced. But the real effective exchange rate for the dollar, taking into account how the currency has moved compared to how it would need to adjust to account for different inflation rates in different countries, shows that the dollar is overvalued (in a chart from Bank of America Merrill Lynch):

This could help the dollar move further downward once a shift begins. It won’t necessarily help it to start that process. Instead, in the the week to come, we have plenty of news that could affect the dollar. In chronological order:  

Wednesday, Oct. 30: U.S. Third-Quarter GDP

If the various “nowcasts” are correct, the U.S. economy at this point is growing at a bit less than 2% per year. This is the Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow forecast, which aims to capture the economy in real time:

Others make similar calculations. The most recent estimate from Bank of New York  Mellon Corp. shows a recent slowing to 1.5% — but not to anything that suggests an imminent recession. 

There’s general agreement the economy is doing nothing special, and the consensus expects growth of about 1.6%. These data are often revised drastically, and are backward-looking; but a significant miss would increase perceptions of risk and bring the dollar up. A stronger GDP, paradoxically, might allow the dollar to weaken.

Wednesday, Oct. 30:  FOMC rate decision

If Bloomberg’s World Interest Rate Probabilities function is to be believed, the market puts the odds of a cut in the fed funds rate at 89.9%. To leave rates unchanged at this stage would be a major shock.

But everything is relative. There will be a press conference from Jerome Powell, its chairman. The market wants him to cut more after this month, even though this would imply an easing cycle previously seen only before recessions. As Joe LaVorgna, U.S. economist at Natixis SA points out, the fed funds rate is still above the two-year Treasury yield. This is unusual, and is close to levels that would imply a recession, so there will be pressure to signal future rate cuts.  

The level of rates in the U.S. has an ambiguous effect on the dollar. The reason for lower rates, fear of recession, will tend to attract flows of money looking for a haven. But lower rates in themselves make the U.S. less attractive as a destination for “carry traders” who seek to place money where it can make a higher return. As this chart from NatWest Markets Plc shows, moves in the dollar during the Fed’s last two easing cycles were very different:

Given the extraordinarily low and even negative rates available elsewhere, a U.S. cut may not be enough to stop the logic that leads people to park their money in dollars, as this chart from NatWest Markets shows:

So, rates are unlikely to move the dollar much in their own right. But a clear steer from Powell that the Fed doesn’t intend to raise rates in December would lead some to fear a mistake, which could damage the economy and increase risk. The dollar will rise again if Powell attempts to hold the line against further rises. 

Wednesday Oct. 30: Possible FOMC indicators on QE

The Fed has said all it wants to say about the asset purchases it will undertake to re-liquify the short-term funding market for banks. There’s an arid technical debate over whether this is “QE” (quantitative easing, the main policy used to try to re-energize the economy after the crisis). But it definitely matters to the dollar. Recent strength has been due in part to a shortage of dollars. As this chart from Gavekal Economics shows, the dollar tends to prosper against the euro when European Central Bank purchases are higher than the Fed’s. So bringing Fed purchases above the ECB’s, at least for now, should be dollar-negative and euro-negative. Any technical announcements on this score need to be watched carefully. 

 

Thursday, Oct. 31: Halloween, Britain Exits the EU (Perhaps)

Britain at this point looks highly unlikely to leave the EU by Thursday’s deadline. The risk that the U.K. will leave without a deal has diminished over the last month, strengthening both the pound and the euro against the dollar. This week’s installments of the Brexit soap opera should reveal how long an extension the country is given. By week’s end we should also know whether it will try to hold an election in December. As ever, there’s plenty of event risk. Anything that smacks of further self-harm by the U.K. would raise risk perceptions and push up the dollar. 

Friday, Nov.1: U.S. Non-Farm Payrolls (and lots of other beginning-of-the-month data)

U.S. employment data routinely drive big market reactions. The unemployment rate is historically low, while risks that this will lead to inflation seem minimally low. The market remains so strong that it seems absurd to fear a recession. When the unemployment rate turns and begins to increase, even when still at a low level, that is a strong indicator of a forthcoming recession. As this chart from Natwest Markets shows, that moment is still nowhere at hand:

When the three-month rolling average rises sharply above its low, we should brace for a recession. There’s no need to do so yet. That said, the recent trend in earnings growth suggests the employment market is weakening. That is another trend to watch. 

Will all of this add up to a weaker dollar, as many hope? All the set-piece events I have mentioned could move the currency decisively. The recent rise in bond yields from very low levels suggests resuming confidence and a weakening dollar. But with global uncertainty still high, with signs of unrest from Chile through Lebanon to Hong Kong, and with investors’ anxiety still palpable, it will take a lot of good news this week to ease the dollar lower.
