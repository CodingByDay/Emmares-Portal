Money Stuff

Matt Levine

ESG

What I should do is launch the Money Stuff S&P ESG Fund, a mutual fund whose stated mission would be to invest in the 500 companies in the S&P 500 index with the best environmental, social and governance ratings judged by my proprietary rating system. Don’t worry too much about how that system works, because it’s not particularly important: There are 500 companies in the S&P 500, and 500 slots in my fund, so it doesn’t matter how you do on the ratings; everyone gets in. 

Why would investors want this? Well, as far as I can tell, investors want two things:

  1. To be told that they’re investing in environmentally, socially and governance-conscious companies, and
  2. To own the S&P 500.

My fund addresses both of these concerns in a straightforward way: It has “ESG” right in the name, and it’s the S&P 500. No other fund can promise both advantages. Other S&P 500 funds don’t have “ESG” in the name, while other ESG funds have tracking error and sometimes won’t match the performance of the S&P 500.

Honestly they don’t have that much tracking error, but that’s only because a lot of ESG funds get pretty close to following my imaginary strategy. Here’s a funny Wall Street Journal story about how “eight of the 10 biggest U.S. sustainable funds are invested in oil-and-gas companies, which are regularly slammed by environmental activists.” Of course environmental funds invest in fossil fuels! As BlackRock Inc. explains:

A BlackRock spokesperson said the firm has designed its sustainable funds to offer investors similar risk and returns that they would achieve in broad market indexes while including the highest ESG-rated companies in each sector.

The highest ESG-rated companies in each sector. The grading is on a curve, and if there’s a whole sector that is not particularly environmentally conscious, then you just invest in the least bad companies in that sector. You couldn’t do it any other way: If you excluded the whole sector just because it is bad for the environment, then you wouldn’t own the same stocks as the broad market, and then sometimes your fund would underperform the index when fossil-fuel companies did well. The goal is not to pick environmentally conscious companies; the goal is to track the S&P 500 while having “ESG” in the name. 

I don’t mean that that’s BlackRock’s goal! It’s the investors’ goal. No one is being tricked here. Eight of the top 10 ESG funds own fossil-fuel companies, and they don’t keep their holdings secret or anything; there is a competitive market for ESG funds, and the ones that focus on index tracking seem to outcompete the ones that focus on environmental, social and governance issues. The amount of ESG that ESG investors mostly want, as demonstrated by their revealed preferences, is the minimum amount, the ESG label plus index tracking. 

By the way this seems to me to be an important theme in finance generally. Investors often want (1) what they want plus (2) a label that validates what they want, that tells them that what they want is a good thing to want. My model of credit rating agencies, for instance, is not so much “they give AAA ratings to bad things to fool investors into buying those bad things” but rather “they give AAA ratings to bad things because investors want to buy those things and would prefer them to have high ratings.” Similarly with sell-side equity research: You rate lots of stocks Buy not so much to trick your clients into buying them, but because your clients already own them and want to be congratulated for their acumen. An ESG fund that buys all the stocks investors would buy anyway, but says that they’re ESG, may not be the best way to achieve environmental or social or governance goals, but it’s probably the best way to give investors what they want.

WeContribute

This August, WeWork—now called the We Co.—announced its initial public offering, filing a prospectus to sell stock to public investors. This went poorly, and by the end of September WeWork had pulled its IPO and was looking for private rescue-ish financing, which it eventually got from SoftBank Group Corp., already its biggest investor.

As it was looking for money in October, WeWork prepared presentations and due diligence materials for potential lenders, and last Friday the company published some of those documents on its website. Here they are. They come to 49 pages, total, mostly graphs and bullet points, and they make an interesting contrast to WeWork’s 252-page IPO prospectus.

For instance here is how the prospectus begins:

We are a community company committed to maximum global impact. Our mission is to elevate the world’s consciousness. We have built a worldwide platform that supports growth, shared experiences and true success. We provide our members with flexible access to beautiful spaces, a culture of inclusivity and the energy of an inspired community, all connected by our extensive technology infrastructure. We believe our company has the power to elevate how people work, live and grow.

Here is how WeWork’s Oct. 11 investor presentation begins:

We have 600 locations, 676K desks, in 122 cities in 32 different countries

Flexible workspace is a market-validated real estate solution that has been around for decades

Conventional wisdom is that flexible workspace will have a small, but growing place in the global real estate market due to its evidenced sustained tenant demand

We think we do it better than anyone because of our member experience and scale — and we have the market share and occupancy to prove it

Here is how the prospectus describes WeWork’s business proposition for its tenants:

We have proven that community, flexibility and cost-efficiency can benefit the workplace needs of everyone from global citizens to global enterprises. We pioneered a “space-as-a-service” membership model that offers the benefits of a collaborative culture, the flexibility to scale workspace up and down as needed and the power of a worldwide community, all for a lower cost. Through iterative product development at scale and significant investment in technology infrastructure, we have demonstrated that we can build better solutions for less money. We are changing the way people work globally and, in the process, we have disrupted the largest asset class in the world—real estate.

We start by looking at space differently: as a place to bring people together, build community and enhance productivity. Philosophically, we believe in bringing comfort and happiness to the workplace. We employ over 500 designers and architects who work relentlessly to create spaces that are beautiful but simple, elevated but approachable, global yet locally unique, all delivered at a high quality without the associated expense. Next, we add a team of over 2,500 trained community managers who foster human connection through collaboration and holistically support our members both personally and professionally. Lastly, with a persistent dedication to improving the member experience, we add products and services to our platform, either by building them ourselves, acquiring them or entering into partnerships. The entire member experience is powered by technology designed to enable our members to manage their own space, make connections among each other and access products and services, all with the goal of increasing our members’ productivity, happiness and success.

And here is how the October presentation describes the business proposition for tenants and landlords:

We can offer space more affordably while charging ~2-2.5x our lease cost because we:

— Buy in bulk

— Have a scaled construction platform with standardized build-out procedures

— Receive subsidies from landlords

— Utilize square footage more efficiently than any lessor we know of, while curating the space to optimize member experience

We average ~4% gross churn, but have a net member retention rate of 121% driven by existing member firms who want more space

Landlords like us because we aggregate demand, can close quickly, and improve the perception of their building

Isn’t that better? It’s brisk and businesslike and actually tells you what you want to know; it describes, at a high and intuitive level, what WeWork’s business is and how it works and how it makes money and why you might want to invest in it. It strips out all the blather about community and consciousness and gets down to business. “Receive subsidies from landlords” just sounds like a way to make money in a way that “foster human connection through collaboration and holistically support our members both personally and professionally” does not.

Part of the difference is, of course, what happened in the two months between WeWork’s first IPO filing and the date of this investor deck: In August WeWork was aiming to be a world-conquering $60 billion company, in October it was aiming to survive as an $8 billion one, so elevating the world’s consciousness was replaced by “a small, but growing place in the global real estate market.” (August WeWork was barely a real estate company at all). Part of the difference is one of audience: In October, WeWork’s audience really was just professionals (potential bank lenders) who wanted to understand the economics of the business (and who had access to all the financial statements, etc., separately), while in August it at least theoretically included retail equity investors who might be swayed by the consciousness stuff.

But a big part of the difference is just one of regulatory apparatus: U.S. securities regulation requires certain disclosures and prohibits others, so the IPO prospectus is long and prosy and full of details while at the same time omitting a lot of what investors might actually want to know to value the business. 

Here, for instance, is a Wall Street Journal story about how the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission pushed back on WeWork’s use of “contribution margin,” a nonstandard accounting measure that it uses to describe how much money each of its locations brings in net of location operating expenses. Contribution margin is not part of U.S. generally accepted accounting principles, and the SEC worried that it was misleading and tried to get WeWork to take it out of the prospectus (where it is used “more than 100 times”). The debate was still ongoing when WeWork pulled the prospectus.

But then when WeWork went out and marketed itself to private investors and lenders, it didn’t just mention contribution margin; contribution margin was the main financial metric in the presentation. WeWork’s description of its business model (slide 16) has a bottom line expressed in terms of contribution margin; contribution margin is the thing that it thinks investors will be most interested in. In fact the private materials have more details about contribution margin than the public ones do, with breakdowns of contribution margin by location maturity (slide 20) and by specific major markets (slide 35) that aren’t in the prospectus. This makes some rough sense: If you are trying to figure out if WeWork is a viable business in the long term, a thing you will really care about is whether it can make more money from its locations than it costs to run them. The prospectus was weirdly opaque about that; the October deck gets straight to it. The presentation aims to give potential investors a forward-looking breakdown of how to model the business, rather than just checking off a long series of SEC requirements. 

I don’t want to make too much of this: The October deck is a summary presentation given to sophisticated investors who could get more detailed information elsewhere (including the 130 pages of historical financial statements and notes in the prospectus), and the fact that it tells a tighter and more appealing story than the prospectus is not necessarily a purely good thing; maybe that more compelling story needs more caveats and warnings. And WeWork’s view of contribution margin may be over-optimistic and cherry-picked. Still many of the complaints about the WeWork IPO were about how investors couldn’t understand or model the underlying economics of the business, and I suspect that part of the reason for that is that the IPO document, under the rules, isn't designed to tell you how to understand the business. That seems bad, and not just for WeWork.

Princelings

There is a rule of thumb in investment banking that the seniority of the person covering a client tends to match the seniority of the client. Senior managing directors call on eminent chief executive officers, hungry vice presidents call on heads of corporate development, and junior analysts trade spreadsheets with corporate assistant treasurers. The senior MDs would have nothing to talk about with the assistant treasurers, and the analysts might embarrass themselves in front of the CEOs.

It is fun to see that private banking takes the same approach with rich families. Here’s a Business Insider interview with Ida Liu of Citi Private Bank North America:

"Typically, the senior banker will cover the matriarch, patriarch; and the junior bankers will cover the next generation of wealth, which is very important to us, as we know how much wealth is shifting over time," Liu, who stepped into her role six months ago, said. "And the associate bankers are covering the millennial clients — so the grandkids of the patriarch and matriarch."

Look honestly I can’t imagine better training for a career in private banking than, like, taking entitled third-generation heirs out to the club. And obviously those heirs will have more fun clubbing with the junior bankers than the senior ones, so it is a good division of labor. But what about sub-millennial clients? Presumably there are some rich matriarchs with toddler grandchildren; when do they get private-banking coverage? Do the most junior private-banking associates bring them snacks on the playground to try to build deep long-term relationships? Do they take them to Raffi concerts? Advance screenings of “Frozen 2”?
