Fall is always a big time for consumer tech, from the big product launches of October to the gift guides and Best Of lists in November and December. Plenty of fascinating things have been on the Ars radar this go-round, but it's hard to ignore one company in particular.

 

Google (Hardware) is really having itself a fall, eh?

 

It hasn't all been bad. While not hardware exactly, Android 10 is fascinating and fresh. And Nvidia produced a top-notch Android TV device, or Google itself bought Fitbit and seems primed to be a major factor in the fitness wearable market, for instance. But somehow Google deciding to launch another messaging app—RCS through the Google Messages app, Google's ninth messaging app after Google Talk, Google Voice, Google Buzz, Google+ Messenger, Hangouts, Spaces, Allo, and Hangouts Chat—isn't even in the Top 5 puzzling releases coming from Mountain View this season.

 

So for this week's Orbital Transmission, we're revisiting the headlining Google Hardware releases from the fall (and outlining the head scratching details about many of 'em). Taken together, it's almost enough to make you long for the days of Google Glass (which, actually, may not be too far away, either).

Future potential meets present-day headaches in Google Stadia

Streaming is the future of gaming—ask practically anyone, as long as that "anyone" is involved in selling games. But the first major foray into this space, Google's Stadia, doesn't quite seem ready for launch. A boatload of features ended up being absent for the platform's release this week, both big (no Google Assistant integration or family sharing) and small (no screen capture capabilities, some title delays). And as it stands at launch, Stadia is too limited and too unreliable, with too little advantage to justify giving up on the established way of delivering and playing games. While there are some interesting fringe benefits to a gaming life in Google's cloud (and the potential for more in the future), today those benefits are not worth the headaches.

 

Google did more than change the branding with its latest Wi-Fi kit

Google Wi-Fi, err now Nest Wi-Fi, might actually be the most successful Google hardware launch of the fall (not the year, Pixel 3a is Also Good™). This latest router release isn't just Google Wi-Fi plus a smart speaker—it's new, improved, and with better coverage. Don't take our word for it; Ars' resident Wi-Fi connectivity masochist, Jim Salter, recently put the new pair offering through its paces in his tricky bi-level, basement boasting Internet dungeon. "The good news is, a two-piece Google Wi-Fi system did cover even my challenging 3,500-square-foot house," he writes. "That's an impressive achievement and a clear win over the original Google Wi-Fi."

 

The biggest draw of the Pixelbook Go is that it's made by Google

When it comes to in-house laptops, Google continues to refresh its hardware just once a year. Until now, those updates have produced luxury Chromebooks with varying degrees of popularity: 2017's Pixelbook won many hearts with its unique design, versatility as a convertible, and solid performance, while 2018's Pixel Slate tablet was a decided flop. This year, the company debuted the very Macbook-ish Pixelbook Go—a more mid-tier ($649) laptop sitting between the entry-level, education-friendly Chromebooks and the more luxury varieties. We like its base model with 8GB of RAM, a comfortable keyboard, and that Google-y minimalist design... but once you get above the base model the bang for your buck ratio really feels off.

 

The Google Pixel 4: So, when can we have the Nexus line back?

Oh, Google—your phones had so, so much potential: software updates faster than anyone else, industry-leading camera and AI, a hardware company without precedent (meaning they could experiment free of history). But four Pixels later, this line of phones is harder to recommend than ever. The Pixel 4 isn't bad in a vacuum, but the rest of Google's Android competition gets better every year, while Google stands still. "This year, Google turned in a weak, timid update to its flagship smartphone," writes Reviews Editor Ron Amadeo. "I'm not sure who to recommend this to at $800 or $900."
