Micromobbin'

Lime made a commitment this week to use only electric vans to pick up and redeploy its scooters by 2023, starting with Paris. Lime said the vans it uses in Paris will be electric by the end of spring this year.

Lime couched the move as an aim to reduce its environmental footprint. But it’s also an effort to build goodwill and to prepare for tightening emissions regulations.

“Lime is a company founded on clean electrified micromobility solutions and committing to 100% electric vehicles in our operations is the next step on our journey in fighting climate change,” said Lime President Joe Kraus. “We’re focused on new solutions that drive a zero-carbon future and are acting today for a greener tomorrow.”

Other solutions companies are trying to reduce their carbon footprints involve charging stations, swappable batteries and autonomous repositioning of scooters. More on that here.

Meanwhile in more morbid news, a Quartz analysis found there have been at least 29 scooter-related deaths since 2018. That’s a small number compared to the more than 36,000 car-related fatalities in the U.S. in 2018 alone, but it speaks to how this new and exciting mode of transportation comes with risks to consumers.

EARNINGS SEASON!!!

Some people love holidays. Me? I love earnings season — and not just because it comes four times a year. There’s drama and dollars. More importantly, it’s a chance to get a closer look at what the heck is going on in these companies.

This week, I was paying attention to earnings from Ford, GM and Uber.

First Uber.

Uber said it will generate a loose form of profit in Q4 2020, earlier than it or investors previously anticipated. As TechCrunch’s Alex Wilhelm wrote, if Uber has turned the corner on profitability, the halo effect from the good news could prove a boon to other on-demand companies.

The important numbers at a glance for Uber in Q4:

  • Revenue was $4.069 billion, up 37% compared to the year-ago quarter.
  • Net losses of $1.096 billion compared to a loss of $887 million in the fourth quarter of 2018.
  • Earnings per share, inclusive of all costs (GAAP), came to a loss of $0.64 in the quarter, above an analyst average of $0.68 in per-share losses.
  • Gross bookings grew 28% to $18.13 billion, from $14.17 billion in the same year-ago period.
  • Uber’s trailing 2019 operating cash flow worsened from a loss of $2.52 billion at the end of the third quarter of 2019 to a loss of $4.32 billion at the end of the fourth quarter.

Now onto the automakers. Ford and GM provided lukewarm outlooks and at least one automaker (ahem, Ford) shook up its management structure after earnings in hopes of correcting the less than stellar outcome.

The main takeaway: labor costs have risen as demand has weakened, putting downward pressure on profits. All the while, these giants are putting money into electrification and other future mobility ventures that may or may not pay off. And that tension will continue. Don’t forget, Ford is in the midst of an $11 billion global restructuring plan.

GM reported revenue of $30.8 billion and earnings per share of 5 cents. That number didn’t include the effects of a 40-day strike that wiped out $2.6 billion in earnings before interest and taxes in the quarter. Losses at the Cruise self-driving unit widened to $305 million from $194 million a year ago.

Ford lost $1.67 billion in the fourth quarter and forecasted a disappointing 2020 that fell short of Wall Street’s expectations. Ford’s botched launch of its Explorer SUV was largely to blame for its sour 2019. For 2020, Ford is projecting full-year earnings of between 94 cents and $1.20 a share, or adjusted earnings before interest and taxes of $5.6 billion and $6.6 billion. The company reported earnings of $6.4 billion in 2019.

After earnings, Ford announced it was changing up its leadership structure by moving Jim Farley to chief operating officer and announcing that Joe Hinrichs, president of automotive, was retiring. Farley will also continue to lead the automaker’s mobility and autonomous-vehicle businesses, a role he’s held since April.

A little bird

We hear things. And when we do, we share them with you.

Softbank Vision Fund, which shook up the investing world with the largest investment fund ever pooled, appears to be imploding right before our eyes.

Financial Times broke the story this week that Michael Ronen, who led the firm’s transportation investments, including in Getaround, GM Cruise, Nuro and Park Jockey, has left. FT reported that his departure is tied directly to the failure of SoftBank to raise any outside investment for the company’s second Vision Fund.

Now, our own sources are telling us that Ronen plans to turn his attention and investments to Israeli startups. We haven’t spoken directly to Ronen yet, and we’ll keep you updated once we learn more or confirm the information.

Ronen isn’t the only valued executive to leave in recent months. David Thevenon quietly resigned in October and Michelle Horn, a former McKinsey partner who joined SoftBank as its chief people officer last year, has left the outfit, too. Thevenon has just joined Balderton Capital in London.

Deals deals deals

We have a couple of trucking-related deals this week (and more in the wings we hear), the latest sign that the sector continues to attract investors.

Much of the investments center around startups promising to make hauling freight more efficient. And there’s a reason why. Using trucks to haul freight accounts for around $12.5 billion of the $17 billion freight market, according to the Bureau of Transportation Statistics. It’s also one of the most inefficient marketplaces with thousands of small and single-vehicle operators and legacy (often paper-based) systems underpinning communications.

Emerge, a Phoenix-based startup that has built a platform for shippers and brokers to find and allocate truck freight more effectively, announced this week it raised $20 million in a Series A funding round led by NewRoad Capital Partners. Previous investors Greycroft and 9Yards Capital also participated.

Then there’s Maven Machines, a provider of fleet management software for commercial transportation. The startup raised $7 million in Series A funding round led by Allos Ventures, with additional participation from Hearst Ventures, Riverfront Ventures, Great Oaks Venture Capital and existing private investors. Maven Machines is putting its capital towards hiring more people.

A couple of other deals worth noting including Turo and Seattle-based Rad Power Bikes.

Rad Power Bikes raised $25 million in a funding round led by Vulcan Capital and Durable Capital Partners LP. Rad Power will use the funding to expand its retail locations and grow its mobile service and “white glove delivery offerings.”

The company has three retail stores located in Seattle, Vancouver, B.C., and Utrecht in the Netherlands. The folks at Rad Power Bikes tell me that the company plans to experiment with different types of retail locations going forward, including pop-up stores and full brick and mortar locations. The next retail location will open in Austin later this year.

Rad Power also recently launched its own mobile service in select markets including Seattle, Austin, Sacramento and Vancouver.  The company is expanding this service to more locations as part of an eventual national rollout.

Turo, the peer-to-peer short-term car rental company, has extended its Series E round by raising another $30 million. That Series E round, which last summer was led by IAC, now includes Manhattan Venture Partners and Allen & Company LLC, and has reached a whopping $280 million.
