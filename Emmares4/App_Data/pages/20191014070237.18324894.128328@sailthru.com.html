Hey y’all, it's Austin. A woman named Elizabeth runs a startup and demonstrates a remarkable ability to convince investors she can pull off the near-impossible. She then faces criminal charges of defrauding those investors. You can probably see why, in the course of reporting a feature article for the latest issue of Bloomberg Businessweek, people frequently compared Quintillion founder Elizabeth Pierce with the creator of Theranos Inc., Elizabeth Holmes.

In an industry that prides itself on move-fast-and-break-things corporate values and reality-distorting CEOs, the line separating a true business visionary from the inspiring-but-flawed hype masters is becoming ever-finer. Although their antics haven’t drawn the kind of fraud charges facing the Elizabethes, the founders of Hampton Creek, UBiome, Uber and WeWork built companies once considered industry-redefining and now serve as cautionary tales.

Pierce’s Alaskan telecom startup, Quintillion, offers some lessons for investors looking for warning signs. When Pierce conceived of Quintillion in 2012, she and her business partners sought to pioneer a first-of-its-kind fiber-optic cable that would connect Asia to Europe through the Arctic. It was a compelling vision, one that could both increase internet speeds around the planet and help close the digital divide in Pierce’s home state. The project would take hundreds of millions of dollars to fund, a scale of investment few venture capitalists had the appetite for initially. Pierce eventually managed to reel in investors, in part by shrewdly leveraging tactics all too common in Silicon Valley.

Here’s an analysis of her playbook, based on hundreds of pages of court documents, as well as interviews with three dozen people familiar with Pierce’s Quintillion fraud:

1. Hostile secrecy. Apple and Google mythologize secrecy as a way to enhance a product’s allure. There are practical needs for this approach, like protecting intellectual property, but Pierce weaponized this tactic as a means to conceal the true innerworkings of Quintillion to everyone around her, including her own employees and financial backers (not dissimilar to the culture of secrecy Holmes built at Theranos). According to court records and people familiar with the matter, Pierce was often the only person allowed to negotiate with the company’s telecom partners; she stored revenue contracts on a personal Google Drive account only she had the password to; and she once physically kicked a subordinate to stop him from sharing accounting data during a phone call with an investor. The over-the-top discretion may sound bizarre now, but people who worked with Pierce at the time said she was simply guarding their trade secrets—just like any other tech company would.

2. Teflon celebrity. Pierce never became a household name like some of her CEO counterparts in the lower 48, but ex-coworkers describe her as achieving rock-star status in the Alaska business community. The narrative she spun helped her become a fixture of the conference circuit, where attendees would gush about her vision for finally bringing high-speed internet to the most remote parts of the last frontier. She appeared in photo-ops and ribbon-cuttings with Alaska Senator Lisa Murkowski and then Governor Bill Walker. The Federal Communications Commission appointed her to lead an advisory committee on rural broadband. Celebrity status seemed to create a blinding faith in Pierce.

3. Don’t-check-the-math business model. Like many tech startups, Quintillion’s business model was originally built on the promise of future revenue, rather than what Pierce could deliver today. Established broadband providers were confounded by her pitch to generate massive sales from wiring tiny communities around the coast of Alaska. “When it became evident that this Alaska installation was going to cost hundreds of millions of dollars to serve five or six North Slope villages, we were certainly scratching our heads,” said Martin Cary, senior vice president of business at GCI, a 40-year-old Alaska telecom. “The math didn’t make any sense.”

Pierce’s fraud was brazen; yet, it fooled coworkers, government officials and institutional investors. What’s most telling is that so few realized anything was awry until it was too late. Pierce, according to nearly every person interviewed, behaved no differently from other tech CEOs, who used some of these same approaches to amass great fortunes. —Austin Carr
