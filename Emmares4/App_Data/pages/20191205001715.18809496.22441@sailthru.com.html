Brexit: Can Britain Decide?

We have a week left until Britain decides. Or at least, that is when the U.K. will hold a general election. Painful recent experience shows that when Britain appears to make a decision, as with the 2016 referendum on leaving the European Union, it doesn’t always act on it. So, what is the likely outcome, and what will this portend for Brexit, the issue that matters most for Britain and the world?

Polls show Boris Johnson’s Conservatives with a clear lead (42.6% in the latest poll of polls), into which the Labour party under Jeremy Corbyn (32.3%) has made a few inroads in the last few days. The Brexit party, which advocates leaving the EU without a deal, seems to have collapsed almost completely, helping the Conservatives. The Liberal Democrats, who advocate revoking the referendum result and remaining, are losing ground rapidly, which is helping Labour. And the chance of a majority for either party is weakened by the likely overwhelming rejection of both in Scotland and Northern Ireland, which voted to remain in the referendum.

The markets are plainly rooting for the Conservatives, which makes sense given the strongly anti-capitalist manifesto on which Labour is running. As shown by this chart by Neil Mellor, senior currency strategist at Bank of New York Mellon Corp., the pound has risen against the dollar almost perfectly in alignment with the Conservative lead over Labour in the polls:

But even if the Conservatives seem almost certain to win the most seats in parliament, they are far less certain to gain more than 50% of them. With a majority, the U.K. almost certainly leaves the EU at the end of next month. With any other outcome, the likelihood is a Labour-led coalition, which would hold a second referendum.

Betting markets currently rate the odds at 69% for a Conservative majority, and 31% for no party with overall control, according to the Oddschecker aggregation site.

These aren’t the kind of probabilities on which most of us would want to stake too much capital. To put the odds in terms that most investors might understand, the following quote from Stephen Bush of the center-left New Statesman makes eminent sense:

As it stands, we are a normal-sized polling error – that is to say, if either or both of the parties are being systematically under- or overestimated by three percentage points – from either a Labour minority government or a Conservative landslide.

In terms of the bell curve of plausible outcomes, the mean would say Johnson in with a narrow majority. Each extra seat in either direction could alter his chances to survive five years, and to choose his Brexit strategy with a relatively free hand. One standard deviation in one direction and we have a Conservative landslide, leaving Johnson in control the way Tony Blair or Margaret Thatcher were in their heyday; a standard deviation in the other direction and Jeremy Corbyn is in power at the head of a minority government.

The U.K.’s antiquated electoral system adds an extra dose of unpredictability. This means that the chances of a big move in currency markets in the early hours of Friday morning are very real. As this chart from George Saravelos of Deutsche Bank AG shows, all Britain’s general elections and referendums in this decade have provoked an immediate currency reaction:

Not even a shock Corbyn victory could create an earthquake to match the response to the Brexit referendum, but there is a lot of difference between these outcomes as far as investors are concerned. While the chance of a Corbyn outright majority remains way out in the tail, the potential damage to sterling should be limited. But a lot of very marginal decisions next week by voters who have little or no time for either Johnson or Corbyn could add up to some extremely consequential changes.

 

Pound Foolish?

Is the currency market sensibly positioned for this? I am inclined to believe not. The rally of the last few days has left the pound as strong as it has been at any time since the referendum, on a real effective basis (taking into account the high inflation rate in the U.K. compared to its trading partners). 

A 6% depreciation since the eve of the referendum doesn’t seem excessive given the predictions of disaster for the British economy in the event of Brexit that were widely made before the vote. How much more value can there possibly be?

One argument in favor of sterling is that the relief of uncertainty should itself strengthen the economy, and with it the pound. The Brexit saga’s deadening impact on decision-making can be seen in fixed capital formation; businesses are less likely to commit to investments if they are uncertain about the backdrop. The chart is no simpler, even though several options have been eliminated. The reason is that the question moves on. Whoever wins next week, the focus will immediately shift to the free trade deal that the U.K. must thrash out with the EU. A deal must be agreed by the end of next year. If it isn’t, and if the U.K. doesn’t ask for an extension, the country would start trading with its closest neighbors on the minimal backstop terms dictated by the World Trade Organization. 

How easy will it be to reach a deal? Saravelos of Deutsche Bank suggests the U.K. will have weak negotiating power. The countries that rely on Britain as an export destination tend to be tiny, such as Belize. But Britain relies on the EU as a destination for 45% of its exports. There is no entity with which the U.K. has weaker bargaining power — and the EU has ample incentive to play it tough.

Politically, free trade negotiations should provide yet more great entertainment in parliament, as different possible deals set different parts of the country against each other. Britain’s exports of financial services have roared ahead over the last few decades, aided by the increased cohesion of the EU. Trying to safeguard access for this industry also means safeguarding London and the Southeast, the country’s most prosperous regions:

Meanwhile, some of the worst poverty in the U.K. is in declining seaside resorts and fishing ports, for whom the recovery of control of British fishing waters is critically important. Could this be negotiated away? Other parts of the country care most about agriculture, or manufacturing. And many Britons are employed by foreign companies, which might threaten to leave if they don’t get a deal to their liking and suffer weaker access to the EU market, complicating things further. 

So, in short, there is every possibility that Britain will fail to agree on a deal. These chances increase if the Conservatives have only a slim majority.

Cole of RBC rates the odds on this happening at 12%. As his estimate of the probability of a Conservative win looks a little low, at 60%, the chance might be a little higher than that. Meanwhile the prospect of canceling Brexit altogether remains alive, at least for another week. If the Conservatives fail to win a majority, the U.K. probably has to undergo the torment and uncertainty of a second referendum, four years after the first. For now, he rates the odds of Brexit cancellation at 24%, which may be generous. 

Put differently, Britain will next year entertain the world by staging either a repeat of 2016, with a second referendum, or of 2019, with brinkmanship over the possibility of crashing out of the EU with no deal. Brexit II, here we come. This time, it’s personal.
