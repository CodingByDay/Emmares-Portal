It has been more than half a decade since NSA whistle-blower Edward Snowden made his dramatic revelations and then disappeared into exile in Russia. Except he never really disappeared. Even while holed up in Moscow, the former IT guy for government spooks became a much-sought-after speaker on the internet civil rights circuit, where he’d appear by video hookup or sometimes teleport into a robot—the Snowbot!—to interact with his fans.

But as Andy Greenberg writes this week on Backchannel, Snowden never fully explained what led him to disclose the existence of the government’s mass-surveillance program—until now. In a new memoir released on Tuesday, he revisits his youth, when he reveled in the freedom and anonymity of a mostly pre-commercial internet, a realm that offered, in his words, “a reset button on your life.”

That was the ideal he says he saw being corrupted by the NSA’s dragnet hoovering of personal data in the post-9/11 push to catch terrorists before they could do harm. “It’s the story of an ambitious geek,” Greenberg writes, “smart enough to shoot up through the NSA’s ranks while keeping intact ideals for the internet that were entirely opposed to those of his employer.”

Snowden describes himself as “an indoor cat,” and he stays up into the early morning so he can more easily communicate with friends and supporters during business hours in the West. For now, he has no plans or even hopes to return to the US, where he faces indictment under the Espionage Act. “My life has always been mediated by a screen. What difference does it make whether I’m looking at a screen in New York or Berlin or Moscow?” Snowden tells Greenberg in an interview. “It’s all the same internet.”

Mark Robinson | Features Editor, WIRED
