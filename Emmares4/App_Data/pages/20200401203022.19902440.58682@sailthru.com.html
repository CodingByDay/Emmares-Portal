If the running joke is to say that was a helluva year when a particularly rough month wraps, March 2020 may be a century. 

It's been more than a month since the first COVID-19, aka coronavirus, death in the US and the associated outbreak—it's almost been a month since we published our extensive, always-updated guide, even. On February 1, we were prepping for another SXSW or eagerly awaiting the new James Bond flick, but such things seem so distant and trivial now in the wake of this worldwide medical crisis. Obviously, COVID-19 has greatly impacted society's health and our medical infrastructure. But when a pandemic like this comes along, its effect has such wide reach that virtually no aspect of daily life can go on as it did before.

 

So for this week's Orbital Transmission, we're stepping back from the medical aspect of COVID-19 and highlighting some of the other ways this disease has impacted the various facets of Ars life. From Silicon Valley to streaming services to security, this disease has already made what could be a long-lasting impact in several disciplines. We're searching for silver linings where we can get 'em—see conspiracy-theorists finding they aren't welcome anywhere on the current Internet—but it's undeniably rough out there in the day-to-day. Stay informed, stay diligent, and do what you can to stay well and sane in this brave new world. 

 

—@NathanMattise

Orbital Transmission 04.01.2020
 

For TV and film, the little guys have an unexpected time to shine

Our current social distancing shutdown impacted the TV/film world swiftly. Anything that was in production or planning on being in production in the near future had to abruptly hit pause, like Stranger Things S4. And given people's inability to physically be in a theater, high profile films banking on big box offices opted to reschedule 'til the fall rather than exploring the VOD route (see Wonder Woman: 1984, now coming in August at the earliest). But for previously-scheduled TV series or smaller films, the current surplus of free time may have presented an unexpected opportunity: more actively-seeking viewers with less stuff to do than virtually ever before. Shows like Killing Eve in fact bumped up their spring premiere dates, and unorthodox film distribution from VOD to drive-ins have seen a surge given some independent film makers are just eager to get their films out into the world.

 

More people online more often has attracted eager exploiters

The pandemic has naturally led people to do what they always do with free time: waste time online. Unfortunately, this larger user base has seemingly attracted several attackers trying to leverage the coronavirus for nefarious digital means. With Zoom's questionable privacy policies already a concern, it can't be good that a researcher has discovered attackers can use Zoom to steal users’ Windows credentials with no warning, too. And though endless emails and websites are promising vital information about keeping safe from the coronavirus pandemic, in fact, a flood of them are scams that push malware, ransomware, and disinformation; attempt to steal passwords and personal information; and conduct espionage operations by hackers working for nation-states. Stay diligent digitally, too, everyone.

 

Even video games are shifting in unexpected ways due to COVID-19

Similar to TV and film, there's quite a large captive audience for newer video games these days. But that doesn't mean the industry at large has avoided the need to shift in some unexpected ways. You can't play without having the hardware, for instance, so Nintendo has had to ramp up Switch production as stay-at-home lifestyles led to a rush on the handheld (it also led to third-party price gouging, woof). Real sports have become eSports for the interim. Games that depend on local co-op and person-to-person interaction, like the famously-in-the-real-world Pokemon Go, had to alter gameplay significantly. Most notably, pandemic-themed game Plague, Inc. has completely inverted, introducing gameplay that asks you to save (rather than destroy) all of humanity from deadly viral foes.

 

Even the biggest of big tech companies have new realities

Hospitality, gig workers, medical professionals, artists, and educators may simply be the most publicly impacted lines of work staring down scary new realities thanks to COVID-19. But no one industry is truly immune to necessitated change, and the biggest of big tech companies now stand as proof. Tesla has pledged to shift its resources towards producing ventilators. Amazon, seemingly operating its distribution arm as normal, now faces increased internal pressure and public scrutiny as warehouse and delivery workers plot strikes and labor action in the face of a lack of resources and hazard compensation. And perhaps the craziest tech niche of all—the rapidly growing and competitive space launch market—is changing, too, though not entirely for now. Arianespace in Europe has halted launches for the time being, but the US Space Force finally got into space for the first time last week (albeit with some normal activities around a Cape Canaveral launch shut down).
