Orbital Transmission 12.11.2019
 

Wait, how old is my killing machine hero in the new Gears of War?

The first thing to know about feature journalism at Ars is that not every piece has to be a deeply reported, narrative work. (You've seen our annual iOS and Android reviews, right? There's no traditional narrative but there is *definitely* a lot to read and a lot of work behind it. Pitch us, all.) Often, some of the core review work done on site can end up being among the most memorable, insightful, and entertaining stories of a given year. Case in point: contributor Nick Cappozoli's review of seemingly a ho-hum Gears of War new release is much, much more than a "play it or not?" breakdown. Instead, he examines how we've gotten to a point where playing as a 10-year-old wielding a machine gun doesn't phase most of us. What kind of messages are baked in the video games we all spend time with in 2019—do they correspond to our reality these days?

 

How a pizza app brought up the future of Web accessibility

Ordering food via mobile phone feels like an almost universal experience in 2019, and it's something many of us likely take for granted. But when Guillermo Robles, a blind California resident who uses screen readers to access the Internet, tried to place an order through Domino's mobile app, neither the app nor Domino's website proved usable by a screen reader. Domino's did not rectify the situation, and this eventually led to a lawsuit that nearly reached the Supreme Court this year. This fall, Ars' Kate Cox broke down why this seemingly little corporate misstep actually had massive implications for the state of the Internet itself—and how it proves the future of Web design needs to evolve.

 

The X-15 may be the baddest plane in history—Joe Engle is its last living pilot

The X-15 was not the first rocket-powered aircraft, but it is probably the best one ever built and flown. The US Air Force and NASA developed the X-15 to better understand flight under extreme conditions, including reentry through the Earth’s atmosphere. Yet more than half a century later, the exceptional plane still holds the world record for speed by a piloted, powered aircraft after William Knight flew the vehicle at Mach 6.70 in 1967. The aircraft flew 199 times and had only 12 pilots, and Joe Engle is the last living one. This fall, he sat down with Ars' Space Reporter Eric Berger to share old war stories and properly contextualize the X-15 on the 60th anniversary of its first flight.

 

How does our most basic Earthly tech function in deep-space?

Speaking of space, it happens to be most exciting realm for the development of tech we don't think twice about down here on Earth. Take CPUs for instance: Curiosity, everyone’s favorite Mars rover, works with two BAE RAD750 processors clocked at up to 200MHz. It has 256MB of RAM and 2GB of SSD. That's the state of the art—but it's generations behind a mid-range iPhone. Or if thinking about connectivity, right now, the most efficient tool is the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter spacecraft, which can relay data at a maximum rate of 6Mbps (a current record for planetary missions). Even the most basic dial-up from years ago would dust this setup. If that seems odd, luckily Ars contributor Jacek Krywko has been talking with NASA, the ESA, and various contractors about these very topics in order to produce guides on deep-space CPUs and communication networks. The engineering challenges involved are literally out of this world. (Sorry.)
