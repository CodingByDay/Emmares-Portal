Our take on the latest developments

The terror attacks of Sept. 11, 2001 brought an eerie silence to America as the skies fell silent and the country absorbed a collective trauma. A similar quiet is now descending on the land as the coronavirus pandemic worsens.

On Thursday, many cities barred large gatherings and schools were closed. The NCAA canceled the iconic March Madness basketball tournament. Disneyland shut down. Many of the routines comforts of daily American life seemed to evaporate, replaced by an urgent need to defeat the virus. The lockdown came as countries around the world laid out similarly drastic steps to slow the spread of Covid-19 and protect the most vulnerable. New York City Mayor Bill DeBlasio likened it to a war. It will get a lot worse before it gets better, he predicted.

Fear also stalked Wall Street, with many investors and traders too young to experience the collapse of Lehman Brothers in 2008 now learning what a real panic looks like. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell the most since the crash of October 1987 as traders stagger toward the end of a week that will surely enter the pantheon as one of the worst in Wall Street's history.

One of the strangest aspects of the outbreak is how it has changed how we experience the everyday, especially the things we take for granted. Right now, it seems, nobody can dine out or take a subway ride without wondering about that person coughing nearby. In the coming weeks, nobody will enjoy the thrilling surge of an unlikely underdog in the NCAA tournament. Nobody will exult in a stoppage time goal in the Champions League or bask in the sonic glory of the New York Philharmonic.

As China's experience shows us, the worst of the virus will eventually pass. Until then, some people say the Internet will help us feel less isolated as we tuck ourselves away behind closed doors. We have Netflix. We have Twitter. Whether that is enough, and for how long it will have to be, is unknown. --Tim Annett
