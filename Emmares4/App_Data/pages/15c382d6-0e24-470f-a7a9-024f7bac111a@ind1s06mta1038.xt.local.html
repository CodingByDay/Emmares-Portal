Hi. The pandemic has forced many people whose job relies on international travel to rethink how they work. This includes everyone from a globe-trotting  photographer exploring his local English country lanes to a scientist who normally studies biodiversity hotspots in the tropics but had to refocus on the English coast.

Today’s Fix the Planet is about a project by that scientist, Mika Peck at the University of Sussex. Unable to research ecosystems in Papua New Guinea and Ecuador as he would usually, he turned to kelp. This seaweed can be found around most of the UK coast, including off Shoreham in the south of England where Peck lives. “I went for a dive, and it was like a beautiful sea meadow. There were lots of cuttlefish, a lot of species, and it kind of blew me away. It was like, hang on a minute, I need to do this on my own doorstep,” says Peck.

Kelp is a potential nature-based solution to climate change. But we won’t know how big a Fix it is until we get a handle on how well it recovers when we remove potential threats to it, which is exactly what Peck is trying to find out. I joined him at sea for a day to find out more. Once you've read this email, make sure you watch our video report on the story here.
How much carbon can kelp store if we allow it to recover? Photo: Nature Picture Library / Alamy

Why help kelp?
This macroalgae helps us in three key ways. First, it locks up carbon and so provides us with another CO2 removal option in addition to tree-planting, seagrass, restoring peatlands and other nature-based solutions to climate change. Globally, kelp and other macroalgae are estimated to sequester about 634 million tonnes of CO2 a year, nearly twice what the UK emits a year. At a more local level, kelp provides a habitat for marine life and can be an important nursery for fish. It also acts as a natural coastal flood defence, soaking up some of the energy in waves. ““This is definitely a nature-based solution to address both climate and biodiversity issues,” says Peck. “On our doorstep, we’ve got a way of regenerating a biodiverse environment, as interesting as a tropical rainforest far away.”

What’s happening with the kelp where Peck lives?
Hard numbers are hard to come by, due to a lack of surveys. But kelp is on the decline off Shoreham and to the west. In the 1980s, there was so much of the seaweed washing up on beaches in the area that it was referred to locally as the “kelp problem” and was even discussed in the UK parliament . The culprit for the decline is also not entirely clear: the Great Storm of 1987 has been mooted as one possible reason, but Peck and others suspect fishing trawlers are a more likely candidate. With that in mind, campaigners fought and won a five-year trawling ban in the area. The idea now is to take a baseline survey of the state of the kelp, and then come back every year to see if it can bounce back. You could call it a rewilding project.

How are they going to measure it?
For three weeks this month, Peck and colleagues have been heading out each day from Shoreham harbour on a small workboat. On the sunny day I joined them, it became clear how hard they had worked to be innovative and keep the costs down to make the survey quick and something that could be repeated every July for the next five years. On my visit, the researchers were surveying three of the 28 sites on their map (see the picture below). They are using three methods: video, audio and environmental DNA, which involves sampling water for genetic material shed by animals ( there’s more on how it works here).

“A typical day now is working well,” says Peck, as he drops one of three baited remote underwater video devices into the water. These “bruvs” are essentially a GoPro and hydrophone attached to a system of metal bars into the water. They cost as little as £2000 each. The team is trialling a cheaper one costing about £200, though the camera may not be up to scratch.

All the footage captured will be analysed to give a picture of the kelp’s extent, health and diversity. The team expects to see sugar kelp (Saccharina latissima), tangleweed (Laminaria hyperborea) and oarweed (Laminaria digitata), which are less “forest-like” than some structural species found further west. Then there’s the audio. Peck has previously found a link between healthy coral reefs in the tropics  and certain types of soundscape, measured by pitch, duration and amplitude. Whether that holds true for south England’s temperate waters remains to be seen.

Lastly, there is the DNA captured in samples of water, which Kat Bruce of biodiversity monitoring firm NatureMetrics sequences in a lab, and then matches against a reference library of marine life. So far, 44 species of bony fish have been found here. Testing for shark species will start soon. Will there be more species in five years’ time if the kelp here recovers? That’s exactly what she and Peck hope to find out.

What’s next?
As the boat heads back to shore, Peck explains that this is just the beginning. More research is planned on the dynamics of how much carbon is locked up in the kelp here and how long it stays there. A partnership with other researchers is planned to see whether it is worthwhile to grow kelp from spores in a lab before planting them out in active restoration projects (the current project is just about letting nature do its thing). A few kilometres east of Shoreham, kelp farms are being considered. A local public exhibition is planned to teach people more about kelp. Meanwhile, the survey team will come back each year to see what – if any – difference the trawler ban is making. “The dream is basically to come back to a healthy ecosystem,” says Peck.
