Surveillance

FISA IN THE HOUSE — A coalition of privacy and civil liberties groups sent a letter to House leadership on Sunday night demanding they amend H.R. 6172 to require law enforcement agencies to get a warrant before searching someone’s internet history. The Senate came just one vote shy of approving such a change last week when lawmakers voted on an amendment from Sens. Ron Wyden (D-Ore.) and Steve Daines (R-Mont.) to the House bill, which would reauthorize provisions of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act. “The Senate vote shows there is overwhelming support for this important reform, which House leadership should include in the Senate-passed version of the bill,” Neema Singh Guliani, senior legislative counsel for the American Civil Liberties Union, which signed the letter, said in a statement.

Last week, overhaul-minded lawmakers like Rep. Zoe Lofgen (D-Calif.) — who scuttled the House's first surveillance package in February — indicated they would seek such an amendment when the House takes up the legislation again. On Friday, House Majority Leader Steny Hoyer said he expected the bill to be considered on May 27.

Right now it remains unclear if House leadership will allow the measure to be altered further. After the Senate passed an amended version of the House bill, the Justice Department issued a blistering statement saying it could not support the amended legislation because it "would unacceptably degrade our ability to conduct surveillance of terrorists, spies and other national security threats.”

RATCLIFFE VOTE DUE TUESDAY — The Senate Intelligence Committee is poised to vote on the nomination of Rep. John Ratcliffe (R-Texas) for director of national intelligence. After initially withdrawing amid Democratic opposition and a tepid Republican reaction, Ratcliffe got a more welcome reception from GOP members of the panel two weeks ago.

John Ratcliffe. | Gabriella Demczuk /The New York Times via AP, Pool

In his on-the-record response to questions about election security, Ratcliffe largely sidestepped . Asked by Wyden about the intelligence community not assessing whether 2016 Russian interference had an impact on the outcome on the election of President Donald Trump — such as examining whether leaked Democratic emails affected public opinion toward Trump — Ratcliffe instead answered about other elements of the IC assessment and Senate studies concluding that there wasn’t evidence available that Russia tampered with voting machines. Ratcliffe also deferred to the FBI and CISA on the security of internet voting.

THEY’RE CALLING IT ‘MUCH ADO ABOUT NOTHING’ — Hackers that targeted a prominent entertainment law firm and leaked information from private documents involving pop star Lady Gaga and are holding a larger trove for a $42 million ransom now claims it has data on Trump. But there’s reason to be dubious that REvil will release sensitive data on the president.

For one, the hacked law firm, Grubman Shire Meiselas & Sacks, has never represented Trump. The firm said it has never paid any money to the hackers despite a claim from the gang that it had. What’s more, a previous REvil data dump that included Trump information wasn’t terribly impressive, said Brett Callow, threat analyst at Emsisoft. The Trump-related document was “simply a pro forma ‘thank you’ letter,” Callow told MC. “This was exfiltrated along with a collection of other documents during an attack on a management consulting company called Brooks International.”

ET TU, BRUTE FORCE — Cyber criminals have adapted to the increase in telework spurred by the coronavirus pandemic, according to Kaspersky Lab, which on Friday reported a 23 percent increase in brute force attacks on databases in April compared with January. “Cybercriminals use brute force to penetrate a company’s network and subsequently launch malware inside its infrastructure,” the security firm said in its report. These attacks most commonly involve the Crusis, Cryakl and Phobos ransomware families, Kaspersky said.

In the same January-to-April period, researchers noticed more attempts to steal payment card data by infecting shopping websites with malware from the Trojan-PSW family. Kaspersky also said that its antivirus software blocked an average of 25 percent more attacks per day in April than in January, with “cookie stuffing” and advertising script injection together representing a “significant share” of the attacks.

DEEP DIVE — European Commission staff are consulting EU treasuries on possible measures for cybersecurity and digital financial assets that are scheduled to arrive in the form of legislation in September. POLITICO Europe Financial Services obtained documents last week, reporting on the preferred options that Commission officials are considering on cybersecurity and crypto assets. Here are other ideas that staff are putting forward:

On cybersecurity, financial companies could increase their capital buffers to ensure they can absorb any losses from an online attack. Demanding more capital, however, might not work for the whole financial industry and does little for supervisors that need information on online attacks, the unofficial “nonpaper” said. The EU could also set up a cybersecurity watchdog that scrutinizes financial firms’ online defenses. But this option would be costly and could also prove “legally challenging.”

On crypto assets, the Brussels executive could create a strict “opt-in regime” for companies that issue or service digital finance assets that are too novel for existing rules to capture. Companies signing up to this regime would get EU passports so that they can offer their services beyond their national markets and tap clients across the bloc. A better policy, in Commission officials’ eyes, would be to harmonize all standards for those who issue and service digital assets, replacing national rules. These standards would include transparency for crypto issuers, plus record-keeping and capital buffers for online exchanges.
