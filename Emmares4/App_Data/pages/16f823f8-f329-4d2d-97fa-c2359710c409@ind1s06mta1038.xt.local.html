Hi readers! Welcome back to New Scientist America. Some of my favorite research is work looking at how we perceive the world – and whether we can push the limitations of our senses – so I was delighted to read an interview with neuroscientist David Eagleman that we published this week. He talks about how flexible our brains are – the scientific term for that is plasticity. This means that when you learn a new skill like, say, playing the ukulele, your brain physically changes, growing to accommodate the new neural connections you’re making.

Eagleman’s research takes this idea and runs with it. He asks: can we create new senses? He and his team have developed a wristband that can buzz in response to sound, delivering vibrations that your brain can read as distinct from one another. Some people who are deaf and have worn these have said they can tell the difference between their two dogs barking, or hear/feel that they’ve left the water in the sink running. I can’t wait to see what else might come of this work. For myself, I’d like to be able to see ultraviolet light or magnetic fields, like birds do. What new sense would you like to have?

A SQUID ON THE HUNT

The giant squid, an elusive creature of the deep, has been captured on video hunting its prey. We used to think these squid were more likely to lie in wait to ambush their prey, but the use of a decoy jellyfish helped researchers study their behavior in the wild. Turns out, they actively patrol the waters, searching for dinner. The decoy was a cool little device called an E-Jelly that mimics the bioluminescent colors jellyfish give off, drawing in the squid. As regular readers know, I’m a big fan of deep-sea creatures, but I must admit even this small-ish giant squid (this one was only about 20 feet from tip to tip) gave me the heebie jeebies. I wouldn’t want to come across one in the depths. Watching this one hunt on video was enough for me.  
 

SHIPWRECK SOLVES A MYSTERY

A few years ago, hundreds of bales of natural rubber started washing up on Brazilian shores and it wasn’t clear where they were coming from. Now, a study of ocean currents has pinned down their source as the SS Rio Grande, a German ship that sank in 1944 and was discovered more than 18,000 feet deep in waters off Brazil’s coast in 1996. One of the bales was stamped with “Product of French Indochina”, and may have been aboard the ship as cargo. The mystery isn’t fully solved, though. It’s not clear why the bales were released in 2018, but something may have disturbed the shipwreck.
 

CIA FUELLED ANTI-VAX SENTIMENT

During the efforts to capture Osama bin Laden, the CIA organized a fake vaccination drive in 2011 in Pakistan, in an attempt to obtain DNA samples of one of his relatives and track him down. This kick-started a number of anti-vaccination campaigns by Islamic extremists and research has now shown that it led to a marked decline in vaccination rates for diphtheria, tetanus and pertussis (DTP), polio and measles. As a result, the prevalence of these diseases rose slightly. The vaccine hesitancy seems to be reversing, but this is a sobering reminder of the knock-on effects that misinformation can have.
 

MICROWAVE WEAPON OR MASS HYSTERIA?

In 2017, people at the US embassy in Cuba began to report feeling unwell, hearing a loud grating sound and experiencing nausea, memory loss and hearing loss. This came to be known as Havana syndrome. Suggestions for its cause ranged from mass hysteria to poisoning, but some believe it was the work of some kind of microwave weapon. The US Department of Defense seems to agree, as it is now funding a project to develop sensors that can detect a radio-frequency attack. Robert Baloh at the University of California, Los Angeles, says there is no evidence that microwave weapons exist, and the creation of the sensors may increase the chances that people believe they are experiencing such attacks.

FOR YOUR EARs only

In this week’s New Scientist Weekly podcast, Rowan talks to our space reporter, Leah, about one of my very favorite and most-hated topics: time. See, time is my nemesis. I hate it, and all its zones. And now it turns out that keeping track of time may be contributing to more entropy, or disorder, in the universe. The experimenters used a tiny atomic clock and found that the more accurate they made it, the more entropy it created in the form of heat. Now, it’s not clear that this relationship holds true for other types of clocks, but just to be safe, I say we quit with measuring time all together, eh? (I’m only kidding.) Leah assures us that the scale of entropy that we as humans create in general, let alone just with timekeeping, is so small that it’s not something we really need to worry about in the grand scheme of things
