Good evening from Los Angeles, wherever you may be. Years from now, we may remember this as the week hearings began for the impeachment of President Donald Trump. But those in the little bubble of Hollywood may remember it for something different: the debut of Disney+. 

This was a pivotal moment in the history of film and TV. The world’s largest entertainment company has embraced the internet, and will now facilitate the transition of viewers from cable and satellite to streaming.

While this may sound hyperbolic, it's not. The owner of the most profitable cable network in the world, ESPN, is giving you another reason to ditch cable. And the owner of  the most powerful movie studio in all the land is going to make a bunch of movies you can watch without paying for a ticket stub.

Netflix may end up ruing the day Disney created Disney+.  Critics like “The  Mandalorian.” Viewers are watching a lot of “The Simpsons” and “Moana.” Investors are loving the early numbers, which suggest Disney reached 10 million customers in a single day.

But this week, and really this moment, is a validation of the business Reed Hastings has created over the past 12 years. After years of confusion about what shows live where on the internet, I think – or hope – it’s about to get easier. Companies are going to keep their shows for themselves, and big programs will not cycle on and off quite as much.

The strong response to Disney+’s debut stands in stark contrast to that of Apple TV+, a dissonance I discussed with Chris Ryan on this week’s episode of “The Watch.”

Some of this is natural. Apple is an outsider coming into Hollywood, so the press was going to be more critical. Apple is hostile towards the media, so that doesn’t help their case either. And while Apple has done a poor job of communicating why it is making shows, and where people watch them, Disney has been very clear. 

There is a certain irony to all of this. The strong execution by Disney is very reminiscent of Apple in technology. Apple has seldom been the first company to create a new product. It watches as other companies identify what people want, and then it comes in and executes that product the best.

Disney is not the first company to stream. It’s not the 10th. But it can still be the best, or second best. That is, of course, assuming those outages this week were just about overwhelming demand. – Lucas Shaw

 
Taylor Swift, queen of the Twitter fight

Taylor Swift and Scooter Braun are fighting again. 

Swift accused her former record label of blocking her from performing her old songs at an awards show or using them in an upcoming documentary, escalating a dispute between Swift and Braun, manager to Justin Bieber and Ariana Grande.

Swift and Braun have been feuding ever since Braun acquired Big Machine Label Group earlier this year. Swift recorded her first six albums for Big Machine, including her biggest hit, “1989," before moving to Republic Records. Swift claimed in June that she wanted to buy back her old albums from Big Machine, but wasn't allowed. 

Big Machine has contested just about everything Swift has said. The label has said Swift was aware of the deal between Big Machine and Braun before it happened, and only chose to object after the fact. And said this week it has not blocked Swift from using music in a Netflix documentary or performing at awards shows.

This is a classic clash of egos in that we have two parties talking past one another, not directly addressing some of the matters at hand. Big Machine hasn't said whether it's stopped Swift from performing her old songs. It only said it hasn't stopped her from performing, which it can't do in the first place. Swift says she learned about the deal between Big Machine and Braun in the media, which is hard to believe if her dad is in fact a major shareholder.

Big Machine would like the public to view the fight as a far-too-typical music industry squabble over who owes whom money. Big Machine claims Swift owes it millions. Swift says Big Machine owes her money.  But even if this just a fight over unpaid royalties, this has blossomed into something much larger.

Swift has framed this as an artist’s effort to own her own work – and a woman fighting against powerful men. Politicians Elizabeth Warren and Alexandra Ocasio-Cortez have framed it as another example of private equity’s sinister role in society. PE firm Carlyle Group backed Braun’s acquisition of Big Machine.

These framings all have flaws. Carlyle is a minority owner in Big Machine, and Swift is not some poor employee laid off because of private equity’s machinations.  But the dispute between Swift and Braun has reignited a decades-old debate in music.

Most artists don’t own the rights to their recordings. For decades, labels have signed young artists before they were famous, providing upfront capital to support the recording of new music.  When the music is released, the label promotes it, tapping relationships with radio stations, record stores and, now, streaming services. The label recoups its investment and then splits the resulting profits, if there are any, with the musicians.

A handful of artists have protested this system, including Prince, who compared it to slavery. While the internet has made it easier for artists to replicate many label functions on their own, especially promotion, most artists still sign to record labels. Swift, for example, inked a new deal last year with Republic, a division of Universal Music Group, the world’s largest music company. (She does own her new music, however.)

Swift continues to threaten to rerecord her old music, which would be unprecedented in modern music history, as far as I can tell. 

Inside the most watched YouTube Channel in the world

That’s the headline of a new story in Businessweek by Ari Altstedter and yours truly. I started looking into T-Series last fall when it was challenging PewDiePie’s status as the YouTube channel with the most subscribers in the world.

T-Series passed PewDiePie in February, and now has close to 120 million subscribers to its main channel. The company’s history is incredible; here are just a few of my favorite details:

  • The company started as a small shop that sold fans and gizmos. Cassettes sold well, so Gulshan Kumar started assembling them and recording music.
  • Gulshan Kumar was murdered outside of a temple, allegedly for not giving into an extortion attempt.
  • His son Bhushan took over the company at just 19 years old.
  • In the mid-2000s, T-Series biggest business was selling ringtones. 
  • T-Series sued YouTube before it started uploading music, but now has more than 200 million subscribers across its network. No company has designed its business to provide what YouTube likes more effectively.
  • Success on YouTube has also helped the company revive its movie studio. It produced two of the 10 highest-grossing movies at the Indian box office this year. 
 

Another weekend, another flop.

The No. 1 movie in the world is “Ford V Ferrari.” The drama about a competition between two automakers grossed $31 million in North America this weekend, and another $21 million overseas.

A remake of “Charlie’s Angels” limped to $8.6 million, and industry executives are once again wondering why so many movies are failing. In the past few weeks, “Doctor Sleep,” “Terminator: Dark Fate” and “Charlie’s Angels” all bombed.

People aren’t blaming sequel fatigue this time, which was the alleged culprit over the summer. Instead, they are genuinely confused.

But the answer seems apparent. It’s the same as last time. Most people only go to three or four movies a year in theaters. If they do choose to go to the theater, it is for something they really want.

Hollywood is making sequels to a lot of movies that nobody craved. The last three “Terminator” movies have underperformed. “The Shining,” the precursor to "Doctor Sleep," wasn’t a hit when it first came out. And while the last “Charlie’s Angels” featured three actresses as the top of their games, this one starred Kristen Stewart and two actresses most people don’t know.

The No. 1 album in the U.S. is Post Malone’s “Hollywood is Bleeding.” The No. 1 song is Lewis Capaldi’s “Someone You Loved.” Bleh.

The most-watched TV show in the U.S. this week may have been the Impeachment hearings. They drew more than 13 million views on TV, and millions more online.

 

The week that was

  1. Billboards are hot again. Media companies are splurging on billboards and cable TV ads to promote their new streaming services. Spending on broadcast and cable ads by streaming services jumped 19% to $209 million over the past 10 weeks, according to data from researcher ISpot.TV.  
  2. Facebook is inconsistent on politics. Instagram is paying celebrities to post more to IGTV, its struggling hub for long-form video. But they can only do so if they agree not to talk about politics or social issues. Instagram CEO Adam Mosseri said there is a difference between allowing political speech and funding it. That is true. But Facebook funds news. It also has erased many boundaries between citizens and news outlets.
  3. Hulu is raising the price of its live TV service. The bundle of live channels will now cost $55 a month instead of $45. RIP skinny bundle.
  4. Apple apes Amazon. The iPhone maker may package a bunch of its services, like news and music, into one bundle as soon as next year. If this reminds you of Amazon Prime, that’s because it is like Amazon Prime. Only instead of free shipping, you pay for a $1200 phone.
  5. Another country is mad at Netflix. The streaming service decided to amend a documentary series after the leader of Poland (and many Polish customers) complained that it was misleading.
  6. CBS’ CEO is getting PAID. Joe Ianniello will get $100 million when CBS’ merger with Viacom closes, most of it because he isn’t being made the CEO. But he will keep working at the combined company.
  7. Apple loses a top programming exec. Kim Rozenfeld is leaving Apple’s TV studio just a couple weeks after the debut of the new streaming service.
  8. Audio erotica. The sister of Snapchat chief Evan Spiegel founded an audio porn company.
