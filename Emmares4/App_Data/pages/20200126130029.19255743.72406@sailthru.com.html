What do people in aviation call what’s left of a bird after it strikes an airplane? The term they've coined for that is snarge. (It's the mashup of "snot" and "garbage." I know, gross.) As Brendan I. Koerner writes this week on Backchannel, they are the unsung researchers who toil in the field of bird-strike prevention. Avian collisions are rare, but they can be catastrophic. Bird-related accidents include the Eastern Airlines flight 375 crash in 1960 (starlings struck, 62 passengers dead), the United Airlines flight 297 crash in 1962 (whistling swans, 17 dead), and Captain Chesley "Sully" Sullenberger's "Miracle on the Hudson" in 2009 (Canada geese, no deaths).

To help prevent such accidents, scientists and researchers have figured out ways to shoo birds away from airports—93 percent of bird strikes happen below 3,500 feet, and more than 70 percent below 500 feet. They have used everything from trash cleanup, propane-fueled sound cannons, and shotguns to green lasers. (Birds are especially sensitive to green light.)

Detecting potentially dangerous flocks at higher altitudes and warning pilots in time remains a problem, though. Radar might help, but for now it is not very good at distinguishing between birds at a safe distance and birds on a collision course. Snarge is blessedly rare. Here’s to the scientists and researchers working to keep it that way.

Mark Robinson | Features Editor, WIRED
