Good afternoon from Los Angeles, wherever you may be. We're doing a quick newsletter this week; I've got a back log of stories you'll read shortly, and a couple surprises in the weeks ahead.

This past week marked the end of corporate earnings season, at least for the media world. If that sounds as boring to you as it does to most people, I apologize. But it does offer us the chance to look across the business and draw a few conclusions.

Lesson No. 1: Cord-cutting is getting much, much worse.

The rate at which people are canceling pay-TV reached new heights in 2019, according to research firm MoffettNathanson. Craig Moffett identifies two key inflection points.

In 2010, the pace of new pay-TV customers fell behind new home creation. (Translation: Some of the people getting houses weren’t paying for cable or satellite.)

At the end of 2014 and start of 2015, pay-TV companies started losing customers, a trend that has only gotten worse.

The picture is particularly stark for satellite companies, which lost almost 4 million customers last year. Now, just 65% of homes have pay-TV. That’s down from 88% at the start of the last decade.

Lesson No. 2: Jump into the deep end, or stay out entirely.

ViacomCBS tried to employ the shiny object strategy this past week. The company tried to paper over the decline in its biggest business, cable networks like MTV and Nickelodeon, with news about future streaming plans. Disney Chief Executive Officer Bob Iger has used this strategy to great success in recent years.

It didn't work. Part of that is because ViacomCBS isn't Disney, the largest entertainment company in the world. But I'd argue the bigger reason is ViacomCBS offered a muddled picture.

ViacomCBS is combining CBS All Access, Showtime, Pluto TV and its library of old movies and TV shows into one streaming service.

ViacomCBS has assets that could work for streaming. It's got kids shows from Nickelodeon. It's got premium dramas from Showtime, stand-up from Comedy Central, broad comedies and reality TV from CBS and reality TV from VH1 and MTV. This company would have been a major contender in the streaming wars a couple years ago, and could still be now.

But at the same time, it licensed "South Park," one of its most popular shows, to HBO Max. It's producing original series for Netflix, Amazon and Hulu. Those companies are all, presumably, rivals of its streaming service.

Now Viacom's argument is that the business model for streaming is uncertain. As a result, it's reluctant to give up the money in licensing shows. All Access couldn't afford to spend more than $500 million to license "South Park" because it only has 5 million to 6 million subscribers.

But there's another way of looking at this strategy: ViacomCBS wants to have its cake and eat it too.

Lesson No. 3: Netflix is bigger than YouTube.

YouTube has more users, and, as a result, is watched more hours each day than Netflix. But Netflix makes more money. The company generated $20.2 billion in sales in 2019, compared to $18 billion for YouTube. That means Netflix is the largest online TV network in the world in terms of revenue, and the largest paid online TV network in terms of subscribers.

Side note: This was a great quarter for transparency in TV’s future. Alphabet decided to report numbers for YouTube, and Disney now owns a majority of Hulu. That means we now have a much clearer sense of the size of two of the largest streaming services in the world. 

Now if only everyone reported viewership in the same way...

Apple lights more money on fire

Apple has delayed the production on its show "Shantaram" until it can get more scripts done. This is a stunning move, and another sign of immaturity at Apple's TV studio.

"Shantaram" is an expensive global production featuring top talent. The studios always planned to take a hiatus after filming two episodes, but now that hiatus is extended indefinitely. Shutting it down will cost millions of dollars, and huge headaches for talent.
