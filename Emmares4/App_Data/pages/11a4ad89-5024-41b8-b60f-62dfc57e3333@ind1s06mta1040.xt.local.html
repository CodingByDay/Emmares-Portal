Hello readers!

Welcome back to Launchpad. Last week NASA made a really exciting announcement just too late for me to write about it in the newsletter, so I’d like to talk about it this week. Venus has been neglected for decades now – both NASA and the Soviet space agency sent lots of missions there in the early years of interplanetary travel, but in the late 1980s the focus shifted to Mars, and while a few spacecraft have visited since, NASA hasn’t. But soon it is going back – twice! And it’ll be joined by a mission from the European Space Agency (ESA).

triple threat

Venus’s lack of popularity hasn’t been without reason. Because of the high temperatures and crushing pressures at the surface – it can reach around 470°C, with pressure 90 times that at sea level on Earth – spacecraft can only survive there for a few minutes before they start to melt and break down. Despite that, it is the planet most like Earth in terms of size and its general composition, and its climate is thought to have been Earth-like in the past.

But nevertheless, of the two missions NASA has selected to return to the planet at the end of this decade will be bound for the surface. Called Deep Atmosphere Venus Investigation of Noble gases, Chemistry, and Imaging (DAVINCI+), it includes a descent probe which is a sphere a metre across, sort of like a super-sized beach ball.

Aided by parachutes, it will sink through Venus’s toxic clouds over 63 minutes, sampling the chemistry of the atmosphere and measuring its structure and weather. Once it emerges below the clouds, it will take hundreds of pictures of the surface in an attempt to help us understand strange surface features called tesserae that some researchers think will be the key to understanding Venus’s geological history. Once it lands, it is only expected to last about 20 minutes. Even that is a feat: so far, only the Soviet Union has landed a spacecraft on Venus.

DAVINCI+ is also tasked with helping settle the debate over phosphine gas in Venus’s atmosphere. Last year, a team of researchers announced that they had spotted this rare substance there and couldn’t figure out any way for it to be produced other than by life. Since then, scientists have been arguing back and forth, not only about possible non-biological ways to produce phosphine, but also about whether it was really there at all. Directly sampling it in the atmosphere would solve one of those debates, but probably only spur the other on.
Credit: NASA/JPL-Caltech
The other mission will be a bit less dramatic. It is an orbiter called Venus Emissivity, Radio Science, InSAR, Topography, and Spectroscopy (VERITAS). InSAR stands for interferometric synthetic aperture radar, a system that will allow VERITAS to peer through Venus’s thick atmosphere to build a 3D model of its surface features. The craft will also carry devices to measure the composition of the planet’s surface and look for active volcanism and liquid on the surface.

ESA’s EnVision mission will complement NASA’s missions. Like VERITAS, it will be an orbiter, and its main goal will be to build maps of all different sorts. One of its main goals is to understand how the planet’s interior structure has affected its surface and atmosphere over time. That could help explain how Venus and Earth turned out so different.

These may seem like very basic questions, but as every Venus researcher I’ve ever spoken to has told me, the main problem with our understanding of Venus is that we know basically nothing about it. Our major questions about Venus include pretty much any question you could come up with to ask about a planet. DAVINCI+ and VERITAS are a start, though, and NASA is giving each team about $500 million to further develop their mission concepts.

I’ll leave you with what planetary scientist Paul Byrne said when I asked him why we need two Venus missions at once: “We can't do Venus with one, or two, or even five missions! No more than we can do Mars, or Jupiter, or the Moon, or anywhere else. VERITAS and DAVINCI+ will not answer all our outstanding Venus questions. But they will enable us to take a massive step forward in understanding why our planetary sibling isn't our twin. And it's very much the case here that two missions are better than one when it comes to exploring somewhere as complicated as Venus.”

what's happening up there

  • Jeff Bezos and his brother will be on Blue Origin’s first crewed flight of its New Shepard rocket in July. He may have competition to be the first billionaire to fly aboard his own spacecraft, though, with rumours circulating about Richard Branson going to the edge of space on a Virgin Galactic craft first.
  • Astronomers found a colossal arc of galaxies that’s so big – 3.3 billion light years long – that according to our best models of the cosmos, it shouldn’t exist.
  • Speaking of things that shouldn’t exist, we may finally have an explanation for an extra-large black hole that was spotted in 2019.
  • While we’re at it, one more thing that shouldn’t exist. There are organic molecules close to the edge of our galaxy, where we did not think there were enough raw materials to make these compounds. They could be the building blocks of life.
