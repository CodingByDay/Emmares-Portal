What happens when savers turn into splurgers? One of the most debated investing questions of late has been a guessing game over where a windfall of spending will land as vaccinated people across the world, with considerable savings to blow, emerge from a year’s worth of sheltering at home. A recent Deutsche Bank survey of Americans and Europeans confirms much of what has sent investors into shares of cruise operators, airlines and other travel stocks: People want experiences they’ve been missing. Of the various potential destinations for pent-up spending, overseas holidays, domestic holidays, restaurants and cafes, and food and drink were the four most popular areas. Pubs and bars, as well as performances and events, also made the top 10. But Deutsche Bank notes that “people are still planning substantial amount of spending on consumer goods,” too, with clothing and footwear, electronics, vehicles and household equipment filling out the rest of the top 10. And the floodgates are already opening: Deutsche Bank’s survey reveals that people are becoming more confident in making large purchases, and that the proportion of people delaying such purchases is on the decline.

Imagine an app on your phone tracking the miles you drive in your car and then sending you a bill each month for a national per-mile road usage tax. Seem far-fetched? It doesn't to the Republican lawmakers who recently introduced the Surface Transportation Advanced through Reform, Technology, & Efficient Review (STARTER) Act 2.0 in Congress.

When electric cars proliferate and people aren't buying as much gasoline anymore, the federal government is going to miss the revenue it currently gets from taxes on gasoline (18.4¢ per gallon) and diesel fuel (24.4¢ per gallon). The STARTER Act would establish a national pilot program to test "vehicle miles travelled" (VMT) reporting systems and revenue collection mechanisms for a new tax that would ultimately replace the current federal fuel taxes. VMT reporting systems to be considered during the pilot program include on-board diagnostic devices; smart phone applications; and voluntary reporting by participants, insurance companies and state motor vehicle departments. Third-party vendors would also be allowed to collect and submit tax payments to the federal government.

The per-mile road usage tax is just one small part of the STARTER Act, which would also provide over $400 billion for traditional infrastructure projects. While the bill as a whole has little chance of advancing, the tax proposal could be picked up by Democrats who are looking for ways to pay for their own, much larger infrastructure plans. So, one day, will you be taxed for every mile you drive? It's possible.
