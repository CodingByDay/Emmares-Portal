Hi, everyone. It's Shira. I don't want to talk about Libra. Please no. Facebook Inc. said this week that it won't move ahead with the proposed cryptocurrency until U.S. authorities approve it, and that's not going to happen soon. 

But I do want to talk about one of Facebook's favorite messages: If the company isn't permitted to move ahead with its half-baked Libra project, or if regulators put too many restrictions on the company in other ways, then China will win.

Behind this message is a legitimate policy debate about how best to stoke innovation and regulate corporations. But it has been twisted into China scare tactics for Facebook's self-interest. 

At a six-hour congressional hearing on Wednesday, Zuckerberg reiterated warnings that if Facebook or another U.S. company doesn't build a global cryptocurrency, then China will do it first and the U.S. will lose.

"I personally worry that if we don't do things like this, whether it's this project or others like it... then eventually we lose our leadership," Zuckerberg told lawmakers.

I get Zuckerberg's broad point, but not as applied to his practical aim to push through Libra.

In part, Zuckerberg is conflating two very different things: the Facebook-backed cryptocurrency and a proposed initiative by China's central bank to issue a digital version of its currency, the yuan. Just as Libra is not trying to replace the Brazilian real, Libra cannot be a counterbalance to a centralized, single-country government currency.

Bringing up the yuan project, as Zuckerberg did several times on Wednesday, seems like a craven attempt to tap into fears that China's currency might become a global default. He also said, reasonably, that Americans should want U.S. companies to build the global financial infrastructure of the future.

But governments are the appropriate actors on establishing global standard currencies, and I'm not sure Libra is the solution for building gold-standard backbones for finance. 

Prompted by a leading question Wednesday from Republican congressman Patrick McHenry from North Carolina, Zuckerberg also said that he wants to make sure there are alternatives to payments technologies such as China's WeChat Pay and AliPay.

OK, but there are already lots of digital payments systems, including Google Pay and Apple Pay, Paytm in India, Ideal in the Netherlands and M-Pesa in Kenya. And by the way, WeChat and Alipay are, at least for now, largely confined to use in China or by Chinese people abroad.  

Sometimes, including at points on Wednesday, it has been politicians and not Zuckerberg holding out Facebook as a solution to vague geopolitical and economic fears about China. The problem is that Facebook itself invites this twisting of its broader policy messages.

Zuckerberg, his top deputy Sheryl Sandberg and others have repeatedly seemed to suggest that if Facebook is weakened by government regulation or is forced to split into pieces, then somehow Chinese internet companies or that country's internet norms will swallow the world. Google's former CEO has said this too, the company's current boss made a similar argument in an interview with CNN. 

There is legitimate debate about whether the internet is fracturing into multiple spheres of influence, with China, the U.S. and other countries each setting divergent norms of free expression. And U.S. policy makers have a legitimate interest in encouraging American companies to pursue the best ideas. But putting responsible guard rails on Facebook or Google does not undermine U.S. technology innovation nor give China carte blanche to write internet rules for the world.

Facebook is an awkward messenger about any policy, as are most powerful corporations. But Zuckerberg and his company are uniquely inept at mixing broad policy aims with self-interested corporate goals.--Shira Ovide
