Little Relief for the Quants

It may not feel like it, but we are now firmly into the relief stage of markets’ response to the coronavirus. That doesn’t mean we have hit bottom. That still seems unlikely to me, unless the ultimate damage from the pandemic proves to be far less than currently expected.  But it does mean that the initial “panic” phase is over. Investors think they are at least reasonably positioned now given the intense levels of uncertainty. It also implies guarded confidence that the extraordinary response by monetary and fiscal authorities around the world has averted an immediate crisis. 

As the chart shows, both bond yields and share prices have hit a bottom, rebounded, and then fallen again, to levels still above their lows, amid reducing volatility: 

We can now begin to draw some conclusions about the strategies that worked during the sudden explosion of uncertainty. First, in the stock market, there was some extra security in the “momentum” factor (betting on winners to keep winning and losers to keep losing). Momentum has been beating value (buying stocks that are cheap compared to their fundamentals) for an almost infeasibly long time, as shown by MSCI’s U.S. indices:

To a much lesser extent, there was also some extra safety in “low-volatility” stocks that are less likely to move aggressively with the market. But even if there was a clearly defined quantitative strategy to deal with the volatility, it looks as though quantitative portfolio managers failed to use it effectively. To the greatest extent in many years, they had their clocks cleaned by “fundamental” asset managers who use more discretion. This startling chart from Joseph Mezrich, senior equity analyst at Instinet LLC, shows just how cold a quant winter the last three months have been:

A slight majority of non-quant funds beat their benchmark during the first quarter, unusually. Most quants were drubbed. This is because the non-quants did what can often lead to underperformance and moved swiftly to try to avoid the worst of the fall. The following chart shows the tracking error, or the degree to which funds tended to diverge from their underlying benchmark. During the first quarter, a lot of non-quant fund managers had the courage to move away sharply. Quants followed their rules and sustained the full losses:

From the point of view of market efficiency, it is healthy to see investors make their own judgments and diverge from their benchmarks. But this still raises the question of just why the quants have done so badly, and what other fund managers did to minimize the damage. I suspect they are only storing up trouble. One of the easiest ways to outperform the index has been to load up on FAANG internet stocks, which have beaten the S&P 500 by 18% so far this year. 

Fundamental managers are staying ahead of the quants by backing their hunch that the FAANGs will help them. With many of the FAANGs on ever more of a collision course with governments, regulators and antitrust authorities, this looks like an inflating bubble.

Time for the Banks?

The sell-off in the first quarter was remarkable for its uniformity. Many stocks fell in alignment. That leads to grounds for hope from the reaction in the last few days. Chris Watling, founder of Longview Economics in London, points out that advancers are exceeding decliners to a remarkable extent. Lots of different investors are placing bets on lots of different stocks — which could be a sign of a bottom. 

Even if we aren’t there yet, are there any sectors that look too cheap and worth buying for the long term? The answer, which requires some guts to say out loud, may be the banks. 

Using price-to-book multiples, the most popular metric for banks, they are cheaper even than at the depth of the Global Financial Crisis in the euro zone and in emerging markets. The big U.S. banks, considered much healthier, are also approaching those valuation lows:

Meanwhile, banks’ underperformance compared to technology is growing quite extraordinary. On a global level, using the MSCI All-Country indexes for banks and information technology, they have lagged further behind almost without interruption since the first concerns about the mortgage market emerged in 2006. 

After the credit crisis, it should be no surprise that banks have stayed out of favor for so long. But this latest dive begins to look overdone — especially in the context of a huge government bailout for commercial credit and for small companies that will be routed through banks and generate fees for them.

As Chris Davis, a veteran investor in financials who runs the Davis New York Venture Fund puts it, “in the long run, their business model isn’t challenged, the financial model isn’t challenged and long-term earnings power remains intact.” Meanwhile, it is possible to buy them for less than their book value of equity, giving substantial protection against further downside. He adds that many bankers are now enthusiastic to have an opportunity to rehabilitate their reputation and play a strong part in aiding the economic recovery. With governments dedicated to keeping banks alive, moral hazard works in investors’ favor.

Watling also points out that the long-term trend of technology versus banks has moved into overdrive — departing so sharply from the trend as to appear unsustainable:

Inevitably with the banks, there is one word of caution: Japanification. If protracted sluggish growth and low inflation lies ahead, then the example of Japan’s bank stocks since the country’s equity bubble burst on New Year’s Eve, 1989, isn’t encouraging. This is the worst-case scenario:

There is room for great disagreement over the future. Helicopter money may ensure that we avoid Japanification, but instead endure severe inflation for the first time since the 1970s. But the basis for looking at the banks is clear: They are priced very cheaply when they shouldn’t be in existential danger, and when other sectors seem wildly over-extended.

 

Whos’s Zooming Who, Part 2

A week ago, I asked: “who’s zooming who?” At the time, the issue was that many investors had piled into a company with the ticker symbol ZOOM, even though it was unconnected to the Zoom videoconferencing system that many of us are using as we isolate. Now, the question seems more sinister.

This weekend brought news that New York City’s Department of Education had barred the use of Zoom for teaching after security breaches. As my own daughter’s high school class was interrupted by Zoom hackers posting hard-core pornography and showing their genitals, this is a decision I wholeheartedly support. Zoom technology may have a great future, but this test of its capabilities appears to have come too early. 

Veteran short-seller Jim Chanos warned against chasing the stocks that appeared to be winning from the shutdown. It looks like he was right. 

 

Survival Tips

We are all much more interested in pandemics than we used to be. Let historians who studied the phenomenon before the coronavirus be your guide. In this series of lectures, given at Oxford last year Professor Mark Bailey explores the effects and legacy of the Black Death, history’s most serious epidemic; while in this series of lectures from 2012 at Gresham College in London, Sir Richard Evans looks at six different plagues. For a wonderfully readable account of the Black Death, try Barbara Tuchman’s A Distant Mirror. It was written in 1978, when she thought it held a mirror to the horrors of the 1970s. It’s even more relevant now. 

If you think these ways of passing the time aren’t exactly uplifting, bear in mind: We are a luckier generation than any that came before. We are at least capable of keeping economic life going, to some extent, while we all stay at home. No previous generation of pandemic victims had that luxury.
