From Recession to Reflation?

We’re living in a 2-2-2 world – 2% growth, around 2% inflation, and a 2% 10-year Treasury yield, in the depiction oft described by Michael Purves at Tallbacken Capital. Of those three measures, it’s yields that look the most prone to overshooting to the upside, considering the 20 basis point rise in the 10-year yield this week.

The Treasury market is on the verge of lurching from “recession” to “reflation” with such urgency that you’d think anything in between was an impossible outcome – even though a “muddling through” backdrop has been the hallmark of the post-crisis economic environment.

It’s unlikely the sell-off in Treasuries could be this severe if other sovereign bond markets weren’t coming under the knife as well. It’s a truly global bond bloodbath, with Japanese yields jumping and French and Belgian 10-year yields rising back into positive territory, on the verge of eclipsing the September swoon in terms of weekly damage done:

Since last week’s Federal Reserve interest-rate cut, the majority of the upside move in yields had been fueled by rising inflation breakevens. That’s even as implied Fed easing over the coming years has tumbled – to the extent that December 2021 Eurodollar futures yields have nearly reversed all of their retreat since the initial Fed easing in July.

In that light, it’s no wonder that real yields finally joined the party on Thursday, with a more than 7 basis point jump.

What we’ve seen in Fed pricing, based on the December 2021 Eurodollar futures yield, is that nearly all of the retreat in short rates since the July cut has been reversed.

“From a fundamental perspective, the repricing has been perfectly consistent with a dovish Fed policy impulse,” writes Jon Hill at BMO Capital Markets. “Inflation expectations and equities are rising, the dollar has depreciated off of multi-year strength, and the curve is steepening as a signal of moderating fears of a policy-error induced downturn.”

It isn’t that sovereign bond yields are selling off on bets that central banks are looking to hit the pause button on easing. It's rather because of a perceived improvement in the economic backdrop, which in turn has left central banks feeling no immediate need to provide more accommodation. Need proof? The 10-year Treasury yield bottoming prior to the last two interest rate cuts from the Fed should be sufficient. Not to mention that the global risk rally has continued apace during this backup in bond yields.

Bond investors are left in an “on the one hand, on the other” position. A sell-off of this magnitude – particularly with the heightened volumes seen on Thursday – can often be the mark of a short-term bottom for a security. Conversely, the medium-term review reveals a consistent series of higher lows and higher highs in Treasury yields, a dynamic that points towards more pain in store for owners of the safest financial asset in the world.

The options market has cast its vote on which way Treasuries will resolve: the put/call skew for the 10-year note has gone haywire, ending Thursday at more than three standard deviations above its long-term average (and rising even more overnight, though with thin activity the moves have an asterisk).

History Is Useless

For investors, the obvious follow-on question to the carnage in bonds is “what does it mean for everything else?” And especially stocks.

Going beyond the overall stock-index level, it’s clear the extent to which the Treasury market is starkly defining winners and losers, as seen in the chart below. 

The bigger query is what such a brisk rise in Treasury yields implies for U.S. stocks at the broad index level. Right now, the relative momentum between stocks and bonds is at its widest since January 2018.

Recency bias (the fourth quarter of 2018 and the start of 2018, before the Volpocalypse) would lead you to believe that such a material rise in yields in short order doesn’t bode well for U.S. stocks. A more comprehensive view would lead you to believe that drawing any firm conclusion is a fool’s errand.

Here’s a look at the 21-day change in the 10-year yield and the subsequent 21-day return in U.S. equities since 2010.

And here’s a look at the 21-day change in the 10-year yield and the subsequent 63-day return in the S&P 500.

If you can see any pattern in these, congratulations – but it looks pretty much like a blob from our perspective. What’s more, both these charts make clear that big moves in the 10-year are associated with a wide range of forward returns on both the left and right tails.

An even broader look at history tells the same tale. Going back to 1965 and taking the z-score of how much the 10-year yield moved on a monthly basis relative to its three-year history and the concurrent stock returns would show that rapidly rising yields are the worst state of nature for the equity market.

But this might be casting the net too wide, with results skewed too much by the massive inflation in the 1970s and early 80s. Looking at just the Greenspan era (starting August 1987) to the present, the story changes. Yes, the most ferocious rises in yields have been associated with negative monthly returns. But rapidly falling ones are clearly worse. And the current Treasury bucket is associated with some of the strongest equity returns.

There’s cause to worry about whether the “good news is good news” dynamic that’s prevailed in recent months could be jeopardized by, well, too much of a good thing. As the Treasury sell-off crescendoed on Thursday, U.S. stocks didn’t set fresh highs in sympathy on an intraday basis, but rather stagnated.

The mechanism for a rates-driven dislocation in risk assets – despite the level of rates lingering far below what prevailed last year – might look a little something like this: the Treasury slide that’s sparking rates volatility could bleed into other asset classes, particularly if spurred by trend-following investors de-grossing across their portfolios.

``Selling strangles in rates was the play on the Fed, and it bled through to lower equity implied volatility too,’’ said Naufal Sanaullah, chief macro strategist at EIA All Weather Alpha Partners. ``Now that we’ve undone that bounce in bonds, we could see some higher cross-asset volatility and whippy action if we crack the rates range.’’

It’s a meaningful risk factor to consider. Or, given the catch-up in global equities that’s occurred in tandem with these rising yields, perhaps this dynamic has a lot more room to run while still remaining part of a broadly risk-on story.

Either outcome – or anything in between – is possible. Just get yourself a better seeing-eye dog than history to navigate these market conditions. It’s anyone’s guess what you’d run into.

EurOwe

Right now, if you want to borrow money, you’re probably thinking of doing so in euros.

China’s first euro bond in 15 years was met with immense demand. Serbia tapped European debt markets, too.

But more importantly is the extent to which corporate America is looking to lower borrowing costs by heading across the Atlantic. Harley Davidson Inc.’s doing it. And the most valuable company in the world is, to boot, with Apple Inc.'s green-bond offering.

That’s why European bond sales are already at a new annual record: it’s part of an American takeover of the continent via corporate “Reverse Yankees.”

According to Societe Generale SA, U.S. corporate debt is the second-biggest component of the iBoxx EUR non-financial index, trailing only French issues outstanding. And it’s on track to overtake Les Bleus within the next 18 months.

Bank of America Corp. analysts, meanwhile, have highlighted around two dozen U.S. investment grade issuers that would be well-served to issue bonds in euros or pounds, to align with the share of revenues they derive from those locales.

That’s why SocGen projects a continued “high correlation in spread movements between euro investment grade and dollar investment grade indices.” On a pure composition basis, European corporate debt is becoming more American.

This increased appetite for issuing in euros has not lent itself towards more appetite for the euro, however. UBS Group AG analysts conclude that foreign flows into European debt are likely to have been hedged in order to boost the yield pick-up. “The role of FX-hedge bond inflows could explain why the improvement in the portfolio investment balance has not boosted the euro,” they conclude.
