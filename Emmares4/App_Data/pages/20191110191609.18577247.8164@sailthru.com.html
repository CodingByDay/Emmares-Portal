Good afternoon from Los Angeles, wherever you may be. Disney+ debuts Tuesday, ushering the world’s largest entertainment company into the streaming age. The service will spread to Australia later this year, and Western Europe in 2020. But before we get to Disney+, a word about the company that inspired this new streaming service.

Netflix demonstrated one of the many pitfalls of running a global TV network this week when its Chief Executive Officer Reed Hastings addressed a controversy over his company's treatment of Hasan Minhaj’s shows “Patriot Act.”

Netflix withdrew the episode in Saudi Arabia earlier this year at the request of the government, which had been criticized in the episode for, among other things, its alleged involvement in the killing of journalist Jamal Khashoggi.

Netflix’s initial explanation was simple: it was complying with local law. This argument didn’t thrill people, but it seemed to pass muster for a few months. Speaking at the New York Times’ Dealbook conference this week, Hastings revisited this defense, only to add some context that made matters worse.

"We're not in the news business," he said. "We're trying to entertain." He then attempted a bit of flattery, saying the New York Times is in the "Truth to Power" business while Netflix isn't.

Hastings is right that he’s not running a news organization, but he does run a company that releases dozens of documentaries a year. He has also talked about his company’s role in bringing new voices from around the world to the fore, and picked his latest chief communications officer, Rachel Whetstone, in part because of her experience in government relations.

Since taking down the episode of "Patriot Act," Netflix has voiced concern about Georgia’s law limiting abortion rights. Why can Netflix do one and not the other? Hastings said the company, based in Los Gatos, California, was more comfortable questioning U.S. law than international law.

But then he said Netflix would refuse to comply with any law banning gay content, even overseas. Why comply with one international law, and not another? 

I don’t envy Hastings. Operating a global service leads to inevitable conflicts between the values of your company, and home country, and those of other countries in which you operate. And the pace of these conflicts seems to be picking up.

In just the past few weeks, Houston Rockets general manager Daryl Morey tweeted support for protesting in Hong Kong, offending the government of China, one of the NBA’s fastest-growing markets. Blizzard punished video game players who supported the protesters, drawing condemnation. And the U.S. government opened an investigation of the social video app TikTok, which is owned by a Chinese company.

All those conflicts relate to U.S.-China relations, but we’ve also witnessed Europe’s regulation of U.S. technology companies, foreign content quotas to preserve local film production and censorship in many countries.

While Western ideals of democracy and capitalism have reigned for many decades, that is no longer the case. Europe is beset by nativists, Latin America is creeping towards authoritarianism and China has bought influence across Africa, Latin America, Asia and the Middle East. The U.S., meanwhile, is led by a president who has withdrawn from a global climate change treaty and a multi-party trade deal while endorsing many authoritarians. 

I’m not sure if we are in a new Cold War, or a clash of civilizations, but CEOs are increasingly going to be asked to balance the imperatives of profit and social good.  The answers may be upsetting.

When pressed on the topic of Hong Kong, Disney CEO Bob Iger declined to offer an opinion one way or the other because taking a position could harm the company. – Lucas Shaw
 
Disney + is here. All hail King Iger

When asked which of his new competitors he feared the most, Hastings demurred. But look carefully at his words, and it’s clear he fears Disney the most.

“There's a bunch of tech companies in entertainment, but Disney is the one we have the most to learn from in terms of entertainment,” he told Andrew Ross Sorkin. Learning is very important to Hastings, who has devoted years of his life (and tens of millions of dollars) to education reform.

If you haven’t heard much about Disney+, I’d recommend reading the latest Businessweek cover story on Iger.

“Disney+ could define Iger’s legacy. Now 68, he’d been scheduled to retire this year. Instead, as part of the Fox deal, he agreed to stay on until 2021. He professes certainty that Disney+ is the right step.”

“It feels absolutely vital for us to do this,” Iger told Devin Leonard. “This is, no question about it, the future of media.”

Iger will step down in two years, or so he says. Iger has been about to retire for years now. The person most likely to succeed him appears to be Kevin Mayer, who is overseeing Disney’s push into streaming.

Chris Palmeri profiled the 57-year-old described by one friend as a “benevolent killer.” Mayer first joined Disney in 1993, and returned in 2005 after a brief stint running Playboy’s web business.

As Disney’s strategy chief, Mayer was instrumental to the acquisitions of Marvel and Lucasfilm, as well as less successful deals like Maker Studios and Vice Media.

“Last year, in a move widely seen as giving his longtime strategy head an operational role, Iger put Mayer in charge of Disney’s new direct-to-consumer division — overseeing streaming services like Hulu, ESPN+ and the new Disney+.”

By the numbers

The No. 1 movie in North America is “Midway.” Roland Emmerich’s take on the World War II naval battle grossed $17.5 million, edging out “Doctor Sleep.” The film added another $21 million abroad, a slight opening considering its $100 million budget.

“Terminator: Dark Fate” slipped all the way to fifth in North America, but held onto the top spot globally. The film is on pace to gross less than $300 million worldwide. “It’s an example of the studios looking at the threat posed by VOD and streaming and giving theatrical audiences exactly what they don’t want,” writes Scott Mendelson.

The No. 1 album in the U.S. is Kanye West's "Jesus is King." West sold the equivalent of 264,000 albums, and claimed the top spot for the ninth consecutive time. The album has been classified as a Christian, gospel, hip-hop/Rb rap album, which makes West the first artist to top all five of those.

 

The week that was

  1. Netflix, HBO to crack down on password sharing. A coalition of media giants may require customers to change their passwords periodically or text codes to subscribers’ phones that they would need to enter to keep watching. (The coalition says it won't go after "casual" password sharing.)
  2. CBS, Univision land soccer's biggest annual event. The two TV networks will pay $150 million a year to air the Champions League.
  3. Apple's services are blocked in China. China is one of the biggest markets for iPhones, but the country hasn't sanctioned Apple's new video service, credit card or video game service.
  4. An HBO book is coming. The author of oral history of "Saturday Night Live" and ESPN is working on a similar project about HBO.
  5. Ari Emanuel now reps the Obamas and Clintons. Endeavor, his media company, acquried Harry Walker Agency, the speaking agency of the first families (and many others).
  6. YouTube fight the FTC. A group of online video creators is protesting the Federal Trade Commission’s plan to regulate kids’ videos on YouTube, claiming new rules will hurt them financially and reduce the quality of programs on the world’s largest video site.
  7. Sandra Bullock and Ellen DeGeneres fight fake endorsements. The celebrities sued internet companies that have used their names and images to sell products without their consent.
  8. NBA streams. The league is selling a new streaming service that will charge a monthly fee for access to live NBA games, and original programs.
