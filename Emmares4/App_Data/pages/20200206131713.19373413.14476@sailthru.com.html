It may have dawned on everyone by now that it’s not possible to work full time while raising young children without the benefit of child care. But child care is expensive. Suggestions, anyone?

This week, U.S. President Donald Trump endorsed one approach that’s not exactly paid parental leave, but somewhere in the extended neighborhood. It would let new parents borrow up to $5,000 against the value of the child tax credit they expect to claim in the years ahead. Employers wouldn’t be required to provide any paid leave.

Finland, a very different country than America, announced a very different approach this week. It already uses taxpayer money to provide 11.5 months’ worth of paid leave for parents. However, the nation’s birth rate is falling, and Finns may be reluctant to have more children because child care remains a challenge. The government will now allot 14 months’ worth of paid parental leave with each new child. In two-parent households, that time is to be divided. —Philip Gray

Pete Buttigieg, the first openly gay presidential candidate, is effectively tied with Senator Bernie Sanders for first place in the Iowa presidential caucuses. Iowa, however, is still trying to sort out its mess. Trump supporters allegedly tried to interfere with the vote counting, and one Democratic official is now calling for a recanvass. 

Employers wary of the new coronavirus must be careful that their precautions don’t violate disability discrimination laws. Already, ride-sharing customers and drivers of Asian descent are reporting racist conduct tied to fear of the virus. 

Trump awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom to Rush Limbaugh, a radio host long known for inflammatory statements belittling people based on race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation and disability. 

Companies with ethnically diverse founders raise more money than all-white startups...eventually. When they first open their doors, however, diverse startups are less likely to win funding.

Corporate boards are finding more female and nonwhite directors in part because big companies are encouraging rising executives, a group more diverse than CEOs, to serve on an outside board.

Actor Joaquin Phoenix called out the entertainment industry’s systemic racism at the British Academy Film Awards, where nominees in the top acting categories were all white. At least more diverse representation on screen is improving.

The U.K. Treasury interviewed two women to be the next governor of the Bank of England. The position was eventually given to a man.

Wages are rising faster in the U.S. for women. They are beginning to dominate professions heavily dependent on skills including negotiation and persuasion, such as lawyers and consultants.

Latino businesses left behind

Latino-owned businesses contribute $700 billion a year to the American economy, even though they are at a disadvantage: Lenders and investors are less likely to support them. “The U.S. is missing a huge driver of growth,” said one entrepreneur of Cuban descent, Ryan Bethencourt. “If we want to keep leading economically like we have, we need to activate the raw talent of people, like Latinos.” The Stanford Graduate School of Business’s Latino Entrepreneur Initiative estimates that without the structural disadvantage, Latino businesses would generate an additional $410 billion a year.
