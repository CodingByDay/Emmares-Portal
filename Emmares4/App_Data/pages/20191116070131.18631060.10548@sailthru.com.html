In foreign policy, writes Kevin Rudd, the former Australian prime minister and an adviser to the Bloomberg New Economy Forum, “words are bullets.” A verbal projectile making the rounds in Washington whenever China comes up is “decoupling.” Rudd (below) notes that the vocabulary has shifted with shocking speed: harsh language on trade has morphed into chilling predictions of a technological Cold War. And now, the talk is about the possibility that the world’s two largest economies could split apart.

Preparations on both sides are well underway. “It reminds me a little of the mobilization plans, schedules and railway timetables of the great powers on the eve of the Great War,” Rudd writes.

Nothing could be more damaging to the prospects of new economy countries.

Manufacturing and trade have been their only reliable pathway to prosperity. True, a few have benefited from multinationals shifting pieces of their supply chains away from China—Vietnam comes to mind. But many more are struggling as slowing trade and investment push the global economy toward recession.

In the run-up to our gathering next week in Beijing, members of the Bloomberg New Economy community have been puzzling over alternative scenarios. Rudd proposes “managed strategic competition,” by which he means the U.S. and China should decide where they can, and can’t, cooperate, and where collaboration is desirable—like a joint effort to cure cancer. Next week’s forum will feature a workshop, led by Rudd, to better define these areas.

In a similar vein, Scott Kennedy, an expert on the Chinese industrial economy at the Center for Strategic and International Studies, proposes the concept of “managed interdependence.” Kennedy and his Washington think-tank colleague Jude Blanchette reject the argument that closer integration with China threatens U.S. security. On the contrary, they say Chinese reliance on Western technology and markets makes America safer.

These China-watchers also dismiss arguments that U.S. companies face the stark choice of remaining true to their values or abandoning China. Rather, they call for “a more judicious approach to integration.” For businesses, that could mean taking a hard look at their supply chains to ensure they aren’t sourcing products made in Xinjiang, where the United Nations says upwards of one million ethnic Uighurs are being detained and subject to mass surveillance. (China contends it’s fighting extremism and that Xinjiang is a domestic matter.)

“A unilateral American attempt to decouple could very well leave the U.S., not China, isolated,” Kennedy and Blanchette write.

Cold War II

Not everybody is so anxious to find the golden mean in U.S.-China relations. 

The economic historian Niall Ferguson, the biographer of Henry Kissinger (they will share the stage at the Beijing conference) actually celebrates the arrival of what he calls “Cold War II,” arguing that the alternative is a world dominated by a one-party state hostile to the rule of law and human rights. 

As for the billions of dollars in tariffs that U.S. President Donald Trump has piled onto China, Ferguson is only surprised that “it took this long for the backlash against China to happen.” In his view, the tariffs are in no way a disproportionate response to China’s trade abuses, including intellectual property theft and currency manipulation.

From Method to Madness

My Bloomberg colleagues Shawn Donnan and Jenny Leonard offer a very different perspective on Trump’s tariffs, illustrating in Bloomberg Businessweek that “what began as method now looks more and more like madness.” 

The initial, modest batch of tariffs developed by Trump’s aides were intended to inflict pinpoint damage on China’s strategic industries without harming U.S. consumers. But Trump’s escalating tit-for-tat tariff war has now ensnared 70% of bilateral trade in goods. The next round of U.S. tariffs, scheduled for Dec. 15, would hit items like smartphones, laptops and children’s toys—essentially a tax on American consumers.

Taking all of the above into account, Rudd sees the world descending into chaos. 

Rather than a “great decoupling,” he sees a “great unraveling” of the global rules-based order. If that’s the case, he argues, it would be “a tragedy,” one that augurs “a new era of the law of the jungle.”

A final word of advice to the U.S. from Bloomberg New Economy Forum speaker Charlene Barshefsky (above). The former chief U.S. trade negotiator who brought China into the World Trade Organization said that, in countering China, the dumbest approach would be to try to copy its protectionist policies and industrial planning. Far better to invest in education, research, and infrastructure. 

“We’ll never out-China China,” says Barshefsky. “And if you spent 10 minutes in the country, you’d know that.”
