Hello again

Welcome back or hello for the first time. This is The Station, your place to learn about what’s going on in the future of transportation. I’m your host Kirsten Korosec, senior transportation reporter at TechCrunch.

This week, coronavirus, which continues to spread, dominated headlines as the health crisis spread globally. Automotive is one of many industries that has been affected.

Auto sales were already sliding globally, but the coronavirus has accelerated the fall. Car sales have taken a hit in markets where coronavirus first took root, notably China. Vehicle sales were down 80% in February compared with the same month of 2019, the WSJ reported earlier this week. In South Korea, car sales fell about 20% year over year in February to their lowest monthly level since 2009 and in Japan they dropped 10.7%.

It’s unclear how coronavirus will affect mobility startups, although at least one venture firm is sounding alarm bells. Sequoia Capital sent an email to its founders and CEO this week calling  coronavirus the “black swan of 2020” and said some companies are already seeing a drop in business activity and experiencing supply chain disruptions. The VC firm also warned that private financings could soften significantly, as happened in 2001 and 2009

There was other news besides coronavirus; for instance, Anthony Levandowski, the star engineer and autonomous vehicle startup founder who was at the center of a trade secrets lawsuit between Uber and Waymo, was ordered to pay $179 million to end a contract dispute over his departure from Google. This hardly ends the Levandowski saga. On the same day that the  San Francisco County Superior Court denied his petition and ordered him to pay $179 million, Levandowski filed for bankruptcy.

I won’t speculate as to what will happen next. But I do expect more fireworks before this is over.

Let’s dig in and see what else happened this week.

Micromobbin'

It was bad news bears for the folks over at Boosted, where the company was forced to lay off a significant portion of its employees. Now, the company is looking for a buyer. Boosted attributed the layoffs to the costs of developing, producing and maintaining electric vehicles and the “unplanned challenge with the high expense of the US-China tariff war,” Boosted CEO Jeff Russakow and CTO John Ulmen wrote in a blog post.

In other news, Bird deployed its electric moped via Scoot in Austin, just in time for the since-canceled SXSW conference.

Lastly, it wouldn’t be a micromobility roundup without some consolidation news. This past week, Last Mile Holdings, formerly known as OjO Electric, merged with Gotcha Mobility. The merger was the result of Last Mile Holdings paying $5 million in cash and $7 million in shares issued to purchase Gotcha.

Deal of the week

There were lots of deals this week, but Waymo takes this week’s prize. As a business unit under Alphabet, the conventional wisdom might have been that Waymo had an endless source of capital. And yet, the company announced this week it raised $2.25 billion in a fundraising round led by Silver Lake, Canada Pension Plan Investment Board and Mubadala Investment Company.

This is the company’s first external investment, which also included Magna, Andreessen Horowitz and AutoNation and Alphabet.

On the heels of that announcement, Waymo also unveiled its fifth-generation hardware system — the fruits of more than two years of engineering. Waymo has integrated the next-gen hardware system into the Jaguar I-Pace vehicles and will eventually roll it out to its big rig trucks.

All of this is notable and newsworthy and many of The Station’s readers have followed these two stories. What got my attention this week was a Financial Times article by Richard Waters that featured an interview with Waymo CEO John Krafcik discussing the funding round. At one point, Krafcik said he’s reconsidering the kind of business he wants Waymo to be.

Here’s the key nugget from the FT interview.

“Our role could be just focusing on the Driver” — the name given to its driverless technology — “and leaving the rest of the customer [experience] to others in the industry.”

Krafcik also hinted that the branding on its Waymo One robotaxi service might change. Instead, the services could leave the “customer touch points” with carmakers themselves, he added.

All of this is pointing to Waymo deepening partnerships with automakers and shifting away from an do-it-all autonomous vehicle company. Stay tuned.

GM's electric offensive

GM launched an electric vehicle offensive this week, unveiling a new electric architecture that will be the foundation of the automaker’s future EV plans and support a wide range of products across its brands, including compact cars, work trucks, large premium SUVs, performance vehicles and a new Bolt EUV crossover that will come to market next summer.

This modular architecture, called “Ultium,” will be capable of 19 different battery and drive unit configurations, 400-volt and 800-volt packs with storage ranging from 50 kWh to 200 kWh, and front-, rear- and all-wheel drive configurations.

TechCrunch contributor Ed Niedermeyer (and my co-host on the Autonocast podcast) was there and reported on the event. Today, he’s out with a new piece that provides an important history lesson. The upshot: take on the electric future, General Motors turns to its Sloanist past. Yup, this is an Extra Crunch article; and it’s worth the read.

Meanwhile, this coming electric vehicle storm is creating some pain points and opportunities in the rest of the automotive ecosystem.

 

Trucks VC general partner Reilly Brennan argues that the move from combustion engines to electric power will fundamentally change today’s auto maintenance and service sector. He writes:

The decline is mathematical. With one-fifth the number of powertrain parts and an almost total elimination of oil (a), the typical automotive dealer will suffer 35% declines in maintenance and service revenue, or roughly $1,300, for an EV versus an internal combustion engine vehicle over a five-year period (b).

With upheaval, comes opportunity, Brennan writes. Read on to get his insights.
