Driving the Day

QUESTIONS ON QUESTIONS — The Area 1 Security report that blamed Russian military hackers for infiltrating Burisma Holdings appears to have begat more questions than answers. Martin reported that congressional leaders whom the intelligence community briefs on the nation's most closely guarded secrets said they were in the dark . "We only learned of this hacking through the press," read a statement from House Speaker Nancy Pelosi, a member of the Gang of Eight. Congress "must be briefed on what the administration knows about this attack and why the president doesn't have a plan to protect our elections."

Meanwhile, cyber professionals are questioning some of the report's assertions, Tim reported. Others have urged skepticism since, too. "We should anticipate this type of operation from Russia, and it's important to think through how to respond to a weaponized leak of something like this," tweeted Nathaniel Gleicher, head of security policy at Facebook. "But that doesn't mean we have clear and validated evidence of success/what happened at this point." Cautioned Graham Brookie , director of the Atlantic Council's Digital Forensic Research Lab: "This story is based on one report from a company that not that many people heard of before last night around 6pm. We haven't seen anything to corroborate or verify."

BUG OUT — The NSA announced Tuesday that it had discovered a critical flaw in the Windows 10 operating system that verifies digital signatures used to stamp software as genuine. The announcement marked the first time the country's largest intelligence organization publicly claimed credit for discovering a big vulnerability, which both the NSA and Microsoft said had not been used by malicious hackers in the wild.

"We do routinely share vulnerabilities," Anne Neuberger, head the NSA's months-old Cybersecurity Directorate, told reporters. "As soon as we learned of it, we turned it over to Microsoft as discussed via the [vulnerability equities process]." In addition to the NSA advisory, CISA issued its second-ever emergency order for federal agencies to cauterize the vulnerability. CISA will hold a call on Feb. 3 with federal CIOs or senior agency officials for risk management of agencies that have not completed the required actions to protect their networks, according to Bryan Ware, the agency's new assistant director. The agency will then deliver a status report around Feb. 14 to OMB and DHS.

HALLELUJAH! — Two whole Democrats in Tuesday evening's CNN presidential debate mentioned cybersecurity. "We have to think about cyber," Massachusetts Sen. Elizabeth Warren said while discussing defense priorities. And former South Bend, Ind., Mayor Pete Buttigieg listed it among the challenges the next president would face.

BOARD TIME — The University of California, Berkeley, and Booz Allen Hamilton today teamed up to offer a framework to boards of directors for governing and carrying out oversight of cybersecurity risk. It centers on how boards position themselves across four "dynamic tensions," which are an "organization's overall risk model or mindset; distribution of cybersecurity expertise on the board; balance between cooperation and competition with other enterprises; and the model for information flows between management and the board," according to a summary of the report. The framework stemmed from interviews with 20 board members with more than 130 years of service across most industry sectors.

Election Security

YOU'RE ON NOTICE — Texas's election office has been warning counties buying new paperless voting machines that they're taking a big risk. The state has to approve every purchase, "and we're putting in our letter a reminder to them that the legislation almost passed requiring paper audit trails, and that probably it will pass next time, and so they could be wasting their money and have to spend it again," Texas elections director Keith Ingram said Tuesday in response to a question from POLITICO at an Election Assistance Commission conference.

At least 14 Texas counties have purchased paperless systems in the past few years, a POLITICO investigation found last August. And while state lawmakers may try again to approve paper-mandate legislation that failed in 2019, their next meeting isn't until 2021 — and experts worry that they'll exempt counties that recently bought paperless machines to avoid saddling them with massive new costs.

Ingram didn't address why his office has continued approving the purchase of paperless equipment. But he noted that most Texas counties are buying ballot-marking devices that produce paper records, and he stressed that "the majority of voters in Texas are going to vote with a paper system this March primary." Meanwhile, Geoff Hale, director of CISA's election security initiative, said that based on current figures, "between 90 and 92 percent" of voters nationwide "are likely to have an auditable record for their vote" in November. "That's progress," he said.

WHAT'S THE GAME PLAN? — Top Senate Intelligence Democrat Mark Warner pressed Secretary of State Mike Pompeo in a letter Tuesday about the State Department's plans to stave off cyberattacks against its information security systems in light of Iran's mounting cybersecurity capabilities.

"The Iranian government's state-sponsored cybersecurity capabilities have grown in sophistication and intensity in recent years," Warner wrote. "These attacks serve both political and economic purposes, and use methods like password spray attacks, scanning for VPN vulnerabilities, DNS hijacking, spear-phishing emails, and social engineering."

His letter cited the department's sore history of information security breaches and asked the secretary about the department's plans to help protect employees from phishing attacks. The letter asks whether the department has addressed hiring concerns for information and IT security personnel at U.S. embassies, as well as personnel changes to address hiring free impacts and security and audit concerns. In addition, Warner asked whether the department is up-to-date on information security audits and conducts routine scanning and patching protocol. Pompeo's response is due on Jan. 31.
