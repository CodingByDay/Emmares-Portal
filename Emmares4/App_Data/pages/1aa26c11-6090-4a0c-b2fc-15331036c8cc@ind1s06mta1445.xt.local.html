Plus: Dementia risk factors, breast scan frequency and why our so-called primitive immune defences may be more sophisticated than we thought
Hello readers, 

This week I have been reporting on the unusual blood clot syndrome seen in a small number of people after having the Oxford/AstraZeneca covid-19 vaccine.

I’m sure you will have seen the news that UK policy changed this week so now an alternative vaccine will be given to people under 30. This came after UK and European regulators concluded that the clots were indeed a possible side effect of the vaccine, after suspicions had been growing for weeks.

It was a busy day for science journalists on Wednesday, when there were three separate press conferences on this subject. At one point, I was watching one conference in London on my laptop, had one in Amsterdam on my TV with subtitles on and was simultaneously swapping messages with colleagues about how we should cover the story.

In the end, New Scientist put a rapid report on our daily coronavirus news round-up, which you can access here (it goes live at 6.00 pm BST every weekday). Then we ran a longer analysis explaining why UK policy is different to that in other countries and how the changes may impact vaccine roll-out. Previously, we have looked at the possible mechanism for this syndrome.  

Amid the blitzkrieg of information, what I found most helpful was the below graphic, produced by the University of Cambridge’s Winton Centre for Risk and Evidence Communication. It shows why UK  authorities decided to put the age cut-off at 30, even though other countries have set it higher.

 

To me, the biggest health risk from the Oxford/AstraZeneca vaccine comes not from the very small number of people who develop a blood clot, but from a potential rise in people who are put off getting that jab, or even other types of covid-19 vaccine. Yet paradoxically, I see the events of the past few weeks as a sign that vaccine safety systems are working well.

It just wasn’t possible to detect a side effect of this rarity in the vaccine trials done before regulatory approval, which recruited a few tens of thousands of people. The rate of blood clots with the Oxford/AstraZeneca jab looks to be about 4 in a million, going by UK figures. So it could only be seen once the jab had been given to millions of people. 

And once the side effect emerged, regulators acted accordingly. They are clearly releasing information as soon as it is available and letting people make their own informed decisions. 

The age group we need to worry about now in the UK is not the under-30s, but people aged 30 to 50 or so, whose willingness to get vaccinated will be a major determinant of the size of the country’s impending third wave. Vaccines are the best tool we have to avoid social distancing restrictions stretching on for another year.
