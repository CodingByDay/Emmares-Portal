From Assyrian friezes depicting mutilation and bloodshed to 19th-century prints by Francisco Goya to gory snapshots taken by GIs in Vietnam, warfare has always been entwined with its depiction. As journalist and author James Verini writes this week on Backchannel, the representation of warfare changed yet again in the early part of this century during the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan. “No longer did you have to have special access to see the most ghastly moments,” he writes. “No longer could censors or editors block your view of reality.” All you needed was an internet connection. This freedom reached its apogee during the Battle of Mosul, for jihadis, yes, and soldiers too. “They shot and shared selfies and group pictures—soldiers with prisoners, soldiers with rubble, soldiers with body parts, soldiers with corpses, soldiers with Americans, soldiers with their mothers. Anything would do … They shot videos of engagements, uploaded and shared instantaneously on WhatsApp and Signal and Telegram, so that fighters on one front could get their lolz or shed their tears watching fighters on another. Brave commanders became YouTube stars.”

Technology and the smartphone enabled this hyperprolific sharing of combat, of course, but what were the people really doing and saying when they uploaded images of the chaos they were witnessing? To answer that, Verini, whose new book, They Will Have to Die Now: Mosul and the Fall of the Caliphate was published in September, turns to another celebrated war correspondent, Michael Herr, for help exploring the answer. In this poignant essay, he examines what it means to look at the horrors of war, and to ask others to look too.
