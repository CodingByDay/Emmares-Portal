Bottom Line: Pivot To Impatience

President Trump’s messaging on the coronavirus pandemic has been a rollercoaster ride. After initially downplaying it, he seemed to be taking it seriously last week. He endorsed public health officials’ calls for Americans to, for the most part, stay home and self quarantine.
 
But watching as the stock market dropped significantly, President Trump reversed course again. He echoed a conservative line that the “cure” can’t be worse than the disease and he suggested he'd like to see the country “opened up” again by Easter. That's a tough promise to make.

The president does appear to have some leeway with about half of Americans. His approval rating this week hit a high for his presidency at 47%, according to an average of the polls. But a record 3.3 million people filed for unemployment this week; the U.S. took over the top spot in the world for known cases of coronavirus; and major outbreaks are happening in more cities than just New York and Seattle.

Trump knows the longer this goes on, the worse it could get for him politically, so he wants it over and done with -- and he wants Democratic governors to be more "appreciative" to stop making him look bad.
 
But as Dr. Anthony Fauci of the National Institutes of Health said this week, "You don't make the timeline, the virus makes the timeline."
— Domenico Montanaro, NPR’s senior political editor/correspondent

Politics Roundup: The Coronavirus Crisis

Coronavirus relief: A historic, roughly $2 trillion coronavirus response bill was approved by Congress this week and signed into law by President Trump on Friday. Here’s a breakdown of what’s in the aid package and who it’ll help.

War of words: As the coronavirus pandemic continues to rage, the United States and China are locked in a soft power struggle over influence and messaging. And this week, the U.S. notched a small victory. 

It’s not the flu: President Trump again compared the coronavirus to the flu this week — but they present very different dangers. NPR fact checked the president’s claim, laying out eight key differences between the flu and the coronavirus.

Testing the truth: The president also misleadingly claimed this week that the United States had tested more people for the virus than South Korea had. But the U.S. population is more than six times the size of South Korea’s. As of Tuesday, South Korea had tested 1 in 170 people, while the United States had tested 1 in 1,090.

Prison response: The U.S. Bureau of Prisons could send home some of its oldest and most vulnerable inmates in response to the coronavirus pandemic, Attorney General Bill Barr announced this week. Prisoners and staff members have tested positive for the virus in New York, Georgia and Louisiana, leading to measures such as lockdowns and increased isolation.

ICYMI: Top Stories

“Steep road” for Sanders: After Vermont Sen. Bernie Sanders helped pass the coronavirus relief package, he told NPR that the coronavirus is changing the way people think about their government. Though he trails Former Vice President Joe Biden in delegates in the 2020 presidential race, Sanders argued that more primary debates will allow voters to hear solutions to how the government is handling the pandemic.

Bloomberg sued: Mike Bloomberg's presidential campaign allegedly promised he would throw his financial and staffing resources behind whoever could beat President Trump. Now that he ended his campaign, staffers who were laid off are suing, claiming he promised to pay them until the general election in November.  

Biden goes online: Former Vice President Joe Biden does not like to be too far away from his supporters — but that’s exactly where the coronavirus pandemic has left him. To make up for the canceled campaign rallies and selfie lines, the presidential candidate is hosting virtual press conferences and broadcasting speeches. 

New on the job: President Trump picked North Carolina Rep. Mark Meadows to replace his acting chief of staff as the federal government and the public started reacting to the coronavirus outbreak. Three weeks and a stint in self-isolation later, Meadows has yet to resign from Congress and fully step into his new role. 

SCOTUS and the pirate ship: A local videographer spent 20 years documenting a North Carolina pirate ship wreck as a contractor for the state. The Supreme Court said the videographer can't sue the state of North Carolina for using his videos without permission.
