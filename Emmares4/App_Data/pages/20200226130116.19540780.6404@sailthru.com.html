The news business is an odd place—one second you're writing about researchers studying perfectly wok-fried rice, the next it's drug dealers who lost their Bitcoin wallets to fishing rods. It's a lifestyle where things with no real connection inevitably become intertwined within your small lens on the world. 

 

Case in point: this week, humanity lost two icons that in no way intersected during their lives yet feel marginally connected to those few news editors out there covering both NASA and geeky pop culture. On Monday morning, LEGO publicly shared news that Jens Nygaard Knudsen, toy builder behind the now iconic minifig, passed away. No more than 20 minutes later, NASA Administrator Jim Bridenstine also took to Twitter and announced the passing of Katherine Johnson, the pioneering mathematician who helped take humanity to the moon in the early space race days. 

 

Maybe Ars is the rare place where both these long overlooked legacies can come up simultaneously for remembrance. So for this week's Orbital Transmission, we're doing just that—sharing a few scattershot thoughts on math, minifigs, and the intersection of two remarkable yet drastically different legacies. And if you'll excuse us, rather than wearing black around the office, we'll be seeking out the earliest NASA spacecraft LEGO sets we can.

Orbital Transmission 02.26.2020
 

Katherine Johnson led a remarkable, world-changing life

What more can you say about Katherine Johnson at this point? The trailblazing mathematician and crucial contributor to NASA's early space program died this week at the age of 101. In her later years, her previously-underheralded role in helping humanity reach orbit and eventually the moon came to new life through the book (then the film), Hidden Figures. She was recognized by the likes of President Obama with a Presidential Medal of Freedom, the US' highest civilian honor, for her simultaneous work of incredible computations while smashing gender and racial stereotypes of the time. Perhaps the most fitting anecdote as the world mourns is a famous one involving John Glenn: To prep the first orbital mission of a US astronaut, NASA engineers had run the calculations on electric computers, but when someone was needed to validate these calculations, Glenn and the rest of the space agency turned to Johnson. “If she says they’re good,” Johnson recalled the astronaut saying, “then I’m ready to go."

 

Hidden Figures is great, but have you read the kids' book?

Maybe the best way to teach the next generation about Johnson's legacy is to do it early—and luckily, author Margot Lee Shetterly and illustrator Laura Freeman created the perfect resource to do just that. Hidden Figures (the children's book) is written by the same author of the book that inspired the movie, and it does a tremendous job of explaining how remarkable Johnson's work was in the context of the social forces and engineering challenges facing her and NASA at the time. Former Ars' editor Cyrus Farivar would read it frequently to his daughter and she adored the story of how Johnson and her colleagues essentially became the human computers needed to send astronauts to orbit. "Today we think of computers as machines, but in the 1940s, computers were actual people like Dorothy, Mary, Katherine, and Christine," Shetterly writes. "Their job was to do math."

 

Maybe we can get a "Women of NASA, part two" minfig set soon

Beyond being the minifig mastermind, Knudsen worked at LEGO for more than three decades, including creating notable early sets like LEGO Space. Coincidentally, his work at LEGO comes close to overlapping with Johnson's at NASA. Space-oriented sets are nothing new—see delights like that beautiful, big Saturn V one—but in 2017, the brick company decided it was time to specifically focus on space-facing minifigs. Through its LEGO Ideas website (where fans can submit ideas for public votes in the hopes of eventually going into production), LEGO developed a "Women of NASA" minifigs set in 2017 to honor early pioneers across the decades. It includes astronomer Nancy Grace Roman, computer scientist Margaret Hamilton, and pioneering astronauts Sally Ride and Mae Jemison. Maybe it's time for a sequel?

 

The next minifig we're pining over is an ISS astronaut

As we all wait on another set of more specific NASA minifigs, we do have one we've been eyeing this spring. In early February, NASA released its latest Ideas-inspired space focused on the International Space Station. It looks robust. The set contains 850-plus pieces and when built stands over 7-inches (20cm) high, 12-inches (31cm) long, and 19-inches (49cm) wide. Loving details include a posable Canadarm2 and two rotating joints that coincide with eight adjustable solar panels. But most importantly for builders and play timers, the set also comes with some delightful extras, such as a brick-built mini space shuttle, a 148-page booklet stuffed with info on the real ISS... and pair of astronaut minifigs.
