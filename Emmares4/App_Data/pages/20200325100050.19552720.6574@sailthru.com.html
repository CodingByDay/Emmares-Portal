It's hard to believe, but it hasn't even been 10 years since the start of the streaming era (beginning roughly back in February 2013, when Netflix pushed out House of Cards as a to-service original). Since then, well, everyone knows how it turned out. On Ars' favorite TV of the decade list, there were five streaming-only series out of ten entries. And the idea of living with many small .

 

Yet even with the recent additions of Disney+ and Apple TV+ to the landscape, the streaming wars haven't even really begun—we might still be just getting started. Film-centric services focused on individuals niches like Criterion, Hi-Yah, or Shudder may only encourage others. And big corporate creators like Warner Bros., HBO, and NBC are only now beginning to get into the game (with Peacock and HBO Max in particular arriving in the next two months).

 

So for this week's Orbital Transmission, we're pausing for a moment to take a quick account of what the leading currently available streamers are offering that might make 'em worth keeping as more and more consumers will have to make choices about subscribing to this but not that. We certainly wouldn't recommend keeping Netflix for the Goop show, for instance, but streaming's current behemoth is one of the easier services to make a case for in 2020. What's worth watching on Apple TV+ these days? (Actually, now that we mention it... let's skip that one for now. Woof.)

The first question for any Netflix user: Who drew the dicks?

Undeniably, Netflix stands as the hardest streaming service to pick a singular favorite from. It's been around longer than the others; it has critically-acclaimed and award-nominated originals spanning genres and formats (even a choose-your-own-adventure, mind you). Three of its shows and one of its movies made our best of the decade lists. And yet, we're not here to recommend Stranger Things, the Ava DuVernay docuseries, or even all those beloved rom-coms. Because of Netflix, there exists a seemingly slapstick mockumentary about a teenager drawing dicks on teachers' cars that's actually about class, disinformation, and growing up in our new hyperconnected reality. American Vandal is really funny, really well made, and so smart it earned a Peabody Award. Watch both seasons, but savor the first the most.

 

It's uncomfortable, but Hulu users must watch The Handmaid's Tale

Picking the best of Hulu presents a different but no less challenging predicament: the platform just hasn't created that many must-see original works (presumably because it started out as a depository for old network programs from NBC). The movie selection is sparse: some really fun documentaries—Becoming Bond, The Amazing Jonathan, Ask Dr. Ruth—and the delightful horror-comedy, Little Monsters. And the shows feel a little flat comparatively: Castle Rock and 11:22:63 are fine genre far, but among the platforms, most high profile work. So with apologies to the super charming and poignant PEN15, for now the answer is still the brutal to watch but tremendously made (and utterly horrific) The Handmaid's Tale, if for no other reason than Elizabeth Moss' continued performance.

 

Amazon leads in film, and The Big Sick may be our favorite

These days, Amazon Prime has some delightful original series—you've watched The Boys or Homecoming, right?—but this service specialized in film long before episodic storytelling became a focal point. Manchester By The Sea is probably the most high profile, but Amazon is also quietly behind modern classics like Honey Boy, Suspiria, Cold War, and The Report. Our favorite, though, is the Kumail Nanjiani love origin story in The Big Sick. It may not be a traditionally Ars-y film, but we have yet to meet anyone who doesn't love it.

 

Sign up for The Vault, but The Mandalorian also makes Disney+

Maybe this debate will become harder as time goes on, but for now there's only one choice when asked for a must recommend original on Disney+: it has to be The Mandalorian. The show took an old-school TV format (the Western) and modernized it for 2020 by setting everything in a familiar galaxy far, far away. We get new information about a beloved old universe (Baby Yoda!), we get some of the best parts of Star Wars on a regular basis (the set pieces, the world-building, etc.), and throughout S1 we consistently got episodic narrative satisfaction in a show nimbly shifting tone. "If the pilot is any indication, The Mandalorian is Star Wars for a generation of fans who remember the film series as an aesthetic, an ethos, and a promise of morally mixed discovery," Ars Culture Editor Sam Machkovech wrote in his early season review. 'And I'm already ready to hitch my space western wagon to this wild ride."
