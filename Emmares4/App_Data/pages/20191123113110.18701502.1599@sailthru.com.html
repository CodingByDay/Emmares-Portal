Hello from car land

Welcome back for another week of The Station. As always, I’m your host Kirsten Korosec, senior transportation reporter at TechCrunch.

It’s been a helluva car week. It started with a heart-thumping drive in a Porsche Taycan Turbo along the Angeles Crest Highway, a weekend jaunt to Joshua Tree National Park to test the camping capabilities of the Volkswagen Atlas and then onto Ford’s reveal of its all-electric Mustang Mach-E SUV and into Automobility LA, the press and trade days of the LA Auto Show. It ended in a surreal display at the Tesla Design Studio in Hawthorne, Calif., where Elon Musk unveiled his Blade Runner-inspired Cybertruck.

There was a running theme, save for that 36-hour trip into the desert: electric, electric, electric. That doesn’t mean other future of transportation stuff didn’t happen. It did. But after years of automakers sniffing around electric drive trains, this time it seemed different. Hell, even Lincoln is giving its vehicles a plug.

It's electric! Boogie woogie woogie

The global market share of plug-in electric passenger cars is still just a glimmer in a gas-powered vehicle world. (The market share in the U.S. was more than 2% in 2018, for instance). But it is changing, and after walking the floor of the LA Auto Show, I’d forecast an acceleration towards EVs.

Automakers have introduced new EVs during the auto show season before. What’s different is that these are actually slated for production.

Ford’s Mustang Mach-E received much of the attention this year, and for good reason. It not only represents the biggest change for the Mustang in its 55-year history, it’s the first product to come out of Ford’s $11 billion commitment to add 16 all-electric vehicles within its global portfolio of 40 electrified vehicles through 2022.

Meanwhile, Mercedes-Benz priced its upcoming all-electric EQC 400 SUV below competitors like the Jaguar I-Pace, Audi released an electric e-tron Sportback with fancy new lights, Hyundai gave its all-electric Ioniq a boost in range from 124 to 170 miles per charge and Lincoln unveiled a plug-in hybrid Corsair Grand Touring model that combines a 2.5-liter engine and a 14.4-kilowatt-hour battery pack. Oh, and how could we forget, Toyota introduced the plug-in hybrid RAV4 Prime, its most fuel efficient and one of its most powerful vehicles in its portfolio.

These are real electrified cars that people can actually buy. And more are coming.

Volkswagen of America CEO Scott Keogh said the ID. Space Vizzion electric wagon concept will go into production in 2022 and GM CEO Mary Barra announced plans to bring an electric pickup truck to market that same year.

All of this EV activity is prompting automakers to think about, and invest in, battery cell and charging infrastructure. BMW is the latest example. The automaker announced it will spend €10 billion euros ($11.07 billion) on battery cells from Chinese battery cell manufacturer Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. and Samsung SDI.

Micromobbin'

Because we need more scooters, Superpedestrian raised a $20 million round for its scooters and vehicle intelligence platform that it plans to sell to operators. The round was funded by Spark Capital, General Catalyst, Hanaco Ventures and Empire Angels. This brings Superpedestrian’s total funding to $64 million.

Superpedestrian’s key offering is the vehicle intelligence platform, which is designed to detect more than one hundred situations that could lead to malfunction, triage each issue and then determine what response to take in order to prevent vehicle damage and rider injury. Superpedestrian has yet to announce its operating partners. It is on track to launch in multiple markets in January.

Meanwhile, Bird took micromobility to a new level with the launch of Birdie, it’s new scooter designed for kids. But don’t worry, it doesn’t have an electric motor. This one is powered by kicks.

Cybertruckin'

In case, you haven’t been near the Internet in the past few days, Elon Musk unveiled Thursday evening the Tesla Cybertruck. I’m not here to rehash the announcement. We have lots of coverage of this below. But as someone who was there, I’m here to add some insights that the live stream could not.

There was a lot more energy in this event compared to the Model Y unveiling in March 2019, which had a downcast tinge to it despite the drinks and revelry. Executives like chief designer Franz von Holzhausen, president of automotive Jerome Guillen and vp of technology Drew Baglino were more visible. I saw them milling about the crowd, taking selfies with attendees as well as rides in the Cybertruck. There was a giddiness in the air, perhaps because of the absurdist nature of the evening.

I was left with one thought as the event unfolded and I watched and listened to those around me. Musk appears to have transcended to a new level of “zero fucks given” (at least in terms of concerns about industry or Wall Street reaction) and the crowd was ready to go wherever he took them. That certainly didn’t translate to everyone that evening, but it was fascinating to watch people talk themselves into Musk’s vision.

I heard many people within moments of the Cybertruck reveal make comments, like “oh no” or “is this a joke?” As Musk rolled through the specs like towing capacity and range that sentiment started to change; he had them hooked, or at least intrigued. While standing in line to take a ride, the most common statement I heard (at least a dozen more times throughout the evening) was “It’s growing on me.”

ˆWill the Cybertruck actually come to market is a whole other question. But if it does, I wonder how many and who will have bought into this vision enough to buy the truck. Send me your predictions; we have at least two years to play prognosticator.

A shift inside the beltway

The federal government doesn’t have specific regulations around automated vehicle technology. Today, there are guidelines. And as along as a passenger vehicle meets Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards, it’s good to go. FMVSS are federal regulations that specify design, construction, durability and other requirements for motor vehicles

But that might be changing. Two hearings on the Hill this past week signal a coming change. During a Nov. 19 hearing by the National Transportation Safety Board, investigators issued a stinging report that blamed the fatal Uber self-driving crash on the safety driver in the vehicle, the victim and the ride-hailing company. It also criticized the federal government for failing to regulate the industry.

A day later, a number of U.S. Senators offered sharp criticism of Uber and Tesla during a Commerce Committee hearing on self-driving vehicles. Officials from the NTSB and National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) were there.

Regulations were a theme. Specifically, the NTSB urged NHTSA to place conditions on autonomous vehicle developers that want to test and deploy vehicles with autonomous features on U.S. roads.
