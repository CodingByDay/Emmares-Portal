Driving the Day

HE'S BACK — Trump once more put forward Ratcliffe as his man to sit atop the intelligence community , stirring up some of the same issues that hampered his selection the first time — allegations that he embellished his record, bipartisan concerns about whether he was adequately qualified and worries that as one of Trump's top Hill supporters he wouldn't be sufficiently objective. In an interview that aired over the weekend, Ratcliffe sought to ease those fears.

As before, Senate Intelligence Chairman Richard Burr (R-N.C.) issued a statement that was noteworthy for how little it said, compared to, say, Burr's statement on Ratcliffe's last full-time predecessor. Top panel Democrat Sen. Mark Warner (Va.) sounded weary about it all. "The last time this nomination was unsuccessfully put forward, serious bipartisan questions were raised about Rep. Ratcliffe's background and qualifications," he said. "It's hard for me to see how anything new has happened to change that."

But this is Morning Cybersecurity, so we have to discuss it, even if it's not the focus of others: Ratcliffe has established some cybersecurity bona fides during his time in Congress. As a freshman he snagged the gavel of the Homeland Security Committee's cybersecurity subpanel, where he worked on the 2015 information sharing law and talked about a bevy of issues with your MC host in an extensive 2016 interview and another in 2017. He conducted oversight of the freshly created CISA and sought to penalize Iranian hackers as an active chairman.

Ratcliffe later left the Homeland panel entirely but continued to work on cybersecurity legislation. You could also probably guess that his criticisms of surveillance in the Russia probe would figure into his tenure, if confirmed. It's certainly on the president's mind.

THE WEEK AHEAD — On the penultimate week of the release of the Cyberspace Solarium Commission report, there are three — count 'em, three — events promoting it. Each event on Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday features at least one member of the panel and other experts, who will discuss the commission created by Congress in 2018 to pit competing teams of experts against each other in a bid to formulate a strategy to protect the U.S. against cyberattacks. At the RSA Conference last week, there were some other previews, and the leaders talked election security in an op-ed, too.

Congress will stay busy on cyber this week, too. Senate and House Armed Services subcommittees hold hearings on DoD cyber operations on Tuesday and Wednesday, respectively. The Senate hearing is closed, while the House's open hearing will feature testimony from Gen. Paul Nakasone, head of U.S. Cyber Command and director of the NSA. Also Wednesday, the Senate Commerce panel hears testimony on 5G supply chain security from Nokia and Ericsson officials , then on Thursday, the Senate Banking Committee will hold a hearing on "Threats Posed by State-Owned and State-Supported Enterprises to Public Transportation."

— ALSO THIS WEEK: The Senate this week will take up energy innovation legislation officially unveiled mere days ago after a year of hearings, a bipartisan bill with a grid security section. The measure, sponsored by Energy and National Resources Chairwoman Lisa Murkowski (R-Alaska) and top panel Democrat Joe Manchin (W.Va.), would create a grant program to help rural and municipal utilities deploy advanced cybersecurity tech, require the Energy secretary to develop a program for testing to identify cybersecurity vulnerabilities in energy supply chain products and more.

The nonprofit group Protect Our Power applauded the bill's security sections. "The legislation's provisions requiring mechanisms to encourage cybersecurity investments, a grant program to assist rural and municipal utilities, and better information sharing on cyber risks all speak to gaps that Protect Our Power has identified in a series of studies," said the organization's executive director, Jim Cunningham.

Election Security

ELECTION SECURITY VIEWS FROM THE FIELD — In Friday's edition of MC, we shared highlights from Eric's RSA Conference conversation with Cisco's Matthew Olney and Sean Frazier about Olney's nationwide travels to help state and local officials with election security. Here are more of their takeaways from those meetings, including what it takes to protect elections and why the situation on the ground remains so challenging.

— Some states are getting creative. In Iowa, a group of county IT staff have "pooled their resources" to help other counties that lack dedicated tech experts. "There's kind of a mutual-aid set up from counties with more resources to counties with less resources," Olney said. "It's great that Iowa's come up with a way for counties to help each other, because they trust each other [and] because they're both in the same situation."

— General security PSAs trickle down to election workers. "Any time I see multi-factor authentication in the news ... that helps downstream," Frazier said. "So the less sophisticated users — people who are kind of transient, temporary workers who come in for elections and [prepare equipment] — they're understanding what these kind of first-line defense protections are."

— Election supervisors know they don't know everything. "The people that run our elections at this point didn't sign up to go against the GRU, but they recognize that that's a thing that is happening," Olney said. "And they also understand that they don't know enough to be successful here. The ones that I've talked to are very eager to accept assistance when it's given in the correct way."

— But there's still some anxiety about researchers. Occasionally, Olney said, he encounters "a sense of, you know, 'Are you looking to cause problems? What's your narrative? Are you going to be a partner or you just trying to score points here?'" Olney compared the environment to the early computer security ecosystem, when manufacturers distrusted people poking around in their products. "That's happening in the election community now," Olney said. "They're just having to learn the same things."

— Things are getting better. "There's still a lot of work to be done," Frazier said. "But it's way better. I'm not an optimist by trade, but I'm optimistic more than I have been."

LATE FOR A VERY IMPORTANT DATE — House Homeland Security Chairman Bennie Thompson (D-Miss.) last week followed Speaker Nancy Pelosi (D-Calif.) in criticizing the administration for missing a deadline to produce a congressionally mandated report on election security to lawmakers. "The threat to our democracy from foreign governments is real, and the Administration's pattern of denial must stop," said Thompson, who said the administration was "further obstructing Congress' abilities to conduct proper oversight, and in direct violation of the law."

MORE CONGRESS STILL — A group of moderate House Democrats on Friday released their top 20 priorities for this year , with "Secure the Digital Economy" among them. The New Democrat Coalition called on Congress to boost spending for "cybersecurity capabilities, standards, resilience, and response for critical infrastructure, the private sector, local governments, and more." It also called on lawmakers to work with their counterparts in other countries to develop international cybersecurity rules, modernize trade deals to emphasize privacy and "enact a pro-consumer, pro-innovation national information, data, and privacy standard, and establish transparency requirements."
