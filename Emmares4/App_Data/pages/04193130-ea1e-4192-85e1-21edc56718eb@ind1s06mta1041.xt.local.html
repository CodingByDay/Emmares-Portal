Hi readers! Welcome back to New Scientist America. I have long had a fascination with the sea – in fact, if I could have a superpower, it would be to have gills or some other way to breathe underwater.

Mostly, my interest lies in the array of delightful and bizarre (and sometimes frightening) animals beneath the waves. But sometimes the sea holds secrets to ancient civilizations, too. One of our recent features is about exploring just such a place, where human-made tools were found on the seabed. Take a trip to Aquaterra , a lost continent submerged under rising seas that also preserved artefacts from humans who lived there more than 7000 years ago.

Now, here are some of my favorite news stories of the week:
REUTERS/Simon Dawson

THE MENTAL HEALTH TOLL OF POLICE KILLINGS

Over the past few years, instances of US police officers killing Black Americans have become commonplace. The unending barrage of news about these killings and the subsequent trials of police officers has an impact on the mental health of Black people, affecting them more than white people during the weeks around these events. One scientist says it may be time to devise reporting guidelines for such cases to minimize the harm caused, as we do with reports on suicide. I think that’s a great idea.

EVEN MORE TYRANNOSAURS

Last week, we published a story about how many Tyrannosaurus rex ever lived on Earth, and this week we have more tyrannosaur news. Turns out, they’re not quite the lone, malicious predators they’ve been made out to be for years. A discovery of several fossils in Utah’s Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument suggests that tyrannosaurs lived together and may have hunted in packs like wolves. Unfortunately, the reason we know that is because this fossilized family died together in a flood. Terrible news for these tyrannosaurs of yore, but a lucky strike for the paleontologists that found them. 

INGENUITY TAKES FLIGHT

We have lift-off! NASA’s Ingenuity helicopter has done its first flight on Mars, rising about 10 feet, turning in the air while it hovered and then touching back down on red dirt. It was short, but extremely cool (just like me). Over the coming days, NASA has planned four more flights with Ingenuity, during which pictures will be taken from farther afield. Then, in its last flight, it may smash down on Mars’s surface. That’s because NASA scientists will be pushing it to its absolute limits of speed and distance until it gives out. If you’ve got to go, may as well do it setting records on another planet.

CLIMATE TARGET UPGRADE

President Biden has announced an ambitious new target for cutting US greenhouse gas emissions by 2030. The new goal aims for a 50 to 52 per cent drop on 2005 levels in the next 9 years, up quite a bit from the previous target of reducing emissions by 28 per cent by 2025. The way that the US will get there is set out in a plan released by the White House, which includes zero-carbon electricity production by 2035, planting more forests and increasing the fuel efficiency of cars and heat pumps in buildings. The US is the second-biggest emitter in the world, so this step is a necessary move to limit runaway global warming.

FOR YOUR EARs only

In this week’s Escape Pod , Rowan, Anna and Josh take a mental trip to some of the places that are most important to the elements we find on the periodic table. Josh, our resident chemistry buff, takes us to Ytterby, a village in Sweden that is home to an astonishing number of the naturally occurring elements. Nearly 10 per cent of these elements (including, of course, ytterbium) can trace their discovery back to one quarry in Ytterby, thanks to a bubble of molten rock that contained them and rose to Earth’s surface a very long time ago.

 THURSDAY 6 MAY | 6-7pm BST | 1-2pm EDT | On-demand


The universe as we know it began in a Big Bang almost 14 billion years ago. In this talk, Jo Dunkley will tell the story of how we have come to know this to be true, and how we have been able to find out how fast space is growing. She will discuss the fascinating conundrum facing astronomers today: the two methods of measuring the rate that space is growing, and the age of the universe, don’t agree. Have we got something wrong in our understanding of the universe? Jo will describe her team’s contribution to answering this question, using a telescope high in the Chilean desert.
