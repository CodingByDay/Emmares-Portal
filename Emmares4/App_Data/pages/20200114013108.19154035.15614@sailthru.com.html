Welcome to the Brussels Edition, Bloomberg’s daily briefing on what matters most in the heart of the European Union.

The European Union takes its first tangible step on the way to a climate-neutral economy today, when the Commission presents its plan to mobilize 1 trillion euros ($1.1 trillion) over the next decade. The cost of the bloc’s energy transition is dizzying, and skeptical countries like Poland will need cajoling — and a lot of money — to get on board. Once Brussels reveals its blueprint, the battle will commence with national capitals over who gets how much. 

—Viktoria Dendrinou

What’s Happening

Trade Talks | The EU’s new trade chief goes to Washington to try to ease transatlantic tensions on several fronts. While Donald Trump has held back on a threat to hit European cars with tariffs, both sides have revived old disputes and triggered new ones thanks to deep disagreements over trade policy. A high-stakes security standoff between the West and Iran adds to the complexity.

Day in Court | Hungary’s crackdown on foreign-funded NGOs will be put to the test in the EU’s top court today in a case pitting Viktor Orban's government against the Commission. The non-binding opinion by a court adviser comes just as the European People's Party is considering expelling his group for its opposition to liberal democracy.

Holding Tight | Angela Merkel is batting away the next generation of German conservatives as they vie for the spotlight in the twilight of her long chancellorship. Her latest challenger: The head of her Bavarian sister party, Markus Soeder, who has argued for freshening up the government. But while there's no shortage of contenders vying for a chance to succeed her, Merkel, for now, is holding tightly to power.

Coal Threat | One of Germany’s biggest utilities plans to open a new coal plant, in what could become the latest flash point in the fractious debate about the fuel that still generates about a third of the country’s electricity. The conflict could threaten Angela Merkel’s climate legacy.

French Pensions | President Emmanuel Macron’s decision not to raise the retirement age could allow him to push through reforms that have bedeviled governments for a quarter century. After weeks of strikes that have crippled public transport, a key early sign appeared in his favor: disruption to train services in France eased as some union leaders tentatively embraced the new plan.

In Case You Missed It

Keep Quiet | ECB President Christine Lagarde wrote to policy makers asking them not to talk publicly about her strategy review before she formally announces it. That may reflect her desire to control the debate over the review, and could reinforce her reputation as someone who likes to run a tight ship.

Here's The Catch | The European Commission warned that a trade deal with the U.K is contingent on a fisheries accord. The unprecedented demand sets the stage for an early confrontation with Boris Johnson, who has vowed to retake control of British territorial waters post-Brexit.

Women First | The European Investment Bank is helping female entrepreneurs break the glass ceiling in a key part of Poland’s economy by signing deals with two leasing companies to fund businesses led by women. The move could help bridge gaps in a country where women remain under-represented in leadership positions in business and politics.

German Hopes | Germany’s banks are hoping their government will soften the blow from tougher capital rules when it takes on the rotating presidency of the EU later this year. Regulators want banks to be better able to absorb losses without asking taxpayers for a bailout, but lenders argue tighter rules risk choking off the flow of credit to the wider economy. 

Chart of the Day

Germany posted a record budget surplus in 2019 amid an economic slowdown and constant calls for increased spending. The federal government had an excess of 13.5 billion euros last year on stronger-than-expected tax revenue and lower interest rate payments, official data showed. Berlin’s fiscal discipline has helped Germany slash its debt dramatically. But the latest numbers are likely to renew calls for increased investments or tax cuts.
