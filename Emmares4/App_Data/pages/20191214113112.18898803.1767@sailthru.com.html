Micromobbin'

Helmets and shared electric scooters should go hand in hand. But companies haven’t quite figured out how to get people to use them, particularly shared ones.

Wheels might have figured out how with the deployment of helmet-equipped e-bikes. On the heels of a $50 million funding round in October, Wheels is launching its “smart” helmet system for its pedal-less e-bikes. The helmet, which locks into the rear fender of the bike, features sensors designed to know when a rider is using it.

In order to incentivize people to wear the helmets, Wheels will initially offer riders at a 20% discount for unlocking and using the helmet for the duration of the ride.

In other scooter news, workers for electric scooter company Spin formed a union in San Francisco. The roughly 40 workers who are in charge of deployment, charging and repairs, are now part of Teamsters Local 665.

Deal of the week


The deal of the week is likely small — no financial terms have been disclosed. But I’m noting it here because it speaks to a mini trend in the world of autonomous vehicles: simulation is still an area that AV companies are investing in.

Waymo announced this week that it acquired Latent Logic, a U.K. company that spun out of Oxford University’s computer science department.

The acquisition is notable for two reasons: it aims to improve Waymo’s simulation technology, which has been a key aspect of its AV development, and it marks the launch of its first European engineering hub in the U.K.

Latent Logic has focused on form of machine learning called imitation learning. Imitation learning models human behavior of motorists, cyclists and pedestrians. The idea is that by modeling the mistakes and imperfect driving of humans, the simulation will become more realistic and theoretically improve Waymo’s behavior prediction and planning.

Simulation, like hardware, has become a hot topic among AV companies in the latter half of 2019.

The news comes on the heels of a report from The Information, that Uber is nearing a deal to acquire a simulation company called Foresight. In early 2019, Aurora acquired 7D Labs, a simulation startup founded by Magnus Wrenninge, former software engineer from Pixar animation, that makes photorealistic synthetic data sets for street scenes.

EV wars

People, it seems, love a good battle. And so, I suppose it was only natural that in the run-up to the Porsche Taycan’s release, the German automaker’s first electric vehicle was often pitted against Tesla.

The Porsche vs Tesla story line has persisted in forums like Reddit as well as Twitter, and in more traditional media. Tesla fans pounced earlier this week when the EPA determined that the estimated range for the Porsche Taycan Turbo, one of several variants, was just 201 miles.

That range puts the Taycan Turbo (yes, the turbo vernacular makes no sense) behind the Jaguar I-Pace. It also trails other electric vehicles outside of the sports sedan category, including the Audi e-tron SUV and the Chevy Bolt EV. But the biggest gulf is between the Taycan Turbo and the long-range version of the Model S, which has an EPA range of 373 miles. The performance version of the Model S has a range of 348 miles.

The range isn’t great. No one will argue that. Although I’ll say my personal experience driving the Taycan, and yes I pushed it and wasn’t attempting to conserve energy, took me beyond 201 miles.

The level of contempt from Tesla supporters has been one of the more fascinating reactions to the EPA estimate. It’s as if they’re being forced to buy a Taycan, which of course, they’re not.

If the end goal is for the industry to shift away from fossil fuels and towards battery electric vehicles, it might be time for folks to take a more nuanced view. A battle between two companies certainly simplifies things. But it’s hardly an accurate view of how the industry really works.

There will certainly be Taycan buyers. The vehicle performs wonderfully well and it’s one of the quietest cars I’ve driven. The bigger question, at least for me, is whether Porsche continues to invest and improve its battery tech for the Taycan and future EVs, like the upcoming electric Macan.

Two other company-generated posts got my attention this week.

Voyage, the autonomous vehicle company that has focused on retirement communities, released details in a Medium post on its “Safestop” feature. The company describes it as a supercharged automatic emergency braking system designed for driverless cars. The system operates independently from the other AV stack. I first reported on this feature in an Extra Crunch article several months ago. This Voyage post provides more detail on the system.

Meanwhile, Cruise CEO Dan Ammann took what would seem like a surprising stance for someone whose previous role was at GM. The Medium post railed against our car centric society and the fatalities, traffic congestion and economic problems it has created. While he noted that public transit should be part of the solution, it was clear Ammann was building up towards a different solution: AVs.

“To make order-of-magnitude — rather than incremental — improvements in transportation, we need to build alternatives that are superior to the status quo in every way,” he wrote.

Beyond robotaxis

Robotaxis have been the focus of the broader effort to develop autonomous vehicle technology, largely because it’s viewed as the best business model. But there is so much more opportunity with AV technology beyond shuttling people from Point A to Point B.

I plan to highlight more companies that are on the fringes of the AV industry. Scaled Robotics, which won the Startup Battlefield at 2019 Disrupt Berlin, is one of these companies.

The company has designed a robot that can navigate the chaos of construction sites and produce 3D progress maps in minutes that are precise enough to detect that a beam or other components of a building is just a centimeter or two off.

The company’s robot autonomously roves construction sites, using a 360-degree camera and custom lidar system to systematically document its surroundings. The robot’s key feature is an object recognition system that allows it to know the difference between a constructed wall and a piece of sheet rock leaned against it, or a staircase and temporary stairs for electric work.

The information is compared to a source CAD model of the building. Software generates a precise picture of the progress being made so a construction crew can easily identify problem areas.
