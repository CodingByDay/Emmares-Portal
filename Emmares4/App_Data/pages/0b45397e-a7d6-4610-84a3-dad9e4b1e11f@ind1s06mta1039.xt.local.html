UK cases drop

The number of people in the UK testing positive for covid-19 has fallen for seven consecutive days, the first such sustained fall since November. This may represent a turning point, but experts warn there are many other explanations for the decline that cannot be ruled out. Most agree that it is unlikely that infections have peaked because we have reached the herd immunity threshold.

Cases may have fallen for other reasons. People may have been cautious in the face of rising cases, or socialising more outdoors. Large numbers of people being asked to self-isolate may also be driving transmission down. Another possibility is that cases aren’t actually falling but detection rates are. Government figures show that the number of tests being done has declined, dropping by 4.9 per cent in the week up to 22 July, the latest date for which figures are available. Read more

NHS warning: Despite the drop in cases, health providers in England have told ministers that the NHS is under severe pressure which is likely to increase.

Genetic secrets

A new, more complete version of the human genome is already bearing fruit after being released two months ago. It has revealed enormous amounts of genetic variation between people that couldn’t previously be detected – variation that may underlie diseases. “There were variants that were hiding in plain sight,” says Megan Dennis at the University of California, Davis.

Other studies suggest that the new genome will finally reveal the functions of seemingly useless “junk DNA”. This DNA is repetitive, which means it has proved difficult to study in detail before now because standard sequencing technology breaks up DNA into very small chunks that are difficult to piece together when the chunks contain repetitive genetic information and so look very similar. Read more

Aperture: Photography’s roots

Richard Learoyd
This image of a vase of poppies was taken by artist Richard Learoyd using an ancient device called a camera obscura. This is created by making a tiny hole in one wall of a room-sized chamber, which acts as a lens and projects an image directly onto photographic paper, without a film negative or digital technology. It is part of an exhibition tracing the history of photography from the 1840s to the 21st century through depictions of plants and botany, showing at Dulwich Picture Gallery, London, until 30 August. More images
