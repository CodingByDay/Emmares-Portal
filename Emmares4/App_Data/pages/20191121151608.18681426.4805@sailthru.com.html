Cigarettes are the leading cause of preventable death in the U.S. But reports of the death of smoking may have been greatly exaggerated.

This week, a regulatory proposal to cut nicotine in cigarettes to well below addictive levels disappeared from a list of long-term federal goals. The measure, proposed in 2017 by former FDA Commissioner Scott Gottlieb as part of an ambitious tobacco agenda, could have led to a very steep drop in sales of cigarettes. Stripped of their addictive qualities, cigarettes, after all, seem to have little to recommend them. One estimate projected the change could save millions of lives.

Now, it appears that won't be happening, or at least, not anytime soon. It's true that the government's efforts to stamp out smoking have already been incredibly successful: The CDC said just 13.7% of adults smoked in 2018, an all-time low. At the same time, however, regulators are grappling with a new nicotine threat in the form of vaping. They are trying to figure how to balance a surge in teen e-cigarette use against the potential that vaping could help those remaining adult smokers quit a deadly dangerous habit.

Vaping advocates say cracking down on their industry too hard could push smokers back to cigarettes -- and potentially put a new generation at risk. If regulators also shrink from the stricter cigarette rules they'd envisioned, those risks could be compounded. The dying embers of the cigarette wars may be ready to flare once again.—Tim Annett

Here's what else we're watching:

Rare coup. Alnylam won approval for a treatment for a rare metabolic disorder that will cost $575,000 a year. FDA greenlighted it months ahead of schedule; continuing a trend of early approvals for breakthrough drugs.

Researcher rebellion. Pfizer's tafamidis treatment for heart failure costs $651 a day. That price isn't sitting so well with some of the scientists who helped develop it.

Co-pay crunch. Medicare prescription plans are much slower to cover new generic drugs than private plans, according to an industry study, a sign that many seniors may be overpaying for their medicines.
