Our take on the latest developments

Across the world, the coronavirus has prompted an outpouring of communal support for health-care workers. Members of the public are stepping onto balconies or outside their front doors to applaud those on the front lines of the pandemic fight—much the way conquering armies or victorious sports teams were once feted.

Even U.K. Prime Minister Boris Johnson, who’s battling the virus himself, emerged from self-isolation for the first time on Thursday to join the national applause from the threshold of the prime minister’s residence.

People join in a national applause for the NHS from their doorsteps, windows and balconies to show their appreciation for all NHS workers, in London.

Photographer: Ian West/PA Images

It may seem churlish to raise questions about a touching gesture of solidarity for the new heroes of our era. But there’s a case to be made for staying inside and avoiding this subtly political act.

The biggest problems health-care workers face in battling the virus have nothing to do with a shortage of emotional support. What they really lack are the tools to do the job, including life-saving ventilators, sufficient intensive-care beds or even basics like face masks and protective clothing.

Even as hundreds of billions of dollars have flowed into biotechnology in recent years, funding the development of miracle drugs to treat non-communicable diseases, corners were cut on the more basic medical needs that could have helped health systems tackle a pandemic.

In countries like Italy and the U.K., the funding squeeze on health systems has been aggravated by a shortage of labor. Brexit and restrictions on immigration could worsen that in Britain by making it harder for the National Health Service to attract doctors and nurses from overseas.

Johnson has championed tough new immigration rules. He also vowed that Brexit would free up billions of pounds in funding for the NHS, a claim that proved to be exaggerated. Wracked by the virus, the health service will get more money. It opened an emergency hospital in London that could eventually treat 4,000 patients. But time was lost and the death toll is rising. Clapping may make us feel better, but we’re not the ones putting our lives on the line—Eric Pfanner
