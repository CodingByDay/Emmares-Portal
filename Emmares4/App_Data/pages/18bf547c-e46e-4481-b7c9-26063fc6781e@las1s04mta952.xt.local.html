Technology and Cybersecurity

ZOOM, REVISITED — Video conferencing company Zoom apologized on Thursday for falling short on privacy and security, and outlined its plans going forward. The company wasn't ready for the surge in usage, CEO Eric Yuan wrote in a blog post . He added Zoom would enact a freeze on rolling out new features to focus on security and privacy issues, prepare a transparency report, enhance its bug bounty program and more. It also further detailed its encryption practices on Wednesday.

Those steps have bought the company some goodwill from the cybersecurity world. Some had already been of the mind that Zoom's woes were overstated . A former NSA hacker who recently revealed two Zoom flaws offered kudos to the company for fixing them and for its security announcements. Johns Hopkins University cryptography professor Matthew Green said the company's claims about their encryption practices "are actually pretty good, although there are some open questions."

But states are unswayed, as concerns about Zoom persist, and multiply. "Multiple state attorneys general are banding together to scrutinize virtual conferencing company Zoom's privacy and security practices," our Tech colleague Cristiano Lima reports in a dispatch for Pros . "the biggest sign to date that its regulatory woes are ballooning as its popularity surges during the coronavirus outbreak." Connecticut Attorney General William Tong said late Thursday that he's "seeking more information from the company about its privacy and security measures in coordination with other state attorneys general." Sen. Richard Blumenthal (D-Conn.) told POLITICO on Thursday that he's discussed the company with several state AGs.

With U.S. government health agencies involved in coronavirus response signing $1.3 million in contracts with the company, the stakes are too high to ignore its problems. New issues are emerging nearly daily: Krebs on Security found a lack of password protections in major corporations' meetings; The New York Times reported the company was displaying LinkedIn data without users' permission; and Cyjax discovered a "Zoom Leaks" forum where members post IDs for unsecured meetings. And digital rights nonprofit Fight for the Future launched a campaign to keep the pressure on Zoom to implement end-to-end encryption.

RANSOMWARE GROUPS' PAUSE ON HEALTH CARE NOT HAPPENING — About two weeks ago, a number of ransomware operators promised not to attack health and medical organizations. So far, not so good; one group apparently broke its pledge the same day it made it. On Thursday, Bloomberg reported that a company working to discover antibodies for the coronavirus, 10x Genomics, suffered a ransomware attack in March.

Brett Callow, a threat analyst at Emsisoft, alerted MC to another attack: The REvil/Sodinokibi operators said it had held the National Eating Disorders Association's data for ransom. "If you refuse to negotiate with us, all documents will be published on (a blog) and published by the media," the group threatened. "If an agreement is reached, the data will be permanently deleted."

THE CONTRACTOR FACTOR — The Office of Management and Budget should issue uniform guidance for national security contractors during the coronavirus pandemic to protect those vital workers and avoid conflicting agency directives that create "uncertainty and instability in the contractor industrial base, if not a permanent loss of capability," Warner wrote to acting OMB Director Russ Vought on Thursday. A government-wide policy is necessary "to avoid draconian cutbacks that may create significant counterintelligence risks," said Warner, who represents a state that is home to many intelligence facilities and their federal and contract workers.

Warner said he wanted to see an OMB directive that "fully endorses and supports contractors teleworking or otherwise working remotely," consistent with their legal and mission obligations. Such guidance, he said, should cover all contractors, whether they work at government facilities or their own employers' offices, and whether they handle unclassified or classified missions. Warner also asked for OMB to promulgate clear guidance for prolonging, expanding and otherwise modifying contracts "to minimize unnecessary disruption in contract execution for the duration of the emergency."

BEEFING UP DEFENSES — Microsoft on Thursday announced that it would greatly expand its Defending Democracy program that protects elections and other democratic processes. "As the world battles the COVID-19 pandemic, we have seen, and others have reported, that nation states and cybercriminals are taking advantage of the crisis by using virus-themed phishing attacks and other techniques to attack critical institutions. We must assume they will use these techniques to target our elections as well," Jan Neutze, the program's senior director, wrote in a blog post.

The tech giant will offer a new service, called Election Security Advisors, that gives election campaigns and election officials the opportunity to assess their network vulnerabilities and take advantage of the company's incident response service. Microsoft will also expand free AccountGuard threat monitoring to campaigns and political parties and offer Microsoft 365 for Campaigns to anyone running for office and political committees at the state level.

GOOGLE WIELDING ITS VAST TROVES OF PHONE-TRACKING DATA IN VIRUS FIGHT — "Google will use its mammoth collection of mobile location data to measure whether people across the globe are following government directives to remain at home during the coronavirus pandemic — or are venturing out to locations such as stores, parks and pharmacies," Pro Technology's Steven Overly reports. "The Silicon Valley behemoth has begun publishing online reports in 131 countries that show whether the overall number of visitors to certain public venues has increased or decreased since coronavirus-era guidelines were put in place."

— Silicon Valley's leading tech giants have stepped up to the plate in recent weeks to use their power and deep pockets to help the world's war on the pandemic — but those same efforts have raised privacy concerns both in the short and long term. "Tensions between privacy and public health have already arisen in multiple countries as governments struggle to respond to the virus," Steven reports.

WHO'S ON SECOND — Iran-linked hackers targeted the personal email accounts of World Health Organization staffers starting on March 2 as the coronavirus outbreak raged, Reuters reported on Thursday. "To the best of our knowledge, none of these hacking attempts were successful," WHO spokesman Tarik Jasarevic said. Iran denied involvement. The story follows another Reuters report that cyberattacks on WHO doubled as coronavirus cases soared and that hackers had set up a malicious site that mimicked its internal email system.

COMMISSION SEEKS FEEDBACK ON GDPR — From our friends at POLITICO Europe's Cyber Insights: The European Commission launched a consultation this week on the General Data Protection Regulation. The contributions will feed into the report on the bloc's flagship privacy overhaul, due in early June, that will focus on international transfer of personal data to third countries and the cooperation and consistency mechanism between national data protection authorities. The consultation is open until April 29.
