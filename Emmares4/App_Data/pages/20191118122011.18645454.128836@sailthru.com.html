Money Stuff

Matt Levine

Aramco

One reason that a company might do a direct listing instead of a standard initial public offering is that it wants to sell its stock at a price set by the market rather than a price negotiated by investment bankers and big institutional shareholders. If you do an IPO, your investment bankers call up big investors and take orders and build a book of demand at some price, and then they come back to you and say “you can sell the stock you want to sell at $20” or whatever, and you do, and then the stock starts trading and usually it goes up 10 or 20% or so. Sometimes it goes down, though; this is not an exact science, and the bankers will never build up a perfect picture of demand that exactly matches the ultimate result in the market.

If you do a direct listing, on the other hand, your stock just gets listed on the stock exchange, and early investors can sell to new ones, and whatever price balances supply and demand is the price. You—the company—don’t sell stock in a direct listing, but the listing does create a price. And then if you want to sell stock a month later, you can: There’s generally no “lockup” in a direct listing, so you can choose to sell whenever the price seems good to you. As with any big secondary offering, if you sell stock it will probably price at a discount to the market price, but the market price will probably be the main reference point for investors thinking about buying the stock after the direct listing.

It is conceivable—I don’t know of any examples, but in theory it seems basically workable[1]—that a company that had no urgent need for money might start an IPO process, meet with bankers, feel out interest from big investors, get disappointing feedback (“you’re not going to be able to price this deal at a valuation above $15”), decide that the bankers and big investors undervalue the company, and scrap the IPO and go for a direct listing instead. You do the direct listing, the stock starts trading, it goes to $25, and then you go do a stock offering, as an already-public company, based on your existing trading price. You sell stock at $23 or whatever—a discount to the trading price, but above what the bankers thought they could get you in an IPO—and then you go to the bankers and say “see we told you.”

One risk here is that you’re wrong and in fact the stock is worth $15, or $10 for that matter; once you do the direct listing the stock might trade down and then you’ll never be able to sell stock at the price you wanted. A subtler risk is that the stock really is worth $25 when you do the direct listing, because only a small amount of stock comes loose in the direct listing, and the demand for that small amount of stock is strong.[2] But if you want to sell a large amount of stock, you’ll need to get more big investors and sell at a much lower price; supply and demand determine the price of the stock, the demand curve slopes down, and the price of the stock after a small initial listing doesn’t tell you much about the price of the stock in a large public offering. That is a real risk. Still, the public trading price of a stock is often a pretty good indication of what people will pay for it, a good starting point and anchor for their valuation. 

I certainly don’t think that this is a complete model of the Saudi Aramco IPO, but it is probably how I’d start to think about it. Saudi Arabia wanted to sell some of its stake in Aramco to big reputable international institutional investors at a valuation of $2 trillion. The investors said “no thanks.” Or, I mean, they were never exactly asked directly, but informal pre-roadshow inquiries by Aramco’s banks suggested that it would be hard to get a lot of demand from international institutions at anywhere near that price. Saudi Arabia was not happy:

Saudi Arabian officials have told bankers they are unhappy with the level of foreign demand for Saudi Aramco, adding to pressure ahead of an investor roadshow due to start on Sunday.

Advisers informed officials this week about a gulf in demand between domestic investors and foreign institutions in the run-up to the much-delayed initial public offering, said two people familiar with the process.

“The issuer expressed high levels of dissatisfaction with what they were hearing,” said one of these people.

Financiers are increasingly confident of bumper domestic demand at a valuation of $1.8tn or higher, banking on a retail allocation above the 0.5 per cent earmarked in the prospectus. Local investors, some of whom have been pressured to invest, have been encouraged to bid at these higher levels.

In recent weeks, institutional investors have told bankers that $1.2tn to $1.5tn is more realistic.

One thing that Aramco could do with that information would be to go out on the roadshow, try to convince investors, hope to get to $2 trillion and be willing to risk pulling the IPO in shame if it failed. Another thing it could do would be to go out on the roadshow, try to convince investors, hope to get to $2 trillion and be willing to risk pricing at like $1.2 trillion if it failed.

But the thing that Aramco actually seems to be doing is cancelling much of the international roadshow, giving up on trying to convince big foreign institutions of a $2 trillion valuation, but listing the stock anyway in Saudi Arabia. By “listing the stock anyway” I mean selling a tiny fraction of it to local Saudi investors at a pretty high valuation, with that valuation being driven by some combination of (1) scarcity, (2) local enthusiasm for a national-champion company, (3) extra leverage for retail investors and (4) maybe implicit threats against rich Saudis who don’t put in orders at a high valuation. Specifically:

Saudi Aramco canceled the London leg of its initial public offering roadshow, scheduled for Wednesday, according to people familiar with the situation, who asked not be named because it wasn’t a public event.

It’s the latest sign that the giant Saudi oil company has scaled back the international ambitions for the IPO because of lackluster demand from money managers. The company had already decided not to market the share sale in the U.S., Canada or Japan.

The deal will now mainly rely on rich Saudi families and other local investors.

And:

Aramco will sell just 1.5% of its shares on the local stock exchange, about half the amount that had been considered, and seek a valuation of between $1.6 trillion and $1.71 trillion.

One possibility is that the deal will price at $1.6 or $1.7 trillion and trade down, and then in a year or so Saudi Arabia will sell more shares to U.S. and European institutions at $1.2 trillion or whatever. But certainly another possibility is that this small 1.5% stub of shares, sold to enthusiastic levered local investors and listed solely on the local exchange, will trade quite well. “With the offer price putting Aramco’s maximum valuation at about $1.7 trillion, there should be room for investors to make some money, said one local investor.”

And if it does trade well among local investors for a while, Aramco can go back to the international markets to sell more, and it can point to the existing trading price as a data point. “See, people are buying it at $2 trillion, so you should too,” that sort of thing. I don’t know if that will work; the factors that drive the local price up—scarcity, enthusiasm, threats—won’t work as well if Saudi Arabia sells a bunch more shares to international investors. But a rough theory of “if people see a trading price, they anchor their valuations to that price” does not strike me as crazy. And if you know that big investors aren’t going to pay up now, doing a small tightly controlled listing and seeing what happens doesn’t really cost you much.

Bond pricing

On the other hand, any capital markets banker will tell you that the way to get a high price in a securities offering is to start marketing the securities with a low price, build up a lot of investor enthusiasm, and then raise the price to meet all of that demand. To be clear, the reason that bankers will tell you this is that it makes their job easier: If they market your deal with a low price, then they are more likely to find enough demand to get the deal done, and if they come to you with a deal at that low price you’re going to have a hard time saying no. (You agreed to market the deal at the low price!) The strategy might work as described—they might get so much demand at the low price that they’ll be able to raise the price above what they could have done if they’d just started at a high price—but the real value of the approach, to the bankers, is that it increases the odds of them getting some deal done and getting paid.

Sometimes it works though:

It was a big day for the bond pros at BNP Paribas SA - they were selling 1 billion euros ($1.1 billion) of the bank’s own debt to a market hungry for their product. So it’s no wonder buyers piled in when the notes were offered at a substantial discount.

As the morning wore on and the bids poured in, the price kept rising. By the time terms were set at noon, the discount was virtually gone.

“Everyone knows the game,” said Suki Mann, former head of credit strategy at UBS Group AG and founder of the CreditMarketDaily newsletter. “Banks make deals cheap enough to get investors in, then, once they’ve got huge books, they ratchet up the price.”

That’s from an article titled “Bond Investors Fume at Price Talk Some Call ‘Bait and Switch,’” and ridiculously the investors are mad that they can’t buy bonds cheap just because others are willing to pay more for them:

Investors squeezed by ultra-low yields are getting fed up -- one fund manager complained to BNP Paribas after the June 25 issue. The data confirm their impressions: the compelling yields that pique their interest at the outset are increasingly vanishing in the marketing.

“It’s gotten much, much worse,” said Matthias Muth, a bond dealer at MRM Securities near Frankfurt and a veteran with more than 35 years in the industry. …

Come on, man! If you are interested at a high yield but not at a lower one, you can pull your order when the yield goes down during marketing. If you’re still interested at the lower yield that clears the market, but you would rather be getting an above-market yield, just … like … why? That is not how markets are supposed to work. “For their part, bankers wonder what the fuss is about. They say pricing reflects nothing more sinister than supply and demand.”

Due diligence

There are some pretty obvious lessons to be learned from this story with the amazing title “FBI tried to warn BofA against ill-fated buyout.” I guess the main one is that if you work at a big bank that has agreed to provide the financing for a buyout of a company, and the Federal Bureau of Investigation calls you up and says “hey FYI that company is a big fraud,” you should … tell … someone? I do not know what happened here:

The FBI tried to warn Bank of America against an ill-fated leveraged buyout led by former Blackstone dealmaker Chinh Chu, telling an executive that “the entire transaction was setting alarms off, and there was a concern that someone was attempting to defraud” the bank. 

BofA faces steep potential losses after ignoring the warnings about now-bankrupt Constellation Healthcare Technologies, which are detailed in FBI files made public by a federal court last week. …

A week before the Constellation acquisition closed, an FBI agent and a Department of Justice lawyer called a member of BofA’s fraud team, according to an official note of the meeting that lawyers for Constellation’s former chief executive say they received from prosecutors. … 

Law enforcement officials alerted BofA to public records showing that Constellation had acquired a series of companies just days after they were created and valued the questionable entities at as much as $20m, according to the documents.

A DoJ official “expressed concern that Bank of America may not be seeing the same thing,” the FBI memorandum states, “and suggested [the bank] ensure due diligence on the transaction”.

But “BofA provided a $130m loan to finance the acquisition” anyway, and “Constellation filed for bankruptcy last year.” The Securities and Exchange Commission and the Justice Department have brought fraud charges against Constellation’s executives for allegedly inflating its value by acquiring more or less fictitious companies. Oops!

One possibility here (“BofA declined to comment”) is that the BofA fraud person was really busy that day and just plain forgot about this FBI call until after the bankruptcy, oh well. That would be weird! Another possibility is that BofA was like “thanks FBI, we’ll look into that,” and the fraud people went to the deal team and were like “what’s all this about Constellation acquiring fictitious companies to inflate its revenue,” and the deal team were like “oh no that is standard in every acquisition, the FBI are just a little naive here, it’s fine,” and the fraud people were satisfied with that answer. That would also be weird, but … possible? Like presumably the deal team had already done some amount of due diligence on Constellation, and reviewed its books and records and financial accounts and gotten comfortable with what they said, and was not going to be thrown off by the FBI saying “hey some of these companies are a little irregular.” The FBI were just late-coming kibbitzers pointing BofA to public documents; the deal team knew what was up. I mean, it turns out they didn’t, but they could reasonably have thought they did.

Even if BofA did get itself comfortable despite the FBI call, it really should have told the buyer, Chinh Chu’s CC Capital. Did it?

A spokesperson for CC Capital said: “At the close of the transaction, CC Capital was not aware of any FBI or law enforcement interest in or investigation of CHT or the transaction.”

Hmm. But I want to move beyond the obvious lessons to a much less intuitive one. The news of the FBI investigation came out in court filings by lawyers for Paul Parmar, Constellation’s founder and chief executive officer, as part of his defense against the fraud charges. The defense is, roughly: Since the FBI knew that the company was a fraud, and since it told the buyers, no one could have been defrauded.

Mr Parmar’s lawyers have seized on the FBI’s warning to BofA in an effort to discredit the charges, arguing that “one simply cannot defraud a party who is in actual possession of accurate information and who insists on proceeding in spite of that knowledge”.

Man! This glorious piece of chutzpah has been a consistent line of defense; here’s an article from last year:

In the court filings, Mr Parmar insisted that Mr Chu was aware the bolt-on acquisitions identified by FT Alphaville were in fact empty corporate shells, despite Constellation having raised scores of millions of pounds via Aim share placements to buy them.

“Yes sure we did fake acquisitions but you weren’t defrauded because we told you about it before signing the deal” is, uh, sure, probably a defense. “Yes sure we did fake acquisitions but you weren’t defrauded because the FBI told you about it before signing the deal” is, on the other hand, an absolutely amazing defense. 

WeKnowEverythingIsSecuritiesFraud

Oh well I mean sure:

WeWork is drawing scrutiny from the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission over whether the co-working company violated financial rules in the run-up to its failed initial public offering, according to two people with knowledge of the matter.

The agency’s enforcement division is reviewing WeWork’s business and its disclosures to investors amid a number of news articles that highlighted potential conflicts of interest and the company’s aggressive fundraising, the people said. WeWork has retained Andrew Ceresney, a top Wall Street lawyer who previously headed the SEC enforcement unit, according to the people.

I want to make a small point of legal realism here, which is that WeWork’s failed initial public offering is one of the biggest and funniest financial stories of the year, and people who work at the Securities and Exchange Commission are humans who live in the world and are aware of how big and funny the WeWork story is. If you’re an SEC enforcement lawyer looking around for things to enforce, and you have a choice of​​​​​​ going after

  1. a high-profile hilarious fiasco, or
  2. anything else,

what would you choose? If you go after the high-profile hilarious fiasco, you will get your name in the paper, and also you can make jokes about it that will be widely quoted. You don’t get those pleasures out of an accounting dispute at some boring industrial company.

Last year’s high-profile hilarious fiasco was of course when Elon Musk briefly pretended that he was going to take Tesla Inc. private. When it was first reported that the SEC was investigating that, I wrote:

Of course the SEC is looking into this. I can’t think of a thing that the SEC would look into more. If Warren Buffett was giving insider tips about accounting fraud at the Fed to Lloyd Blankfein so that he could help Donald Trump and the pope insider trade against the Illuminati, the SEC team investigating that would be scheming to get transferred to the Elon Musk team. The Elon Musk team is already shopping for the commemorative flamethrowers they’ll get when they finish. 

Same here, ceteris paribus. Commemorative kombucha taps. A commemorative private jet filled with commemorative weed.

The moral here is … don’t have a huge high-profile embarrassing debacle? I don’t know, you probably already knew that. But there is a sort of Matthew Effect where if you have a high-profile embarrassing debacle, every debacle investigator is going to be lining up to make your debacle a bit worse. It is not exactly a model of efficient deterrence or anything, but it is fun for them

Now, the thing about Tesla is that Musk tweeted false things that affected the price of its publicly traded stock. That is pretty straightforwardly securities fraud, and eventually Musk settled with the SEC and paid a fine (without admitting guilt). The thing about WeWork’s failed IPO is that it never went public, so it had no publicly traded stock to affect; even if you think that its IPO prospectus was inadequate I don’t think that the SEC is going to go after it for, like, attempted securities fraud. It had bonds outstanding, though; maybe the disclosure to bondholders was also somehow inadequate. And it had private stock investors, some of whom have already sued. It will take more effort to find securities fraud at WeWork than it would at a public company, but I’m sure a lot of the SEC’s best minds will be on the case.

WeWork is preparing to cut at least 4,000 people from its work force as it tries to stabilize itself after the company’s breakneck growth racked up heavy losses and led it to the brink of collapse, two people with knowledge of the matter said.

Dating

Here, via Tyler Cowen, is a paragraph by Dan McMurtrie on online dating:

The age dynamic creates significant inter-age cohort competition in the female population and increased overall competition in the male population. This can be conceptualized as the market becoming more efficient, which naturally leads to many market participants anecdotally expressing unhappiness with the status quo as they incorrectly identify an inability to produce low effort excess returns as the circumstances being “unfair.” Basically, the same thing is happening in the dating market as is happening in the Hedge Fund market: things are getting more efficient, very few are pleased about it, and there are lots of strange advice books, blogs, and videos coming out.

I write about that dynamic in hedge funds from time to time. (I wrote about it above under “Bond pricing,” for that matter.) Sometimes it seems to me that a good unified theory of modern society’s anxieties might be “everything is too efficient and it’s exhausting.”
