Expect the peak in COVID-19 infections in the U.S. to be around Labor Day. New cases are rising fast at 110,000 per day. The Southeast part of the U.S. and especially the Gulf Coast is getting hit hard. But evidence from the U.K., which got hit with a Delta surge back in June, is that the infection rate peaks in about six to eight weeks, and then retreats. Missouri was the first state to see a surge in Delta cases back in mid-June, and may be at its peak now. If its rate holds this next week or two, it will show whether the U.K. experience is likely to be replicated here in the U.S. One positive from the U.K. experience is that while hospitalizations rose, the death rate stayed low. Hospitalizations have risen fast in the U.S., again straining bed capacity in some places, but so far the death rate has stayed low. But since deaths tend to lag hospitalizations, the month of August will prove whether this will be the case or not here in the U.S.

The fast peak in infections may make the debate over mask mandates in schools moot. Policies are being set in place now, with many school districts requiring them, but the expected decline in the rate this fall may allow a relaxation soon after school starts. Parents of young children have shown the greatest resistance to mask mandates in schools. It is likely that an expected approval of vaccines for children under 12 sometime this fall will also help to lessen the contentious debates.

John Stoltzfus, chief investment strategist at Oppenheimer Asset Management, reiterated his firm’s bullishness in his Monday “Market Strategy Radar Screen,” asserting that Oppenheimer continues to favor equities and remains overweight U.S. stocks at the moment. However, unlike many strategists that have expressed a clear preference in the value-vs.-growth debate, Stoltzfus seems to think the path forward involves a little of Column A, a little of Column B. “For style, we prefer a barbell approach with both value and growth in what is likely to remain a low interest rate environment, even after yields move higher in response to what we expect will be a sustainable economic expansion stateside that will feed a global economic recovery,” he says. Indeed, Stoltzfus espouses truly having it all, suggesting that dividend portfolios would do well to include GARP (growth at a reasonable price) stocks, “particularly for investors seeking current income with potential for capital gains.”
