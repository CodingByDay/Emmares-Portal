Hi. I don’t know about where you live, but round my way, I’m noticing a growing number of Nissan Leafs, Renault Zoes, Tesla Model 3s and other electric cars.

A report out today suggests this is a global phenomenon, led by Europe and China. Almost 1 in 20 cars sold globally last year were electric, the International Energy Agency’s research found.

Read this week’s Fix the Planet to find out what’s driving this surge, what the IEA thinks will happen next and why it isn’t just electric cars but battery two-wheelers and buses that are making inroads.
An "electric forecourt" that opened in the UK last year. Photo: Chris Ratcliffe/Bloomberg via Getty Images

Where does electric vehicle take-up stand?
Timur Gül at the IEA says the global growth in electric vehicles (EVs) last year was remarkable. Many expected electric car sales to collapse in the face of the pandemic – which overall car sales did, down 16 per cent – but battery-powered ones continued soaring. Around 3 million were registered in 2020, taking their market share to 4.6 per cent. There are now just over 10 million on roads worldwide, out of a global stock of more than a billion cars. Jacopo Tattini at the IEA says China and Europe provided the backbone of last year’s growth. And for the first time, EV sales in Europe overtook those in China.

Why are they thriving in Europe?
“It’s difficult to single out reasons, but I would say there are three general pillars,” says Gül. The first is existing policies by European governments. For example, 2020 was the EU deadline for car-makers to make 95 per cent of the cars they sold emit 95 grams of carbon dioxide per kilometre on average, which is almost impossible without selling more electric models. Failure would mean fines. Secondly, several countries, notably Germany, France and Italy, put in place extra incentives for buying EVs. Lastly, Gül says there is now cachet among European drivers to own one, especially in cities ( many of which still suffer illegal levels of air pollution from diesel cars). “Undoubtedly, there is a certain appeal,” he says. The US is lagging, but president Joe Biden’s new carbon plan last week made it clear that electric cars will be key.

What does this mean for carbon emissions?
We know electric cars are lower carbon than ones with combustion engines, even taking into account battery manufacture and the dirty electric grids in some countries. Nonetheless, the CO2 savings today from electric vehicles are fairly small. The IEA estimates that about 50 million tonnes more CO2 would have been emitted in 2020  if they were petrol and diesel vehicles, roughly Hungary’s annual emissions. Interestingly, most of the savings come from two and three-wheeled vehicles (more on that in a second).

The number of electric cars on roads globally. Green is China, blue Europe, orange United States and grey the rest of the world. Photo: IEA

What is going on with chargers?
As readers without driveways have pointed out, these matter. There are now 1.3 million publicly accessible chargers globally, a third of them fast-charging ones. However, installation rates were only up 45 per cent year-on-year in 2020, compared with a rise of 85 per cent in 2019. The IEA thinks that is probably due to disruption from covid-19. Still, some cool charging projects came online last year, not least the UK’s first “electric forecourt”, a futuristic, solar panel-clad site in Essex dedicated to fast-charging EVs.

What is the story beyond cars?
Despite companies such as Volvo and Tesla making bold promises on electric lorries, it’s still super early days. The total number on the world’s roads grew to just 31,000 last year. Electric buses are a different story. There are now 600,000 globally, almost all of them in China. The IEA also points out that electric car numbers are dwarfed by another category: electric two and three-wheeled vehicles such as electric motorcycles. There are now 25 million globally, and 60 per cent of two and three-wheelers sold in China last year were electric.

What about cost?
Tattini says the major reason EVs remain more costly upfront than fossil fuel-powered vehicles is their battery. “That is still expensive even if costs are rapidly declining,” he says. He expects price parity for EVs and internal combustion engine vehicles will arrive by 2030. But the total cost of ownership – in other words, also considering the price of refuelling and charging vehicles – “will come much earlier”, he believes. It is worth noting that other car industry watchers think the upfront cost of EVs might reach price parity earlier than the IEA thinks. Bloomberg NEF expects that point to arrive in the mid-2020s.

So what next?
EVs have continued in the fast lane this year. Global sales in the first quarter were up around 140 per cent compared with the same period in 2020. China and Europe are leading again on absolute numbers, although US sales doubled. Tattini says governments’ covid-19 recovery plans provide an opportunity for a further boost, and there are already signs of charging infrastructure and battery factories featuring heavily in plans.

The IEA projects the number of EVs to grow from 10 million today to 145 million by 2030. But to meet the climate goals of the Paris Agreement, that will need to be 230 million EVs. What does that mean for power? The vehicles today are a minnow in terms of electricity demand, consuming about 80 terawatt-hours of the 20,000 TWh produced each year. If there are enough cars by 2030 to meet the Paris climate goals, that will leap to around 860 TWh, about 2 per cent of worldwide electricity consumption.

By then, the CO2 savings of this transition will be significant. The IEA reckons net savings of 410 million tonnes of CO2 if there are 230 million EVs by 2030– that’s more than is emitted by the UK, the world’s fifth biggest economy.
