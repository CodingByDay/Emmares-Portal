Hi – It’s Giles in London. Facebook Inc. lost a court case Monday against John de Mol, a Dutch billionaire who was annoyed that the social media giant failed to stop fraudsters pumping out fake Bitcoin ads that featured his face alongside promises of untold crypto-riches.

The judge agreed. People who responded to the ads lost their investments, which hurt de Mol’s reputation because the victims believed he was involved in the ads.

I guess this is the right decision. De Mol is the guy who invented the reality TV series ‘Big Brother’ – which is like a newer version of MTV’s Real World. We all have reputations to maintain.

During the court case, Facebook boasted that it was developing innovations to stop scammers’ actions, such as a library that lists which accounts are responsible for which ads. During the hearing, it even said it could divulge advertisers’ details when provided with a court order.

This may seem like Facebook is being helpful, but a court order every time you want to know who is using your face without permission seems like a trial in itself. A better idea – de Mol argued – is to apply a filter that would block any future ads containing his name or portrait alongside anything to do with cryptocurrencies.

Silicon Valley is built on the premise that technology can solve problems, as long as tech founders get to choose the problem. Facebook’s lawyers didn’t like being told to use technology to solve de Mol’s problem. They argued that if you build such a filter, it wouldn’t be flawless, and could block legitimate advertisements.

Facebook’s Dutch argument came down to the premise that technology isn’t perfect – which is a weird position for a tech company to make. Be it fake ads or fake news – Facebook doesn’t really want to take sides.

If this is starting to sound familiar, that’s because it is.

Facebook’s executives for years have said they’re uncomfortable choosing what’s true and false. It took accusations of foreign states influencing the 2016 U.S. presidential elections for Facebook to come up with a political ad library, and even that isn’t exactly transparent.

In the end, the court agreed with Facebook. It said that creating such a filter might indeed be really hard work. But then it ordered Facebook to build it anyway. In fact, every day there is a scam post using the image of de Mol, Facebook must pay 10,000 euros ($11,000) up to a maximum of 1 million euros.

The court’s reply was simple. You’re a tech company. Write some code and sort it out. --Giles Turner
