Dispatches from Tel Aviv

Mobility in Tel Aviv reminds me a lot of this sandwich. (pictured below). It’s colorful, varied and all mixed up. It’s a bit messy at times. In short: there’s a lot going on. But somehow, despite the traffic jams, less than favorable public transit and unreliable ride-hailing, it all seems to work.

That’s partly due to an assortment of single occupancy modes of transportation such as scooters, bikes, mopeds, motorcycles and hover boards that allow people to piece together their commute. (Although it should be noted that two people riding a stand-up scooter is a common sight here.)

Micromobility is perhaps the biggest visible change on the streets of Tel Aviv since my last visit 18 months ago. Back then, there were scooters and mopeds. But they were all privately owned. Shared, dockless scooter companies like Bird and Lime showed up a few months later.

The infrastructure isn’t perfect and the Gett drivers complain, but in certain parts of the city there are dedicated paths for bikes and scooters that keep them separated from cars. Everywhere else, it’s a bit of free-for-all.

There is an effort underway to improve mobility options for the people who live and visit Tel Aviv. It was one of the underlying themes at this year’s Smart Mobility Summit, one of two really big Israeli events dedicated to transportation and startups. (The other one is EcoMotion Week, which will be held in spring).

The two-day summit, which is hosted by Israel’s Prime Minister, had an array of speakers, demos and startups. The big themes were shared, electric and autonomous along with related fields like sensors, cyber security and fleet management. Ideas for how mobility companies could better cater to segments of the population who observe Shabbat also came up.

And then of course, there’s venture. It’s everywhere and in every form in Tel Aviv these days. The venture arms of just about every major OEM has set up shop in Tel Aviv, most of which are located in one of two hotspots in the city. And they were all at the Smart Mobility Summit.

For instance, one company at the summit was Fleetonomy. I mention them because they’re an example of an Israeli startup that has attracted the attention and investment of OEMs, in this case, Jaguar Land Rover’s venture capital and mobility services arm InMotion Ventures. Fleetonomy and InMotion recently announced a partnership to launch and operate a premium chauffeur service in London called Havn.

In short: investors are here en masse in search of talent and tech. If you’re not here yet, well you’re missing out. That doesn’t mean investing here is easy or a slam dunk. It’s been a hard year for hardware startups, with Oryx Vision the latest startup to close up shop.

Micromobbin'

A few weeks ago, I wrote about some job listings that suggested Revel, the electric shared moped company was expanding into Texas. The job listings said Texas City, which seemed like an odd location. It turns out, the city is actually Austin.

The company announced Friday it launched a fleet of 1,000 electric, mopeds in Austin. The mopeds can be rented by licensed drivers over the age of 21 who pass an initial safe driving record background check. Revel’s Access program will also be offered in Austin, which gives riders who qualify for any local or federally administered assistance to receive. 40% discount on riding and a $25 credit.

With the expansion to Austin, Revel now has more than 2,400 mopeds in three U.S. cities. That’s a big number when you consider the company just launched in 2018.

Revel first launched in Brooklyn and Queens and later expanded to Washington D.C.   Each Revel comes with two U.S. Department of Transportation-certified helmets and can carry up to two riders. Revels are limited to local streets and are capped at a speed of 30 miles per hour. Rides cost $1 per person to start, followed by $0.25 per minute to ride and $0.10 per minute while parked.

The company announced in October that it raised $27.6 million in a Series A round led by Ibex Investors. The equity round included newcomer Toyota AI Ventures and further investments from Blue Collective, Launch Capital and Maniv Mobility.

Hailing a driverless Waymo

Just before Israel, TechCrunch contributor Ed Niedermeyer got an exclusive ride in a driverless Waymo Chrysler Pacifica minivan in the Phoenix suburb of Chandler.

I went along to check out the ride as well as Waymo’s operations center. You can read both of Ed’s stories here, which includes an Extra Crunch piece that examines how the hardest part of Waymo’s 10-year pursuit of autonomous vehicles might just be getting people to enjoy the ride.

Waymo’s UX challenge is getting people to enjoy the ride

While Ed was interviewing folks, I took stock of Waymo’s operations center (the original one, not the new spot in Mesa). A lot has changed since I was here in 2016. Waymo has expanded in every way, including people, space and vehicles.

Dozens of self-driving Chrysler Pacifica minivans were neatly parked in the warehouse-like structure. Nearby, sat the old Firefly prototype. And there was a constant flow of safety drivers coming in to take their vehicles out for testing or as part of Waymo’s ride-hailing service.

The upshot: Everything I saw here pointed to a company in hyper growth mode.
