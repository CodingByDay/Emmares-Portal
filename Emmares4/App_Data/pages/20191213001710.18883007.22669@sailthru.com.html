FOMC: Love, Peace and Harmony

The last Federal Open Market Committee of 2019 was also the least interesting. The Fed’s chieftains of monetary policy largely agree with each other, they also agree on where monetary policy is going next, and the markets agree with them. Many feel that the “dot plot” which shows the interest rate predictions of each FOMC member has outlived its usefulness. But it does at least show spectacular unity:

Looking further out, the great majority of the FOMC expect no change to target rates next year. There are only four dissenters, and they only expect one hike. The market still disagrees on that, with fed funds futures signaling one rate cut next year, but no more. That cut is thought unlikely until July. So everyone is expecting a period of calm.

Meanwhile Jerome Powell also made sure to underline the Fed’s current dovishness in his press conference, stressing risks to the downside rather than the upside. This time he avoided any slips or surprises. That made for a very quiet day on the stock market, which perhaps shows that dealers had taken a lenient Fed for granted. However, the reaction in the currency markets was more interesting. The dollar had one of its worst days in a while, and is now as far below its 200-day moving average (according to the trade-weighted index kept by Bloomberg) as in July, before a presidential tweet escalated the trade war rhetoric:

As the dollar has been buoyed by “safe haven” demand, this shows that tensions are alleviating fast. That is strange when the major event risks of the U.K. election (on which more later) and the decision on whether to impose tariffs on China lie in the immediate future. But it makes a lot of people happy. A weak dollar is wholeheartedly desired by the U.S. administration, and also reduces the pressure on emerging markets. And there now seems to be unanimity that the Fed will stay on pause for a while, and that there will be no need for any great return for risk aversion.

The only real risk in such a united scenario is that it creates the ideal conditions for policymakers and currency traders to step on a rake. After a blissfully quiet afternoon with Jay Powell, I think we should all treasure the gloriously curmudgeonly response of Steve Blitz, U.S. economist for TS Lombard:

History has shown that whenever the Fed is pretty unanimous leaning in one direction, the economy/inflation is about to zig in the other direction. Fading the Fed when it is so certain has rarely been a bad trade.

And with that concludes the shortest note on an FOMC meeting that I have written in a long time. Thanks, Jay.

 

The Rain Falls Down on a Humdrum Town….

Stop me if you think you’ve heard this one before. The U.K. is about to go to the polls, and foreign exchange traders are behaving as though the result is a foregone conclusion.  Much has been written about the election, and much more will be written once the polling stations close at 10 p.m., U.K. time. Many hours later, we should know the outcome. Writing 24 hours before the polling stations close, it looks as though the Conservatives will probably have a narrow working majority, but that the risks to the pound will be firmly to the downside.

If we believe the betting sites, the chance of an overall Conservative majority is a little above 70%. Polls suggest that Labour is a long way behind, but is closing with far stronger momentum thanks in large part to a disastrous few days of campaigning by the Conservative prime minister, Boris Johnson. Although undoubtedly a charming man, Johnson is an Etonian who may end up looking simply too elitist for voters who normally support Labour to stomach. The choral piece currently being rehearsed by the choir of Balliol College, Oxford, his alma mater, shows that many doubt his veracity. 

Another critical issue is that many voters remain undecided, and that turnout could be critical. It is decades since Britain last asked the electorate to turn out to vote in December, and unfortunately the national weather forecast for election day is dreadful. This is the U.K. Meteorological Office’s weather map for noon on election day:

Even by British standards, it looks like being a horrible December day. I have no idea which party a low turnout would favor; the important point is that political analysts and foreign exchange traders have no idea either. Add to the weather the fact that many British voters are inclined to ask what difference their vote will make. The Brexit imbroglio may have been great entertainment, but it has left an angry, embittered and divided populace in its wake, which will leave many people feeling that nobody deserves their vote. This must increase the uncertainty surrounding the vote.

And yet this is how the pound has performed compared to the euro since early October when Johnson took a famous walk in the woods with his Irish counterpart Leo Varadkar and came up with a compromise Brexit plan. The rally continues, barely dented by Johnson’s narrowing poll lead of the last few days:

I wouldn’t question that a Conservative majority would be far more market-friendly than the likely alternative of a messy coalition led by the aggressively ideological faction that currently runs the Labour party. But it looks as though foreign exchange traders are taking a lot for granted. They are also remarkably forgetful. The last two British national votes, the Brexit referendum of 2016 and the general election of the following year, both resulted in market-unfriendly surprises and sharp falls for sterling. Remarkably, the pound goes into this election stronger than it was before the election of 2017. Doubtless currency traders hope that this time they will at last get what they want. But it does seem a lot to ask.
