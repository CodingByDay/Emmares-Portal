It's the sugariest, "don't make me wear a turtleneck under my costume"-est time of year again. If the real world doesn't feel scary enough day-to-day, Halloween 2019 arrives this week to consolidate society's spookiness. (Your candy crusades are likely statistically safe, at least.)

 

To be honest, we're mild Halloween participants at best around Ars. We'll queue up a classic horror film, sure, but dressing up or heading to costume parties hasn't been an annual occurrence as of late. Instead, we celebrate by maybe firing up an appropriately ghostly game (Luigi's Mansion, anyone?) or maybe by creating a mincemeat tribute to Barb and re-binging Stranger Things 3. 

 

 

But this week's Orbital Transmission is for everyone else, the enthusiastic Halloween supporters that may still need a little costuming creativity. We've envisioned plenty of appropriately spooky (or satirical... mostly satirical) getups for 2019 merely from keeping up on the news. Hopefully, these are so easy to execute you can save your Elliot Alderson hoodies for your next Comic Con.  

 

—@NathanMattise

Orbital Transmission 10.30.2019
 

You can always dress as a ghost—of a polio strain, of the White House infosec team, et al

An old sheet with two eyeholes might be the oldest, most lo-fi Halloween costume in history. But the news continually provides new ghost inspirations, and this week is no different. First, modern medicine scored another big victory with health officials confirming that one crippling strain of Polio has been wiped out, moving humanity one step closer to completely eradicating the highly infectious virus from the planet. But if you prefer a less triumphant ghost, look to DC. Despite an election rapidly approaching and raising the likelihood of targeted digital attacks, the White House decided it doesn't need as robust of an infosec team. The chief of the White House's computer network defense branch—who wrote a memo after submitting his resignation—warned that the White House was likely headed toward another network compromise and theft of data.

 

Congressional testimony Zuckerberg should be the costume of the year

Every craft store worth its salt sells those giant googly eyes, right? Snag 'em, queue up the nearest suit, and prepare to be robotic and evasive for the remainder of the night. The Facebook CEO is becoming quite familiar with Capitol Hill given his repeat appearances over the last year-plus, but his latest visit was particularly "deer-in-the-headlights." Ostensibly testifying about Facebook's cryptocurrency efforts, Zuckerberg had to account for a lot more during his chat with the House Financial Services Committee. For a sense of how it went, here's a quote from Rep, Joyce Beatty (D-Ohio): "In your opening statement, you talked a lot about civil rights. I think we should probably phrase it a little differently: that your work with civil rights work is a result of the number of lawsuits you've had."

 

Pick your favorite Watchmen (HBO version)

Dressing like Rorschach is so mid 80s. But with HBO and Damon Lindelof's Watchmen adaptation now two episodes in, it's safe to say the universe has been pleasantly expanded. If this new show is truly only a single-season experiment as Lindelof has hinted, its strong start indicates it could be another hallmark mini-series along the lines of Chernobyl. Regina King's Sister Knight would be a worthy get-up this Thursday, but the mysterious Panda Cop will win you friends among the Internet-faithful.

 

It's a Tupperware salesperson, but from the Pleistocene

Vintage Tupperware rep is sneakily a crowd-pleaser any year (let Hairspray's Edna Turnblad be your inspiration, folks). But this year, you can be very vintage. A team from Tel Aviv University recently discovered humans have been saving leftovers for at least 200,000 to 400,000 years, after they studied the preservation techniques of Neanderthals and early Homo sapiens at the Qesem Cave in Israel. These Tupperware trailblazers appear to have stored marrow-rich deer bones for several weeks, relying on the bones and their outer layer of dried skin and flesh to keep the marrow relatively fresh.
