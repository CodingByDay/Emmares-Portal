The Big Picture: Identity Politics

Coronavirus has come to dominate U.S. politics. The tension between health and the economy is at the center of a political split between Democrats and Republicans.

But comments Friday from the two men who will be on the ballot in the 2020 presidential election are a reminder that identity politics and the culture war are going to factor in prominently, too.

“Well, I tell you what, if you have a problem figuring out whether you’re for me or Trump, then you ain’t black,” former Vice President Joe Biden, the presumptive Democratic nominee, said Friday toward the end of a radio show centering on issues within the black community.

The Trump campaign called the comments “racist.” South Carolina Sen. Tim Scott, the only black Republican in the U.S. Senate, called Biden’s comments “condescending and arrogant.”

Biden later walked back the remark during a call with black business leaders. "I shouldn't have been such a wise guy," Biden said. 

Also Friday, President Trump threatened to “override” states that don’t allow houses of worship to reopen, deeming them “essential.”

Health experts — and Trump’s own administration — have urged that large gatherings in close proximity not take place. But Trump often takes on the role of the aggrieved victim.

He used the culture divide as a way to fuel his 2016 presidential bid, and his remarks Friday were a reminder he’ll do it again.

Biden’s comments were the kind of gaffe that had veteran Democratic strategists wringing their hands at the prospect of him being the nominee — given his well-known history of making racially insensitive remarks. Of course, Trump has a well-documented history of inflaming racial tensions.

So while coronavirus, governmental competence and the economy are likely going to be principal motivators for voters, don’t forget just how important the culture war and identity politics will remain.
— Domenico Montanaro, NPR’s senior political editor/correspondent

Politics Roundup: The Coronavirus Crisis

Avoidable deaths: Some 36,000 people in the United States might still be alive if the government had acted to implement social distancing rules just one week earlier, an analysis from Columbia University has concluded. The United States is the hardest hit country in the world by the virus, which has killed nearly 100,000 Americans so far. 

Trump takes hydroxychloroquine: President Trump told reporters this week he had begun to take hydroxychloroquine, a controversial treatment he has suggested for protecting against the coronavirus, despite expert warnings of potential side effects from the drug. Hydroxychloroquine has traditionally been used in the treatment of malaria and lupus. Two studies are currently underway in examining its potential in curbing infections in coronavirus patients.

The bots are tweeting: Researchers at Carnegie Mellon University have found that about 45% of tweets sent about the coronavirus were likely sent by bot accounts rather than actual humans. Researchers say they are not yet able to conclusively establish the origin of the accounts, but they appear to be a propaganda effort aimed at sowing division among Americans. 

“Plan for the worst”: The Republican National Committee should brace itself for “a very big challenge” in hosting the August nominating convention, North Carolina Health and Human Services Secretary Dr. Mandy Cohen said. "Hope for the best, but plan for the worst," Cohen said, as the state begins to enter a modified Phase 2 reopening. This comes after GOP officials said they still plan to gather some 50,000 people for the event.

Fact check on Trump and the WHO: President Trump on Monday hit send on a harsh critique of the World Health Organization’s handling of the coronavirus pandemic, in the midst of the agency’s annual assembly. In his missive, Trump accused the organization of botching its approach to the global pandemic and threatened to halt funding and possibly even leave the group as a result. NPR fact-checks the president’s claims.

ICYMI: Top Stories

The virtual 2020 campaign: The coronavirus pandemic has brought traditional presidential campaigning to a standstill. Former Vice President Joe Biden is stuck at home in Delaware, while President Trump is deprived of his rallies. As NPR’s Tamara Keith and Asma Khalid report, the battle for the presidency is now largely being waged on the Internet.

“Opportunity zones” extension: The White House says it might extend "opportunity zone" tax breaks to help struggling neighborhoods in the wake of the coronavirus pandemic. But critics say the program has benefited wealthy investors more than local residents.

Trump threatens states over mail voting: President Trump threatened to withhold federal funding to two states with Democratic governors who have worked to expand mail-in voting during the coronavirus pandemic. Though Trump later appeared to back off the threat, it’s the latest example of his escalating rhetoric against mail-in voting — and it’s unclear what, if any, legal means Trump could withhold aid from those states. 

Graham “unmasking” requests: GOP Sen. Lindsey Graham requested info this week on Obama-era officials who sought intelligence info on President Trump's campaign and transition teams. But it's not possible for officials to have known what names had been redacted, or masked, in intel reports before making those requests. 

2020 fundraising: NPR is tracking how much money the campaigns of President Trump and former Vice President Joe Biden have raised so far during the 2020 election cycle. Here’s a look at the latest numbers, which show Biden’s campaign raised $43.7 million in April, compared to the Trump campaign’s $16.9 million.

The Shot: Veepstakes

Vice presidential picks rarely have an impact on the outcome of general elections. But in 2020, that's poised to change. Joe Biden has committed to choosing a woman as his running mate, and given the presumptive Democratic nominee's age, his pick is perhaps the most consequential in decades. Biden's choice for VP will need to be ready to become president on day one, and has to fulfill the first rule of being a running mate: First, do no harm.
