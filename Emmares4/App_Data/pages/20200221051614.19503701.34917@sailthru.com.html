Dearth of Divergence

The U.S. dollar is on a tear. U.S. stocks are at all-time highs versus their global peers.

And yet, it’s the exceptionalism in U.S. Treasuries that stands out as one of the more eyebrow-raising dynamics in financial markets right now. Since the coronavirus became a part of the market backdrop, there has been no divergence between U.S. and German yields. In fact, the spread between the two has marginally narrowed.

It’s a testament to the pre-eminence of Treasuries as a destination for global money in search of safety. Because nothing about the data suggests that the spread between U.S. and German yields should be narrowing now – quite the contrary. The U.S. economic surprise index is climbing higher; the euro-zone’s has rolled over.

Tentative hopes that Treasuries were beginning to get more sensitive to domestic data were misplaced. U.S. bonds didn’t really sell off that much at all this week in response to the strongest building permits figures since 2007 or a white-hot Philly Fed report. And the tentative range that the 10-year yield was in has gotten wider with a breach of 1.5% for the first time this year.

“Despite strong U.S. data, weak global results and more Covid-19 fears over the last couple of days have pushed U.S. rates into a bull-flattener,” wrote analysts at Bespoke Investment Group. “Booming U.S. housing activity is no salve for bond bears, who are being overruled by global risk factors that investors have fled from in the interests of U.S. interest rate markets.”

It’s a safe bet that the U.S. yield curve would be steeper right now were it not for the coronavirus. But calls for a bear-steepened curve coming into 2020 had an integral global component. Near or sub-zero yields in Europe and Japan serve as a breaking force on how much yields can rise – it’s very uncertain how much wider the spread between short and longer-term yields would be given the contraction in Japan’s economy in the fourth quarter and aforementioned poor performance of European numbers lately.

Stocks and Bonds

The 21-day correlation between the net change in 10-year Treasury yields and the daily percent change in the S&P 500 recently hit its highest levels in nearly a decade.

On its face, this seems odd: we know that U.S. stocks are up over this stretch and yields down. So what we can infer from this dynamic is the enduring asymmetry in Treasury market swings: When stocks go higher, yields inch higher. But when they fall, the offsetting reaction in yields is much larger.

This confluence is why rumors of the death of the 60/40 portfolio and risk parity strategies continue to be greatly exaggerated.

Junk Fever. Ketch It.

The most frequently cited vulnerability in the credit market (well, besides energy bonds) for quite some time has been the massive amount of BBB bonds that could become so-called “fallen angels” and join high yield.

Well, Kraft Heinz got downgraded to junk and Macy’s and Renault SA may be on their way after having received their first high-yield ratings.

Neither IG nor HY seem to be freaking out too much. Investors are still concerned about how tight spreads across a variety of markets are.

BofA Securities’ Hans Mikkelsen offers his take on why the fundamentals for BBB bonds aren't that scary:

“Back in 2014-2017, it felt late cycle and interest rates collapsed, prompting US IG companies to respond like they are supposed to by leveraging up. That left many companies with little capacity to add more debt (and remain IG) while numerous others are still actively deleveraging following large scale M&A activity. Hence, the difference this late cycle environment is that a large share of IG issuers - mainly in BBB land - have stable or improving fundamentals, while a smaller than usual subset - mostly A and AA rated industrials - lever up as usual.”

One issue that’s typically raised about fallen angels is the ability of the high-yield market to absorb longer-term bonds. That’s pretty much been the story for Kraft: the recent price declines on longer-term bonds have dwarfed ones that come due much sooner.

Citi’s Michael Anderson, who flagged that Kraft has now become the largest high yield issuer by duration, turns this dynamic on its head:

“With a yield in the mid 3% range for 10-year paper, the attraction of Kraft won’t be its yield. After all, this is in line with the best high yield credits. The attraction of Kraft would be its duration given the market’s acute lack of duration. It’s not the market value underweight that can hurt a portfolio’s relative performance. Instead, it’s the duration underweight.”
