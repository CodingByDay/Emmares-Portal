Bouncing Back from Rejection
We recently asked you to tell us the best way you've learned to handle rejection and got tons of great responses. Here are just a few from Muse readers who shared what's helped them in the past:

Set a Timeline
“I have allowed myself to feel the full weight of the emotions. I haven’t denied the fact that I felt bad or hurt but I set a timeline to wallow. Once the time is up, I then reassure myself it is just a temporary setback, that there are many, many more roles in the market and my time will come. I savor past victories, forge ahead, make necessary tweaks, and get back on the wagon!”
— Bukola Adisa, head of controls design

Objectively Evaluate Feedback
“As tough as it [was], I swallowed my pride and asked for the reasons why I was rejected. Then, after I've had a chance to marinate, I create an Excel sheet and include whether the feedback is objective or the rejector's personal preference. For those points I agree with, I take steps to incorporate them into my personal development goals.”
— Sharon Chua, production manager

Try Again and Again
“Own it and apply again. I’ve been rejected from lots of things over the years, from high school plays to my dream internship in college. It always sucks and finding a way to get over that initial gut punch is hard, but almost everything that I’ve tried a second time I’ve eventually gotten, including my dream internship at The Second City. I even talked about being rejected the first time in my interview and how my willingness to try again showed my resilience.”
— Allison Freed, video producer

Look for Inspiration
“Sometimes I blast some music—'Whatever It Takes' by Imagine Dragons is a great pump-up song to get me back on track. I also look at the walls in my home office, which are covered with inspirational quotes...[which] inspire me to get back to applying for new roles and opportunities. They also remind me that better days are ahead, not just for me, but all of us.”
— Matt Noonan, freelance content producer
