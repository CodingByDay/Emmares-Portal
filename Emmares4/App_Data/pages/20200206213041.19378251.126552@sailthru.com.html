Back when I was delivering pizzas for a living, the smartphone revolution was still at least a decade away. So, I did what millions of food-transport technicians before me had done: I used maps to get around. Folding maps, laminated maps, hand-drawn maps, and that pinnacle of urban way-finding, the spiral-bound Thomas Guide.

Of course, all of my humble analog maps would soon get replaced by one vastly superior digital equivalent. Now I can instantly find the best driving route, the best sushi, the best bus line, the best dive bar, or the best walking tour, all with a tap. The revolution is over; digital maps won.

Google Maps wasn't the first map service on the internet when it launched 15 years ago, but with its inclusion on the iPhone a couple of years later, it quickly became the most important. And though it faces stiff competition today from Apple, Google Maps continues to grow. Real-time data, augmented reality views, crowdsourced reviews, and even targeted ads—Google Maps has it all.

This week, WIRED's Lauren Goode spoke with Alphabet CEO Sundar Pichai and the head Google Maps, Jen Fitzpatrick, about the places the product has been, and the course Google is charting for Maps' future.

Also this week, Arielle Pardes checks in on the state of internet dating, and Louryn Strampe tests a bunch of home-delivery meal kits to find out which one's best.
