Industrial companies have to be feeling like they’re stuck in a Charlie Brown cartoon these days. Every time things start to look up, some nasty surprise swoops in and takes away their football. First, the trade war kneecapped a recovery off the oil-price downturn in 2015 and 2016 that was just starting to pick up steam. Now, the coronavirus threatens to derail a hoped-for rebound from the trade doldrums.

Commerce Department data released this week showed orders placed with U.S. factories for business equipment unexpectedly jumped 1.1% in January, excluding defense and aircraft spending. While these data can be volatile, that’s the strongest gain in a year and reinforced the idea that even if the manufacturing sector wasn’t kicking off 2020 with the roar that some expected in the wake of the U.S.-China trade truce, the trajectory was positive. Unfortunately, the numbers were old news before they were ever released because they don’t reflect the impact of the now-global coronavirus outbreak.

The number of new cases reported outside of China exceeded those within the country for the first time this week. Iran, Italy, Japan and South Korea are the new hotspots, and concern is growing that the virus has been spreading undetected in the U.S. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention confirmed the first domestic patient who had no known ties to an infected area or contact with any known carriers, and a top official warned that businesses, schools and local governments should prepare for major disruption. Concerns are mounting, with airlines extending China-related flight suspensions to South Korea, Japan and Milan. Nestle SA and JPMorgan Chase & Co. suspended all nonessential international business travel, while other companies have made sharp reductions in allowable trips. Even online shopping is likely to be dragged down, with Amazon.com Inc. merchants pulling advertising on concerns they won’t have enough inventory and PayPal Holdings Inc. warning of a slump in cross-border e-commerce activity.

As my colleague Michael McKee put it on Bloomberg TV, you can almost throw a dart at a board and come up with a company warning about the impact of the coronavirus on their results. Almost, because on the U.S. industrial manufacturing side of things, the updates have been fairly limited so far. Emerson Electric Co. is the exception, warning Friday that the hit to current-quarter revenue from the virus would be worse than previously anticipated at as much as $150 million. More cuts are coming. Even companies likely to see increased demand for face masks and protective gear such as 3M Co. and Honeywell International Inc. are going to suffer elsewhere in businesses responsible for a much bigger portion of their overall profit. 3M also makes electronic and automotive components and had been hoping for at least a stabilization there after a dismal 2019. But in electronics, Microsoft Corp. said Wednesday that its Windows personal-computer unit won’t meet sales guidance for the fiscal third quarter because of supply-chain disruptions. On the automotive side, the China Passenger Car Association noted a 92% plunge in car sales excluding minivans in the first two weeks of February, while ongoing factory shutdowns at the likes of Nissan Motor Co. and Honda Motor Co. raise questions about the ability to produce cars even if demand sharply recovered.

Honeywell’s aerospace division has been a high-flier for the company, with even the U.S.-China trade war and the grounding of Boeing Co.’s 737 Max failing to put too big of a dent in passenger traffic growth. But the coronavirus may be what finally brings the boom times to an end. Safran SA this week predicted as much as a 5% drop in 2020 revenue, but it’s assuming that whatever disruption is created by the coronavirus doesn’t extend beyond the first quarter. That feels optimistic at this point, with United Airlines Holdings Inc. pulling its 2020 guidance because it’s not confident that the conditions needed to meet its numbers – including the virus running its course by mid-May and travel patterns returning to normal five months later – will be met. Vertical Research Partners analyst Rob Stallard cut his view on Safran shares to hold, warning that the company may be underappreciating the impact on airlines. Fewer flights mean less wear and tear on planes and less need for the spare parts and repair work that Safran thinks will make up for some of the financial hit from the Max production halt. 

Both full and empty containers with nowhere to go are piling up at the Port of Los Angeles in a sign of the logjams that will weigh on activity even when supply-chains do start to come back online, executive director Gene Seroka said in an interview with Bloomberg News. He predicts volatile shipping activity even into the fourth quarter. But the hit to airlines and other consumer-related companies is significantly more concerning for manufacturers than any short-term supply-chain pileups. The trade war nudged the industrial economy into a modest recession, but the consumer had kept chugging along. The inverse – where the consumer slumps but manufacturing is steady – is virtually impossible, says Gordon Haskett analyst John Inch. If Goldman Sachs Group Inc. strategists are right that overall U.S. corporate profit growth drops to zero in 2020, how confident is the consumer going to be? 

WEEKLY BOEING UPDATE
A good benchmark for Boeing finally putting the 737 Max crisis behind it will be when the company can manage just one week without a negative headline. Needless to say, this wasn’t the week. The New York Times reported late last Friday that federal prosecutors are investigating whether Boeing knowingly misled the Federal Aviation Administration during the approval process for the Max about the nature of flight-control software linked to two fatal crashes. The queries stem in part from comments by former chief technical pilot Mark Forkner in those infamous employee messages. Forkner, through his lawyers, has denied any dishonesty. In the meantime, Boeing reportedly put three of his former colleagues on administrative leave. Elsewhere on the cultural-overhaul front, Boeing announced two long-serving board members — former General Electric Co. executive Michael Zafirovski and former insurance executive Edward Liddy — would step down. They’ll be replaced by Qualcomm Inc. CEO Steve Mollenkopf and former United Technologies Corp. CFO Akhil Johri, whose engineering and aerospace expertise, respectively, would seem to be a better fit. The shakeup is overdue, but it’s an odd juxtaposition to CEO David Calhoun, who is now the second-longest serving director. Point being, more needs to be done. 

As far as the plane itself goes, the FAA is recommending the inspection and possible replacement of a metallic lining on the Max engine casings that’s meant to protect the underlying wiring from lightening strikes. In the scheme of things, this is minor: the FAA estimated each inspection would cost only $425, while each modification (if needed) would cost $595. The positive way to look at this is that we are now getting down to the nitty-gritty on the Max review and a certification flight should be happening soon. The Seattle Times, however, reports that this crucial step in the un-grounding process is unlikely to happen until late April amid difficulties in correcting a malfunctioning indicator light and a bias in the FAA to force Boeing to rewire the planes to better guard against short-circuits. That risks nudging the Max’s return date somewhat beyond the mid-2020 target. Compensation for airlines dealing with the protracted grounding in some cases reportedly will include the cost of the flight simulator training Boeing is now recommending for Max pilots. For the suppliers, Boeing has agreed to cover all payments for engines delivered by GE and Safran’s CFM International joint venture in 2019 and 2020 for Max jets that can’t yet be handed over to their airline customers. Spirit AeroSystems Holdings Inc. will get $225 million in cash from Boeing to support work on the Max, which it expects to resume at a slow pace in March. 

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Thyssenkrupp AG agreed to sell its elevator unit to a private equity consortium including Advent International and Cinven for 17.2 billion euros ($18.9 billion). This puts an end to a long and competitive sale process and sets the company up for a much-needed cash infusion to help pay down its heavy debt and pension obligations. While a rival private equity cohort that included Blackstone Group Inc. and Carlyle Group Inc. had reportedly made promises to protect the Thyssenkrupp brand, invest long-term in the elevator operations and allow the company to retain a minority stake, it appears the Advent group was willing to offer the highest price, with some help from the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority. The Advent group is essentially matching the failed offer of Thyssenkrupp rival Kone Oyj without the antitrust hurdles that buyer faced — but also without the same potential cost savings, notes my Bloomberg Opinion colleague Chris Hughes. It’s fair to ask how they justify that, particularly amid the coronavirus sell-off, Chris writes. The losing bidders may still have an opportunity to buy a piece of Thyssenkrupp, if they want it. The company plans to review the other parts of its business — which lose money in some cases and make little of it in others — for possible additional divestitures. The plant-building operations and automotive and industrial parts businesses seem most likely to wind up on the sale block. 

Boingo Wireless Inc., which provides WiFi service in airports and train stations, has received takeover interest and is exploring its options, people familiar with the matter told Bloomberg News. Shares of the company have slumped amid a stretch of weak profitability and volatile cash flow, but Northland Securities Inc. analyst Greg Gibas estimates Boingo could fetch $19.50 a share — a 57% premium to its average price in the month leading up to the news of a potential sale — based on multiples obtained in comparable recent telecom transactions. Oppenheimer analyst Timothy Horan sees a higher price tag if Boingo is valued like a tower company, noting American Tower Corp. and Crown Castle International Corp. could find revenue and cost synergies. Of course, it remains to be seen what the recent coronavirus fears and the concurrent slump in any stocks related to aerospace do to these sale talks. Elsewhere, Carlyle paused the sale of engine-parts maker Paradigm Precision because the Max grounding was dampening bids. 

Brink’s Co., known for its armored cars that transport gold bullion and other valuables, agreed to buy most of the cash-handling operations of the U.K.’s G4S Plc for $860 million. It’s the largest acquisition for the company and fits with its goal of expanding into new markets abroad. Interestingly, the deal will close in multiple phases, with more than half of the enterprise value to be paid in 60 days and the remainder to be completed before year-end, pending necessary licenses and approvals. The two companies have been reported to be in talks since September; the ultimate valuation should be a relief to investors at about 7.5 times adjusted Ebitda before synergies. Brink’s itself had a valuation of 9 times its expected 2020 Ebitda at the time of the deal’s announcement.
