If you’re one of the many Americans planning road trips as the weather warms up, keep in mind that a few parts of the country could see some brief shortages of gasoline at stations in the most congested areas. Why? Not because there is any shortage of gas itself. Per the Department of Energy, stockpiles of gasoline in storage are right around where they typically are at this time of year. But there may not be enough tanker truck drivers to haul all the fuel where it’s needed, as quickly as it’s needed, according to industry sources. Many drivers retired last year when their work slowed down due to the pandemic, and the trucking industry was already dealing with high turnover and not enough qualified drivers.

Now, with demand for gas expected to be heavy because of so many people taking long overdue vacations, the lack of truck drivers could lead to a few stations running dry, at least briefly, says Jeff Lenard, vice president of strategic industry initiatives at the National Association of Convenience Stores. However, he adds that no one should cancel their travel plans for fear of running out of gas. Convenience stores, which dispense most of the gas sold in the U.S., are used to coping with supply disruptions, either due to natural disasters like hurricanes or a shortage of truck drivers. Also, Lenard says, the Department of Transportation can raise the caps on how many hours truckers can drive in situations where fuel is critically short, which should mitigate any shortages that do crop up. Basically, think of this as a potential inconvenience, not a major worry.

UPS gets up: Back in mid-February, we put you, ahem, a step ahead with insight into how United Parcel Service (UPS) and FedEx (FDX) represented “growth at a superb price,” according to CFRA analysts. Fast-forward to today, and both have exceeded the market (UPS +24.7% and FDX +12.6% to the S&P 500’s 7.0%), with UPS in particular standing out this week after its blockbuster earnings report. Profits jumped 141% year-over-year and beat analysts’ expectations, sparking a sharp, multiday rally in shares. So, what’s next for the stock? The analyst community as a whole remains fairly bullish, with 15 Strong Buys/Buys versus 10 Holds and 3 Sells/Strong Sells. Raymond James’s Patrick Tyler Brown (Strong Buy) is among the most optimistic, writing that he continues to see “building industry tailwinds in the parcel complex anchored by consumers' sustained shift toward e-commerce and a strong pricing landscape.”
