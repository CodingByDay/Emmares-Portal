Micromobbin'

This week, micromobbin’ is all about Uber. Yes, we know other companies had stuff going on.

But Uber had a big week in the world of scooters and bike share that included the release of a new data tool, the launch of an adaptive scooter pilot and a letter to the House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure lobbying for better infrastructure to safeguard vulnerable roadway users.

The letter, authored by Uber and sent on behalf of PeopleForBikes, the North American Bikeshare Association and the League of American Bicyclists, is aimed at changes in the upcoming reauthorization of the Fixing America’s Surface Transportation (FAST) Act of 2015. Uber supports legislation that would expand improved bike infrastructure on city streets and unlock funding for bicycle-friendly policies. Uber called for legislation that would require new mobility lanes be added to every street once it is re-paved and advocated for congestion pricing to fund mass transit.

Uber’s letter coincided with the release of a free, publicly available data tool that will combine JUMP bike volume with a complete city street view.

Shin-pei Tsay, Uber’s director of policy, cities and transportation, told me that the idea behind this tool is to help people understand and advocate for the best bike infrastructure possible. The data tool, which has launched in San Francisco, Washington D.C. and Paris, lets users compare weekday and weekend volume, and filter the data based on the time of day.

 

And finally, Uber is deploying two types of adaptive scooters as part of a pilot program in partnership with San Francisco Bike Rentals. This is part of Uber’s permit with the city of San Francisco, which requires all electric scooter operators to pilot adaptive scooter share.

This sounds great, but let’s be clear: this is incredibly limited for now. Upon launch, just four vehicles will be available. The plan, Uber says, is to monitor response from riders, keep engaging with community organizations and expand the program based on feedback.

Deal of the week

Electric vertical take-off and landing startups had a big 2019 with reveals, partnerships and funding rounds that brought a new level of attention to this burgeoning industry. But this is 2020 folks and so we must go bigger.

Joby Aviation, which aims to launch its own electric air taxi service, announced that it raised $590 million in a Series C funding. Toyota Motor Corp. was the lead investor, ponying up $394 million. Baillie Gifford and Global Oryx along with prior backers Capricorn Investment Group, Intel Capital, JetBlue Technology Ventures, SPARX Group and Toyota AI Ventures rounded out the group. Joby also gained Toyota Motor Corporation EVP Shigeki Tomoyama as a new board member.

Joby Aviation has now raised a total of $720 million, making the Santa Cruz, California-based company the best-funded eVTOL startup in the industry.

The funding round is another signpost on a road of deals between eVTOL companies and automakers or aviation manufacturers. For instance, South Korean automaker Hyundai announced plans in January to build an air taxi with Uber.

Then there’s German startup Volocopter, which closed $55 million in funding in 2019 in a round led by Zhejiang Geely Holding Group Co., Ltd, the Chinese company that owns Volvo and Lotus and is the biggest investor in Mercedes-Benz maker Daimler. Even Porsche is getting in on the action through a partnership announced in 2019 with Boeing to work together on developing a concept for a “premium” eVTOL.

Other notable deals this week:

  • NextNav raises $120 million for its indoor-positioning technology

  • Bolt, the billion-dollar startup out of Estonia that’s building a ride-hailing, scooter and food delivery business across Europe and Africa, raised about $56 million from the European Investment Bank

  • Hyundai and Kia put more than $110 million in UK startup Arrival, a five-year-old startup that aims to make electric vehicles in microfactories.
  • FlashParking, an Austin-based developer of parking management software and services, raised $60 million in new financing from the private equity investment firm L Catterton.

Cruise's call to action

In the next couple of weeks, the California Department of Motor Vehicles will release disengagement reports submitted by every company that currently has a permit to test autonomous vehicles on public roadways.

These reports are meant to provide details on “disengagements,” a term that means the number of times drivers have had to take control of a car. The DMV defines a disengagement as any time a test vehicle operating on public roads has switched from autonomous to manual mode for an immediate safety-related reason or due to a failure of the system.

It’s supposed to give the DMV insight into the development and testing of autonomous vehicles. But it is also closely watched and covered by the media because it is one of the only pieces of publicly available data that provides any information on what these AV developers are up to — at least in California.

The problem is that these reports are wildly different; each company provides varying amounts of information, all in different formats. There is also disagreement over what is and what is not a disengagement.

AV developers — and me — have grumbled quietly about these reports for some time. Now companies are starting to be more vocal. Cruise co-founder and CTO Kyle Vogt posted a blog on Medium that called for a new metric to determine whether an autonomous vehicle is ready for commercial deployment.

“It’s woefully inadequate for most uses beyond those of the DMV,” Vogt wrote. “The idea that disengagements give a meaningful signal about whether an AV is ready for commercial deployment is a myth.”

It seems that Cruise is working on a new metric — based on my own sources and the wording of the post. It turns out, so is Waymo.

Waymo has started to pull the human safety driver from behind the wheel of its Chrysler Pacifica minivans in Chandler, Ariz. These driverless vehicles are now starting to be used by users of its Waymo One ride-hailing service. To determine when it was safe enough to do this, Waymo developed a set of methodologies. It seems the company might be prepping to share those with others.

A look at lidar startup Baraja

Lidar companies had a moment at CES 2020 and in the coming weeks expect some deep dives into some of the startups we talked to. One of these is Baraja, a company that has been pretty quiet until now.

If you’re unfamiliar with lidar, stop reading and check out this handy primer. OK, now that you’re back we can proceed.

As editor Devin Coldeway explains, to picture how Baraja’s lidar works, you need to picture the cover of Pink Floyd’s “Dark Side of the Moon.”

 

Let me elaborate: Imagine a flashlight shooting through a prism and illuminating the scene in front of it. Now imagine you could focus that flashlight by selecting which color came out of the prism, sending more light to the top part of the scene (red and orange) or middle (yellow and green). That’s what Baraja’s lidar does, except naturally it’s a bit more complicated than that.

Coldewey is our resident expert and fan of optics. He’s also a contrarian and good at debunking tech. So I took notice when he called Baraja’s approach astonishingly clever.

“Not just because of the fascinating optics (something I’m partial to, if it isn’t obvious), but because it obviates a number of serious problems other lidars are facing or about to face,” Coldewey writes.

Chatting with Mobileye's Amnon Shashua

A few weeks ago, I laid out my plan for 2020. It’s a blueprint of sorts that includes more interviews with founders and CEOs, investigations into companies and their tech and in general, a greater emphasis on analysis and scoops in hopes of providing a clearer view into the complex and often opaque industry I call “the future of transportation.”

My latest contribution to this plan is a lengthy interview with Amnon Shashua, Mobileye’s president and CEO and Intel senior vice president. There was a lot to talk about, and we do. But my main aim was to get an answer to a question I asked him during the TC Sessions: Mobility conference last July. And that is, why would Mobileye want to get into the business of operating a robotaxi business?

Shashua mostly answers my question and ends up revealing a lot about his strategy. It’s clear that he’s intent on taking aim at Waymo and other AV developers that intend to operate robotaxi services. Acquisitions are clearly in the future as well, so startups consider yourselves informed.
