Mario Draghi: The Banker Who Did What It Took

Mario Draghi has given his last press conference as chairman of the European Central Bank. It was a downbeat affair, and he will hand over the reins next week. But he can say that he kept the promise he made in by far his most famous speech, from 2012. He said that he would do “whatever it takes” to save the euro, and promised that it would be enough.

As his term ends, we can see that it was indeed enough. Back in 2012, the bonds of several peripheral euro zone countries were under attack, as investors bet that the single-currency area would be forced apart. As Draghi leaves, the political risks to the existence of the euro have fallen sharply, as can be seen in the far narrower spreads at which the bond yields of countries like Spain, Italy and particularly Greece trade over equivalent bund yields. 

At the time of “whatever it takes,” I expected that Draghi would soon be put to the test by a speculative market attack on some country’s bonds. This might have forced him into buying their bonds, and showing the market the color of his money. That never happened. The market never directly tested his promise. However, seven years later, we can see that it still took Draghi a lot to save the euro. The cost wasn’t as spectacular or immediate as some expected, but it was high, and it may yet prove unbearable.

Here, with the aid of a Bloomberg terminal, is an illustration of what it has cost. First, Draghi did eventually have to cut rates to zero (his predecessor Jean-Claude Trichet had actually raised them twice, even as the sovereign debt crisis was breaking out). Bond yields for the core countries of the euro zone dropped even lower. And it is probably for the stunning phenomenon of negative rates, rather than the rescue of the euro that Draghi is destined to be remembered:

These days, rates are of less importance in monetary policy than they used to be. Draghi attempted to avoid using asset purchases, and actually shrank the ECB’s balance sheet once the worst of the sovereign debt crisis had passed. But as a deflation scare followed, he had no choice but to start buying assets. He leaves with a balance sheet double the size of the one he inherited. The ECB only relented and started buying at the point when the Federal Reserve stopped. As for the euro, it survived. It lost value, but its biggest fall, at the end of 2014, was due to the sudden collapse in the price of oil — a factor beyond Draghi’s control . He leaves with the euro 5% weaker than when he arrived, or slightly more than that in real terms once relative inflation rates are taken into account. Unlike the Fed, the ECB has no mandate to control unemployment. Draghi’s record on this score, however, could have been worse. Arriving in the teeth of a dreadful recession, particularly in countries like Spain, he soon saw euro zone unemploment as high as 12%. It is now down to a more palatable 7%. However, the comparative performance of the U.K., also part of the EU single market, which had an austere fiscal policy throughout his term, and which punished itself with Brexit political uncertainty, shows that there is something very much awry with the eurozone’s labor market:

This also shows itself in inflation. He certainly did not let it get out of control, but at an average of almost exactly 1% per year for his term, euro-area inflation was a symptom of a painfully depressed and sluggish economy. This was in part due to a vicious cycle that the ECB couldn’t resolve. The euro zone is more dependent on bank finance than is the U.S., but its banks were particularly bloated entering the credit crisis. This left the area with a critically weakened banking sector, with many investors simply not trusting that the loans on their balance sheets were as healthy as the accountant said. Reviving confidence in the euro zone’s banks was a critical ECB task, and success in this area was only marginal. They  traded for half the value of the total equity shown on their books when Draghi arrived; and they trade for 60% of their book value now. 

As for the stock markets of the euro zone, it wasn’t Draghi’s job to boost them. Nevertheless, many investors will blame the ECB, among many others for waiting too long to try to stimulate the European economy.  The euro zone’s underperformance of the rest of the world has been spectacular. That, through the lens of the Bloomberg terminal, is the Draghi legacy. But there are deeper ways to measure this. The financial metrics in the terminal may be growing less important as we look forward. And that is what is most concerning.

Fly like a bumblebee, sting like a...

Try listening to the full 10-minute “whatever it takes” speech. Beyond the famous soundbite, what is striking now is its sheer bullishness, and its naivete — that is not too strong a word — about European social and political cohesion. Draghi explained that the euro was like a bumblebee, which was supposedly scientifically incapable of flying, and yet it flew. Now it had faced problems, and it would need structural reforms to turn it “into a real bee.” Those reforms, which he said were well advanced, involved convergence, with “more Europe, not less,” including fiscal union and a banking union with a common banking supervisor for the entire EU, on which an announcement was due soon.

The euro zone had enjoyed better inflation, employment and productivity than the U.S. or Japan over the previous decade — something that isn’t true of the seven years since the speech was made — and it had much lower debt. Most importantly, it boasted a “a degree of social cohesion that you don’t find in the other two countries [the U.S. and Japan] certainly, and that is a very important ingredient for the structural reforms” that would allow the euro to turn into a fully fledged real bee. 

That assessment was breathtakingly wrong. The idea that the euro zone, with all its languages and conflicting cultures, had more cohesion than Japan was in any case laughable. Japan has many problems; but it might well be the most cohesive nation on earth. Seven years later, it is no longer laughable because the steady ebb of cohesion grows ever more alarming. 

Beyond the Gilets Jaunes in France, we have the rise of the far right in various countries, a party formed by a comedian wielding power in Italy, and separatist movements in Spain. That is before we even mention the U.K., which is still in the EU, even if it was never in the euro. The reforms that Draghi imagined would be necessary to buttress the long-term safety of the euro are nowhere in sight.

Draghi delivered on his promise to preserve the euro. But it came at the price of economic stagnation that is testing the social cohesion of which he boasted. The loss of social cohesion makes it ever less likely that the EU will make the reforms necessary to bring coherence to the euro project. And that social cohesion, in itself, has become a critical concern for investors.

Back to Brexit

That brings us, inevitably, to Brexit. The latest episode in what has all the elements of a playground scrap has seen Prime Minister Boris Johnson challenge Labour’s Jeremy Corbyn to an election in December. Labour still doesn't want this. 

There’s no logic to an opposition party turning down a chance to take power. And there’s no logic to the prime minister suspending attempts to pass his deal and have an election instead, when only a matter of days ago it looked as though he could win over enough Labour MPs to his cause. His obvious course of action is to spend a few more weeks making sure that his deal is passed, exit the EU, and then triumphantly call a general election. 

So Britain’s politicians continue lamentably to control the agenda. I wrote a column Thursday on William Riker, the political scientist who showed that power resides in controlling and shaping the agenda — theories that offer a clear explanation of why the Brexit process has gone so disastrously wrong. For further reading, Riker’s The Art of Political Manipulation, offers examples from Ancient Greece through the Lincoln-Douglas debates to the present day. I would particularly recommend it to Britain’s current band of inept politicians. 

But the most important question about the election may be whether Britain could survive the process without a total breakdown of cohesion. Thursday saw the publication of what might be the most shocking public opinion survey I have ever read, produced by Yougov for Cardiff University and the University of Edinburgh. It shows that a majority of Britons, including a majority of both Leavers and Remainers, think violence against MPs, and serious injuries for members of the public in violent protests, would a “price worth paying” if it achieved their desired outcome for Brexit. 

There are no words.

Investors and financiers prefer the clearly enumerated terrain of economics and finance. But expect investors now to spend a lot of time trying to understand sociology and political science. Conflagrations around the world have forced these subjects up the agenda. Mario Draghi thought that social cohesion was a necessary condition for necessary reforms. Now, even in the traditionally stable U.K., it seems to be breaking down.
