Welcome to the Turning Points Pop-Up Edition, a daily update from the New Economy Forum this week in Beijing. Looming over the forum is of course the U.S.-China trade war, but the threat of a disruptive technological Cold War may be the greater challenge. 

The forum, taking place in the Chinese capital for the first time, was founded on the conviction that the massive shift of wealth and power from West to East carries the enormous risk of geopolitical conflict. Moreover, the Western-led order is turning itself upside down just as a revolution powered by artificial intelligence gains momentum.

This combination—emerging technologies and ascendant economies in Asia, Africa, the Middle East and Latin America—is what we call the “new economy.” This week’s gathering of 500 of the world’s top business and government leaders will debate the institutions, policies and frameworks required to accommodate this new world order. —Andy Browne

Today’s Top Stories

The Coming End of the American World Order

Here’s an interesting question, posed by Ian Bremmer of the Eurasia Group, a forum delegate: “Where exactly will the new Berlin Wall stand?” Will Europe align with the U.S., or break apart, looking both east and west? What about India, or South Korea, or Brazil? Will the U.S., fearing China’s technological advance, abandon its free-market principles and build a “tech-based military industrial complex” that looks more like China? Bremmer (below) has two good ideas, aimed at preserving an open digital commons even as China walls off its Internet. First, an organization equivalent to the United Nations Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change to formulate rules for the digital world. Second, a digital World Trade Organization—a World Data Organization to promote online openness and transparency.

Advantage China, Thanks to a Huge 5G Network

China’s state-owned wireless carriers debuted 5G mobile phone services a few weeks ago, a milestone in the country’s push to become a technology power. Cheap plans in China will boost smartphone penetration, as they’ve done in South Korea. And despite U.S. restrictions on Huawei, its phones are holding their own in the competition. In the U.S.-China contest for 21st century connectivity, the early results are in: Advantage China.

Beijing Scrutinizes U.S. Bid to Control Chipmaker

A U.S. chipmaker’s attempt to acquire a peer with a valuable Chinese affiliate has triggered scrutiny from Beijing. China’s antitrust regulator is closely monitoring Diodes’s proposed $428 million takeover of Taiwan’s Lite-On Semiconductor, responding to complaints a deal will deliver its Shanghai-based affiliate On-Bright Electronics into American hands.

Why Argentina Keeps Ending Up In a Debt Crisis

It’s familiar territory for Argentines: An economic crisis triggered by mounting deficits and debt, and political instability with sharp swings between governments of the right and left. In that climate, it’s hard to see ahead—one reason Argentina ranks low on Bloomberg’s index of investment as a driver of development.

How China Is Advancing In Cancer Research 

Doctors have for the first time in the U.S tested a powerful gene-editing technique, Crispr, in people with cancer. Three patients with advanced cancer have been treated so far. The results? Too early to say. “The good news is that all of them are alive,” Dr. Edward A. Stadtmauer tells the New York Times. Doctors in China have already started using Crispr to treat various types of cancer. Clearly, joint U.S.-China research in cancer treatment would benefit humanity. Promoting that kind of cooperation is one of the goals of a U.S.-China Task Force that begins work Wednesday at the New Economy Forum.
