Imagine your company’s largest market is the U.S. and your second-largest and fastest growing market is China. Now suppose you’re told you have to choose one.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Corp., better known as TSMC, denied this week that it was in such a predicament. That was after a report said Washington was pushing the company to stop supplying chips to Huawei, its second biggest customer after Apple. Taiwan’s government also said no such American request had been made.

Whatever the situation may be at TSMC, the bind that U.S.-China tensions have put many businesses into is real. The National Basketball Association is still reeling from having had to apologize to its Chinese fans for what a team executive tweeted, while also needing to show those in America it’s standing up for the same executive’s right to expression.

And that balancing act is getting harder. A survey out this week showed that the ongoing confrontation between Washington and Beijing is beginning to affect what Chinese shoppers buy.

The backdrop is Singles Day. China's online shopping bonanza, started a decade ago by Alibaba and celebrated every Nov. 11, saw more than $30 billion of merchandise sold in a 24-hour period last year. A survey of this year’s potential shoppers, however, found three-fourths saying they may not buy American. Why? The top explanation was national loyalty.

But decoupling isn’t inevitable. A potential phase-one trade deal seems likely and should, when signed, help cool tensions. There is also much about America that continues to enjoy great popularity in China, despite the stand-off. Take for example Alibaba's decision to ask Taylor Swift to headline its online telethon for Singles Day this year.

Come Monday, whatever Chinese shoppers decide to do with their money will be very much worth watching.

Phase-One Optimism

China's currency, one of the best barometers for how the trade war is trending, 

strengthened above seven-to-the-dollar this week for the first time since August. The optimism behind that move began with Beijing saying a "consensus in principle" had been reached. President Donald Trump then told reporters the two sides were planning to sign a dealsomewhere in the U.S., Commerce Secretary Wilbur Ross said negotiations weremaking good progress and the two sides appeared to agree on a "phased" roll-back of tariffs.  Add in the high-profile sentencing of three Chinese fentanyl smugglers, a subject Trump has made great hay over, and it's easy to see why markets turned bullish. Though of course there were also reminders of why it's been so tricky to predict where this trade war is going.

Asian Trade 

That phase-one deal isn't the only trade agreement Beijing has on the burner. China is also promoting an Asian pact that includes nations across Southeast Asia, Japan, South Korea, Australia and New Zealand. But not India, which dropped out this week amid domestic protests against the agreement. While losing the world's second-biggest nation by population will leave a bruise, Beijing's motivation for sealing the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership remains in tact. The deal should help to buffer some of the economic pain from the trade war with America and could also strengthen Beijing's regional influence.

Canadian Freeze

There was a development in Canada’s ties with China for Prime Minister Justin Trudeau to celebrate this week, which has not been the norm since Huawei’s chief financial officer was detained in Vancouver late last year. Beijing, which banned imports of Canadian beef and pork in June after discovering certification for a cargo was forged, announced it would begin allowing shipments again. Trudeau was quick to tout the benefits for his country’s meat producers. But there’s a cloud around that silver lining. Beijing’s green-light is far more likely a function of its need to fill a yawning shortfall in meat supplies caused by disease than it is an indication of détente with Ottawa. China is in fact busily buying meat from all over the world as it tries to rein in surging domestic prices. Any signs of a thaw remain hard to spot on the horizon.

Payment Services

One common complaint for visitors to China – if you believe them – is that they want to pay for lunch, but can’t. The ubiquitous adoption of mobile payments means pulling out cash in China is now as likely to end with a waiter saying they can’t make change as it is to settling a bill. Spotty acceptance of foreign credit cards and regulations requiring a Chinese bank account in order to use mobile payments have made things worse. AliPay and WeChat Pay, China’s dominant payment services, this week announced plans to address this problem. Ant Financial, owner of AliPay and an affiliate of Alibaba, will offer a top-up service for travelers. Wechat owner Tencent is working on ways to connect its platform with credit cards issued outside of China. Not only will that make China a bit easier for visitors, it could also give AliPay and Wechat a boost in their efforts to win more customers abroad.
