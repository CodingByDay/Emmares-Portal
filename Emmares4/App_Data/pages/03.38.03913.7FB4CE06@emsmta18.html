Grumpous asks: If a black hole is an object so dense it creates a funnel in SpaceTime, how do they keep getting larger?

Another Monday morning headspinner for me, Grumpous. Your question was much longer than this, but a lot of others have asked about black hole sizes, so let's hit that this week. I'm sorry to disappoint, but this is an area of astrophysics still being investigated. Supermassive black holes, at the center of galaxies, can be hundreds of thousands of times more massive than our sun... Their size is unfathomable. But how do they get that size? Black holes feast on the matter in their vicinity and slowly gobble it up. Over time, galaxies collide with one another, bringing more food to the black hole's table. This is one way black holes might get larger. However, oddly enough, they also shrink over time (a long... long... long time) by losing Hawking radiation to space (named after famed astrophysicist Stephen Hawking). Eventually, this will cause them to wink out of existence, the theory goes. But this process would take longer than the entire age of the universe to occur! Wild.
