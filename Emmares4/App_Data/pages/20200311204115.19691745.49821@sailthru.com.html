Virus 'worse than 9/11' for hotels

Hotels around the U.S. are suffering a "triple punch to the gut," industry execs say, as the coronavirus crisis forces more would-be travelers to stay put. On Tuesday, the WHO declared the Covid-19 outbreak a pandemic, and the U.S.'s top infectious-disease expert said it was 10 times deadlier than the flu. Now, after weeks of conference cancellations and corporate travel restrictions, hotels are eyeing hiring freezes, cutting amenities and pulling their 2020 earings forecasts amid future booking doubts—especially as spring breakers cancel their trips left and right. “This is just the beginning of the tidal wave,” said one owner, "the bottom is falling out.”

More:

  • Room revenue for U.S. hotels was down 12% from last year but major cities saw much bigger drops, including by 46% in San Francisco, 35% in Seattle and 20% in New York.
  • On Tuesday, a group of 150 travel organizations published a letter seeking to reassure Americans that it's still safe to travel and warn that an excess of caution would threaten the U.S. economy.

This is not normal

Weaponizing water. Odd weather is wreaking havoc on the freshwater-rich Himalayas, as snow cover shrinks, glaciers melt, and the monsoon season shifts, creating a political tool among nations reliant on the region.

The future is now

"Midnight Zone." Scientists are set to embark on a mission 1,000 meters below the Indian Ocean in a cutting-edge sub that dives deeper than Mt. Everest is tall and has LED lights powerful enough to mimic the sun.

What's good

Dirty work. A Hong Kong public transit company is deploying remote-operated, disinfecting robots to its fleet of subway trains that can kill bacteria and viruses, including Covid-19, in difficult-to-reach areas.
