"Sell in May"? Maybe … maybe not. With the S&P 500 index coming off of three straight months of gains and trading in record-high territory, the old “Sell in May and go away” adage seems all the more timely this year. But should you really worry about a portfolio reassessment right now? It depends. While May is one of the worst months in terms of average returns for the S&P 500, per Yardeni Research, perhaps "It should be 'buy in May and sell July/August,'" says Stephen Suttmeier, chief equity technical strategist at Bank of America Merrill Lynch. "Monthly seasonality suggests selling in the strong month of April, buying weakness in the risk-off month of May and selling in July to August, ahead of September, which is the weakest month of the year." Jeff Carbone, managing partner for Cornerstone Wealth, views “Sell in May” from more of a sector perspective this year, advising investors to take profits from stronger-performing sectors such as financials, energy and technology, and adding defensive positions in sectors such as utilities and consumer staples. Our advice? Guidance like this is great for seasoned traders and those who prefer to be more active with their portfolios. But if you’re a buy-and-holder, ignore “sell in May” altogether –it’s a headache you don’t need.

Over one million jobs were likely added in April, a government report is likely to show this Friday, according to the editors of The Kiplinger Letter. The unemployment rate should dip below 6.0%. In fact, the strong demand for workers in a reopening economy should be evident in continued declines of the number of workers unemployed for less than six months, and in the number forced to work only part time because of slack business conditions.

Manufacturers are still being dogged by parts and materials shortages, and a shipping crunch. Orders backlogs are at a record high. A shortage of computer chips has forced a cutback in production of some motor vehicles, electronics, appliances, etc. Steel, copper and lumber are also hard to come by. And of course, where there are shortages, there are rising prices. Prices of these items are at record highs. Shipping rates are also 50% above normal for most of the country because of a shortage of truck capacity.
