Micromobbin'

Following in the footsteps of Lyft and its partner Uber, Lime launched a subscription plan for its shared electric scooters. Called LimePass, riders who subscribe get access to unlimited free scooter unlocks, instead of paying $1/€1 per unlock, for a week at a time.

LimePass is now available in the U.S., Australia and New Zealand. Lime plans to roll out the pass to additional markets early next month. The cost of LimePass varies by market, but Lime says most U.S. cities will cost $4.99. That means, in order for it to be worth it, you would need to ride Lime scooters at least five times a week. It’s a smart move for Lime because the company will still make money on each ride, and perhaps incentivize people to even ride more.

Meanwhile, Skip is gearing up to appeal San Francisco’s decision to deny its permit application to operate shared electric scooters in the city. That hearing is on Jan. 23, 2020. Also in San Francisco, the city allowed Jump, Spin, Lime and Scoot to increase their fleet sizes. It appears that the geofences of these services have also expanded along with the number of scooters.

Coming into next year, we can also expect Uber to “double down on micromobility.”

A win for autonomous delivery

In the ecosystem of autonomous vehicle technology startups, delivery is one area that we expect to hear more about, especially 30 days from now. Here’s why.

California has decided to allow the testing and commercial use of light-duty autonomous delivery vehicles on public roads, provided companies have the proper permit from the state’s Department of Motor Vehicles.

“The adoption of these regulations means Californians soon could receive deliveries from an autonomous vehicle provided the company fulfills the requirements,” DMV Director Steve Gordon said in a statement. “As always, public safety is our primary focus.”

The revised regulations, approved Monday by the Office of Administrative Law, will let companies operate an autonomous delivery vehicle as long as it weighs less than 10,001 pounds. This means qualifying vehicles can be passenger cars, mid-sized pickup trucks and cargo vans carrying goods such as pizza or groceries, the DMV said. Class 3 through Class 8 vehicles — which includes semi-trucks and walk-in delivery trucks — don’t qualify.

Companies must apply for a deployment (commercial use) permit with the DMV to charge a delivery fee. The DMV can begin approving new applications in 30 days from its December 17 announcement.

Meanwhile, other “traditional” delivery startups continue to raise money and make deals.

  • Spain’s Glovo, an on-demand delivery app platform which operates in Europe, Latin America and Africa delivering food but also other urban conveniences from groceries to pharmaceuticals, has bagged another €150 million (~$166M) in a fast-following Series E round led by Abu Dhabi state investment company, Mubadala.
  • Manna, a B2B drone delivery “as-a-service” company, announced an additional funding round of $3 million, led by Dynamo VC, a logistics-focused fund. The move brings Manna’s total seed funding to $5.2 million.
  • Uber is in advanced stages of talks to sell its food delivery service Uber Eats in India to local rival Zomato as the American ride-hailing giant looks to cut its global spending, three people familiar with the matter told TechCrunch. The deal values Uber Eats’  India business at around $400 million, one of the sources said. As part of the deal, Uber may invest between $150 to $200 million in Zomato and get a sizable stake in the 11-year-old Indian firm, people said.

Deal of the week

Only one deal belongs here this week — and that’s the proposed mega merger between Fiat Chrysler and Peugeot, which will create the world’s fourth largest automaker by sales volume. (Why do these things always happen while I’m on vacation?)

There is plenty to analyze and unpack with this deal. But let’s focus on the why, the potential wins and remaining weak spots that this combined company will face if the merger is finalized.

The combined company will produce some $4.1 billion in cost savings without closing factories, according to PSA and FCA. Those cost savings, some 80% of which are expected to occur in the first four years, will be directed towards autonomous vehicles, electrification and other mobility technology.

What’s driving this? There is a growing desire among automakers to consolidate, or form partnerships, in an environment of increasing regulatory pressure, declining sales and rising costs associated with next-generation technologies such as autonomous vehicle technology.

The pursuit of autonomous vehicle technology, an area where FCA and PSA trail their peers, is expensive. So is electrification, which an increasing number of automakers are focusing on. Together, FCA and PSA might not win at the electric or autonomous vehicle game. But they stand a much better chance of not losing.

Here’s some information the companies shared about how they plan to redirect the cost savings.

There are weak spots, namely that bringing two big companies together is risky and promises to be loaded with challenges. The companies could prevail, but we have all seen failed automotive mergers before. Remember Daimler’s merger with Chrysler?

Sonnet X

Death be not proud, though some have called thee

Die not, poore death, nor yet canst thou kill me.

— Donne

The opening lines of Sonnet X seemed relevant this week with the news — and the official reaction from corporate — about the demise of Share Now (known as car2go) in North America.

Share Now is the result of Daimler and BMW deciding to join their mobility services into one company. After that tie up occurred in March, a lot of rebranding ensued. Daimler’s car2go, which had some success in North America, became Share Now. BMW’s car-sharing service ReachNow was folded into this new company.

These joined forces were apparently not strong enough to withstand the “volatile state of the global mobility landscape” and Share Now announced this week that it is pulling out of North America as of February 29, 2020. In North America, Share Now operated in Montreal, New York, Seattle, Washington, D.C. and Vancouver.

Share Now said the decision was made based on two extremely complicated realities. Cutting through the company-speak it sounds like parking, and the associated cost to that infrastructure issue, was an issue.

“While we had remained hopeful that we would be able to come to a solution – especially these last few months – we are ultimately not in a position to commit to the level of investment necessary to make the North American market successful both in the near and long term,” the company said in a statement.

Ah but wait, it’s not just North America that is problematic. Share Now will also cease operating in three European cities, Florence, Italy, London and Brussels. Instead, the focus will switch to remaining European cities where it operates and where it thinks the service remains viable.

Share Now’s decision to shut down in some cities in order to survive in others isn’t quite as bold as Donne thumbing his nose at death. But it does show an effort, and perhaps a futile one, to stave off death a little longer.

Uber's legal woes

Uber keeps hitting legal snags in Europe. This time, as reporter Natasha Lomas writes, a regional court in Frankfurt, Germany banned the company from sending ride-hailing requests to rental car companies via its app.

The upshot: The court’s ruling essentially outlaws Uber’s current model in Germany. Uber can appeal.

Meanwhile, back in the U.S., Uber agreed to pay a $4.4 million fine to settle a 2017 charge from the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission pertaining to sex discrimination and retaliation.

The investigation by the EEOC, which launched the investigation following reports pertaining to Uber’s workplace while under the leadership of then CEO Travis Kalanick, found reasonable cause to believe that the company “permitted a culture of sexual harassment and retaliation against individuals who complained about such harassment.”

Uber is an interesting beast. It has inspired both love and hate, not to mention popularizing an economy built off of contract workers, not employees. It’s also sparked a rush of other startups focused on the idea of ride-hailing, each with its own twist.

Take Blacklane, for example. The Berlin-based transportation-on-demand startup focuses on the higher end of the market, targeting corporate workers, executives and those who have the means to pay extra for higher levels of service when they travel.

I bring up Blacklane because CEO Jens Wohltorf said something in an interview with TechCrunch editor Ingrid Lunden that I found notable.

“In the first couple of years, VCs always asked me the question of how we would react to the big ride-hailing players. How do we compete, and how could we be more similar?” said Wohltorf. “Today, it’s changed 180 degrees. Now the question is ‘what’s your strategy to be different?’”

To be clear, Blacklane is rare creature in the ride-hailing world: it’s quiet and growing at a healthy rate.

I’ll leave you with some numbers to chew on.

Nearly $81 billion has been raised by 428 ride-sharing startups, according to data from Crunchbase. About 75% of that is concentrated in about 10 companies led by Uber and Didi, which have raised around $25 billion and $21 billion respectively. That top 10 list also includes Grab, Lyft, Ola, Chinese trucking company Manbang, and bike companies like Ofo, HelloBike and Meituan.

Take the rest of the funding and distribute it amongst the field and you get about $48 million per startup, all things being equal. Of course, all things are not equal in the industry. The disparity between the likes of the big 10 and smaller startups like Blacklane are evidence enough. Still, Blacklane shows that the industry is still evolving and there’s room, albeit just a sliver, for new startups to carve out a market.
