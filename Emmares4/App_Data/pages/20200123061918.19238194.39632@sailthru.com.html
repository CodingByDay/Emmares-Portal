In climate news today...
  • U.S. firefighters died in a plane crash battling a blaze in Australia.
  • Britain is told to  transform its land use to hit 2050 emissions goals.
  • Aviation risks being the climate “villain,” Rolls-Royce’s CEO warns.
  • China’s solar industry is bracing for a steep cut in state subsidies. 

Four days ago, China said it will ban single-use plastics in supermarkets, shopping malls and food delivery services, just a few years after reshaping the world’s recycled plastics trade. A day later, the Bank of International Settlements said climate change is a “green swan” and could cause the next financial crisis. Plastic bags and central banks, different though they may be, are useful parameters for the next decade in business, finance and climate. 

The BIS book is rich with the language of “Pigovian taxation” and “Gaussian-centered distribution.” But it’s also straightforward in laying out how climate risks are deeply uncertain and non-linear. Bloomberg Opinion’s Liam Denning places China’s unprecedented bag ban in that green swan framework as a “fiat dislocation that’s hard to model.”

We know more about climate risk than the BIS suggests. We know what causes climate risks; we know which sectors, countries and transformations of matter create greenhouse gas emissions; we know what company activities contribute to climate change, and where, and why. Green swans have green parameters.

Three big sectors are similar in terms of total impact. Power and heat emit barely more than agriculture, forestry and land use. Industry is much bigger than transport. Those are the parameters to which green swans can flock, the physical domains in which they will manifest (or wreak havoc). I’ll offer another lens on the above chart: it also addresses the areas in which companies can create new decarbonization pathways and new business opportunities.

We should distinguish between those where pathways are visible and likely, given today’s technology, and those where they’re much less certain. Electricity and transport are the most visible (see BloombergNEF expectations of the global power sector and road transport). Industry is harder to change, for many chemical and thermal reasons, and so is agriculture. The technical term for sectors where it’s hard to reduce emissions is “hard to abate.” That doesn’t mean impossible, and it also makes clear where innovation is necessary.

We have an emerging sense of climate change as real, present and profound. You can see that in Australia, where after weeks of wildfires, environment is now the most important issue. You can see it in brand engagement in the U.S., where a PRWeek/Morning Consult study found that the environment and climate change are areas where the public wants brands to speak out more.

You can even see it in changing dating behaviors. Dating app OKCupid now allows users to filter out “anyone who thinks climate change is fake news,” as Emily Atkin notes.

Two recent business surveys show the corporate imperative to address global warming. Deloitte’s latest quarterly CFO survey (see page 19) has a module specifically on climate change, which shows that employees are the biggest source of pressure:

PWC surveyed CEOs, finding the benefit they see in “investing in climate change initiatives” has doubled since 2010 (see page 36).

Note too how the carbon-negative plans of Microsoft and Starbucks emphasize how important employees will be in the effort. We can imagine how critical those efforts will be to their retraining and attracting those employees, too.

I’ll end by returning to those green swans. Some investors see black swan events as opportunities, in that they offer far-outsized returns for taking bets against expectations of “the standard framework of Gaussian-centered distributions.” Some investors are making a similar green swan bet at the hard science, hard-to-abate end of things: Obvious Ventures is raising math-nerd amounts of new money for its energy, mobility and resources thesis; Real estate fund Fifth Wall is raising $200 million for its Carbon Impact Fund.

All of this is why Kara Swisher, one of Silicon Valley’s most trenchant observers, says she has hope for the next decade and a prediction that “the world’s first trillionaire will be a green-tech entrepreneur.” Green swans may happen, but they’ll have green parameters, perhaps with opportunities as outsized as the potential risks.
