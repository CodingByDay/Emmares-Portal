Good afternoon from Los Angeles, wherever you may be, and happy Sunday to all my Laker fans out there. 

Coronavirus has supplanted the presidential campaign as the one topic we must address during the first or last 10 minutes of a conversation. I will leave the impact on the broader economy to people much smarter than me. But this was the week it began upending the entertainment business in a big way.

MGM postponed the release of “No Time to Die,” the latest James Bond movie, a move that could cost the studio an estimated $30 million. While that’s the first major release shelved, it won’t be the last. Theaters are closing all over the world, and U.S. movie studios rely on foreign markets for the majority of their sales.

(For what it's worth, CNN’s Frank Pallotta predicted Universal’s “F9”  (aka Fast and Furious 9) will be next. That's a good guess since that franchise in particular relies almost on viewers overseas.)

Two of the biggest music festivals in North America, Ultra in Miami and South by Southwest in Austin, announced they would not take place this month. The next big question mark is Coachella, which takes place in the California desert across two weekends in April.

It seems inevitable, especially when you look at it through the eyes of James Hamblin, a staff writer at The Atlantic:

Italy has banned weddings and funerals for 16 million people. Japan has closed schools for a month. France and Iraq have banned public gatherings. The U.N. has canceled all physical meetings to address climate change. Americans are uncertain what to do about Coachella.

And yet, we know what this is all about: money! South by Southwest waited on the city of Austin to cancel the event so it could collect insurance money. I have to imagine Goldenvoice, the promoter of Coachella, is waiting for a similar moment. I have to imagine because Coachella has yet to say anything (or even respond to my calls and emails).

The loss of major live events underscores the economic cost of a major epidemic. The cost is not to fat cats in corporate offices, but the people who actually work on them – the below the lines folks relying on a new TV show or movie to pay the bills, the traveling band hoping SXSW would be their big break, the bars and restaurants that serve visitors to Miami or a crew in Italy.

And yet, whether you are relaxed about the virus' potential impact to your well--being, or a doomsday prepper, we should be able to agree that canceling live events is a no-brainer until the virus is under control. 

One final word on media coverage: Hot take culture was not built for global disasters. The best thing anyone in the media can do is be honest about what they do and don't know, warn people about legitimate dangers while reassuring them so as not to stoke panic. In my case, I know very little. So take care of yourself, stay calm and eat well. – Lucas Shaw

Spotify’s new pitch has the music industry on edge

Justin Bieber

Photographer: Mike Windle/Getty Images

Spotify is asking record labels and artists to pay to advertise their songs within its app, arguing that they’ll reach new fans and increase their popularity. The service has already introduced one tool that allows artists to advertise new releases and is pitching a second that would charge them for data and analytics.

The effort is controversial because Spotify, which offers promotion and data for free, is now asking musicians to pay for them. Spotify is also diluting music rightsholders’ share of its sales in the process.

But this is the very reason Spotify is attempting this. The company is under pressure to prove to investors that it can turn a profit, and reduce the share of its sales that go to music companies.

Musicians Want to Save the Planet. Uh Oh.

A growing number of musicians are taking action to make their tours less destructive to the environment. Angelina Rascouet and I looked at the demands, which range from banning plastic water bottles to planting trees.

But – a big but -- there’s almost no way of avoiding carbon emissions produced by a tour, which involves moving hundreds of people and tons of equipment across large distances. And since it’s also the single biggest source of income for most acts, hitting the road is inevitable for bands of all sizes.

Lisa Pomerantz, who books travel for acts including Jack Johnson and Childish Gambino, says that real change will require action by venue owners, concert promoters, and the fans. Having an artist, say, ban water bottles or plastic straws from their dressing room goes only so far. “Right now,” she says, “it’s like putting lipstick on a pig.”

Pixar stumbles out of the gate

Photographer: Pixar

“Onward” is the No. 1 movie in the world, grossing $68 million globally this weekend. It grossed $40 million in North America, which is the slightest domestic start for a Pixar movie since "The Good Dinosaur.”

This will embolden experts who project Disney will have a down year at the box office. The last two years, Disney has released Pixar sequels (“Toy Story 4,” “Incredibles 2”) that topped $1 billion worldwide.

Five stories you may have missed

  1. ViacomCBS is shopping Simon & Schuster. The book publisher, whose authors include Stephen King and Jenny Han, is expected to fetch at least $1.2 billion.
  2. Quibi raised another $750 million, and announced its first slate of programs. The short-form video app will feature 50 shows when it debuts April 6.
  3. Twitch will surpass 40 million users in the U.S. next year, according to eMarketer. A nice reminder that the site, owned by Amazon, is a fraction of the size of YouTube, and smaller than TIkTok and Snapchat.
  4. Hachette decided not to publish Woody Allen’s memoir after members of its staff protested. Allen has been accused of sexual assault by his daughter Dylan Farrow.
  5. The Directors’ Guild of America signed a new deal with major Hollywood studios, eliminating one potential ally for writers in their contract negotiations.
