The Pfizer vaccine is only 64% effective at preventing infection by the new Covid-19 Delta variant, according to new data from Israel, down from 95% against the original version of the virus. But it is still 94% effective in preventing severe illness. In other words, it’s possible that the U.S. could see a future infection spike, similar to the one that the U.K. is experiencing, but that the hospitalization and death rate should remain low. The primary danger is that a spread of infections will hit the unvaccinated hard, as it is already doing in Missouri and Arkansas. About a third of the U.S. adult population remains unvaccinated, in many cases by choice. The economic impact could be that even the vaccinated may be hesitant to travel to, or engage in normal activities, in areas that are seeing outbreaks.

Moderna has announced that it has begun a trial of its mRNA flu vaccine with 180 participants. The next frontier for mRNA vaccines is their potential application to other viruses and diseases. Current flu vaccines tend to be only 40% to 60% effective because researchers must choose which strain to target six to nine months in advance. mRNA technology will cut that time gap significantly, making it more likely that the correct strain can be targeted.

Howard Silverblatt, senior index analyst for S&P Dow Jones Indices, has just released his Q2 report on S&P 500 dividends, and the data looks good … albeit not great. Common-stock dividend increases collectively came to $15.4 billion across the index -- more than 130% better than the $6.7 billion delivered in Q2 2020, but admittedly 24.2% slower than the $20.3 billion in Q1 2021.

That said, the median percentage increase in Q2 was 8.3%, up from both 4.8% in Q2 2020, and 7.7% in Q1 2021. Most importantly, investors are still well on their way to another highwater mark in dividends collected. “Based on continued vaccination and reopening,” Silverblatt says, “dividend payments are expected to increase 5% for 2021, which would be sufficient to set a new annual record payment (a potential 10th consecutive year of record payments).” He wryly adds that the projection also is greater than the average non-executive wage change for the year.
