Overloads
 
Sending a high number of players into the same area in order to overload the opposition's players

If you're coming up against rock-solid defences or your attempts to play out from the back aren't working, overloads may just be the answer to your problems.
 
The use of overloads can often help to beat even the very best defences. Overloads can allow your players to draw out defenders, create a large range of passing options, and make piercing runs in behind the opposition's back line. Drilling your players on overloads can help to significantly improve their play in tight areas, helping them to make the most out of a range of scenarios all over the pitch. Most importantly, coaching your players on overloads encourages them to play creatively and positively at all times. These sessions can be run regularly, and aspects of them can be incorporated into a number of drills in order to ensure overloads become second nature for your players.

Though for some coaches the idea of using overloads can seem daunting, they're an invaluable skill that the best coaches utilise every game. Additionally, coaching your players on overloads stands to benefit both your attacking and defending players.
 
Today's Sessions
 
 
Overloading the midfield
 
This session focuses on creating and exploiting receiving angles and overloads in midfield to play the ball with quality behind the opposition defence.

In elite football and in elite youth development, ball management metrics such as achieving pass completion rates of around 80 per cent at high ball speeds under very high pressure, and the ability to accelerate and decelerate to get pressure on the ball and change direction at high speed over 1,000 times per game while still being able to manipulate the ball with quality, all require regular training. The practices here allow for high repetition in functional ways in order to develop, refine and maintain these actions and enhance performance in matches.
 
 
Switching play and creating wide overloads
 
This is a possession session and it’s focused on switching play to create overloads in wide areas. It is designed so that players have to make decisions on when and how to switch play. It also works on the tactical demands on the players to create overloads once the ball comes across the pitch.

Creating overloads is an important part of both our game plan and our playing system at Sheffield United. For us, it’s important to come up with different activities to help us work on this aspect of our play, especially when the practice is specifically geared towards bringing out the topic.We can run this kind of session on any day of the week. 
 
 
Creating overloads in tight spaces
 
This training session is all about creating overloads in tight spaces when in possession. Three is the magic number here and this session encourages players to use the three-player support system to create overloads.

Players are fully engaged due to the simplicity of trying to support the ball with a minimum of three players and it also helps to improve receiving and releasing skills and will encourage quick play.

The session progresses into a game-related 11v11 practice where players can implement what they have learnt from the opening activity.
 
 
Attacking narrow with overloads
 
This session covers many aspects of quick and narrow attacking play in central areas, concentrating on making attacks in and around the penalty box.

It develops the ability to take on defenders in 2v1s and progresses to decision making in other kinds of overloads, showing players when and where to shoot and what type of shot to use. It also teaches them when and where to cross and what type of cross to use, as well as working on combination play in tight areas and showing attackers how to unlock tight defences.
