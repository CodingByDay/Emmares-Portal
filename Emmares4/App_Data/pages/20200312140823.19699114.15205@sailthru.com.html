“Cancel culture” has taken on a whole new meaning. In an effort to limit the spread of the virus that causes Covid-19, the NBA has canceled games, the NCAA has canceled fans and the Council on Foreign Relations even canceled a conference about the coronavirus itself. Italy, which just closed most stores to limit spread of the pathogen, is among 15 nations to have shuttered all schools. In the U.S., Seattle schools are closing, too.

For single working parents, and for households where both parents are employed, school closure is a logistical and financial nightmare, especially for women, who often end up sacrificing work and taking responsibility for childcare. Women are also disproportionately on the front lines of the coronavirus response—as nurses, flight attendants, teachers and service industry workers.

Not all interruptions to normal life constitute a crisis, however. Who would object if the U.S. government delays the income tax deadline? And for the wealthiest New Yorkers, the crisis has provided an excuse to leave the less lucky behind as they flee to homes in the Hamptons. —Philip Gray

Did you see this? 

House Democrats introduced a bill to offer paid sick leave and other aid to soften the blow to Americans from coronavirus. President Donald Trump, whose widely criticized speech Wednesday night helped send the stock market to new depths, rejected the proposed legislation, saying it contained unneeded “goodies.” Congressional Republicans oppose it, too. They don’t want companies to pay for sick leave, but they also don’t want the government to pay for increases in healthcare for the poor. 

There’s something big standing between Trump and a second term: a gender gap.

A shift in #MeToo justice: Harvey Weinstein, the former movie mogul convicted of rape, has been sentenced to 23 years in prison.

Goldman Sachs Asset Management says it will insist that board slates worldwide include at least one woman. Facebook’s board is now 40% female.

A fund in South Africa aims to boost participation of black entrepreneurs after their exclusion during apartheid.

A pandemic just beginning to be felt 

The global response to the pandemic won’t be short-lived. Bloomberg Businessweek calls the outbreak a “wealth destroyer” likely to be felt for generations. But it’s also an opportunity for economies to evolve, because of the growing number of businesses telling everyone who can work from home to do so. This group includes 60% of people in management, business and financial occupations. Meanwhile, in this moment of “social distancing,” even religious services are moving online.
