Hi all, it’s Eric. We’re two years into the revolt of rank-and-file employees in Silicon Valley, and their bosses seem to be realizing what they’re up against.

This week, Bloomberg News reported that Alphabet Inc.'s Google had created a browser surveillance tool to automatically identify staffers who create calendar events involving more than 10 rooms or 100 people. Employees are accusing the company of attempting to monitor their efforts to organize protests or discuss labor rights. Google says these claims are “categorically false.” It gives a more innocent explanation: It’s just trying to stop employees from spamming their colleagues with invitations to extraneous meetings.

Either way, the suspicion over the tool shows just how much trust has broken down between Google’s management and the most restive portions of its workforce. In May, the company tried to cut down on leaks by reminding employees they could be fired for accessing sensitive information. In August, it posted new internal rules discouraging workers from debating politics at the office.  

Good luck with that. Over the last two years coders have begun defying their bosses to engage in labor organizing. They’re leaking stealth plans to launch censored search engines in China, and demanding an end to cooperation with armies, border patrol agencies and the fossil fuel industry. Google in particular is in the midst of an open revolution, which has included literal protests. Even Facebook Inc., whose workforce has been happier to toe the company line, finally had a major leak that caused political headaches for Chief Executive Officer Mark Zuckerberg.

Who can really blame these employees for being disgruntled? Their bosses’ decisions are inspiring fury from Congress. They’ve paid out mutli-million dollar bonuses to alleged sexual harassers, and rely on huge contract workforces, who do their jobs under conditions many full-time employees view as exploitative. These CEOs either seem to insist on retaining absolute power in face of calls that they retire (Facebook), are getting rich while employees face layoffs (Uber and WeWork), or they’ve basically disappeared off the face of the Earth (Google).

Yes, the life of a Silicon Valley coder is pretty cushy. They make lots of money in some of the most glamorous cities in the world, and to top it off, they usually get free lunches.

Many of these employees would acknowledge their coddled, pampered lifestyles. And that mix of financial independence and high expectations has actually made them well-situated to push back against their employers. The favorite Democratic presidential candidates of tech workers are Elizabeth Warren and Bernie Sanders—the two candidates most inclined to break up tech workers’ employers. Some of the biggest checks to Warren came from the type of elite coder who's made a lot of money working in tech, without becoming a top executive.

But employers can make the lives of activists less comfortable. The leaders of Google’s protests have been steadily leaving the company. And those who have stayed are apparently worried about booking conference rooms.

Google employees derided the tool with a Harry Potter reference, comparing it to Professor Dolores Umbridge’s move to restrict student groups. “Google decree number 24: no employee organization or meeting with over 100 participants may exist without the knowledge and approval of the high inquisitor,” read one meme posted to an internal message board.

The question now is what employees will do when protesting becomes less comfortable. As Google edges out activist leaders and monitors employee groups, will employees lose faith? Or will Google’s version of Dumbledore’s Army have better mettle? —Eric Newcomer
