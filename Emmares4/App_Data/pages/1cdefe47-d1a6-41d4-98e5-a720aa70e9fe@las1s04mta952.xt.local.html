In the States

FIRST IN MC: SENATOR REQUESTS GAO RANSOMWARE REVIEW — Washington is still at a loss for how to help state and local governments under a scourge of ransomware attacks, as your MC host recently reported. Now, New Hampshire Sen. Maggie Hassan, the top Democrat on the Senate Homeland Security Subcommittee on Federal Spending Oversight and Emergency Management, is asking a federal auditor and investigator to look into what agencies are doing, and how.

"We seek a review by the Government Accountability Office to describe federal efforts to assist state and local government entities in protecting their networks and systems from the threat of ransomware and in responding to ransomware incidents," she wrote in a letter Thursday. "This review should also evaluate whether federal agencies involved in these efforts are effectively coordinating with each other."

PUBLIC CONFESSIONAL BOOTH — Acting White House chief of staff Mick Mulvaney told reporters on Thursday the administration froze $400 million in Ukraine aid in part because Trump wanted Ukraine to investigate a Democratic National Committee server. "Did he also mention to me in [the] past the corruption related to the DNC server? Absolutely," Mulvaney said of a conversation he had with Trump. "No question about that. But that's it, and that's why we held up the money," he said in part of a longer answer about corruption in Ukraine.

When a reporter followed up by asking if the move was a quid pro quo, Mulvaney responded: "We do that all the time with foreign policy. ... Get over it. There is going to be political influence in foreign policy." His comments contrast with Trump's repeated denials of a quid pro quo taking place. According to a White House summary of a July 25 call, Trump asked Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky to investigate a conspiracy theory about the DNC email server hacked by Russian agents in 2016 maybe having been in Ukraine. Mulvaney later Thursday tried to walk back his remarks.

The Justice Department said it was in the dark. "If the White House was withholding aid in regards to the cooperation of any investigation at the Department of Justice, that is news to us," a senior DOJ official told reporters.

A POWERFUL VOICE, SILENCED — The death of Rep. Elijah Cummings (D-Md.) on Thursday leaves a big void on Capitol Hill, and because he was the longtime top Democrat or chairman of the Oversight panel, it leaves a big void on cybersecurity in particular. His legacy on probing the issue is too long to summarize, with the gruff but beloved lawmaker spending time this year alone asking questions about the Baltimore ransomware attack, Trump administration private emails and the president's communications with Russian President Vladimir Putin.

He was a driving force in seeking a Russian election hacking investigation , and he devoted considerable time to issues like the president's cellphone use, financial firms safeguards and archaic federal IT. Over the years he demanded answers on breaches at Equifax, the State Department, the Postal Service and so many more.

TOR SABOTAGE — ESET researchers revealed a campaign today that uses a shanghaied version of the official Tor browser to spy on darknet users and steal their bitcoin. Active for at least five years, the campaign mostly targets Russians and worms its way in by tricking users of the anonymous network with a notification that their version of the browser is outdated, the company said. The thieves have made off with at least $40,000 that ESET could discern but the amount is likely larger.

RLAs GET A HOT GIRL SUMMER — Pennsylvania on Thursday announced it was joining the short list of state and local governments conducting tests of risk-limiting audits. "This pilot project will allow us to explore audit procedures that will further strengthen Pennsylvania's election security profile and provide confidence to the voters that their votes are being counted accurately," acting Pennsylvania Secretary of State Kathy Boockvar said of the November project.

The tests will happen in Mercer County and Philadelphia using new paper-based systems in partnership with the Pennsylvania Department of State, along with experts from the Election Assistance Commission, the Brennan Center for Justice, Common Cause Pennsylvania, Democracy Fund, Michigan University, VotingWorks and Verified Voting.

Lobby Watch

HOW MUCH HUAWEI SPENT TO HIRE MICHAEL ESPOSITO — From our friends at Influence: Huawei paid the lobbying firm Federal Advocates more than $1.6 million to lobby the administration between July 24 and Sept. 30, according to a disclosure filing. Michael Esposito, Federal Advocates' president and a fundraiser for Trump's reelection campaign and the Republican National Committee, lobbied the White House and the Commerce Department for Huawei, according to the filing.

Huawei isn't the first Chinese company to spend big on efforts to influence the Trump administration. ZTE spent millions of dollars on Washington lobbyists, including former Sens. Joe Lieberman and Norm Coleman. Still, $1.6 million is an extraordinary amount of money for a company to pay a single lobbyist for a little more than two months of work. For comparison, Qualcomm spent $1.3 million in the second quarter of the year to retain a team of eight lobbyists at the law firm Covington & Burling in one of the biggest contracts on K Street.

Huawei's fortunes in Washington may be improving, though. The New York Times reported last week that the administration "plans to soon issue licenses allowing some American companies to supply nonsensitive goods" to Huawei, which would be a victory for the company. Huawei also retains the law and lobbying firm Squire Patton Boggs. Huawei paid the firm $80,000 for its work between Aug. 30 and Sept. 30, according to a disclosure filing.

WHAT SENATE APPROPRIATORS WANT FOR STATE DEPARTMENT — The Senate Appropriations Committee report for the fiscal 2020 State-Foreign Operations spending bill directs Foggy Bottom to share with appropriators any attempts to breach, and successful breaches of, the department's classified and unclassified systems. The first report is due 45 days after the bill becomes law, with quarterly updates thereafter.

The committee report also instructs the National Endowment for Democracy, a democracy promotion nonprofit, to produce a report on what kind of funding, strategy and other resources are needed to modernize election assistance in areas such as foreign interference, media manipulation, quantum computing and sophisticated cyber threats.
