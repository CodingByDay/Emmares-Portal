April 29, 2021

Dear Reader,

For decades, astrophysicists have struggled to explain the existence of ultrahigh energy cosmic rays — subatomic particles carrying as much energy as a well-hit tennis ball that every now and then strike our planet from parts unknown. What could possible produce these extremely energetic particles? The answer may be uncovered via a new generation of radio-based searches for neutrinos, as our lead story details. Read on for stories about new limits on the Earth’s supply of space dust, the beginnings of a new Chinese space station, and more. This week, we also mourn the passing of Michael Collins, the “forgotten astronaut” of Apollo 11 who after that historic mission became one of the most thoughtful chroniclers of the early space age.

Lee Billings, Senior Editor, Space & Physics

QUOTE OF THE DAY

"I am alone now, truly alone, and absolutely isolated from any known life. I am it. If a count were taken, the score would be three billion plus two over on the other side of the moon, and one plus God knows what on this side."

Apollo 11 astronaut Michael Collins, who died yesterday at the age of 90, on his lonely sojourn piloting the orbiting command module while Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin explored the lunar surface.
