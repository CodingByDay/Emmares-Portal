What a week. Throughout the Western world, we’ve all had to make significant, uncomfortable adjustments to our daily lives in an effort to slow the spread of the deadly coronavirus. There is apparently no limit to the ways in which normalcy will be redefined. For many manufacturers, that has included heeding the growing call to repurpose factories and engineering talents to increase production of vital medical products needed to combat the outbreak. Corporate America and its brethren overseas take a lot of flak (including from me), but at a time when the world needs them, companies from Airbus SE to General Electric Co., 3M Co., General Motors Co. and LVMH have raised their hands and stepped up, and I for one am extremely grateful. 

While it’s hard to spot any kind of light at the end of the tunnel in the U.S. and Europe right now, China is offering some encouraging signs of a swift rebound in demand for everything from flights to factory work as the number of new cases in the country decline. We can only hope to follow a similar pattern in the West. The nature of the demand may evolve — perhaps companies rethink how much business travel is really essential, for example — but I’m not especially convinced of that yet. It seems more likely that the fundamental change is going to come behind the scenes in the ways companies run their businesses and allocate capital. This may come about as a result of changes in the perception of what is prudent, or because the government mandates a rethink, much as it did when it bailed out the banks in the financial crisis. I pulled out two broad themes that are top of mind for me right now for the industrial sector, but I know there are many others and I’d love to hear your thoughts as well. 

SUPPLY CHAINS: I wrote a few weeks ago about how the coronavirus could provide further impetus to unravel far-flung, globalized parts networks that had already been challenged by the U.S.-China trade war. One reader suggested at the time that the idea of optimizing costs and production capabilities across various geographies wasn’t broken, but rather the dependency on China was what needed to be rethought. Certainly, relying on China for goods was a liability in January and early February, but relying on domestic or European factories is now just as much of a crap shoot. 

You could make an argument that the coronavirus crisis actually reinforces the importance of globalization and healthy relationships with trading partners. Despite warnings from various health-care experts, the Trump administration placed tariffs on nearly $5 billion of medical products — about 26% of all U.S. imports in this category — as part of its trade war with China, Chad Bown of the Peterson Institute for International Economics wrote in a March 13 note reported on by Bloomberg News’s Shawn Donnan. While exemptions were granted this month for products including masks and protective gloves, the measures are late, temporary and incomplete, with the result being that American customers are likely the last choice for Chinese suppliers, Bown wrote. This shift in the trade balance has made us more dependent on Europe for some key supplies at a time when the region is starting to restrict the flow of goods to protect its own people. It would have been helpful if the White House hadn’t spent the past few years badgering our allies with tariffs. 

Rather than rethinking where factories are located, companies may be more likely to reimagine what those factories look like. Robots can’t get coronavirus, for example, and the shift toward automation should only accelerate. Industrial software can help companies optimize productivity even if they’re operating with skeletal work crews and can allow them to better anticipate when spare parts may be needed so they can plan ahead for extended freight times. And we may be witnessing the dawn of a golden age for 3D printing. As companies race to deepen the world’s supply of medical products, some are turning to 3D printing, known as additive manufacturing in the industrial world, as an easier path to production than retooling machinery, particularly when it comes to heavily-regulated devices such as ventilators. Vauxhall, Airbus and Renishaw Plc are among those planning to use 3D printing to make ventilator parts for the U.K., according to the Guardian and the Financial Times. It’s easy to envision the technology being used more by governments seeking to ensure adequate stockpiles for the next pandemic as well as companies hoping to better gird their supply-chains from disruption. Never have the working capital benefits of 3D printing been on greater display. 

BUYBACKS, JOB CUTS: Way back in August — when our biggest problems were a trade war and an inverted yield curve (how quaint) — I wondered whether manufacturing companies would be able to use the traditional tools of buybacks and layoffs to respond to a recession in the current political era. The situation we’re now facing is beyond anything that could have been imagined, but these questions remain relevant. As it stands, CEOs appear to be doing everything they can to avoid mass job cuts and the animosity toward share buybacks is at a fever pitch, particularly for any company now seeking for a bailout. While we have seen significant layoffs at overseas airlines including Qantas Airways Ltd. and Norwegian Air Shuttle ASA, their U.S. counterparts have been more surgical, relying so far on voluntary temporary leave arrangements, early-retirement options and hiring freezes. There seems to be a general interest in not piling on to the economic malaise that’s less avoidable for small businesses and restaurants forced to close their doors on government orders. CEOs “seem to understand that the most important thing they can do to help the country get through the crisis is to keep putting money into the pockets of their employees — even if they don’t have much to do,” writes my Bloomberg Opinion colleague Joe Nocera. Boeing Co. is reportedly evaluating layoffs among other cash-preservation measures, but its sizable workforce is its greatest leverage in pressuring the federal government into giving it a bailout. So it seems likely to hold on to that bargaining chip as long as it can. 

Any company that takes government money is likely going to have to give something up in return. At a minimum, that includes deluxe pay packages for the CEO and board, bulky dividend payouts and any hope of resuming share repurchases in the near future. The biggest U.S. airlines have been criticized for spending 96% of their free cash flow over the past decade on buybacks, and the $40 billion-plus that Boeing spent on repurchases is coming under much-deserved scrutiny as well. Stunningly, Nikki Haley, the former ambassador to the United Nations and governor of South Carolina, stepped down from Boeing’s board this week because she disagreed with its pursuit of a $60 billion aid package for it and its suppliers. CNBC has reported that Congress also isn’t terribly keen on bailing Boeing out of a crisis that it arguably created through its own mistakes. While I agree it’s hard to find a less sympathetic company than one whose arrogance and misjudgment so recently led to the deaths of 346 people, I struggle to believe the country will actually let the company fail. And President Donald Trump has been more supportive of backstopping Boeing. He too, though, has said he would support bans on buybacks in exchange for funds.

Against that kind of backdrop, it’s going to be optically problematic for even healthier industrial companies to do big buybacks in response to the coronavirus slowdown. For example, Honeywell International Inc. is sitting on more than $10 billion of cash, but it will be difficult to justify share repurchases should the company participate in any broad bailout package for Boeing and its suppliers. Non-aerospace manufacturers have an easier path and I expect some will turn to repurchases, but the bar is clearly higher this time around and may be for the foreseeable future.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
GE has received U.S. regulatory approval for the sale of its biopharma business to Danaher Corp. for $21.4 billion. The deal is now expected to close March 31. This is a vital step in CEO Larry Culp’s efforts to reduce GE’s debt burden as the company weathers the coronavirus storm. The plunge in interest rates and asset values has likely ballooned its pension and long-term care insurance liabilities, while the market turmoil has strained the commercial-paper market that GE and many of its peers have historically relied on for short-term funding.

Lockheed Martin Corp. announced that CEO Marillyn Hewson is stepping down as of June 15 and will be replaced by board member and American Tower Corp. CEO James Taiclet. I am somewhat fascinated by the unusually large number of surprise CEO changes announced in this time of turmoil both in the industrial sector and elsewhere: United Parcel Service Inc., Bombardier Inc. and Walt Disney Co. are some recent other examples. It’s hard not to think that to some degree, these long-serving executives are looking to get out before the coronavirus fallout upends their legacies. While the new Lockheed CEO is an outsider, Hewson will stay on as executive chairman and Frank St. John, currently president of the rotary and mission-systems business, will become chief operating officer. 

KKR & Co. bucked the virus gloom and doom and announced a 4.2 billion pound ($4.9 billion) takeover of Pennon Group Plc’s waste-management arm Viridor Ltd. There was even an auction with other private equity and infrastructure funds! It appears dealmaking really is like a cockroach: whatever the situation, it will never die. It’s interesting that there’s so much activity in trash deals. Recall that garbage-hauler GFL Environmental Inc. boldly braved an initial public offering the first week of March. Bloomberg News points out that other infrastructure assets are also seeing high interest, with Macquarie Group Ltd. agreeing to buy the remaining stake it doesn’t already own in electricity distributor Viesgo and a number of firms making bids for a stake in Abu Dhabi National Oil Co.’s natural gas pipelines
