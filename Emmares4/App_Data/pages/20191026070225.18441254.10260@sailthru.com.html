Does the Trump administration seek to “decouple” from China? “The answer is a resounding ‘no’,” declares U.S. Vice President Mike Pence. Nor does the U.S. seek to contain Beijing, or pursue confrontation, he insists. Coming from the administration’s chief flame-thrower on China, these remarks, delivered in a speech at the Woodrow Wilson Center, count as an olive branch.

True, Pence admonished China over domestic human rights abuses and for exporting censorship, and he held up democratic Taiwan as a model of freedom for the mainland. He also repeated Trump’s unsupported boast that the U.S. “rebuilt China over the last 25 years,” as though the Chinese people themselves played no part in the construction project. But Pence aimed his real zingers at American companies, including Nike, for what he considers selling out U.S. values in the pursuit of profits in China. He accused the National Basketball Association of acting like “a wholly owned subsidiary of the authoritarian regime” by siding with the Chinese Communist Party in a row over a tweet by the Houston Rockets general manager in support of Hong Kong democracy protesters.

While Beijing responded sternly to his comments about human rights and democracy, the government did show some receptiveness when it came to deescalating trade tensions.

Indeed, after 18 months of conflict, the outlines of a new White House approach to China are becoming clear. Trump needs to patch things up just enough to sign a trade deal with President Xi Jinping in Chile next month and prevent the U.S. economy from tanking ahead of the 2020 elections. But he doesn’t want to go too far and expose himself to criticism from the growing ranks of China hawks in Washington. Pence’s somewhat toned-down rhetoric sought that elusive balance.

For their part, Chinese leaders are weighing the need for a truce as their own economy slows versus the imperative to preserve as much of its planned economy as possible. That includes the massive subsidies to state enterprises fueling Chinese technological ambitions.

The upshot of all these calculations is that Trump will likely do what he insisted he would never countenance: Sign a partial trade accord with China. The agreement will evade one of the most contentious issues—industrial subsidies—at the root of complaints by U.S. businesses about skewed competition. What will he get for his about-face? Chinese agricultural purchases worth an initial $20 billion a year, which is roughly where they were before the trade bust-up. There will also be Chinese concessions on forced technology transfers and intellectual property theft. Plus, China will further open its financial services markets.

So much for the “Phase One” deal. Critics will argue that it’s not a whole lot to show for a trade war that helped plunge manufacturing in America into recession and is crushing enthusiasm for business investment—a trade war started by Trump that’s almost driven the global economy off a cliff. Skeptics in Congress are bound to question monitoring arrangements for China’s pledges on intellectual property. Who knows whether the two sides will ever agree to more comprehensive “Phase Two” or “Phase Three” accords.

Nor will this mini-deal do much to resolve the U.S.-China security competition that underlies the trade problems. Listen to Mike Pompeo, the secretary of state, not Pence, on this subject. Pompeo is determined to keep China in its “proper place in the world.” That sounds an awful lot like a containment policy that Pence denies pursuing.

Zuckerberg gives up on China

Meanwhile, U.S.-China technology decoupling continues apace. Facebook co-founder Mark Zuckerberg (above) highlights the growing schism. For years, it seemed that the social media platform was ready to join the U.S. corporate kow-towers that Pence so roundly assailed in his speech. Zuckerberg even asked Xi to give his baby a Chinese name (Xi demurred) and famously took a jog in Tiananmen Square on a hazardously polluted morning. To access China would have required Facebook to censor its platform. But as Rupert Murdoch discovered a long time ago, Western media moguls cannot do enough to persuade China to open its markets.

Zuckerberg seems to have finally given up. Still, walking away from a potentially massive business opportunity in China has at least enabled him to sound more righteous in America, where Facebook is under attack over everything from political advertising to misinformation. “Now, we have more freedom to speak out and stand up for the values that we believe in and fight for free expression around the world,” he told students at Georgetown University.

There are echoes here of Google’s decision to pull its search engine out of China in 2010 rather than censor its results. That decision burnished Google’s reputation in the West (“Don’t Be Evil” was its corporate motto at the time), although the company soon regretted its exit and has sought a return that would entail embracing an even more onerous version of the censorship regime it once rejected. 

Chinese students shun U.S. business schools

Anybody still unpersuaded that the U.S. and China are decoupling (despite what Pence says) should take a look at the latest data for applications by international students to elite U.S. business schools.

At the Tuck School of Business at Dartmouth College (above), they’re down 22.5% this year, largely as a result of U.S. visa restrictions. China is the biggest source of foreign students for these business programs. More and more Chinese students are instead opting for schools in Asia.
