Inside: The world's average temperature is rising faster than previously thought as COP25 began in Madrid. It's not easy being a carbon-neutral oil company. PG&E could have prevented California's deadliest wildfire. Investors press Brazil on soy deforestation. — Emily Chasan 

Sustainable Finance

As climate envoys from almost 200 nations gather in Madrid at COP25, all eyes are on the world's top polluter: China. A lot is on the line as average temperatures are rising so quickly the planet could be on a path to see global warming increase by as much as 5 degrees Celsius by the end of the century.  While U.S. President Donald Trump still plans to withdraw from the Paris climate agreement, the U.S. is still negotiating carbon trading rules at the event. Read more in Bloomberg's running update of what's happening at COP25.

Officials in Brussels are trying to advance some ambitious ideas about how to police the market for green investments. Money committed to address climate change has jumped 54% in the past year. New rules could specify when an investment is green — for example agriculture investments would have to specify they increase carbon storage, and energy companies would face more pressure over lifecycle emissions.

Billionaire Chris Hohn is urging fund managers to come clean about their contribution to global warming and their "appalling" proxy voting records. His $30 billion London-based firm TCI Fund Management sent letters to Moody's, Charter Communications and other companies demanding better emissions disclosure. 

More insurers are backing away from coal. At least 35 companies, representing $8.9 trillion in assets, now have coal-divestment policies, up from 19 in 2018, according to a report from the Unfriend Coal campaign. Axa was the latest to say it would curb coal underwriting. It also plans to double its green investment level to $26 billion by 2023.  

In Brief

  • Bank of England Governor Mark Carney accepted the role of UN special envoy for climate action and finances. 
  • BlackRock raised $1 billion for wind, solar and battery storage. 
  • The green bond tag is too much for some issuers, Moody's said. 
  • French financiers are resisting calls to divest fossil fuels.
  • Princeville Capital closed on its $300 million climate tech fund backed by Leonardo DiCaprio.
  • Circulate Capital has raised $106 million from plastics companies to tackle ocean plastic in Asia.  

Environment

Coastal damages alone could cost the U.K. economy more than 12 billion pounds ($15.4 billion) a year by 2050 if no action is taken to combat climate change, according to the WWF.

While global carbon emissions growth is slowing, emissions from burning fossil fuels likely increased by 0.6% this year. That's down from 2.1% in 2018, according to a report from the Australia-based Global Carbon Project, but it's not enough to keep climate goals on track. 

U.S. President Donald Trump’s administration asked a court to toss California’s lawsuit seeking to preserve its power to regulate tailpipe emissions, saying Washington has the last word in setting fuel economy standards. Meanwhile, Audi is restructuring and cutting jobs to revamp for electric vehicles and Hyundai plans to spend $17 billion on electric and driverless cars over the next six years. The move to electric vehicles could mean a loss of 80,000 auto jobs, particularly in Germany and the U.K. and U.S.

It's not easy being a carbon-neutral oil company, but Spain's Repsol took the occasion of COP25 to pledge it would become one by 2050. It wrote down its oil and gas assets by $5.3 billion this week. The oil company's plan relies on technology that hasn't been invented yet, said Bloomberg Opinion's Chris Hughes. Separately, Equinor said it will retreat from Norway's oil exploration plans in the Arctic.

More sectors of the economy are set to electrify as battery costs come down, according to the latest BloombergNEF Battery Price Survey. The market for lithium-ion batteries will increase about fivefold to $116 billion by 2030, even as average prices decline.

Social

Two weeks before a general election, the U.K. Labour Party stepped up attacks against five big companies, including Uber and Amazon, saying they “exploited, ripped off and dehumanized” their workers.

Disabled workers in London are paid 15% less than peers. 

Connecticut’s $36 billion public employee pension funds are divesting holdings in companies that manufacture guns and ammunition.

Governance

Investors with $3.2 trillion in assets, including Legal & General, sent a letter to Brazil President Jair Bolsonaro's government, urging it to hold up the country's 2006 soy deforestation pact.

Sasol, South Africa’s second-biggest polluter, angered some investors by refusing to put a resolution on its response to climate change to shareholders and could face legal challenges over the issue.

Bankrupt utility giant PG&E for years failed to properly inspect and maintain its transmission system — an oversight that led a live wire to fall and ignite the deadliest blaze in California history last year, a California Public Utilities Commission investigation found.

A group of government pension funds, including New York and Connecticut, said they will refile a shareholder proposal seeking an independent board chair at Facebook. 

New York City Comptroller Tom DiNapoli told Bloomberg's Global Responsible Investing Forum he prefers to be an investor over a divester  while JUST Capital CEO Martin Whittaker said top-performing companies tend to pay people better, have more women on boards, are more philanthropic and have a lower environmental impact.
