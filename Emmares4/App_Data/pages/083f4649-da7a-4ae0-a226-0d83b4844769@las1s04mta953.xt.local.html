Markup Spotlight

SHIELDS UP — The House Administration Committee will mark up a bill (H.R. 4617) today that would seek to shore up election security from foreign interference and disinformation campaigns. Zoe Lofgren (D-Calif.), the panel's chairwoman and bill sponsor, will offer a substitute amendment to reserve a section for elements outside committee jurisdiction, according to a committee spokesperson.

Republicans will put forth 10 amendments outlining where they say the bill fails to adequately address the interference carried out by Russia in the 2016 presidential election, according to a GOP spokesperson. The legislation includes provisions from two stand-alone bills: One ( H.R. 3281) that would seek to block deceptive practices in elections and another (H.R. 2592) that would bolster disclosure requirements for online political ads.

YOU CAN NEVER BREAK THE CHAIN — The House Homeland Security Committee today hosts leading government and industry officials to discuss how they're working together to secure the supply chain. Bob Kolasky, leader of the DHS National Risk Management Center, will testify alongside the co-chairs of the public-private Information Communications Technology Supply Chain Risk Management Task Force: Robert Mayer, senior vice president of cybersecurity at USTelecom, and John Miller, vice president of policy and senior counsel at the Information Technology Industry Council.

Panel leaders have a high opinion of Trump administration supply chain work, mostly — but will offer some caveats in their prepared opening remarks. Chairman Bennie Thompson (D-Miss.) will criticize the administration for banning China's ZTE then reversing course, "apparently to advance his trade agenda. ... Our national security is not a bargaining chip." And while the administration has taken steps against Russia-based Kaspersky and China-based Huawei, "We need to do a better job of identifying and prohibiting companies like these from infiltrating our supply chain," read the opening remarks of top panel Republican Mike Rogers of Alabama.

Miller will make some recommendations in prepared testimony, among them: make use of an ongoing compilation of federal risk management efforts to establish policy; narrowly tailor any laws or regulations to battle "concrete and identifiable risks"; enhance coordination between his task force and the Federal Acquisition Security Council; and deepen international collaboration.

2020 Watch

DEM DEBATE ON CYBER: SOME IS BETTER THAN NONE — Democrats made some brief mentions of election security during their latest debate Tuesday night, and even those brief mentions amounted to the most in any debate so far. Sen. Cory Booker said it wasn't acceptable for President Donald Trump not to call out Russia on 2016 interference. Former Vice President Joe Biden criticized Trump for believing Russian President Vladimir Putin over the U.S. intelligence community on whether the Kremlin meddled. Andrew Yang identified democracy-hacking as one of the technological challenges the U.S. must face.

Sen. Amy Klobuchar touted paper ballots, the election security measure that experts most frequently recommend. And Sen. Kamala Harris talked up her legislative efforts with Klobuchar on election security legislation and her desire to hold Putin accountable for 2016 interference.

AT LEAST SOMETIMES YOU GET A CAT — Digital Shadows found more than 550 fake election web domains set up against presidential candidates and other political sites using the practice of typosquatting, where the culprits exploit typos to redirect internet surfers elsewhere, the company revealed today.

The majority found after 2016 and through late September (68 percent) try to steer visitors of one campaign to others , such as "elizibethwarren.com" going to Trump's main campaign page. The next most common (24 percent) are harmless and sometimes even friendly, as with "eliabethwarren.com" alerting users they had a typo alongside a picture of a kitty cat. The remaining 8 percent are worrisome, with evidence they are designed to spread malware or are used for other nefarious purposes. Current international regulations make it difficult to discern who's behind the mischief, Digital Shadows said.

TIME IS MONEY — Business losses due to downtime from ransomware attacks are four times higher than the average ransom request, according to a report out this morning from the IT services firm Datto Inc. Attacks in 2019 cost small and medium-sized businesses an average of $141,000, compared with the average hacker demand of $5,900, the firm found. And the cost of downtime per incident jumped 200 percent over the average cost of $46,800 last year.

SEXTORTION BY BOT — The Phorpiex botnet, aka Trik, has spread malware for a decade. But it only recently started blasting out a sextortion spam, according to research out today from Check Point. The bot that has infected some 450,000 machines is sending bogus blackmail utilizing a database of leaked passwords connected to victims' email addresses. And the emails are alarming: "My malware gave me full access to all your accounts (see password above), full control over your computer and it also was possible to spy on you over your webcam." Of course, that's not true, but some people are paying. Check Point tracked almost $90,000 in payments via bitcoin. The sample extorsion note asked for $800 in bitcoin: "It's a very good offer, compared to all that horrible shit that will happen if I publish everything!"

SAY IT LOUD — Too many companies keep quiet after digital data theft occurs — and that isn't good for anyone, argues Robert Knake in a policy brief out today from the Council on Foreign Relations. If more companies go public about cyber-enabled intellectual property theft, it'll compel greater investment to protect against hackers and greater public awareness about the nature of threats, Knake writes. If victims stay mum, businesses will be less inclined to make serious investments to prevent intrusions. A senior fellow at CFR, Knake recommends the SEC require companies to report IP losses due to cyberattacks and to report on cybersecurity preparedness even if major breaches haven't happened. He also calls on the Trump administration to revise the cybersecurity Executive Order 13636 to make notification the default.

BEYOND CYBER — NATO convened its latest Information Assurance Symposium on Tuesday, expanding the three-day conference's purview to include topics like data and cloud computing. In his keynote address, NATO Communications and Information Agency General Manager Kevin Scheid announced that his organization will create a Data Science Center to centralize its information and will shoot to train 10,000 cyber experts over the next five years.

DIPLOMACY CORNER — A DHS official met with his Greek counterparts last week and discussed cybersecurity, according to a department summary released Tuesday. The meeting "covered evolving cybersecurity threats, emerging technologies, and the expansion of 5G," according to an account of the second United States-Greece Strategic Dialogue attended on Oct. 7 by Chad Wolf, who's serving as the DHS undersecretary of strategy, policy and plans.
