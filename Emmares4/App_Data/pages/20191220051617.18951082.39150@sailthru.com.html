The World According to USTs

The price action in Treasuries defined financial markets in 2019, making it a year of two thirds and one third: a rarely-relenting rally followed by a partial, choppy retracement. 

How other asset classes – especially stocks – reacted in response to the ebbs and flows of Treasuries was anything but stable during these different phases.

At the start of the year, the Federal Reserve’s recalibration of its balance sheet and interest-rate trajectory – before any 2019 cuts were being aggressively priced in – were crucial in providing some underpinning for the risk rebound. By mid-March, the 63-session correlation between five-year real yields and the S&P 500 Index was at its most negative since 2010. The implication: the Fed wasn’t going to tighten until something broke, removing one downside risk that preoccupied investors.

Soon thereafter, that narrative ran out of road. Worsening conditions in the global economy and a reignited trade war meant that a “bad news is bad news” backdrop dominated by May. 

That is, of course, until Fed Chairman Jerome Powell’s assurance in early June that the central bank would “act as appropriate” provided a month’s worth of further fuel. It saw the full suite of U.S. asset classes – stocks, government bonds, investment grade credit and junk – at all-time highs in early July.

From the start of the third quarter through the Fed's final interest rate cut of the year, investors were more or less able to set their watches to a negative correlation between stocks and bonds. Equity investors were even able to embrace a three-sigma shock to yields (to the upside) in September amid good news on activity and trade. That gave something of a sign that markets might be able to transition away from central bank life support – without much angst amid a backdrop of stabilizing economic data. In fact, equities soon needed yields to rise to be able to do the same.

For the final two months of the year, the correlation has waned and then waxed — with the dominant feature being a stock market that refuses to go down much, no matter what the bond market is doing.

Tip the Cap to Powell

The 2018 Fed talk about the potential need for restrictive policy, a “long way to go” in tightening, and the balance-sheet contraction being on “autopilot” contributed to that year's fourth-quarter market maelstrom. If blame for that can be laid at the Fed’s feet, then officials also deserve credit for the extent of the pivots made in 2019 to sustain the expansion: a full-fledged shift towards a proactive interest-rate and balance-sheet policy, albeit with a couple hiccups along the way for the latter.

The Fed acted as the world’s central bank in easing policy in response to developments abroad, aiming to limit the foreign weakness spilling over to domestic shores. And it indicated a willingness to keep rates low so long as inflation fails to rear its head. 

Here are two “mission accomplished” charts for Powell to close out the year: First, the spread between two-year and 10-year Treasury yields has uninverted, and hit its highest level of 2019 on Thursday – signaling that traders don't believe the kind of prolonged period of Fed easing that might be associated with a recession is required. In addition, interest-rate broker Albert Marquez flags a tumbling in the implied volatility of near-term Eurodollar futures, a sign that markets agree with the Fed that policy, and the economy, are indeed in a good place.

Secular Stagnation Entrenched

Even with yields well off their lows of the year, 2019 showcased global sovereign bonds as reflecting a low-growth, low-inflation future consistent with the secular-stagnation thesis.

Bigwig investors surveyed by the New York Fed see the federal funds rate averaging 1.94% over the next decade, the lowest reading in the survey’s five-year history and more than a full percentage point below the peak. That also implies that either a negative real rate will be the norm over the next 10 years, or the inflation part of the dual mandate won’t be achieved.

And even though traders have become less pessimistic about the prospect of the annual increase in the CPI gauge of inflation averaging less than 2% over the next five years, the cost of protection in the event price pressures exceed 2% over the same time frame remains dirt cheap. It's even lower than during 2015 and 2016, when oil’s collapse wreaked havoc on market-based measures of inflation expectations. 

Similarly, a variety of survey-based measures of U.S. inflation continued their drift lower.

While Treasury yields didn’t hit record lows in 2019, the average G-4 10-year yield (U.S., Germany, U.K., and Japan) did. At the front end, so too did the Fed discover the limits of divergence, and get closer to convergence on policy rates with its global peers. Coming into this year, this recoupling was “supposed” to happen the other way – with the other central banks starting to tighten policy.

Leaving 2019, the message from the bond market is that the near-term outlook isn't dire, but the scope for any meaningful improvements in activity and inflation over the long haul is slim.

Don’t Be a Basic Yield Curve-r

Yield-curve inversions have never really been a sign that an economic downturn is right around the corner, but 2019 would seem to reinforce the notion that spreads between different Treasury maturities are useful for forecasting what the Fed is going to do, if not the economy.  

Perhaps an element of Goodhart’s Law is at play (that is, “once a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure,”) effectively watering down the importance of the yield curve.

More likely, it’s important to recognize that the nature of the inversion matters. The Treasury curve bull-flattened into inversion and bear-steepened (or twist-steepened, for three-month/10-year) out of inversion. This is not the typical pre-recession pattern, and in fact the 1998 precedent would have told you that this phenomenon wouldn't be associated with a downturn.

 

Don’t Reason From a Price CCChange

Uncharitable observers might say that macro bears used the 3%-to-5% drawdowns in the junkiest of junk bonds in the way that the inebriated use a lamppost: for support rather than illumination.

A point this newsletter made throughout the year, particularly in the second half, was that an acute focus on CCC rated securities as a leading indicator for risk assets or the U.S. economy was consistently missing the forest for the trees.

Many of the struggles were centered on the energy sector: strip this out, and CCCs didn’t suffer a drawdown bigger than 2.3% in 2019. Even BBBs as a whole — the best performing grade this year — endured a bigger drawdown than CCCs ex-energy on a total-return basis. That’s also a reminder of how much duration was able to drive returns across the credit universe this year.

With all the handwringing over the highest of the high yielding, you wouldn’t guess that they’re poised to deliver high single-digit returns this year. This group of borrowers also retained solid access to the primary market through the year, even during rough patches for price performance, with only a handful of exceptions.

If anything, the lesson to be learned here is that the notion that the companies that survived the 2014-2016 crude crash were equipped to weather a prolonged stretch of low oil prices was off the mark.

In July, Michael Anderson at Citigroup Inc. made an argument as to why investors ought not treat this class of debt as pure beta. Of the 24 major CCC issuers he analyzed, he found 14 that were just too leveraged, while nine had seen the fundamentals of their industries meaningfully shaken.

“Whether it’s a rural hospital dealing with urbanization trends or a brick-and-mortar retailer fighting for relevance, the fate of much of the CCC universe is more a recovery-rate story,” he added. “Idiosyncracies are very likely to drive performance.” 

There’s the deep-dive micro view on why reasoning from the price moves in CCC bonds to get a sense of the economic outlook — in either direction — isn't advisable. 

Death of 60/40 Has Been Greatly Exaggerated

One big fear for portfolio managers is having to completely tear up the asset-allocation playbook – the idea that bonds won’t provide an effective offset to stocks in risk-off episodes.

Such concern was heightened heading into 2019, given that the year before had seen both stocks and bonds deliver negative returns, with the two biggest bouts of market turmoil linked to incidences of rates rising briskly.

This year should allay some anxiety on that front: the degree of protection provided by U.S. Treasuries was nothing short of amazing. The 10-year yield is down roughly 75 basis points year-to-date. However, if you count only sessions in which the S&P 500 fell at least 0.75%, the 10-year yield is down a whopping 140 basis points, and then some. Duration served as a highly effective cushion in the rare instances when things weren’t breaking equities’ way.

As such, a basic 60/40 portfolio is enjoying its best year since 1998 and AQR’s high-volatility risk parity fund is poised for its best year versus the S&P 500 on a total-return basis since inception. Inflation, obviously, would be the big potential disruptor to both asset classes, but it isn’t high on the list of imminent market boogeyman, having been in hiding for so long.

EurOwe

A major 2019 theme: borrowing in euros, with Deutsche Bank AG proclaiming the currency to be one where “everyone wants it as a liability.”  

Corporate America is taking over the European nonfinancial credit market with Reverse Yankee issuance.

Many sovereigns also took advantage of the low rates, and seemingly perpetually depressed currency. Such as China, which sold its first Eurobond in 15 years.

A resilient U.S. dollar and the start-of-year blowup in leveraged yen selling made the choice of funding currency for any carry trade much easier this year. It was tough to not make money in euro-funded carry in 2019.
