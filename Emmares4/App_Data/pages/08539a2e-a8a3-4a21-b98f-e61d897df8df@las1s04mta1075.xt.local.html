On the Hill

THE PARTY’S JUST GETTING STARTED — As one piece of critical infrastructure after another gets hit with a ransomware attack, many experts believe it’s no longer a question of if an industry is targeted, but when? “No industry is off limits,” Michael Daniel, president and chief executive of the nonprofit Cyber Threat Alliance, told Martin in his latest dispatch following last week’s ransomware attack on giant meat supplier JBS.

As Congress returns from the Memorial Day recess this week to a flurry of cyber hearings, the question of how to prevent the inevitable next set of attacks — and quickly — will loom large. First, Colonial Pipeline Chief Executive Joseph Blount testifies before the Senate and House on Tuesday and Wednesday, and then Senate Homeland on Thursday takes up the nominations of Jen Easterly to lead CISA and Chris Inglis to be the first national cyber director.

"The first thing we have to recognize is this is the reality, and we should assume, and businesses should assume, that these [ransomware] attacks are here to stay and, if anything, will intensify,” Commerce Secretary Gina Ramaindo said Sunday on ABC’s “This Week With George Stephanopoulos.”

Before buckling in, here’s a reminder of some of the questions that are top of mind for lawmakers:

— For Colonial, questions include whether companies should pay the ransom (which Colonial did), which agencies should oversee cybersecurity for pipeline operators (as House committees are tussling over whether that duty should stay at TSA or shift to DOE) and whether the TSA’s new reporting mandates for pipeline operators will be enough. Also expect questions about the logistics of the pipeline intrusion, with Bloomberg reporting late last week that hackers gained access through a compromised password that was used to log into a remote VPN.

— At the long-awaited nomination hearings, Easterly and Inglis will face questions staring down the entire administration right now: What needs to happen to improve interagency information sharing following an attack? How can CISA, an agency founded in 2018, bolster its reputation in the private sector so companies know when to turn to it? And how should the administration approach its growing Russia problem?
Ransomware

PAYMENT DECLINED — It’s time to ban paying ransoms to hackers, Energy Secretary Jennifer Granholm said Sunday on NBC’s “Meet the Press.”

“We need to send this strong message that paying a ransomware only exacerbates and accelerates this problem,” Granholm said, who added that she didn’t know if either Congress or President Joe Biden is ready to go that far. “You are encouraging the bad actors when that happens.”

— Typically, senior officials’ statements on ransomware have expressed understanding for why companies pay while repeating the FBI’s traditional advice for victims to keep their wallets closed. For instance, shortly following the attack on Colonial, White House cyber adviser Anne Neuberger told reporters she recognized that “victims of cyberattacks often face a very difficult situation” when determining whether to pay and said the administration is reviewing its policies on the issue.

While lawmakers aren’t quite ready for an outright ban, Senate Intelligence Chair Mark Warner (D-Va.) and Sen. Roy Blunt (R-Mo.), a member of the panel, said in separate interviews on “Meet the Press” that they were open to a debate and further regulation. “That’s a debate worth having,” Warner said. “I’m not sure what the answer is at this point.” Blunt said lawmakers have to do a “better job” of finding ways to trace ransomware payments, saying that “you can’t trace the ransom payment of choice now,” namely cryptocurrency.

— The private sector also appears open to the debate, at least. Ransomware Task Force co-chair Chris Painter told FedScoop in a recent interview that any such ban needs to be rolled out incrementally and should be paired with support systems for targeted companies, like a fund to help firms hit by ransomware.

A LOOMING ‘TERRORIST’ THREAT — Intentionally or not, FBI Director Christopher Wray’s recent remarks likening the ransomware threat to the agency’s response to 9/11 heightens the stakes for Biden as he prepares to address the issue during the upcoming G7 summit and meeting with Russian President Vladimir Putin.

“There’s a shared responsibility, not just across government agencies but across the private sector and even the average American,” Wray said in an interview with The Wall Street Journal published Friday.

This isn’t the first time the FBI has said rising cyber threats are similar to the Sept. 11 attacks, but Wray said the hacks of Colonial and JBS have highlighted the toll that attacks can have on everyday Americans. He said the bureau is reviewing 100 types of ransomware, many with Russian roots.

— Wray’s comments come after other Justice Department officials told Reuters late last week that ransomware will now receive a similar priority level as terrorism.

Putting ransomware into the terrorism bucket raises expectations for Biden to deal with the problem at this weekend’s G7 summit and his meeting with Putin next week, and as the administration weighs potential retaliatory measures against Russia for housing the two criminal gangs blamed for the Colonial and JBS attacks. “Responsible states do not harbor ransomware criminals,” White House press secretary Jen Psaki said Thursday.

And Psaki’s statement rang true for lawmakers and officials throughout the weekend: “Over the past 15 or 20 years, as we have had these series of cyberattacks from North Korea, from Russia, from China, we really haven't responded. We have been a cheap date,” Sen. Angus King (I-Maine), co-chair of the Cyberspace Solarium Commission, said Sunday on CNN’s “State of the Union.” “The adversary has to understand they will pay a price… and, thus far, they really haven't felt that.” (Not that we know, anyway.)

— Warner also used his “Meet the Press” appearance to call for establishing international cyber norms, which have been discussed widely in the global policy arena in recent years. Granholm said the president has been “working with our allies” and “focused on the international regime” around ransomware.

Not helping much: Putin on Friday said it was “absurd” to accuse Russia of having any involvement in the recent JBS attack and called the allegations an attempt to stir trouble before the summit. (The FBI has attributed the attack to ransomware-as-a-service group REvil, which is said to be based in Russia.)

Report Watch

COLONIAL’S LINGERING IMPACT — Cyber criminals have launched a new phishing campaign that takes advantage of the fears stirred by recent ransomware attacks, researchers at anti-phishing cyber firm INKY warned in a report Friday . In the campaign, perpetrators have sent emails from the “Help Desk” with instructions to download malicious malware disguised as a “ransomware system update.”

— The malware downloaded is “Cobalt Strike,” which was linked to two-thirds of ransomware attacks in one quarter last year, per Talos Intelligence. INKY did not disclose how many users were targeted or might have downloaded the malware, but clicking on the link in the email would cause the download to start in a new browser tab immediately.
