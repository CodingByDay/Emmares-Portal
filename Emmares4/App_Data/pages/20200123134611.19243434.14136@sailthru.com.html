What happens to tourist destinations if everyone stays home?

Mainland Chinese usually make up 80% of Hong Kong’s visitors, but protests against Beijing rule since June have scared them off. High-end retailers have lost many of their key customers—those big-spending shoppers from the mainland. After seven months of pro-democracy demonstrations, the city is suffering top to bottom as countless workers in the service sector lose their jobs. “The working classes all feel the pain now,” one Hong Kong professor says.

The Lunar New Year, falling on Saturday, is usually a bonanza of tourist dollars not just for Hong Kong, but all across China as hundreds of millions travel. But this year could be different: With the deadly coronavirus spreading from the mainland to Hong Kong, Thailand, Singapore and even the U.S., along with travel restrictions affecting millions, this year’s holiday may see a lot of people staying home. —Philip Gray

What is Bumble selling? 

Bumble, the dating app said to be “by women, for women,” might more aptly be described as “marketed to women," according to Bloomberg Businessweek. The company claims to empower female users, but it has no data to show that it does. Is it safer than other apps? Who knows: Bumble merely documents that it’s perceived to be safer. The only data Bumble could offer comparing levels of harassment among dating apps showed that women would be better off on Hinge or eHarmony. The feminist shtick has worked out well for Bumble, but it’s hard to say that it’s done anything for women.
