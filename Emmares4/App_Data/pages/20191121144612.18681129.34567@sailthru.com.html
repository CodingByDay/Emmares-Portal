China is a bit of a paradox when it comes to the climate crisis. It’s the world’s largest carbon emitter and has enough coal-fired power plants in the pipeline to match the entire capacity of the European Union, according to a new report. Yet it has spent more money than any other country on clean energy and provides cheap exports of green technology. During Bloomberg’s New Economy Forum in Beijing this week, China’s special representative for climate change affairs told an audience that the nation has sought a “multilateral system” on climate negotiations and is willing to work with the U.S. to rein in global warming. —Josh Petri

 
“As wealth gets more and more concentrated and more and more energy is used up by the few, the many become resentful and it undermines our sense of politics and a sense of community.”

—Former U.S. President Barack Obama addressing global economic inequality and climate change on Nov. 20 at a sustainable building conference.

 
 
Top stories

European airlines are preparing a two-pronged response to a growing public backlash over carbon emissions: Extending the use of offsets in the short-term, while embracing sustainable biofuels in the long-term. 

A small German search engine is working to counteract deforestation and forest fires by planting trees every time you browse the web.

Shoppers will soon be able to work out the true carbon footprint of their purchases, thanks to an app.

Great whales are the carbon-capture titans of the animal world, absorbing an average of 33 tons of C02 each throughout their lives. That’s equivalent to thousands of trees. A recent study put the value of each animal, taking into account the value of carbon sequestered, at $2 million.

Fire tornadoes—or “catastrophic pyrocumulonimbus wildfires”—are set to become a more common feature of the Australian landscape later this century as climate change continues to intensify. Not only will they occur more frequently, but earlier in the spring and summer. These devastating fires are so large and ferocious that they generate their own weather. 

 
What we’ve been reading

Denmark has emerged as a laboratory for sustainable living. Copenhagen is trying to become the first carbon-neutral capital by 2025. Leaders hope the Nordic nation will show the world how to go green without going bankrupt.

It’s almost Thanksgiving, so once again, it’s time to argue about (or avoid) the most pressing issues facing society. New polling data show most Americans intend to avoid the subject of climate change this holiday season. 

Coldplay has decided to stop touring after raising concerns over the environmental impact of their concerts. “We’re taking time over the next year or two to work out how our tour can not only be sustainable [but] how can it be actively beneficial,” said frontman Chris Martin.
