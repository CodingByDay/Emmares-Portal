Inequality Is for Companies, Too

Inequality isn’t just for people. It also afflicts companies. And the roots of corporate inequality could also have much to do with the deepening social inequality that is causing pain and anger around the world.

Two things bring these thoughts to mind. The first is the protests around the world, and particularly in Chile. These were sparked above all by inequality, as I wrote yesterday, and suggest that dissatisfaction with inequality is coming to a head.  The second is the remarkable statistic that just two companies, the eternal tech rivals Apple Inc. and Microsoft Corp., are now worth almost as much as the entire Russell 2000 index of smaller companies. ‘Twas not ever thus. The big get bigger and, evidently their share prices massively outperform the index that many take as the benchmark of the small-cap world in the U.S. Microsoft’s results after the bell on Wednesday didn’t create great excitement, but the chances are high that these lines could cross soon.

Such bigness has done no harm to Apple and Microsoft’s shareholders of late, but can it possibly be healthy? Tim Wu, of Columbia University’s law school, wrote a tract last year called “The Curse of Bigness,” which argued passionately for tighter antitrust enforcement. In a comment on my latest piece on Chile this morning, he made it clear that oligarchy and the concentrated power of companies was an essential part of the problem. The “present revolution has nothing to do with democracy or freedom, as the Western governments assert; France, Chile and Spain are all democracies,” he said. “Hong Kong is probably the freest place on earth. It is about oligarchy.”

Very similar arguments come from the current Bloomberg book club selection, “The Myth of Capitalism” by Jonathan Tepper, which we will be discussing online next month. He suggests that lack of antitrust enforcement has allowed companies to grow bigger and exploit their market power in a way that ensures a worse deal for their customers, and leads to the kind of social ills that are leading to unrest from Chile to many points north.

Building a strong competitive position was once viewed positively using Warren Buffett’s folksy phrase that he looked for companies that could “build a wide economic moat.” Now, it’s increasingly seen as exploiting an anti-competitive position. It’s also a political choice. To quote Chris Brightman, chief investment officer of Research Affiliates LLC: “The distribution between labor, capital and rent extraction is a political choice, not a force of nature. If we are going to have a decisive break with the present environment of the stock market rewarding companies that create moats rather than investing, it will require a change in our politics, and also in our regulatory policies.”

Is moat-building really damaging society? Andrew Lapthorne, chief quantitative global equity strategist at Societe Generale SA, published a big report on industry concentration two years ago, which introduced some possible qualifications to the argument. The numbers are now slightly out-of-date, but still relevant. If we look at the market share of the top five companies by revenue in each sector. we see that U.S. concentration is indeed increasing, but only after it reduced significantly during the economically vibrant (and merger-happy) decade of the 1990s:

Concentration grew after the dotcom bubble, then, but on this measure it isn’t so much worse than it was way back in 1990. And the U.S. isn’t the world. Using the Herfindahl index — a standard statistical measure where a higher figure shows greater concentration — we can see that that industries around the world grew less concentrated during the 1990s. But since then, while the U.S. has seen the biggest companies bulk up again, concentration has remained lower in the rest of the world. As many countries are emerging from a far more statist past, where government-dominated monopolies tended to control the economy, this isn’t necessarily surprising:

Meanwhile, we find that while the total number of U.S. stocks has shrunk significantly since the turn of the millennium, other countries have more than balanced this. This is true both by the total number of listed companies, and by the revenues they generate, where western Europe and Japan, in particular, have declined. 

Is concentration primarily an American phenomenon, then? And is it damaging? This chart from Lapthorne shows that the five biggest companies in each U.S. sector are indeed massively more profitable than the rest. This gap has widened significantly in the last 20 years. But the median profitability of “the rest” is not much lower than the global median profitability. 

The natural assumption is that the biggest companies are exploiting their market dominance and behaving in a collusive or oligopolistic fashion. But it may be that they are just exploiting their size to lever up in a way that other companies don’t, or can’t. I pointed out last week that U.S. companies had leveraged up post-crisis while European companies had deleveraged. It looks as though this phenomenon is primarily driven by the largest companies:

This isn’t a subject that will go away, and the book club selection will give us ample opportunity to discuss it further. But it’s possible at least to question whether excessive industry concentration is more than an American phenomenon, and whether it’s leading to truly abusive behavior. All feedback, as we keep reading Jonathan Tepper’s book, is most welcome.
