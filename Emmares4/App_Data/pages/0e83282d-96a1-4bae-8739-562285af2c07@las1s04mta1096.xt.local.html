Lobby Watch

SHOW ME THE MONEY — The U.S. Chamber of Commerce shelled out big money to lobby Washington last year ($58.2 million) and the fourth quarter ($16.9 million) on a whole slew issues including cybersecurity, according to its fourth-quarter lobbying disclosure filing out this week. "Cyber" is mentioned 28 times among its lobbying issues, including cybersecurity legislation, "China's intellectual property regime industrial policies and cybersecurity issues," international cybersecurity policy and "deepening business and governmental operational collaboration against foreign cyber threats." Other top spenders in the fourth quarter that lobbied on cybersecurity include the National Association of Manufacturers ($8.38 million), the NCTA — The Internet & Television Association ($4.6 million) and Boeing ($3.4 million).

Amazon and Facebook led the pack among tech companies that lobbied on cybersecurity in the fourth quarter at $4.21 million and $4.43 million, respectively. Both disclosure filings include lobbying issues related to encryption and data breaches. Google surprisingly ponied up less money than others at $2.7 million for the fourth quarter compared to $4.9 million in the same quarter last year.

LOOK OUT (IN A COUPLE DIFFERENT DIRECTIONS) — The FDA and DHS issued alerts Thursday warning about cybersecurity vulnerabilities in several types of GE patient monitors. The vulnerabilities "may allow an attacker to remotely take control of the medical device and to silence alarms, generate false alarms and interfere with alarms of patient monitors connected to these devices," according to the FDA alert . Both agencies said they were not aware of any cyberattacks resulting from the vulnerabilities, first discovered by CyberMDX. GE first issued an alert about the vulnerabilities in November.

Also this week, CISA said it was "aware of a recent increase in targeted Emotet malware attacks," referring to the sophisticated Trojan. The alert comes after Proofpoint warned of a new Emotet campaign targeting the U.S., Canada and Mexico, taking aim in particular at the pharmaceutical sector.

NOT US, NSO SAYS — NSO Group denied any connection to the news this week about Saudis allegedly hacking the phone of Amazon owner Jeff Bezos, after a technical report identified its malware as possibly playing a role. "NSO is shocked and appalled by the story that has been published with respect to alleged hacking of the phone of Mr. Jeff Bezos," the company said in a statement. "If this story is true, then it deserves a full investigation by all bodies providing such services to assure that their systems have not been used in this abuse. Just as we stated when these stories first surfaced months ago, we can say unequivocally that our technology was not used in this instance."

The spyware firm said it had implemented strict human rights policies: "As the first cyber company to undertake these type of policies we are happy to engage with the U.N., Mr. Bezos or any other body in trying to fully understand these issues and to set guidelines and capabilities to assure the protection of human rights in the sale and use of surveillance equipment."

STAMP OF APPROVAL — The centrist Blue Dog Coalition on Thursday endorsed bipartisan election security legislation that give NIST and the National Science Foundation more powers to defend elections from hackers. The group backed the Election Technology Research Act, H.R. 4990, which would expand the 2002 Help America Vote Act to regulate more types of election equipment and direct NIST to conduct additional security research. The bill, already approved by the House Science Committee, "will help to ensure that the technical underpinnings of our election architecture are solid," said Rep. Mikie Sherrill (D-N.J.), a Blue Dog member and the measure's chief sponsor. Last year the Coalition backed a package of election security bills.

CHATTERJEE ON HUAWEI — From our friends at Afternoon Energy: FERC Chairman Neil Chatterjee in a letter called Huawei an "increasing threat to our nation's energy supply chain." Chatterjee was responding to a bipartisan group of senators who asked FERC in December to consider banning Huawei from entering the U.S. solar market. Huawei, the world's largest maker of solar inverters, has rejected assertions that its equipment poses a risk to the U.S. electric grid . FERC recently established a cybersecurity division, and Chatterjee wrote that the commission will "continue to work with our partners in government and the private sector to curb the threat posed by malicious foreign actors."

THIS WE'LL ENCRYPT — U.S. Army soldiers were told to use encrypted messaging apps when they deployed to the Middle East as part of a troop surge meant to contain Iranian aggression, Military Times reported on Thursday. The leadership of Task Force Devil, part of the 82nd Airborne Division's 1st Brigade Combat Team, instructed service members to use Signal and Wickr for all communications to prevent enemy forces from intercepting any information that could compromise U.S. personnel. "These are the two apps recommended by our leadership, as they are encrypted and free for download and use," brigade spokesman Maj. Richard Foote told Military Times, adding that soldiers must connect to a military VPN to conduct "official business." Iran has frequently tried to spy on U.S. service members, including through fake Facebook accounts and fake, malware-infected job-search software.

THE REVIEWS ARE IN ON HOUSE E&C'S PRIVACY DRAFT — Today marks the deadline for interested parties to submit feedback on the House Energy and Commerce Committee's bipartisan staff-level privacy draft bill, which the panel circulated in late December. Ahead of that, our colleagues at Morning Tech touched base with an array of industry groups and consumer advocates that are submitting comments to take their temperature on the proposal. Here's what they said:

Privacy hawks say it lacks teeth. Dylan Gilbert, policy counsel at consumer group Public Knowledge, said certain provisions within the bill "afford companies too much latitude to dishonor consumers' and users' fundamental right to privacy online and take the teeth out of the law's enforcement and accountability mechanisms." And in a letter to the committee , the Electronic Privacy Information Center re-upped its call for the creation of a new data protection agency: "Current law and regulatory oversight in the United States is woefully inadequate to meet the challenges and creating a new Bureau of Privacy at the FTC will not solve this."

Industry groups find the disclosure requirements onerous. While consumer advocates are calling for strengthened transparency provisions on things like algorithmic decision making, trade groups are calling for narrower requirements. "Several provisions in the bill call for detailed descriptions and disclosures of data practices, algorithms, decision-making, inferences, and pricing strategies. Many of these decisions and practices are proprietary," said Jamie Boone, VP of Government Affairs at the Consumer Technology Association. "It is essential the text be updated to provide protection for confidential business information."

The two elephants in the room: Groups remain endlessly divided over whether a federal bill should fully override state laws and give consumers the right to bring lawsuits over privacy mishaps, two thorny disputes that House E&C staffers tabled in their draft. But there's seemingly universal agreement on one thing: The committee needs to overcome those issues to advance a bill — and fast. "We hope the committee will be in a position to quickly move a bill forward," said Craig Albright, VP of Legislative Strategy at BSA | The Software Alliance.
