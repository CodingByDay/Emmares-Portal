In climate news today...

The U.S. must do more to head off climate risks, Bank of England Governor Mark Carney told Bloomberg at Davos.

Spain has cut the use of coal at its power plants faster than anyone thought possible.

French oil and gas giant Total won a contract to install 20,000 car charging points in the Netherlands.

And the world’s most-profitable hedge fund is now a climate radical.

The global elite gathered in Switzerland this week for a different kind of Davos. Or at least that’s the picture some are trying to paint, making a very public show of balancing ideas tied to “stakeholder capitalism” with rising alarm about the risks of climate change. But stakeholder capitalism (or do we call it climate capitalism?) isn’t all about balance: The idea of a kinder, gentler company that incorporates the interests of employees, customers, the environment and shareholders will probably be more about ruthless prioritization. Some CEOs are saying they want governments to create more incentives for them to accomplish a green transition, but they may want to look closer to home before asking for a handout. At least one company has gotten the memo, though: This week, Airbnb sought to revamp its corporate governance ahead of an expected IPO by tying executive bonuses to “safety, sustainability, diversity and accountability.”

Some of the world’s largest asset managers committed to making their investment portfolios carbon neutral by 2050. While they’ve been more willing to suggest an end to using coal or fossil fuels as good business, carbon neutrality requires a new look at all sectors of the economy, from cement to cattle. Very few equity investment portfolios can say they’re currently aligned with a world that’s 1.5 to 2 degree Celsius warmer, so such a strategy will require more engagement and even more capital. Allianz is already asking sovereign wealth funds to join in the effort, and the World Economic Forum requested all companies attending Davos commit to a net-zero emissions target by 2050. 

Quant investors might be better at ESG. Anyone who’s really dug deep and tried to use environmental, social and governance data knows it can be a struggle. But some quant investors good at filling gaps in bad data think they may have found a workaround. 

Sustainable Finance In Brief

  • A ‘green swan’ climate event could trigger a global financial crisis, the Basel-based Bank of International Settlements warned. 
  • BlackRock Vice Chairman Philipp Hildebrand said policy and lawmakers will be key to fighting the climate crisis: It turns out CEOs don’t want to go it alone. 
  • Fidelity International’s CEO says active fund managers are the best placed to lead on climate issues. 
  • JPMorgan started a development finance group aimed at funding the United Nations’ sustainable development goals; Carlyle combined its global infrastructure, power and renewables teams.  
  • Japan’s GPIF pension fund said it invested $3 billion in green, sustainability and social bonds last year.

Carbon negative, resource positive

Giving more than you take is the new standard in corporate climate responsibility. Proclaiming that being carbon neutral is no longer enough, Microsoft last week set what’s perhaps the most ambitious climate target yet. The tech giant plans to spend $1 billion to remove all of its historical carbon emissions from the atmosphere and become “carbon negative” by 2030. That’s about 16 million tons of CO2 equivalent. If the software giant tried to capture that amount of carbon in a single year, it would have the same effect as increasing all U.S. forest acreage by 2.8%. And that’s just one company from a sector that’s historically not been all that carbon intensive. 

Hold the whipped cream. Starbucks announced plans to become “resource positive,” unveiling a move toward reusable cups while cutting water use and carbon emissions. The coffee retailer has faced shareholder pressure to do more about its environmental footprint. The top takeaway: Adding whipped cream to millions of Starbucks drinks emits 50 times as much greenhouse gas as the company’s private jet.  

Tesla created demand for electric cars, but it’s the only automaker that looks likely to benefit from it. 

The fashion industry’s take-and-dispose model is ready for a makeover. The sector could unlock $123 billion in value by addressing water use, energy, chemicals and waste, Barclays said in a new report. 

China unveiled a sweeping plan to curtail its plastic use and said it will start limiting non-degradable plastic bags in major cities.
