​​​​​Hey ya’all, it’s Austin. Over the last week, I’ve been dreaming about what Mickey Mouse could accomplish if he moved to Cupertino, California. Just days after resigning from Apple Inc.’s board, Walt Disney Co.’s Bob Iger floated the idea of a merger, in an excerpt from his upcoming memoir. The Disney chief executive officer wrote, “If Steve [Jobs] were still alive, we would have combined our companies, or at least discussed the possibility very seriously.”

With the streaming wars heating up, it’s an intriguing notion. As more technology companies enter the television arena, they’re hampered by a lack of content. Inversely, old-school media companies have struggled to attract tech-savvy cord cutters to their digital platforms. Iger’s what-if serves as a reminder of how much potential has been lost by each side of the equation undervaluing the other: At Apple, Jobs and Tim Cook long called TV a “hobby” before it became an area of “intense interest,” while Disney delayed its streaming ambitions before deciding to invest billions of dollars.

Apple and Disney will suddenly become competitors in a couple months, when they both introduce their new streaming video services. And, as Netflix Inc. CEO Reed Hastings put it, “It’s a whole new world starting in November.”

This hostile business environment is good for TV fans, who get lots of new shows, movies and gadgets at lower prices. But it’s proving costly for service providers, who are having to invest big in risky experiments. In recent days, Facebook Inc. introduced a Portal TV device; AT&T Inc. is considering spinning off DirecTV; Roku shares tanked after renewed fears over its set-top boxes’ distinctiveness; and NBC introduced its Netflix-rival, Peacock. Even dating-app Tinder announced it will be getting into original content.

Imagine what the landscape would look like today if Apple had acquired Disney. Jim Stewart, the New York Times columnist and author of the inside-the-Magic-Kingdom tome DisneyWar, argues that the companies should have formed a joint venture a long time ago. Apple, which just revealed that its streaming service will come free for a year to buyers of its new hardware, would have given Disney mass distribution, whereas Disney’s decades of film classics and modern blockbusters would have offered Apple a leg up over Amazon.com Inc., HBO and Netflix.

But it wasn’t meant to be. “There remains this kind of mutual underestimation on both sides of how valuable what each party brings: How valuable is content creation? How valuable is the distribution platform?” Stewart explained on CNBC. “The people who own the platform undervalue content creation, and vice versa.”

The biggest exception was a disastrous one: AOL-Time Warner’s $165 billion merger at the height of the dot-com bubble. Since then, tech companies have stayed away from big media acquisitions, leaving an opening for telecoms to snap them up. Meanwhile, Disney has focused on being the ultimate media juggernaut. Under Iger, Disney has acquired Lucasfilm, Marvel Entertainment, Pixar and iconic assets from 21st Century Fox, for a combined value of roughly $87 billion.

In Silicon Valley, where intellectual property is defined in terms of algorithms and design patents, it’s hard to imagine Apple dropping so much cash for the rights to Bart Simpson or Buzz Lightyear. Apple executives frequently talk about the “magic” of their products. As Apple faces calls for it to spend some of its $200 billion in cash to scoop up a Hollywood studio, perhaps this was the biggest underestimation of all: that the technology supporting its products is more magical than what’s on the screen.​​​​​​ —Austin Carr
