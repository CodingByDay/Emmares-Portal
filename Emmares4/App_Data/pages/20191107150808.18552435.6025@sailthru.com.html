OK, boomer—younger generations want to hear all about how hard it was for you to get your start. This week brought news that recent college graduates—the youngest millennials—are struggling to find work. The unemployment rate for college graduates aged 22 to 27 was 4% in September, higher than the average for all U.S. workers.

Meanwhile, rates of depression, hypertension and high cholesterol among millennials rose between 2014 and 2017, according to insurance companies. An economist said unemployment and poor health were intertwined, a “vicious cycle” for the generation.

There’s one consolation for millennials, however: Even if they have no hope of buying a home, at least they won’t face foreclosure. —Philip Gray

Did you see this? 

McDonald’s fired its chief executive, Steve Easterbrook, for violating company policy by having a romantic relationship with an employee. What does this mean for the future of home delivery for Big Macs? And for inappropriate workplace entanglements?  

The Bank of England ended its use of a term considered “racist, sexist and offensive.” Now the bank will refer to the quiet period of communication restrictions before an election as a ... “quiet period.” 

The neighborhoods where most Hong Kong residents live have been exposed to tear gas as police try to suppress democracy protests. The chemical—banned in warfare under the Geneva Protocol—drifts into homes in the densely populated city, sickening residents, Bloomberg Businessweek reports.

T-Mobile introduced a simple step to be more welcoming to trans individuals: Employees can now add their preferred pronouns, or “ask me my pronouns,” to their name tags.

Five deans of U.S. business schools explain how they are pitching MBAs when many prospective students see little upside to paying, in some cases, six-figure tuition. B-schools are scrambling, Bloomberg Businessweek reports, as applications plummet thanks to low unemployment and Trump administration immigration policies that are scaring away international students.

Housing in the Golden State costs more than twice the national average. Only an increase in supply is likely to ease the excess of demand in California: “Broadly speaking, there is no solution to the California housing crisis without the construction of millions of new houses,” one expert said. How did the state become America’s housing market nightmare?

Equal leave for equal parenting

Goldman Sachs raised the bar for parental leave at major Wall Street banks—extending paid time off from 16 weeks to 20 weeks. It also eliminated the distinction between “primary” and “secondary” caregivers, one way of trying to close the gap between how much leave men and women take to care for a new baby. (Because, as Bloomberg reported this summer, Wall Street dads find parental leave easier to get than to take.)
