Apparently without irony, the Census Bureau designated April 1 as Census Day, a national reminder that you should fill out your decennial questionnaire. (It’s easy: For most US residents there are only seven questions.) That the operation is taking place under what amounts to a near-nationwide lockdown is its own kind of black humor. And as Adrew Whitby writes this week on Backchannel, the census may never be the same in a post-Covid-19 world.

Traditionally, the government keeps track of any households that don’t voluntarily return a census form and sends an census-taker to those residences to get the information in person. Under pandemic restrictions, that approach may be unworkable. A possible alternative may be to use so-called administrative records—information about people already in government tax, school, and property databases.

The process of counting noses in the US has long driven technology forward (IBM even has its roots in an early census tabulation machine). Today, as a virus rages across the globe, the undertaking may end up tapping into technologies that already exist.

Mark Robinson | Features Editor, WIRED
