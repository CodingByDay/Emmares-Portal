Industrial companies have spent the last few years selling or spinning off anything that wasn’t nailed down, all in the name of becoming more focused and less vulnerable to economic swings. The Electrical Products Group Conference last May – a key annual industry event that as of now hasn’t been cancelled for 2020 – featured pitch after pitch from CEOs claiming that all this breakup work had positioned their companies for wider profit margins and even sales growth through a recession. Then the coronavirus came for China, and the rest of Asia, then Europe and the U.S. Now, with growth threatened worldwide, all of those assertions are about to be sorely tested.

Companies that have recently completed spinoffs – from Honeywell International Inc. and Danaher Corp. to Pentair Plc, Dover Corp. and DuPont de Nemours Inc. – have been dragged down with the rest of the sector. That’s not to say the businesses these companies parted with would be much of an asset right now. Would Honeywell be better off if it was still selling thermostats to consumers? Doubtful, although interestingly the Resideo Technologies Inc. business it spun off is only down about 5% since the virus sell-off began in earnest in mid-February, compared with Honeywell’s about 10% slump. Dover might be down about 15% over that time period, but its Apergy Corp. energy business is down more than 30%, so there’s no love lost there.

United Technologies Corp., though, couldn’t have asked for a worse environment to become a stand-alone aerospace company. United Airlines Holdings Inc., JetBlue Airways Corp. and Southwest Airlines Co. warned this week of a swift drop-off in demand for domestic U.S. flights, while Deutsche Lufthansa AG said it was cutting capacity by up to 50%. And there are questions about whether the softness and order

deferrals seen in wide-body jets may start to seep into the narrow-body market as well. The International Air Transport Association forecast as much as $113 billion in lost passenger revenue this year for airlines. Notably, Airbus SE didn’t sell a single plane in February, although it didn’t register any cancellations, either. Even the defense exposure United Technologies is gaining through its merger with Raytheon Co. doesn’t look entirely defensive. Lockheed Martin Corp. has instructed workers at an F-35 plant in Italy to work from home, and the Pratt & Whitney engine team in Cameri has been given similar instructions, according to Ellen Lord, undersecretary of defense for acquisition and sustainment. But the coronavirus fallout is likely to whack the Carrier climate control and Otis elevator divisions that United Tech is planning to spin off, too.

Overall, most of these breakups still make strategic sense, and given the benefit of hindsight, I think both CEOs and investors would repeat them. But perhaps breakups are better understood as a salve for a stock price held back by idiosyncratic challenges at a certain business in an otherwise positive environment, rather than a buffering effect for true, broad-based recessions. There’s still so much we don’t know about the coronavirus, including how quickly people will return to work and normal life if it’s contained. There are positive data points coming out of China: Reservations for domestic flights and hotels are recovering. Import and export cargo flows have rapidly rebounded back to normal volumes, according to CargoMetrics data reported by FreightWaves, but it remains to be seen if those levels will hold or if it’s more a reflection of clearing out backlog. The coronavirus is shaping up to be a true black swan event that simultaneously wreaks havoc on both supply chains and demand, making it harder for governments and central banks to steer the economy toward recovery. And in that kind of scenario, even the best-positioned companies are going to be challenged.

Consider Danaher. The company, now largely focused on life sciences and diagnostics, spun off its Fortive Corp. industrial-products business in 2016 in the name of focus and better access to capital for growth opportunities. It’s been widely regarded as one of the better breakups, and both companies have been winners for shareholders over the long term. But when it came to the coronavirus sell-off, investors weren’t all that discerning: both stocks were down in the range of 10%. Part of the problem there may be that neither company pays much of a dividend that might offer some stock support in a slump, and both companies may be limited in big share repurchases with Danaher set to buy General Electric Co.’s biopharma business for $21.4 billion and Fortive digesting its own string of acquisitions and a planned spinoff. 

For at least the present moment, Trane Technologies Plc, the former climate segment of Ingersoll-Rand Plc, looked to be the exception to the gloom and doom. Ingersoll-Rand completed a Reverse Morris Trust divestiture of its industrial business to Gardner Denver Holdings Inc. and started trading as Trane on March 2. While the shares have had their ups and downs, Trane is closing out the week up about 8%. That compares to the Morris Trust entity (now dubbed Ingersoll Rand Inc.), which is down more than 10%. A big part of Trane’s sales pitch to investors is that it has positioned its heating, cooling and refrigeration businesses to address the “unprecedented challenges of urbanization, natural resource scarcity and climate change.” Those longer-term trends aren’t going away, and arguably the coronavirus is the result of them, so that seems as decent a place to hide out as any. 

WEEKLY BOEING UPDATE
Oh dear. Boeing Co. CEO Dave Calhoun gave an interview to the New York Times and was startlingly candid in his assessment of the 737 Max crisis. The honesty is appreciated, although I don’t know that he did himself or the company any favors. “It’s more than I imagined it would be, honestly,” Calhoun said, describing the problems he is confronting. “And it speaks to the weaknesses of our leadership.” Having defended former CEO Dennis Muilenburg in November as having “done everything right” in managing the Max fiasco, Calhoun now characterizes him as the kind of person who would run “over the rainbow for the pot of gold on stock.” I wonder what gave that away. Was it the more than $30 billion that Muilenburg spent on buybacks over the course of his tenure, with the blessing of Calhoun and the rest of the board? Was it the widely reported push to coerce suppliers into handing over some of their lucrative profit margins? Or the decision to bid aggressively on a trio of recent military aircraft contracts on the risky assumption that the company would eventually build way more planes than the initial programs called for? Or the repeated overly optimistic timelines for the Max’s return, several of which had already fallen by the wayside when Calhoun made his November comments?

Obviously, Muilenburg made mistakes. But I would have thought it would be equally obvious by now that when you have served on the board of a company for a decade, you lose the right to point the finger at someone else. The real kicker is when the Times asked Calhoun why he didn’t forgo his $1.4 million annual salary, let alone the special $7 million bonus tied to strategic goals and a regular bonus of $2.5 million. “Cause I’m not sure I would have done it,” Calhoun said. What a profile in accountability. As for the Max itself, Federal Aviation Administration chief Steve Dickson said he expects the certification flight to occur in “a few weeks”, which is also what he said on Feb. 6. So do with that what you will. Canadian regulators are reportedly pushing for additional pilot guidance that may delay the plane’s return in that country.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Jack Welch, the influential CEO that made GE the world's most valuable company, died on Sunday at age 84. My colleague Joe Nocera’s great column about Welch’s impact on the idolization of shareholder value is worth a read. I want to spend some time thinking about his legacy at GE, specifically. On that front, it’s impossible to view Welch in a vacuum separate from the ill-advised decisions of his successor, Jeff Immelt. His death reminded me of a conversation I had with a long-time follower of GE when I first started covering the company. While Welch is blamed for creating the GE Capital behemoth that nearly strangled GE in the crisis, Immelt also pushed into risky lines of lending before later trying to rein in the financial arm, he told me. He pointed to liabilities tied to excessive interest rates for a consumer-finance business in Japan and the 2004 acquisition of the WMC subprime mortgage business, among other things. While Welch turned GE into the epitome of corporate sprawl, Immelt was no savant of capital allocation. Was he following Welch’s lead? Sure. But when you’re CEO for 16 years, at a certain point, things stop being the fault of the guy who came before.

It struck me that in some ways, GE under new CEO Larry Culp is becoming more like the GE of Jack Welch, not less so. Culp’s focus on the day-to-day of GE’s operations and frequent musings on the benefits of lean manufacturing is very Welch-like. So, too, is the company’s still overly complex relationship with numbers. Culp has made more progress in turning around GE than most imagined, but he did so in part by playing the game of Wall Street expectations and resetting the bar to incredibly low levels. While there have been improvements in disclosures, there remains a disconnect between profit and cash, as well as a variety of difficult-to-parse links between the capital arm and the corporate level. “One of the main issues we have underestimated here is the financial leeway the business model here provides in a normal environment, and the moving parts that can swing one year to the next that make things look better than what the trend line is,” JPMorgan Chase & Co. analyst Steve Tusa wrote in a March 2 report upgrading GE shares to neutral that coincidentally came out hours before news of Welch’s death did. Many more-intentional remembrances were written this week, but there was hardly a more fitting tribute to the man's legacy than that one.

M&A Crickets. Canceled flights and a global market meltdown don’t tend to be particularly conducive to getting deals across the finish line, so there’s not a lot to write about this week. It’s telling that the biggest announced transaction across any sector around the world was Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.’s $11.5 billion takeover of coronavirus test-maker Qiagen NV. Trash-hauler GFL Environmental Inc. boldly went ahead this week with the largest Canadian initial public offering in six years after canceling a previous attempt last year; it did not go well. The company raised $1.425 billion, less than initially targeted, and the shares have fallen more than 10%. U.K. industrial conglomerate Smiths Group Plc’s attempt to sell its $3 billion medical-equipment unit has reportedly stalled and the company may now go back to its original plan of spinning off the business.
