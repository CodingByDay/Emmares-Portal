For the real story on Boeing Co., look not at its own earnings commentary, but that of Southwest Airlines Co.

Southwest CEO Gary Kelly had a lot to say about Boeing’s 737 Max crisis when the airline reported third-quarter results this week. The jet’s more than seven-month grounding in the wake of two fatal crashes has wiped out $435 million from Southwest’s operating income. Southwest expects the damages to grow through 2020, given the length of time needed to reintroduce existing Max jets into its fleet and the pushout of fresh deliveries. Kelly made a repeated point – in the earnings release, on the call and in interviews with CNBC and Bloomberg Television – of noting the company hadn’t yet agreed on a settlement with Boeing.That’s notable in light of Boeing’s decision to hold its estimate for customer compensation at $5.6 billion, net of insurance, and avoid another big charge in the third quarter. “We did not see a change” this quarter in compensation liabilities, Boeing’s CFO Greg Smith said on the company’s own earnings call this week. “That does not mean that we could not see a change going forward.” You can say that again.

What may give Kelly more leverage is the news that Southwest is thinking more seriously about whether it's strategically responsible to operate an all-Boeing 737 fleet, as it has almost consistently since launching in the 1970s. Kelly said in July that changes to Southwest’s long-term fleet strategy needed to be “fully explored.” It appears there’s now a more formal review process in place, with the board set to examine in 2020 whether it makes sense to diversify and buy Airbus SE jets. Relying on a single plane model allows airlines to save money on parts and training, a significant enough benefit that it's quite rare for budget carriers to operate mixed fleets, my colleague David Fickling notes. But Kelly told Bloomberg News that the Max crisis “trips the wire” on a discussion over whether the risk of a future sidelining of a key plane outweighs those advantages.

This gets to the heart of an issue raised by S&P this week when it lowered its outlook on Boeing’s credit rating. Among other things, the firm noted the risk of a “lasting impact on Boeing's reputation” and “a further loss of market share.” The extent of the damage remains to be seen. Switching away from a single-model fleet would be such a radical move for Southwest that it's possible the real motivation for reviewing the policy is simply to frighten Boeing into offering better deals. Fickling points out that Ryanair Holdings Plc, the biggest 737 customer after Southwest, has made similar noises in recent months and may buy second-hand Airbus A320s from the liquidation of travel agency Thomas Cook Group Plc. But whether Boeing loses some of its profit margin or some of its customers, these appear to be early signs of a more severe commercial comeuppance for Boeing’s aerospace dominance than we’ve seen thus far.

The “early” fourth-quarter return of the Max that Boeing projected in July clearly isn’t happening. All major operators are now planning to go without the plane for the key holiday season, including Southwest, which isn’t scheduling flights on the Max until Feb. 8. Boeing still expects the plane to be cleared to fly before the end of 2019, but this is an accounting assumption used to determine the cost of producing the remaining jets in Boeing’s 737 backlog. It’s not a verdict on the regulatory process. Investors still breathed a sigh of relief at the general lack of fresh disappointments related to the Max in Boeing's earnings report. But I, for one, do not take a lot of comfort from Boeing’s plans to hold a “follow-up” call once it has more clarity on the plane’s return to service and the associated financial impact.

 

The prospect of the Max becoming even more of a political football has increased after the publication of instant messages that appear to indicate internal hesitation over a Max flight-control system years before that same software was implicated in the crashes. Boeing’s incomprehensible decision to turn over the troubling instant messages to the Justice Department in February – the month before the second crash – but withhold them from the FAA until recently has not endeared it to the regulator. A review of the messages by three experts indicates the concerns involved a bug in a flight simulator being tested, rather than the software itself, according to the Seattle Times. But that nuance may not help Boeing curb the political outrage when CEO Dennis Muilenburg testifies to Congress next week. A report this week by Indonesian investigators on the Lion Air crash faulted Boeing for making the flight-control system vulnerable to erroneous sensor readings and making incorrect assumptions on how pilots would respond to a system that they hadn’t been made aware of in training.

Southwest’s Kelly, for his part, told CNBC that a fourth-quarter return of the Max depended on the rest of the regulatory review process going “pretty perfectly” and that he wasn’t as confident as Boeing in the timeline. The real kicker in that CNBC interview came when Kelly was asked if he would like to see Boeing CEO Muilenburg replaced. “We’re not happy,” he replied, before going on to defend Boeing commercial airplanes chief Kevin McAllister who “inherited this mess” and stepped down this week. “We work less at the corporate level with Dennis,” Kelly said. “There’s no question that there have been a lot of issues that have come to light over the last year.” What a ringing endorsement.

THE GOOD, THE BAD AND THE UGLY IN EARNINGS
If you were looking for evidence of trade-war pain in this week’s batch of results from industrial companies, you certainly got it. Caterpillar Inc. reported its first quarterly earnings decline since 2016 as macroeconomic uncertainty kept customers on the sidelines; Stanley Black & Decker Co. cut its guidance as it continues to battle tariff and currency pressures; and 3M Co. is now on track for its worst annual organic sales growth since the financial crisis amid ongoing weakness in China and a deepening manufacturing slowdown. There was little sign of that “better-than-feared” mentality that came to some industrial companies’ rescue last week. Those that emerged with a positive share-price reaction from their earnings were the companies that successfully convinced investors that they could protect their earnings through a downturn, whether via share repurchases, production cuts or cost control. Either that or they are an aerospace or defense company.   

Raytheon Co.’s strong third-quarter results and guidance boost were an interesting contrast to the glum outlook for United Technologies Corp.’s Carrier unit, justifying United Technologies’ decision to merge with the former and spin off the latter. Raytheon’s recent win of a U.S. army contract to develop Patriot missile radar systems creates a $20 billion opportunity to sell the technology both at home and abroad, Chief Financial Officer Toby O’Brien said in a phone conversation after earnings. O'Brien will assume the same role at the combined company. On the aerospace side of things, while the environment for the industry remains strong, there is some cause for concern. Boeing plans to trim production of its 787 Dreamliner after expected orders from China failed to materialize. It also pushed out the first delivery of the 777X to 2021 amid issues with a General Electric Co. jet engine. The robust commercial aerospace cycle of the past few years has been underpinned by a relatively smooth regulatory process with global coordination. To that end, Boeing CEO Muilenburg’s concession that the FAA is taking “a little longer than originally planned” to assess its Max fixes raises the odds that future regulatory reviews will also run into delays.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Danaher Corp. warned this week that it doesn’t expect to receive regulatory approvals for its $21.4 billion purchase of General Electric Co.’s biopharma business until the first quarter of 2020. The companies had previously been aiming to close the deal before the end of the year. To help facilitate antitrust approval, Danaher is selling certain biomolecular-characterization, chromatography-hardware and other assets to Sartorius AG for $750 million. It’s a relatively minor divestiture for Danaher, which will be just fine. For GE, this delays a key cash infusion as the company races to reduce its debt load and stares down a looming industrial recession that could pressure its health-care and aviation businesses. GE is set to report results next week.

AMS AG renewed its 4 billion-euro ($4.4 billion) bid for Osram Licht AG after a team of rival private equity suitors dropped their pursuit “for the time being.” I just find this story fascinating; everything about it is the opposite of what you would expect. The hottest bidding war of 2019 is over a maker of automotive lights that was doing horribly, but maybe – if you squint your eyes and tilt your head slightly to the left – has the potential to benefit from the push into autonomous driving. Osram’s labor union is seriously ticked off that the company wasn’t able to sell itself to the private equity firms because it thinks they would have offered better job security (yes, you read that right). Not enough Osram investors voted in favor of the AMS bid the first time around, so it will drop the acceptance threshold. It’s pursuing its offer through a new bidding vehicle to avoid having to get the blessing of Osram’s board, much to the chagrin of the labor union and AMS’s own shareholders who still don’t really see the point of all this. I am not sorry that this weird story isn’t over yet.   

United Parcel Service Inc. caught investors off guard this week when it announced that chief operating officer Jim Barber would retire, removing one of the most logical successors to CEO David Abney. In an interview with Bloomberg News, Abney expressed confidence in UPS’s “deep bench” of leaders who can replace both Barber and himself. But I wonder if Barber’s departure in fact paves the way for another external hire. Over the past few years, Abney has gone to the outside to hire his chief financial officer, chief transformation officer, chief marketing officer and supply-chain logistics head. It’s just one of the many ways that UPS is distinguishing itself from its heavily internally-focused rival, FedEx Corp. UPS showed further improvement in its operating margin in third-quarter results released this week, and trimmed its capital-expenditure budget. That’s a sign that its push to invest in newer planes and automated systems is paying off and the company won’t need to spend as much as previously thought. Such progress remains elusive at FedEx.
