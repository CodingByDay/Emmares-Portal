Micromobbin'

This week, let’s jump over to India and check out Bounce, the Bangalore-based startup that just raised $105 million.

Bounce, formerly known as Metro Bikes, operates more than 20,000 electric and gasoline dockless bikes and scooters in nearly three dozen cities in India.

The company’s approach to growth is what got my attention. Bounce used to deploy its own operations team in each city and then flood the market with its scooters. It has changed that strategy and is now using the thousands of mom-and-pop shops in India to expand its reach.

The startup’s new approach is to launch in the cities with its own vehicles, but over time, these micro-entrepreneurs deploy their own bikes and scooters, according to co-founder and chief executive Vivekananda Hallekere. They are still using the Bounce app, and are part of the platform, but they don’t have to be locked into its scooter ecosystem.

Bounce has already partnered with more than 250,000 shops and merchants.

The startup isn’t alone in this approach. Amazon has partnered with more than 20,000 mom-and-pop stores in India to use them to store and deliver packages.

Meanwhile, Skip is preparing to appeal a decision by the San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency (SFMTA) that upended its business. In September, the company was not issued a permit to operate in San Francisco, which came as a surprise because it had previously received a permit to operate as part of the city’s pilot program.

Ahead of the appeal hearing, TechCrunch caught up with Skip CEO Sanjay Dastoor to learn about the company’s game plan and why he thinks it can prevail in a battle that other electric scooter providers have lost.

One more data thing. Startup advisor, policy wonk and writer David Zipper put out an interesting tweet this past week. It read: “Among micromobility co’s, only Bird is able to share vehicle/trip data in anything close to realtime. Multiple sources tell me that everyone else needs at least a few minutes to transmit data via MDS, GBFS etc.”

It’s possible that other companies have the backend architecture to do this. But here’s why it matters if they can’t.

MDS, which means Mobility Data Specification, is a digital tool developed by Los Angeles Department of Transportation to capture real-time and anonymized data on vehicles. MDS is mainly being used with dockless vehicles like scooters and electric bikes, but it could be expanded to manage ride-hailing or eventually autonomous vehicles.

As Zipper noted, the LADOT, which requires micromobility companies to share data, originally envisioned cities using MDS to exchange updated, realtime information and rules with mobility companies. “That doesn’t work with time lags,” he tweeted.

Cruise reveals Origin

Cruise has kept a fairly low profile ever since it punted on its goal of launching a robotaxi service in San Francisco in 2019. The GM subsidiary that’s backed by Honda, Softbank Vision Fund and T.Rowe Price, is starting to make some noise.

The company’s big reveal received the majority of the attention, although there was one other story I ferreted out that I think also deserves a moment or two. Cruise CEO Dan Ammann unveiled Origin, a “production ready” driverless vehicle designed for ride-sharing. The vehicle  was the product of a multi-year collaboration with parent company GM and investor Honda.

This vehicle doesn’t have a steering wheel or pedals and that means it cannot operate on public roads in the U.S. unless the federal government issues the company a waiver. That’s something Cruise and GM are already pursuing with a different vehicle — a driverless version of the Chevy Bolt EV.

The event itself didn’t provide a lot of details. But in the scrum after the event, I was able to ask Ammann a few questions that were informative. For one, expect the Cruise Origin to show up on corporate campuses of its partners while it waits for federal approval. That means it will likely be tested and used at GM and Honda campuses.

I’ve also heard rumblings that the vehicle could be used in other settings with private roads, like say that big event happening this summer in Tokyo.

Finally, ahead of the reveal I also published an article looking into Cruise’s push into hardware. An AV developer prototyping and developing hardware like sensors isn’t particularly remarkable. What Cruise is doing — basically amassing a team of hundreds of people — is what stands out.

I was able to confirm that Cruise has spent the past 18 months building up a hardware team that now numbers more than 300 people. The company has a hardware lab in the basement of its Bryant Street building.

But Cruise has bigger ambitions. The company plans to dedicate the entire Bryant Street location, a 140,000-square-foot building that once served as its headquarters, to the hardware team, according to sources familiar with Cruise’s plans. Some software engineers will also stay there. Just about everyone else will move over to Cruise’s new office at 333 Brannan Street, the former Dropbox headquarters.

A little bird

We hear things, but we’re not selfish. Let’s share.

At last count, the California Department of Motor Vehicles has issued autonomous vehicle testing permits to 65 companies. The permit requires a human safety driver behind the wheel. To test driverless vehicles on public roads, a separate permit is required. Just one company, Waymo, holds a driverless testing permit in California. AutoX has applied for a driverless permit.

There’s a different permit required for companies that want to operate a ride-hailing service using autonomous vehicles in the state. The California Public Utilities Commission is the regulatory body that handles ride-hailing services and AV developers are learning how much power these regulators have.

Only four companies, AutoX, Pony.ai, Waymo and Zoox have permission to operate a ride-hailing service using autonomous vehicles in the state of the California. And under CPUC rules, the rides must be free.

Ok, now the good stuff. Expect a fifth company to join the ranks soon. We hear that Aurora, the self-driving company backed by Amazon, Sequoia Capital and T. Rowe Price Associates, has received the OK from the CPUC to operate a ride-hailing service.

Meanwhile, companies have been pushing the CPUC to scrap the “no charging for rides” rule. I spoke to a few folks from different AV companies who tell me they’re getting close and expect to reach to resolution this year, and possibly before the summer.

Daimler makes some changes

One story that didn’t get a lot of attention, but should, is a report in German publication Automobilewoche that Daimler is shutting down Lab1886, the innovation lab responsible for developing and piloting a number of new business models for the company, including car-sharing service car2go, Moovel and Mercedes me. Lab1886 has about 200 employees scattered at locations in Atlanta, Berlin, Beijing and Stuttgart.

I had heard similar rumblings that included the shutdown of another smaller division of 30 people called Digital Life that is focused on digital services. When I reached out to Daimler, a spokesperson said that the claim that Lab1886 is closed is false.

Daimler later added that the innovation lab is now taking the next step and “is developing further into an open incubator in order to be able to develop new business models in the future, not only for Daimler AG, but also for other companies. To this end, we are currently in discussions with potential external partners.”

If that’s what is happening, then it is not being made clear to employees who were told operations were ramping down and resources were being shifted to other areas. These folks, who are looking for jobs within Daimler and in some cases, outside of the company, say that the company is pulling back from some of these areas to focus on its core business of producing and selling vehicles, and investing in electrification.

Stay tuned as we figure out what’s going on in Daimler.

What’s happening over at Daimler is occurring at other automakers too. These companies were trying to diversify their businesses. It’s not easy. At the same time, automakers are eliminating more than 80,000 jobs during the coming years, according to data compiled by Bloomberg News late last year.

Expect more shuffling and shifting around as OEMs make some tough choices on what to invest in.

What's ahead for lidar

As I have noted before, CES 2020 was packed with lidar companies exhibiting in larger spaces. It was a surprising moment for me because in my mind lidar had peaked in 2017.

And as Devin Coldewey noted, lidar’s big moment at CES 2020 seemed at odds with reports from last year that the industry was in the midst of a correction. Coldewey dug in deeper to find out what the heck is going on and why.

The upshot? Adapt or die. Yup, this is another Extra Crunch story. See a pattern here?

I also spoke to a number of lidar founders and heard similar things. More specifically, they told me that as lidar becomes commoditized, companies have to add value.

Luminar, which wasn’t mentioned in the article, is one example. The company launched a subscription service during CES that includes perception software along with its lidar sensor. Ouster introduced two new high-resolution digital lidar sensors. But what might not be as well known is that Ouster says it can configure beam spacing, which would allow a customer to choose a select number of individual lasers and have them spaced in the grouping you want for specific use-cases.
