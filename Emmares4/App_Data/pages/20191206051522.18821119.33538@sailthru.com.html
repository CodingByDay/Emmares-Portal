Monetary Nails Meet Fiscal Screws

Monetary policy has had to be the hammer for every nail of trouble that's popped up since the last global recession – arguably because of a failed response from fiscal authorities.

The lack of robust, proactive fiscal policies have left the structural forces (globalization, demographics and the shale revolution, to name a few) weighing on inflation and real interest rates. True, it's unclear how much fiscal policy alone can offset such dynamics. But it’s clear that monetary policy is passing the baton, and the question for 2020 is whether it will be grabbed by a fiscal anchorman. That will determine whether the pace of what’s presumed to be a late-stage global expansion trundles along, accelerates or –  in the worst case – comes to an end (helped there by escalating trade tensions driven by the U.S.)

Japan, New Zealand, India and South Korea are among those warming to the need for government support to allay a slowdown in a part of the world buffeted by the U.S.-China trade war and China’s more measured approach to propping up its economy.

The $64,000 question is whether the euro area will join its Asian peers in embracing a more debt-friendly stance. And on that front, Germany took part in the loudest two-basis-point rise in yields imaginable.

On Thursday morning, German bunds contributed to losses across core sovereign debt after Chancellor Angela Merkel’s coalition partner, the SPD, outlined a list of demands including infrastructure spending, more aggressive expenditures to counter climate change and a higher minimum wage in exchange for continued support for the government.

(Whether any such fiscal fillip will actually come to pass is somewhat irrelevant to markets in the near-term, just so long as bond traders or their computers are willing to act on these headlines).  

Beyond that, for the first time in recent memory German opinion polls appeared to add fuel to a global bond retreat as both bunds and Treasuries slumped to session lows after voters indicated a marginal increase in discontent with the ruling class.

For now, an end to German fiscal intransigence remains the Treasury market’s second-best version of Lucy pulling the football away from Charlie Brown – surpassed only by the elusive resolution to the U.S.-China trade war.

A monetary-to-fiscal hand-off, combined with a resolution of trade tensions, might be required to revive animal spirits in C-Suites in a such way as to spur investment. That’s because central banks – whose ability to reflate economies is inversely proportional to the level of policy rates – don’t appear to be as proactive in alleviating a slowdown as they have been in the past year. The global economy in 2019 received the largest cumulative easing since the financial crisis, CIBC research shows. So 2020 may end up being boring by comparison.

A prelude to the potential snoozer: the Reserve Bank of India surprised by holding rates unchanged this week. The Bank of Canada is attempting to make it as evident as possible that it won’t follow the Federal Reserve in rate reductions unless the data force its hand. Christine Lagarde’s European Central Bank isn’t a huge fan of negative rates.

And economists appear to agree with the Fed’s characterization that the economy is in a good place, with those surveyed by Bloomberg expecting the central bank to keep rates on hold for the next two years. That would roughly tie 2013 to 2015 – when the Fed ended QE3 before lifting rates for the first time this cycle – as the longest stretch of unchanged policy in the post-Bretton Woods era.

In financial market pricing, this narrative is reinforced by the normalization of interest-rate swaptions (the implied volatility of two-year Treasury yields over the next three months lags behind that of 10-year yields by nearly the most this year), implying that the inflection point for central bank policy has come and gone, and another isn't on the horizon.

It all goes to a point made by Bank of Canada special advisor Chris Ragan two years ago: “All central bankers would like to get back to a time when monetary policy was normal. And monetary policy is normal when it’s boring.”

One tide is going out, but what it leaves behind is anyone’s guess.

PMI IMProving

Taking stock of global sovereign borrowing costs, it’s an open question whether investors should be discouraged at how much bonds imply a low-growth, low-inflation future or encouraged by the extent to which this asset class has moved away from recessionary pricing. The latest series of purchasing manager indexes offered, net-net, signs that the outlook is improving.

Better mood music around U.S.-China trade talks (which has recently become more mixed) is too recent to account for any improvement in the data. There’s no indication that Chinese fiscal stimulus – or such an impulse from other locales – can take credit for the recovery either.

So either the very-slightly lagged effects of Fed easing and lower global bond yields is reinvigorating global activity, or – as the IIF’s Robin Brooks has long argued – the elimination of an inventory overhang in global manufacturing tells a story of a more natural bottoming out and recovery in global activity. This has coincided with the stock of negative-yielding global debt shrinking from over $17 trillion to about $11.5 trillion.

 

If the global economic cycle has rebounded without reattaching many meaningful non-monetary training wheels, the risks to activity heading into 2020 may be more tilted to the upside for the first time since global economic surprise indexes were powering into positive territory ahead of the election of Donald Trump in November 2016 and the promise of U.S. fiscal stimulus that followed.

Two Largest Economies, Two Largely Different Bond Markets

Tumultuous would be the best word to describe developed-economy bond markets this year. But shifts in sovereign borrowing costs likely belie the magnitude of any underlying changes in the contours of activity. Sure, manufacturing matters, but services matter more. And those PMIs have stayed in expansionary territory all year.

Comparing the state of the sovereign bond markets in the world’s two largest economies offers an interesting contrast in how we should tell the story of the macroeconomy in 2019.

China’s 10-year yield is experiencing its smallest annual swings since 2012. This makes sense: Beijing has been the straw that stirs the drink for global activity since the aftermath of the financial crisis, and Chinese fiscal policy and monetary hasn’t meaningfully changed this year (enough to affect the data). Granted, the lack of truly free markets could be playing a role here, as traders suspect state support has had a role in financial market stability.

Meanwhile, the 10-year Treasury yield is in the midst of its biggest annual range since 2013, the year of the taper tantrum.

The difference between the range in Chinese bonds versus Treasuries is the widest since 2011.

Ultimately, it’s worth combining the two markets to get a comprehensive view. In the U.S., perception changed much more than reality, but investors were mollified by the Fed’s desire to cushion U.S. growth and address risks stemming from the global economy. In China, policy makers stuck with an uneasy balancing act between stabilizing growth and avoiding excessive credit expansion – which should be raising as much concern for activity outside its borders as within.
