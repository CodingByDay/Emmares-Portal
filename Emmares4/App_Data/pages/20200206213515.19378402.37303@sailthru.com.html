Inside Wuhan's quarantine

Amid surging coronavirus cases and a death toll that tops 600, China is intensifying its mandatory lockdown of Wuhan, the city where it all began. Authorities in Hubei's sprawling capital, home to more than 11 million people and 22,000 confirmed infections, were ordered to round up and send all patients to mass quarantine camps. Uninfected residents were directed to daily report their body temperatures to local health officials.

Meanwhile, the city's already overwhelmed infrastructure is confronting a health care system that's stretched thin. For just over two weeks the quarantine has bottled in thousands of sick, infectious people, leaving doctors and nurses to scramble for hospital beds, essential medical equipment and more staff.

The streets, however, remain largely empty. Wuhan’s airport and planes sit dormant. Bridges are practically devoid of vehicles. And central areas are mostly vacant. "I feel like I'm playing a game," said one local. "It's like Resident Evil."

This is not normal

Steep slump. Extreme heat spikes are triggering a mass die-off of wild bumblebees, which fertilize billions of dollars worth of U.S. crops annually, said a study that “confirmed positively that climate change is a factor.”

The future is now

Cutting-edge data. Researchers near the North Pole are venturing deep into the Arctic Ocean to pilot a mini fridge-sized robot beneath the ice to get the closest look yet at the Arctic as the epicenter of climate change.

What's good
