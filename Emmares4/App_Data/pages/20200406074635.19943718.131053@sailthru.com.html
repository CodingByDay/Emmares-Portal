Our take on the latest developments

Even as Britain’s Queen Elizabeth II invoked a spirit of wartime sacrifice in warning of dark days ahead, signs of light appeared across much of the world in the fight against the coronavirus.

The rate of daily fatalities fell over the weekend in Europe’s worst-hit nations, including Italy, Spain, France and the U.K. Even the U.S., the new focus of the outbreak, reported what Vice President Mike Pence described as “glimmers of progress.”

As Winston Churchill said in 1942, almost three years before the end of World War II, it’s too early to call this “the beginning of the end.” The latest U.S. numbers could just be a blip. Even in Europe, which seems further along the virus timeline, countries are moving at different paces. Some have extended lockdowns of millions of people beyond Easter. Others, like Sweden, that took a more lax approach early on are now talking about tightening up.

People at a cafe in Stockholm on March 26. With many places in lockdown, Sweden is starting to look like a global outlier in its response.

Photographer: Mikael Sjoberg/Bloomberg

The weekend developments suggest virus-fighting quarantines are showing results. If the tide is turning, the key questions now are how quickly the pandemic retreats and how long those draconian measures will be needed. Without careful monitoring and control, a resurgence is possible.

In Wuhan, China’s erstwhile coronavirus epicenter, the outbreak receded quickly after peaking in February. A resumption of movement in and out of the city is scheduled for this week, more than two months after it was sealed off.

Elsewhere, there’s growing political and economic pressure for a roadmap out of the crisis. Austria on Monday outlined next steps, including reopening some shops starting next week, while Italy is expected to shed light on its plans soon. In many places a halfway-house approach seems likely, with public gatherings remaining restricted.

Without some clarity, the public’s patience could wear thin. So far, societies that describe themselves as “free” have largely accepted unprecedented peacetime restrictions despite extraordinary economic and social costs. The apparent success of those measures could be their Achilles’ heel— especially if the coming Easter weekend is sunny.—Eric Pfanner
