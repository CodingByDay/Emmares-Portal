For over a decade, consumer packaged goods companies like Kimberly-Clark and Procter & Gamble have been targeted by activists for turning Canadian forests into toilet paper. Yet despite occasional flare-ups of public protests and subsequent corporate sustainability pledges, most of these companies remain addicted to turning ancient virgin timber into the most basic of bathroom products.

The surprising persistence of the issue came to mind this past week as the World Economic Forum unfolded in Davos and climate activists racked up new corporate sustainability pledges. BlackRock said it would redeploy the $7 trillion in assets under its management away from coal, while oil CEOs discussed adopting far more ambitious carbon reduction targets. Of course, Greta Thunberg was everywhere giving tongue-lashings.

But the lesson of toilet paper is one of caution. Big companies do not really change on a dime. Even under lots of pressure. Even after they’ve made public pledges.

If the toilet paper narrative tells us anything, it is how much more sophisticated companies have become about defending their products in the language of sustainability while making only nominal changes.

Last fall, the National Resources Defense Council published a report called “The Issue With Tissue,” grading brands by their impact on the boreal forest. After Charmin Ultra got an F, parent company Procter & Gamble quickly announced a sustainability pledge and claimed its butt-wiping product was actually a “responsible choice”.

“It really points to the importance of shining a spotlight on the problem,” said Shelley Vinyard, Boreal Corporate campaign manager for NRDC and co-author of the report. “For a while there wasn’t as much scrutiny and the industry made a lot of gains by continuing the status quo.”

Vinyard and the NRDC learned a valuable lesson from the toilet paper tussle. “The U.S. marketplace has power,” she said. “And we should be using it.
