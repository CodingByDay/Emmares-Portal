It shouldn’t take a crisis for the CEO of a top airplane manufacturer to take a ride on the company’s best-selling jet.   

Boeing Co. Chief Executive Officer Dennis Muilenburg appeared before Congress this week to try to explain how two freshly delivered 737 Max jets could fall from the sky, killing 346 people in the process. The two-day testimony was predictably bruising for Muilenburg, but nothing quite summed up how out of touch he appeared than when he said he didn’t recall flying on a Max before the crashes prompted a global grounding. (He has since flown on the jet as a software fix was demonstrated). The comments came in response to a question at the House hearing on Wednesday from Republican Representative Garret Graves of Louisiana, who was trying to make a supportive point that a CEO who flew on a plane can’t have thought anything was wrong with it. Oops.

I realize the CEO of General Motors Co. might not personally test drive every new car model, and I don’t think Muilenburg knew there was an issue with the Max before the crashes. But that is the problem: There seems to have been an awful lot of things about the day-to-day at Boeing that he didn’t know or didn’t even try to know. Muilenburg’s admission of no pre-crash Max flights followed the unfathomable disclosure to the Senate on Tuesday that he hadn’t fully read until recently the batch of 2016 instant messages among top test pilots that appear to reflect internal hesitation over the flight-control system implicated in the Max crashes. He was generally aware of documents of that kind, but apparently didn’t ask to read them and relied on his lawyers to get the messages to the appropriate authorities after the first crash. 

Muilenburg did appear genuinely sorry and emotional when addressing the families of the crash victims. He took the biggest steps toward accountability yet, acknowledging three specific mistakes in the design of a Max flight-control system. These included making it dependent on a single sensor and relying on overly optimistic assumptions about pilot reaction times. Muilenburg also said no executives would receive bonuses this year, which frankly shouldn’t have even been a question. But as lawmakers were only too happy to point out, he’s not offering to pay back the $13 million in non-equity incentive pay he pocketed in 2018 — a year in which Boeing exceeded its free cash flow and profit goals but 189 people died as a Lion Air-operated Max crashed into the Java Sea. An anecdote in Muilenburg’s opening statement about growing up on an Iowa farm wasn’t humanizing so much as it was a foil to his current position and an excuse for lawmakers to keep badgering him about his massive pay package. 

The performance is apt to have long-term consequences for Muilenburg, who was repeatedly called upon to resign by lawmakers, but it’s not clear that it will for Boeing. There was a lot of noise at both the Senate and the House hearing about a need to rethink Boeing’s relationship with the Federal Aviation Administration and put more guard rails on the regulatory review process, but there were few, if any, specific proposals from the lawmakers and Muilenburg didn’t offer any of his own. He instead argued that the system of delegating authority to company experts made planes safer, but was willing to support “evaluating that concept” of getting the balance right. That suggests Boeing is angling for a fiddling around the edges versus the kind of dramatic overhaul that could force real change. I don’t think it’s a coincidence that Muilenburg’s opening statement included a list of Boeing factories across the U.S. that he said he had visited recently, a convenient reminder to those lawmakers whose constituents depend on the company for job.

When pressed on whether Boeing prioritized profits over safety, Muilenburg disagreed with that premise but reminded lawmakers that Boeing is the “only U.S. builder of big commercial airplanes remaining today” and that it operates in “a competitive environment.” Both of those statements can’t really be true. Boeing effectively enjoys a duopoly with rival Airbus SE, and that is one reason its share price hasn’t taken a bigger hit from this crisis and why airlines aren’t rushing to swap out their Max orders for other options. The Max crisis has revealed, among other things, the danger of concentrating so much power in the hands of one company. But that dynamic looks unlikely to be challenged by Congress any time soon. That’s fortunate for Boeing shareholders; less so for the rest of us. 

FINDING A BOTTOM? 
“We’re way past the point of figuring out if it’s an industrial recession or not. It has been an industrial recession for a while.” That was XPO Logistics Inc. CEO Brad Jacobs in an interview with Bloomberg News this week after cutting his company’s 2019 revenue outlook to a decline of as much as 4%. He’s right: The signs of a slump have been there for the better part of a year in those businesses most vulnerable to swings in demand, even if those signs have only recently gotten bad enough to catch the attention of the broader market. That, of course, raises the question of when things will turn back around. There were some encouraging news this week. U.S. factory activity continued to contract in October, but at a slower clip than the previous month as a gauge of new orders improved, according to a Friday report from the Institute for Supply Management. The data were worse than expected but also were were likely weighed down by the nearly six-week labor strike at GM. A separate manufacturing gauge from IHS Markit released the same day showed the pace of activity hit a six-month high in October, as the veil of uncertainty holding back orders started to dissipate for some companies.

It’s way too early to call this a comeback. What makes this downturn unique is that there’s not only weaker demand in certain markets — there also are a cluster of hard-to-predict political variables ranging from the trade war to the upcoming 2020 U.S. election. That make it much more difficult to rely on past precedent to predict the end of the slump, Gordon Haskett analyst John Inch wrote in a report this week. He takes issue with Parker-Hannifin Corp.’s prediction this week for its sales slump to bottom out in the middle of fiscal 2020, even as it lowered guidance to reflect an anticipated 6% decline in core sales and announced an increased restructuring plan. On the cost-cutting front, manufacturing employment and wages remained weak in the Labor Department’s October update, particularly in key swing states that helped President Donald Trump get elected in 2016. Perhaps that pushes Trump toward a trade deal that would further arrest the uncertainty weighing on manufacturers, as Bloomberg Opinion’s Robert Burgess writes. Both sides touted progress on Friday. We’ll see. One company that does seem to have finally found its own unique bottom is General Electric Co. Read more about its third-quarter earnings report here.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Fiat Chrysler Automobiles NV agreed this week to merge with Peugeot-maker PSA Group in the latest sign that the rising costs of electrification and emissions compliance has convinced car manufacturers that bigger is better. The deal comes about five months after the collapse of Fiat’s merger discussions with Renault SA as the process of wrangling approvals from the French government and Renault’s strategic partner, Nissan Motor Co., proved too much of a headache. There’s plenty of risks to the Peugeot combination as well, but the stars may be more aligned this time, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Chris Bryant. While Renault would have helped Fiat diversify away from a slumping European car market, Peugeot CEO Carlos Tavares was able to turn around the money-losing Opel/Vauxhall business he acquired from GM and may be able to do the same with Fiat’s operations in the region. 

The deal with Peugeot is billed as a merger of equals, and like most deals of that sort, it’s far from equal. Investors in Fiat — including the billionaire Agnelli family — will receive a 5.5 billion-euro ($6.1 billion) dividend, plus a robotics arm valued at an estimated 250 million euros. Peugeot holders will get the company’s stake in parts maker Faurecia SE, which is worth only 2.6 billion euros. In other words, Fiat investors are taking more out of this combination than they’re putting in, as Bloomberg Opinion’s Chris Hughes notes. Citigroup analysts don’t expect the terms to be revised, but are optimistic Peugeot shareholders will be rewarded with greater-than-expected cost savings. That’s no easy task, considering that the 3.7 billion euros in annual savings the companies are targeting notably doesn’t include factory closures in an effort to win government support.  

Anixter International Inc., an industrial distributor of specialty wire and cable products that’s backed by Sam Zell, agreed to sell itself to an affiliate of private equity firm Clayton, Dubilier & Rice for $81 a share, or $3.8 billion including debt. The bid is only an 18% premium to Anixter’s average price in the 30 days leading up to the deal announcement and investors are already wagering it will be topped. Anixter has traded above the offer price since the deal was announced and the stock was on track to close at about $84 on Friday. The terms of the merger agreement allow Anixter to solicit superior bids over the course of 40 days, and the $45 million termination fee it would be on the hook for if it abandoned the Clayton, Dubilier deal is relatively small. Raymond James Financial Inc. analyst Sam Darkatsh wrote in a note that it would be a “strategic blunder” for rival distributor Wesco International Inc. not to make a counteroffer. He says the company can afford a $90-a-share bid for Anixter as a base case.
