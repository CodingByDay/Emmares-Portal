Transportation

FLIGHT ATTENDANTS, PREPARE FOR HACKING — Improved information sharing and expanded public discussion are crucial to protecting the aviation space from hackers, the Atlantic Council concluded in a report published today. "Limited or ineffectual information sharing is leading to opacity of risk among stakeholders, and arguably obfuscates the scale of the aviation-cybersecurity challenge and the way forward," the authors wrote. In addition to reworking aviation standards to encourage information sharing, they also recommended that all aviation contracts include cybersecurity provisions addressing issues such as vulnerability management and risk assessment.

The report also stressed that public engagement was vital, because aviation cybersecurity was "still little discussed outside the sector, leading to risks of misperception and inaccuracy." And aviation technology must be designed with cybersecurity in mind, the report said, as part of an urgent effort to "reduce the rapidly expanding digital attack surface of the aviation sector, with a default of designing for simplicity, security, and resiliency."

The overwhelming majority of aviation stakeholders believe that current cybersecurity regulations are ineffective, according to a survey of 244 stakeholders included in the report. Those stakeholders also generally agreed that the media failed to "report aviation cybersecurity issues in an informed and balanced manner." At the same time, most respondents said their organizations planned to spend more money on aviation cybersecurity. The Atlantic Council will host an event this afternoon to discuss the report.

WHILE YOU'RE HERE, SERGEY — Pompeo on Tuesday publicly warned Russia not to interfere in next year's elections. "I made our expectations of Russia clear," Pompeo said during a news conference with Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov after the pair met behind closed doors at the State Department. "The Trump administration will always work to protect the integrity of our elections. Period." Pompeo said there was "no mistake" about Moscow's assault on the 2016 presidential race.

The comments seemingly marked a reversal by Pompeo — from perpetuating last month the debunked conspiracy theory that Ukraine, not Russia, hacked the DNC in 2016 back to the position he held as CIA director that the Kremlin was responsible. Lavrov shrugged off the accusations of past or possible future interference, calling them "baseless." "There are no facts that would support that. No one has given us this proof, because it simply does not exist," he said of 2016.

— RELATED: The abuse of power article of impeachment in a resolution that House Democrats unveiled on Tuesday rests, in part, on President Donald Trump's resistance to the idea that Russia intervened on his behalf in the 2016 election. The article of impeachment cites Trump seeking public investigation from Ukraine of "a discredited theory promoted by Russia alleging that Ukraine — rather than Russia — interfered in the 2016 United States Presidential election."

FBI Director Christopher Wray this week again rejected that theory. "We have no information that indicates that Ukraine interfered with the 2016 presidential election," Wray said. He added, "Well, look, there's all kinds of people saying all kinds of things out there. I think it's important for the American people to be thoughtful consumers of information and to think about the sources of it and to think about the support and predication for what they hear."

LET'S NOT ENCRYPT? — Senate Judiciary Chairman Lindsey Graham (R-S.C.) seemed to reverse his position on encryption by calling on companies to design products to allow law enforcement access at a hearing of his panel on Tuesday. The panel demonstrated largely bipartisan support for that sentiment, too. Senators raised the prospect that if the likes of Facebook and Google don't take action, Congress would take action for them, with Graham suggesting a move could come by the end of next year.

RICHARD AND THE DREAD GAZEBO — Top former U.S. government officials aided the United Arab Emirates in creation of a cyber surveillance program, sidestepped restrictions against assisting in the hacking of Americans or U.S. servers and exploited a loophole to keep their security clearance while working for foreign spies, Reuters reported in an investigation published Tuesday . Former White House security official Richard Clarke, other former White House officials and ex-NSA employees all figure into the initiative going by the name DREAD. Reuters reports that a federal grand jury has been investigating whether those involved in the project violated U.S. hacking laws.

Report Watch

— The kind of vulnerability that abetted the Equifax breach is back with a vengeance, WatchGuard revealed in a report out today . During the third quarter of 2019, three Apache Struts vulnerabilities — including the one from the Equifax breach — climbed onto the top 10 list of most popular network attacks. "The report also highlights a major rise in zero day malware detections, increasing use of Microsoft Office exploits and legitimate penetration testing tools, and more," according to a summary.
