There are wars of words, and there are real wars. The world saw the former this week, and came perilously close to the latter.

The world's most famous fugitive, Carlos Ghosn, is in Lebanon after the former Nissan CEO escaped from jail in Japan. He's been reluctant to detail his disappearing act but eager to criticize prosecutors and ex-colleagues. They lobbed bons mots right back.

Thousands of miles away, fears of all-out war escalated after Donald Trump approved a missile strike that killed a top Iranian general. Iran lobbed its own missiles at U.S. military bases in Iraq, but then appeared to stand down — at least for now.

If you were busy keeping track of all the lobbing, here are some stories you may have missed. —Michael Tighe

Xi's Next Headache

​​​​​

​​​​​

The Republic of China is about to do something that's anathema to the People's Republic of China: hold democratic elections. Taiwan voters choose a president on Saturday as Xi Jinping signals impatience with their refusal to accept Beijing's rule.

Conflagration Down Under

The wildfires scorching Australia are claiming lives, property and part of its GDP. Goldman Sachs says private investment, farm production and tourism will suffer immediate hits, though a government pledge of $1.4 billion in spending could be a cushion.

Safety Schools

Hong Kong parents anxious about their children's safety are seeking refuge in Singapore's classrooms. Private schools in Singapore say applications from the former British colony are surging as months of violent street protests show no sign of ending.

Surviving the Apocalypse

Shopping has never been easier, and that's making things even tougher for retailers. Marketing and merchandising that worked in the past doesn't anymore, so these four CEOs wrote their own winning formulas. Their chains not only survive, they thrive.

Shelling the Pentagon

Some contractors benefiting from the U.S. Defense Department's $350 billion spending plan are hiding their foreign identities and using overseas factories. Those shell companies are blamed for manufacturing substandard parts for torpedoes and F-15 fighter jets.

Dealing With Japan

Japan is perfecting the art of the deal in Asia. For the second straight year, Japanese firms outspent their Chinese rivals in M&A, logging more than $200 billion in foreign and domestic transactions. The trend should continue in 2020 because of these key drivers.  

Catholic Church Bankruptcies

The Catholic Church in the U.S. is deploying a legal strategy popularized by Fortune 500 executives to minimize its liabilities in thousands of sexual-abuse lawsuits. Dioceses are filing for Chapter 11 as a way to settle claims while retaining much of their assets.

Paging Dr. Ganja

​​​​​​

​​​​​​

Dr. Ganja is becoming a popular health practitioner in Thailand. The bespectacled leaf in a lab coat is educating people about medicinal marijuana as the government opens its first clinic and invests in its first indoor growing facility.
