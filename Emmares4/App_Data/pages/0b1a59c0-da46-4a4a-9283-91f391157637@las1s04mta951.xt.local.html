Russia

THE EMPIRE STRIKES BACK — A seemingly long-dormant Russian hacking group implicated in the 2016 DNC intrusion resurfaced this summer with ESET discovering more victims, including a European Union country's Washington embassy, the firm disclosed today. By all evidence, the espionage campaign that also hit three European ministries of foreign affairs — conducted by a group ESET calls Dukes (aka Cozy Bear or APT29) — is linked to one from 2013 that ESET named Operation Ghost. The hackers retooled to stay hidden and continued their operations rather than disappearing entirely.

"Operation Ghost shows that the Dukes never stopped their espionage activities," ESET said in a blog post. "This campaign also shows that APT threat actors going dark for several years does not mean they have stopped spying. They might pause for a while and reappear in another form, but they still need to spy to fulfill their mandates." Also notable: "The Dukes have leveraged online services such as Twitter, Imgur and Reddit to act as primary Command and Control (C&C) channels for their first-stage malware."

DO NO HARM — Cybersecurity is as much a tech issue as a patient safety one, New America argues in a report out this morning. The think tank is proposing 17 recommendations to improve cyber hygiene in health care, upgrade infrastructure and solve workforce shortages.

Bolstering FDA requirements for medical device security, creating government-subsidized cyber health care apprenticeships and offering tax incentives for health care organizations that retain cybersecurity staff are among their ideas. Several other recommendations call upon Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, HHS and other agencies to provide regulatory guidance for the health sector.

NOT BUSTING THE BUDGET — Two House bills with cybersecurity implications recently received clean bills of health from the Congressional Budget Office. A measure (H.R. 1975 ) that would require the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency to create an advisory committee with private-sector and local-government representatives would cost approximately $2 million over a five-year period, or less than $500,000 per year, the CBO said. The office based its estimate on "the administrative costs of similar advisory committees," including "staff salaries, travel costs, and other expenses."

Meanwhile, a second bill ( H.R. 4432) that would require DHS to assess the threats posed by drones "would not require substantial action by the department and would cost less than $500,000," according to the office, because DHS is already doing similar work.

SUPER DUPER SECRET — The U.S. executed a covert hacking operation against Iran in late September following attacks on two Saudi Arabian oil facilities, according to a Reuters story published Wednesday. The operation appears to have taken aim at Tehran's ability to spread "propaganda." One official said the strike affected physical hardware, but did not provide additional information. Iran denied the attack happened, with Iran's Minister of Communications and Information Technology Mohammad Javad Azari-Jahromi saying, "They must have dreamt it." It may take months before the strike's impact and its ripple effects emerge.

DIFFERENT TAKE ON ENCRYPTION — Law enforcement officials in the U.S., U.K. and Australia have denounced further encryption efforts on Facebook. President Donald Trump has gone on the offensive against whistleblowers. But Mexico is championing both encryption and whistleblowers. In fact, the government is asking people with knowledge of federal corruption and misconduct to protect their anonymity by using the encrypted Tor browser, according to a report from El Universal.

Promoting Tor is meant to support Mexico's Secretariat of the Civil Service's "Alert Citizens" platform, which launched in July. Inspired by WikiLeaks, the service has received more than 1,800 complaints since its start related to corruption, sexual harassment and human rights violations.
