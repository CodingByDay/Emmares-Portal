Inside: Climate talks proceed in Madrid. Intel releases granular data on its workforce. EU green taxonomy deal fails. The $1 coal plant. A $1.6 trillion stock market repricing. Australia's "extraordinary situation." — Eric Roston

Sustainable Finance

The world's climate diplomats have gathered in Madrid to negotiate details about how carbon markets should work. Many of the nations that are thought of as luxury holiday destinations came to Madrid to show they are seeing more violent storms, threatening tourism. Greta Thunberg called out CEOs and world leaders for greenwashing. The world is on track to warm by almost 3 degrees Celsius by the end of the century, double the rate scientists have identified as needed to constrain the worst of climate change. 

The European Union’s 28 states failed to reach agreement on landmark regulation for green finance, tripping up the bloc’s attempts to be a leader in the field. Envoys meeting in Brussels rejected the deal over concerns about how to define green investments, according to people familiar with the talks.Countries that support the inclusion of nuclear energy on the list wanted to revise the text and negotiations will continue. 

The European Union’s biggest business leaders threw their weight behind a plan to make the bloc climate-neutral, a commitment that could be adopted at a leaders’ summit this week.

World stock markets are likely to start seeing an impact from climate change in the next five years, as policy changes to reduce global warming could force a net $1.6 trillion repricing in sectors from energy to agriculture, according to a new report.

Life-insurer Storebrand ASA has removed fossil-fuel producers from all funds managed by its Swedish unit — which manages $24 billion. 

In Brief

  • Pimco will start its first green-bond fund, the Pimco Climate Bond Fund.
  • Ikea made a minority investment in e-commerce company Optoro, which helps companies eliminate waste and inefficiency from their package return process.
  • Jean Rogers, founder and former CEO of SASB, has joined the Long-Term Stock Exchange as its chief resilience officer.

Environment

Nearly 10 million people — about two-fifths of all Australians — are under siege from fires stretching across more than 600 miles. David Fickling of Bloomberg Opinion writes a first-person account of the public-health dangers that fires bring — and the Australian government's failure to respond. “This is an extraordinary situation,” he writes. In Sydney, the air is so bad it's setting off fire alarms.

ExxonMobil won a high-profile case over its accounting for the financial risks of climate change. New York State had sued the oil giant, believing that it misled shareholders about the way it estimated costs of future climate-change regulation. The Attorney General’s office “failed to prove… that ExxonMobil made any material misstatements or omissions,” wrote New York State Supreme Court Justice Barry Ostrager.

Chevron is taking an $11 billion write down on its natural gas assets, in one of the largest-ever writedowns of fossil fuel assets. 

The timing and nature of the U.K.’s exit from the EU could shift the continent’s carbon market. Britain accounts for about 7% of permits issued.

Plastic bag makers warn they can’t make products that meet the requirements of New York’s plan to eliminate single-use plastic bags, which goes into effect March 1.

Puget Sound Energy agreed to sell its 25% stake in a large coal plant —for $1—to NorthWestern Energy. The low price reflects the environmental risk that comes with owning coal plants. 

Social

Intel offered an unprecedented amount of data on pay, race and gender for about 51,000 U.S. workers. The results are not flattering. Of 52 top executives, who all earn more than $208,000, 29 are white men, 11 are Asian men and 8 are white women. The remaining tally is 1 each for Asian women, Hispanic women, black women and black men, with no Hispanic men among top-tier executives. 

How much salary would you give up to work fewer hours? That’s what researchers from the IMF recently asked Japanese workers, who said that they’d give up nearly half of their income to avoid putting in 45 hours or more of overtime a month, writes Rachel Rosenthal of Bloomberg Opinion.

Citigroup promoted 14 women, or 31% out of the total 45 managing directors named in Asia this year. That’s up from only eight last year, or 21%.

Venture capital has struggled to make inroads in funding women and minority founders. A new trade group promoting "builder capital" models launched last week, saying the issue may be structural. Other funding models like debt, holding companies and revenue sharing may be more supportive for entrepreneurs in trying to build long-term wealth, the group said.

Governance

The SEC is investigating claims that a Virginia-based advocacy group was behind suspicious comment letters submitted in support of a rule change sought by large corporations. Lawmakers asked Chairman Jay Clayton what he was doing to address concerns stirred up by this Bloomberg News story.

China is running up against its own deadline to require more than 3,000 listed companies to disclose ESG metrics. Despite a goal to kick off the program next year, reporting guidelines have yet to surface, Bloomberg News reports.
