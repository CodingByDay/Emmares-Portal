A Correction of a Panic

The U.S. stock market has sold off more than 7% over the last five trading days. This marks only the fourth time it has done so since the bull market got going in earnest after the debt ceiling crisis in 2011. After each of the three previous selloffs there was an immediate rebound. 

Unfortunately, it is harder to see how we get such a rebound this time. Unlike the Chinese monetary authorities or the Federal Reserve, the coronavirus isn’t susceptible to financial pressure. Unlike Jay Powell, it cannot make a U-turn. And even if it stages a retreat it will take us a while to see it. So there is no obvious news headline that could sort things out. 

Further, the 10-year Treasury yield hit an all-time low during Tuesday’s trading, while fed funds futures adjusted to predict two rate cuts of 25 basis points each by the election in November, with a 50-50 chance of a further reduction by the end of January next year. Lower rates are of course no use against a respiratory infection. They might help mitigate the economic effects of the virus, but as the market is already firmly priced on the assumption of rate cuts to come, and enjoys remarkably low borrowing costs already, a big reduction probably wouldn’t have the same impact as some of those that preceded it.

Unlike “Volmageddon” two years ago, when the popular trade of betting on volatility to stay low melted down spectacularly, this episode hasn’t seen the reversal of any clearly extreme positions. There is ample reason to believe that many segments of the market are overvalued, and have been for a long time; but the moves of the last few days aren’t about those excesses. 

Instead, this is a very unusual “correction” that is aiming to correct economic expectations in the light of news, rather than excesses in the market. Given the extreme uncertainty over exactly how much economic damage the effort to contain the virus will eventually cause, that attempt is surprisingly calm and orderly. Losses are centered on sectors that obviously stand to be affected most, such as airlines, hotels and cruise lines, and by companies that have specifically alerted that they expect damage to their numbers, such as Apple Inc. and MasterCard Inc.:

Tuesday saw a dramatic selloff in which the S&P 500 fell almost exactly 3%. But it didn’t include any attempt to damp down excesses. Indeed, the NYSE Fang+ index, including the most fashionable names of the moment, slightly outperformed the S&P for the day, while growth stocks did slightly better than value, despite a prolonged prior run:

Even with the 10-year Treasury yield at a historic low, there was also no clear run for the normal shelters. Bitcoin, the fashionable refuge of the moment, fell a bit. Gold, which has been rising at a torrid pace, actually dropped slightly during the American trading day, while the 30-year Treasury yield arrested its decline.

When we turn to the dollar, again something strange is afoot. The dollar has been treated globally as a haven of late. The euro has been weak. There is a significant outbreak in the euro zone (in Italy), while the U.S. has been spared to date.  And yet the euro has actually strengthened against the dollar this week, even as emerging market currencies (led by Latin American and eastern European rather than Asian countries) have sunk to a fresh all-time low in nominal terms:

The best explanation for this may be that investors are reducing their risk by closing out carry trades, which means buying back euros  — hence strengthening the currency against the dollar — and selling off exposures to emerging market currencies. But again this serves to emphasize that this is an unusual correction. 

Rather than a panic, this is an orderly attempt to deal with a serious issue. Banks and investment groups are doing their best to work out the potential scale of the problem, and they are largely doing so sensibly. This is not a market panic. However, the headlines of the last few days are alarming, there is panic in populations that are directly affected, along with something close to it in areas that are still free of the virus. This can be described as a market correction of a real world panic. 

If this is the good news, there is still plenty of bad news. Plenty of people are making an honest attempt to work out how bad the coronavirus will be. I have been bombarded with research by people who normally, like me, restrict themselves to finance and economics. But the assertions and estimates are all over the place, and serve only to make clear that nobody knows. Now we have established that this virus is different from the SARS or Ebola epidemics, there is really no useful precedent in recent decades. 

Beyond these sobering thoughts, the extreme underlying issues for markets and the economy remain. This continues to look like a wildly top-heavy stock market supported only by extreme valuations in the bond market. (For more, read this column). An overvalued stock market pales into insignificance when compared with the spread of a contagious and potentially deadly disease; but it remains a problem, and the events of the last few days have done nothing to remedy it.
