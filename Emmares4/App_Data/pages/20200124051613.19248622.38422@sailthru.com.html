Mark It Zero (in Real Yields)

And just like that, the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield is on track for its biggest one-week drop since November and fast approaching the bottom of its three-month range.

Oil prices certainly aren’t doing yields any favors, with the recent slide in crude weighing on breakevens. But the majority of the decline in 10-year rates since the start of the year is attributable to tumbling real yields. They even traded below zero on Thursday. News about the spread of the coronavirus out of Wuhan, China, may be the proximate cause for a persistent haven bid for bonds.

It’s difficult to make the case that the retreat in yields is sending a meaningful signal about the economic backdrop. Many of the potential non-economic reasons for the strong start to the year for sovereign debt (rebalancing, reversal strategies) were expounded upon in last week’s edition. 

The last time real yields were hurtling towards zero in August, expectations of where nominal rates would be in a year’s time were falling aggressively in tandem. To the extent the bond market reflects future expectations, the one-year forward yield for the 10-year Treasury isn't sending anywhere near as dire a signal about the economy’s ability to get off the mat.

While the bond market may be in a bit of a tizzy, the Federal Reserve is still seen holding the course (possibly because stocks aren't far from last week's all-time highs). The 20-day volatility of December 2020 federal funds futures is at a contract low, close to zero. A sidebar: the ACM measure of the 10-year risk-neutral yield is north of 2.60%, an unlikely reading given that that's roughly a quarter percentage point above the peak federal funds rate in the last tightening cycle. By extension, that’s a reminder that although the term premium may be a valid philosophical phenomenon, the ability to track it in real time leaves much to be desired.

To be fair, there has been a disconcerting macro development, as flagged by Citigroup strategist Jeremy Hale. The seasonally adjusted Conference Board leading indicator index for the U.S. economy has turned negative on an annual basis.

“On only one occasion has the indicator crossed below zero and the economy not entered into a recession within 18 months (1995),” he writes.

But Fedwatcher Tim Duy, for one, argues that the 1995 scenario may be the most relevant experience to current circumstances.

By this time next week, investors will have been able to digest a slew of preliminary PMI readings from around the world, along with the Federal Reserve policy decision and press conference. The developments of the next seven days may be enough to turn this bond rally from innocuous to insidious in a hurry, or mark the start of investors re-capturing some of the optimism surrounding the global economy.

Canadian Curveball

(We’re not talking about something that baseball’s Larry Walker, who was just voted into the Hall of Fame, would knock out of the park here.)

The Bank of Canada opened the door to a rate cut this week and trimmed its 2020 GDP projection, with fourth-quarter figures for 2019 expected to show the economy is at stall speed.

The odds of easing by the BOC's April meeting went from a little above one-in-five to around three-in-five following the news, with the loonie slumping against the greenback. The dovish pivot raises the stakes for Canadian retail sales figures, especially given an odd disconnect between income growth and spending.

If Justin Trudeau had a communications strategy like Donald Trump's, he might be complaining about the central bank stifling growth by now. After all, activity on an annual basis has decelerated much more sharply in Canada than the U.S. since the BOC's tightening cycle commenced in mid-2017, and its policy rate is currently higher.

Given that the dominant product in the Canadian mortgage market is the five-year fixed rate, it’s also probable that changes in monetary policy north of the border have faster transmission to the real economy than in the U.S. And while the Bank of Canada was able to free-ride on the spree of central bank easing last year, that might not be the case in 2020.

"While Canada benefited last year from lower global interest rates without the Bank of Canada having to lift a finger, an apparent bottoming in global growth (at a low level) and détente on the trade front may reverse the fall in yields over the next several months," writes Toronto-Dominion Bank economist James Marple. 

Ian Pollick, a rates strategist at Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce, highlighted a remark from Governor Stephen Poloz that may have implications for trading the Canadian curve. Poloz said that if the bank does move, “it would not be a cut against a hypothetical or a possibility” but would mean that the forecast was showing a “meaningful shortfall on our inflation target.” For some, that could read as a pledge for the bank to be behind the curve. 

“It would optically appear they were playing catch-up” if the bank did cut rates in coming months, writes Pollick. “That is a world in where terminal rates have to fall further and stay suppressed longer, which means 5-years can outperform 2-years quite easily.”

The logic would be that failure to be proactive would end up forcing the BOC to lower rates even more over time. Pollick reckons such a set-up would flatten the two-year, five-year yield spread and see the 5s10s steepen.

However, there’s at least one scenario in which the central bank could move even without material economic weakening.

The prior two Bank of Canada staff papers used to assess and update the neutral rate (done once a year, in April) used the median longer-term dot from the Federal Reserve’s most recent summary of economic projections as a proxy for the global neutral rate, and simply tacked a 50 basis point range on either end (2018, 2019). In this instance, it would call for lowering the neutral range to 2% to 3% (down 25 basis points from the previous estimate).

If the Bank followed a similar schematic this time around, monetary policy would be slated to get mechanically tighter. That is, a lower neutral rate means the current policy stance will no longer be as stimulative as the BOC thought, and a rate cut would be required to maintain the same degree of monetary accommodation.

Live Like a Prince

Move over Prince Harry. Bridgewater’s Bob Prince has staked his claim on the royal-themed news cycle.

Okay, that’s a bit of a stretch. But the co-CIO of the world’s largest hedge fund caused quite a stir when he proclaimed “the end of the boom-bust cycle.”

His thinking: the ebbs and flows in activity are primarily a product of credit cycles, which in turn are driven by central banks. Since central banks are relatively tapped out in terms of delivering conventional stimulus via rate cuts and material tightening is out of the cards thanks to structural disinflation, the era of booms and busts is over.

To his point, check out measures of five-year and 10-year forward implied volatility for two-year Treasury yields, which have cratered.

Fed Chairman Jerome Powell in his 2018 Jackson Hole speech observed that “whatever the cause, in the run-up to the past two recessions, destabilizing excesses appeared mainly in financial markets rather than in inflation.”

Dealing with the aftermath of those excesses left global central banks with an enduring legacy of bigger balance sheets. 

But there are other explanations for the demise of the old cycles: the regulatory environment and risk appetite on the part of financial institutions could be an even bigger driver.

Social safety nets in advanced economies are also key. Keeping in mind that consumption is the key to economic growth, and considering how populations are aging, it will be up to governments -- with their access to the monetary printing presses -- to ensure the purchasing power among the elderly. And that cohort is all the more powerful given its electoral pull. More moderate swings in the economic cycle could be the natural result of this dynamic.
