It didn’t take long for America’s manufacturers to turn from ally to punching bag for President Donald Trump.

Faced with the greatest public health crisis the country has seen in a century, companies are trying to do their part. 3m Co. and Honeywell International Inc. are aggressively expanding production of personal protective gear; Walmart Inc. and other retailers are opening up their parking lots as virus testing sites; distillers and cosmetics makers have started churning out hand-sanitizer; and automakers are willing to repurpose factories to make ventilators. These companies have done this not because the president ordered them to, but because it's the right thing to do. The White House has been only too happy to tout their efforts in its daily press conferences, until Friday when a Corporate America that has been surprisingly selfless and quick to action somehow got painted as the villain in this story.

General Motors Co. and ventilator-maker Ventec Life Systems Inc. had been planning to launch a joint venture to ramp up production. They’d worked around the clock for a week to convert an auto-parts facility in Kokomo, Indiana, secured suppliers for some 700 components and reportedly even started hiring. They did all of this even as the administration debated how many ventilators were actually going to be needed to fight the coronavirus and how much it was willing to pay for them, with the New York Times reporting that the Federal Emergency Management Agency had balked at an overall price tag of more than $1 billion. In a separate tweet he called on the government to “invoke ‘P,’”an apparent reference to the Defense Production Act in a language I am not going to even pretend to understand. The irony of course is that the president is the one with the power to invoke the 1950s-era law that allows the government to press U.S. industry into service on matters of national need. He has technically done so, but has been reluctant to actually apply it in practice. FEMA said Tuesday it did invoke the Defense Production Act with regard to manufacturing of testing kits and masks, before later backtracking and saying it was able to procure the supplies on the private market.

As I wrote earlier this week, the benefit of relying on corporate volunteerism is that it allows the president to shift the blame for the country's troubling shortage of vital medical equipment to companies that are trying to help. This is part of a longstanding trend of an antagonistic reliance on corporate America by Trump. The Lordstown plant that he mentions is no longer owned by GM. It was sold to an electric-truck startup in a deal that Trump touted as “GREAT NEWS FOR OHIO” despite the fact that the venture's parent company had negligent revenue. In 2018, Trump said he was “not happy” about a GM plan to cut 14,000 jobs; that news came less than two years after he praised a hiring and investment push that was mostly a PR stunt.

U.S. manufacturers can’t fill the health-equipment void on their own. Apart from funding questions, there are significant uncertainties as to how these ventilators should be allocated once they are produced. GM and Ventec said in a statement that they're aiming to deliver the first ventilators from their partnership in April and planning to scale up their manufacturing capacity to produce 10,000 a month. They have opted to go ahead without a federal contract and sell to buyers regardless, according to Bloomberg News. But how do they decide which buyers are most deserving? In a letter this week to FEMA administrator Peter Gaynor, the Advanced Medical Technology Association (AdvaMed) called for the agency to set up a program whereby manufacturers contribute their products to a national stockpile that the government can then decide how best to distribute. “Many health-care providers, as well as state and local governments, are trying to buy ventilators,” Scott Whitaker, CEO of AdvaMed said in the letter. “Some of these potential purchasers should have a higher priority than others based on the acuity of patient needs in their areas. It is difficult for manufacturers to establish these priorities.”

Point being, U.S. companies shouldn’t be put in the position of deciding who lives or dies. The government doesn’t have to be, either, if it can get its act together and gives manufacturers some actual direction.

BAILOUTS ARE HERE, BUT STRINGS ARE ATTACHED
The House of Representatives on Friday approved a staggering $2 trillion stimulus package, paving the way for much-needed relief for companies and citizens that find themselves dangerously low on cash in the wake of the extensive shutdowns necessary to combat the deadly coronavirus. The bill includes $61 billion in grants and loans for U.S. commercial airlines, cargo carriers and contractors providing ground and catering services. Boeing Co. is eligible to tap into $17 billion of loans designated for companies deemed critical to national security, as well as a piece of the broader roughly $500 billion pool of corporate assistance. Appearing on Fox Business on Friday, Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin was at pains to emphasize that this isn’t a non-refundable bailout and that taxpayers will be compensated for any aid. His terms may sit better with some companies than others. 

U.S. airlines are prepared to draw on up to $25 billion of federal grants to fund their payroll, despite the possibility that the government could demand equity or other securities in return, Reuters reported. All aid appears to include restrictions on share buybacks and executive compensation. Airlines may be reluctant to touch a second $25 billion pool of loans if they can raise funds elsewhere on less onerous terms. A stipulation in a draft proposal would have prevented the companies from accessing the government loans without first demonstrating they had exhausted all other options, JPMorgan Chase & Co. analyst Jamie Baker wrote in a report on Wednesday. Of course, the longer fleets are grounded across the world, the greater airlines’ needs and the fewer their options.

Boeing, on the other hand, currently has no intention of taking government aid, Mnuchin said Friday. The company had previously called for $60 billion in public and private liquidity, including government loans, for both it and its suppliers. “Right now, Boeing is saying they don’t need it,” Mnuchin said of a potential government check. “If we do something with Boeing, the taxpayers will be fully compensated." Even with Mnuchin's comments, the exact status of aid for Boeing is unclear. With the bill just clearing Congress, the company may still be reviewing its options. But statements from Boeing CEO Dave Calhoun earlier this week appeared to indicate that the company viewed the government as a lender of first resort. Speaking to CNBC on Tuesday and Fox Business on Tuesday, he bristled at the suggestion that a large aid package amounted to corporate welfare and should come with strings attached. The implication was that if the terms became too onerous — or really if there any terms at all — it would walk away. 

While Calhoun acknowledged to CNBC that aerospace industry balance sheets may have to evolve to better withstand moments like this, he opposed having the government regulate capital-allocation decisions and said an extension of a buyback ban beyond the use of a loan “seems silly.” That would amount to Congress “trying to solve problems other than the natural disaster we're facing,” he said. “This is the role of the government,” Calhoun told Fox Business on Tuesday. “The government is supposed to step up to get us from one end to the other.” It's interesting that Boeing backs government involvement when it involves a short-term liquidity injection, but is less supportive of things like increasing the Federal Aviation Administration’s direct oversight of airplane designs or paying taxes, not to mention the sharply critical eye it’s taken toward government subsidies for rival Airbus SE in the companies’ long-standing World Trade Organization dispute. Boeing has no interest in giving the government an equity stake that would allow taxpayers to make a return on this investment, Calhoun said. “If they force it, we just look at all the other options and we’ve got plenty of them,” he said.

It’s fair to ask why Boeing was ever seeking aid at all if it has access to all of these other options, but also worth noting that it remains unclear what in fact those options actually are. While Boeing's stock slumped on Friday amid a broader sell-off, the shares are still on track to finish the week up more than 70% amid optimism things aren’t quite as dire as they seemed just days earlier. The bond markets have been less enthusiastic, with yield spreads widening since early February, when Boeing’s biggest problem was still the prolonged grounding of its 737 Max jet. Fitch Ratings cut Boeing’s credit rating to BBB this week, joining S&P and Moody’s Investors Service in ranking the company in the lowest tier of investment-grade credit. “The credit markets at the moment are closed to us,” Calhoun said on Tuesday. Canaccord Genuity analyst Ken Herbert said Boeing may eventually be forced to take the government package, estimating the company is burning through $3 billion in cash a month with the delivery outlook for its commercial business this year shaky at best. 

I suppose there’s always the possibility that Warren Buffett could step in as he did with many other companies during the financial crisis, but he would want some kind of equity, too, and it's likely his terms would be much more expensive than those of the government. At the end of the day, as with most things when it comes to Boeing, this comes down to colliding notions of pride and hubris. On the one hand, despite the deep hit to its reputation from the Max challenges, Boeing still clearly views itself as a company of great importance, one that deserves to be a part of any sweeping distribution of government money. On the other, as such a great and important company, it seems unwilling to play by the rules that others will have to follow. Separately this week, Boeing CFO Greg Smith said a joint venture with Embraer SA — in which Boeing agreed to pay $4.2 billion for an 80% stake in a regional-jet alliance —  remains strategically important.
