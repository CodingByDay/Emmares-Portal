More nation-state than U.S. state, California is a land of superlatives: the most populous, the most prosperous and the fifth-largest economy in the world. Lately, however, the state is also the most incendiary in the union—and not just because of its wildfires and accompanying blackouts. The state has also sparked a heated debate over whether its future, as the climate crisis accelerates, remains golden, or is headed for twilight. California amplifies America’s own anxieties about its setbacks—as well as its hopes and resiliency. —Josh Petri

 
“Ideally, you’d want a leader to sit down and say, ‘Should we build a wall? Should we retreat? Consider all of the options.’ But that’s not really what happens.”

—A.R. Siders, an assistant professor at the University of Delaware’s Disaster Research Center. She is the lead author of a study on how North Carolina, a bellwether for American efforts at climate change adaptation, is doing it wrong. Decisions on projects and infrastructure are being made not on the basis of what’s effective or sensible in the long-run, but on issues of taxes and wealth. And as is often the case, the poorest are bearing the brunt.

 
 
Top stories

More than 11,000 experts from around the world are calling for a critical addition to the main climate strategy of dumping fossil fuels for renewable energy: there needs to be fewer humans on the planet.

President Donald Trump formally began withdrawing the U.S. from the Paris climate accord.

Consumers are increasingly aware of their food’s carbon footprint. So it shouldn’t come as a surprise that plant-based meat companies are now selling themselves as climate-friendly.

NASA flew a plane carrying gas-imaging equipment above California and made a discovery that surprised even the state’s own environmental agencies: A handful of operations are responsible for the vast majority of methane emissions.

The answer to the renewable energy industry’s biggest challenge is emerging in the Australian outback. Early next year, the Kennedy Energy Park will combine 43 megawatts of wind and 20 megawatts of solar with a 2-megawatt Tesla lithium-ion battery. Hybrid projects like these aim to tackle a problem faced by climate change deniers, and grid planners, across the globe: how to firm-up intermittent renewable power so that the lights stay on when the sun doesn’t shine or the wind doesn’t blow. It could also be a precursor of what’s to come in the next decade.

 
What we’ve been reading

Italy will become the first country to require students in every grade to study climate change. Starting next year, all public schools in the European nation will include about 33 hours of lessons a year in their curricula. 

Collecting and eating weeds isn’t a normal activity—yet. But as humans become increasingly dependent on a few select species for food, it might not be the worst idea to add some weeds to your diet.

Policymakers wring their hands at the threat climate change poses to economic growth, suggesting we can’t save the planet and support emerging economies. Yale professor Narasimha Rao shows that cutting inequality can improve our ability to mitigate climate change.
