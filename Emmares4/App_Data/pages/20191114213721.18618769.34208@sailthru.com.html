It was jarring to hear Hong Kong’s senior superintendent for police describe the city as being on the brink of breakdown this week, but it wasn’t outlandish.

Violence escalated to new extremes, sparked by the death of a protester who’d fallen from a parking garage near where police were dispersing demonstrators. In the subsequent maelstrom, two men were critically injured.

One was a protester, who in a confrontation with a policeman was shot at point-blank range. The other was a man who appeared to be arguing with protesters when someone doused him with a flammable liquid and set him on fire. Both incidents were captured in shocking videos that then fueled further confrontation.

The rhetoric out of Beijing was likewise noteworthy. China’s official Xinhua News Agency described Hong Kong as being at “its most critical juncture.” The People’s Daily called on the city’s students, who’ve been among the most extreme protesters, to remain “rational.” But it was the Global Times newspaper, known for its more nationalistic stance, which cut to the chase when it warned of direct military intervention.

Chinese troops mobilizing on the streets of Hong Kong is a nightmare scenario. It could spark a knee-jerk exodus from the city’s financial markets, drag down property prices and prompt multinationals to reconsider their presence in the city.

And for China, it would give supply chains greater impetus to migrate out of the mainland, a trend already accelerated by American tariffs. And it could make a deal to deescalate the trade war so politically costly in the U.S. as to make such an agreement infeasible.

Spreading Misinformation

The prominence of social media has been a hallmark of Hong Kong's protests. It’s one reason why the demonstrations have remained relatively leaderless. It also helps explain the recent surge in violence. When Alex Chow, the protester who fell from a parking garage, died last week, posts swirled on social media asserting he was being chased by police when he fell, or was even pushed. Those claims have been roundly denied by the police and have yet to be substantiated by any mainstream news outlets. But that’s not stopped it from fanning anger. The unintentional, and sometimes intentional, spread of false information is having as notable an impact on the city's streets as tear gas and burning barricades.

Surging Pork Prices

Chat with a Chinese economist at a party and she might tell you this joke: while CPI means consumer price index in most countries, in China it's short for Chinese pork index. It's kind of true. Inflation data released this week showed Chinese consumer prices gained at the fastest pace in seven-years. Fueling that increase was a 101.3% increase in pork prices thanks to a plunge in supplies after African swine fever decimated hog herds. What's not funny is that this surge has come as prices at the factory gate have fallen for four consecutive months, meaning businesses are becoming less profitable and the economic outlook more uncertain. Not the best time for the price of pork, the staple meat for most Chinese families, to double in price.

Deal or No Deal 

The likelihood of the U.S. and China reaching a phase-one trade deal got a bit more dicey this week. It began with President Donald Trump saying he hadn't yet agreed to any roll back of tariffs, a seeming contradiction of White House economic adviser Larry Kudlow, who'd said earlier that there'd be concessions on levies associated with any phase-one agreement. Then in a speech at the Economic Club of New York, Trump threatened to "substantially" raise tariffs if a deal isn't reached, though he also added that an accord was close and could happen soon. Meanwhile, it emerged that negotiations had become bogged down around the question of China's commitment to buy more American farm goods, though Beijing's decision to lift a four-year ban on imports of U.S. poultry did suggest that some progress was made. The one constant about this trade war has been how hard it is to gauge what will happen next.

Debt Threat

The health of China's small lenders is becoming an increasingly bigger headache for Beijing. In May, regulators were forced to seize a troubled bank for the first time since 1998 and at least two others have needed bailouts since then. Then last month rumors started by an obscure social media account sparked a run on a rural lender. With economic growth slowing and authorities showing no let up in their campaign to rein in China's sprawling shadow banking industry, the plight of these institutions looks more likely to worsen than to improve. And based on the latest developments in Beijing, policy makers seem to be preparing for things to get more difficult. One step being considered is encouraging trouble lenders to merge. Local governments have also begun injecting capital in some banks. But whatever happens, regulators have made clear that they see bankruptcies as the last option.
