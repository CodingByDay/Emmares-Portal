Treating the medical problems faced by America's homeless people starts with putting a roof over their heads.

That's the idea being tested by UnitedHealth in a program that provides subsidized apartments for some homeless patients on Medicaid. The goal: Break the cycle of expensive repeat hospital stays by giving patients with complex medical issues a safe, warm, dry place to lay their heads.

The health-care industry has been abuzz over the idea that improving housing, nutrition, or transportation will also improve patients' health and reduce medical spending. So-called social determinants of health are a burgeoning field of study.

UnitedHealth's early results show the promise and limits of the industry’s ability to ameliorate patients’ difficult social circumstances. For the most staggeringly expensive patients, housing is a clear cost-saver. One man’s monthly medical cost dropped from $12,945 when he was on the streets to $2,073 in housing. But the savings aren’t sufficient to justify housing many patients with serious but less costly medical and mental-health needs.

Ultimately, the failures of America’s social safety net are landing on the doorstep of the health-care system. The industry, in turn, may attempt to address social needs, but if doing so doesn’t pay for itself, the response will always be outmatched by the scale of the problem.—John Tozzi
