China is plenty familiar with crisis, be it protests in Hong Kong, tariffs from Washington or Covid-19. What’s new is a growing sentiment domestically that sees China as a refuge relative to the rest of the world.

It’s not hard to see why. As the coronavirus spreads overseas, there have been plenty of missteps by other countries trying to contain infections, a fact Chinese media haven’t been shy about highlighting. At the same time, the steps China took to curb the virus appear to be working, a point Beijing has been keen to drive home.

That juxtaposition was especially apparent this week as President Xi Jinping arrived in Wuhan for his first visit to the outbreak’s epicenter. Xi’s trip and some easing of the quarantine on the city and surrounding Hubei province were the strongest signals yet that Beijing believes the worst is over.

Other parts of the world look less optimistic. Indeed, Robert Redfield, director the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, told American lawmakers this week that “the new China is Europe.”

Adding to that sense of sanctuary are financial markets. In the wake of a historic plunge in oil prices this week, China’s yuan held near its strongest level since January, even as other currencies saw spikes in volatility. And as stock markets across the world plummeted, shares traded in Shanghai and Shenzhen have held near multi-week highs.

But there’s also plenty to suggest that this view of China as refuge may be misplaced. While China has made substantial progress containing the coronavirus, it still accounts for the vast majority of cases globally. And whereas Beijing has been fighting the virus for months, most other countries are just beginning to grapple with widespread infections.

Then there’s the question of whether China can be a sanctuary if the rest of the world is in turmoil. That’s a quandary officials are grappling with already. While the number of new domestic infections is trending sharply down, the instances of travelers from overseas bringing the virus into China is rising. And as China’s factories restart production, they now face the risk that foreign demand for their goods will crater.

In a world that’s more interconnected than ever, the idea of a safe haven may well be out of date.

Economic Reboot

There’s little doubt that 2020 began with China’s economy getting walloped by the coronavirus. The question now is how big a rebound Beijing wants. If first-quarter growth comes in at 4% as most economists expect, getting to 6% for the full year — just shy of 2019’s 6.1% — could need a stimulus package on the scale of what was unveiled after the global financial crisis. That 4 trillion yuan package has been controversial, with many blaming it for a subsequent surge in China’s debt levels and the building of proverbial bridges to nowhere. The approach Beijing picks for this crisis will have broad implications not just for China, but also the rest of the world. With global growth slumping to levels not seen since the Great Recession, how fast the Chinese growth engine runs is more important than ever.

Oil Shock

China is the world’s largest importer of oil, and so when prices plunge as they did this week, it should in theory come out a winner. In practice, things are a bit more complicated. The coronavirus, for one, has suppressed demand across the economy. And while the government looks like it may buy shipments for the country’s strategic reserves, purchases more broadly will be slower in coming, meaning the benefits will also take longer to materialize. Lower energy costs could also add to the drag on factory prices, which have been in deflation for seven of the last eight months. If businesses can’t charge more for their goods, they’ll also find it hard to hire and invest. The geopolitical implications of lower oil prices for countries from Venezuela to Iran will also not make the lives of Beijing’s diplomats any easier.

Property Shakeout

Business has been bad in China. The coronavirus has slammed car sales, dried up travel and forced restaurants to shut. But even amid this widespread downturn, the hardships of the property industry stand out. More than 100 real estate firms filed for bankruptcy in the first two months of 2020 after fewer than 500 did so all of last year. It’s partly due to a slide in transactions as buyers stay home. But even before the outbreak, the sector was under pressure from government curbs aimed at reining in home prices. Compounding the problem is the prevalence of smaller regional firms. Take Hubei for example. The province where the virus first emerged is home to 4,300 developers. One upshot is consolidation. For those companies able to survive, there could be significant opportunities to pick up assets.

Blame Game

Covid-19 is the official name of the disease that’s infected more than 120,000 people around the world. It’s not what everyone calls it though. “China virus” is one moniker that’s been used, including by a supporter of Donald Trump’s in a tweet that the president then retweeted. Trump, during a televised national address, described it as a “foreign virus” while U.S. Secretary of State Mike Pompeo has called it the “Wuhan virus.” Beijing has pushed back against such names, accusing them of being a way to blame China for what the World Health Organization officially designated a global pandemic this week. The finger-pointing between the world’s foremost powers about the origins of this crisis does not augur well for finding a solution to it.
