H.E.R. Releases New 'Fight for You' Music Video From 'Judas and the Black Messiah' (EXCLUSIVE)

When director Shaka King was looking for an artist to deliver the end credit song for “Judas and the Black Messiah,” he went to Grammy winner H.E.R.

Of the qualities that made her the perfect artist to complete the task, he says, “versatility in musicianship, soul, artistic and personal integrity. She’s a throwback.”

King’s approach was to give the Oscar-nominated singer-songwriter notes about what he didn’t want. He says, “I really wanted to give her as much space creatively as possible – while expressing a desire to hear something contemporary with echoes of 1968. When I heard the Curtis Mayfield inspiration, I was like, ‘Yes!’”

'The Boys' Costume Designer Laura Jean Shannon on the Infamous Homelander Outfit

The characters in Amazon’s “The Boys” are not your typical superheroes. They work at Vought Industries and spend all their time in their suits ready for action.

Voight was an organization formed during World War II by the Nazis with the sole intent of creating superheroes  to gain profit and money. Those superheroes were known as The Seven.

Leading them team is John, aka Homelander. With his super hearing and super strength, Homelander can also fly.

Costume designer Laura Jean Shannon was responsible for crafting the looks of this superhero. She says, “It was imperative to bake details into his design that hinted at the complexities of character that would present themselves as season one unfolded. We needed to provide the audience with a quintessential hero evocative of the superheroes we have all grown accustomed to seeing... a true American hero.”

The first thing she landed on was that he would be based upon a classic superhero construction made from a latex foam rippling muscle suit that had fabric adhered to it, to enhance the visual that Homelander himself was indestructible and superhuman as opposed to the suit creating a protective shield.

Shannon explains, “Once my team and I decided that pieces from his comic origin would be the DNA that would live within our version, we began researching iconography and uniforms from historical fascist regimes.”

She found a stretch twill fabric that would give him some gravitas with a military officer quality.

Screen printing tech came in handy to repeat the eagle pattern.

“The eagle theme carries into his sculpted epaulets and belt,” Shannon explains., “He is the human embodiment of a walking American flag. All the while, it’s hiding in plain sight that each of these elements is infused with sinister roots.”

'Collective’ Filmmaker Alexander Nanau on Shooting a Key Scene

Courtesy of Magnolia Pictures

Alexander Nanau wore three hats for the Oscar-nominated documentary, “Collective.” He served as director, producer and cinematographer that tells the story of the 2015 fire at the Colectiv nightclub which killed 64 people and injured hundreds. 

In a new clip, Nanau explains the scene that took place the day after the team of journalists broke the story about the diluted disinfectants that were used in the case of the Colectiv fire victims.  

Journalist Catalin Tolontan, the survivors and parents of those who died of infections in hospitals were about to meet in a TV studio for a live show on national television.  Nanau says, “It was important to me to try and capture the awkwardness of the situation where these people now meet once this terrible truth was discovered by the journalists.”  

Tolontan would admit on public television that the Romanian press, including him and his team, had failed the Colectiv fire victims because they believed the authorities and their lies for too long. Only now they fully understood that these people and the whole society were systematically lied to through every state institution. Say Nanau.  “I’m glad I was just filming Tolontan when it happened.” 

He adds, “It was important to me to capture and describe visually the tension between these people meeting in this very technical and cold environment of a huge TV studio. I needed to be close to them, but at the same time I also needed to understand visually how a whole country was watching them live on TV.”

His objective was to frame the characters in a cinematic and emphatic way as this was all unfolding.

Behind the Costumes of 'Pinocchio' 

Courtesy of Magnolia Pictures

Oscar-nominated costume designer Massimo Cantini Parrini on the costumes of "Pinocchio."

"The inspiration for the costumes of Pinocchio comes after a lot of research. I always need a lot of research to face a historical period, and the research of the costumes of the work that I have to face is the most interesting part of my work. I was born as a costume historian and therefore for me it is fundamental.

"I started looking for paintings of the Tuscan "Macchiaioli" painting, the colors and light of those paintings were important elements for me and the director Matteo Garrone. I took a lot from my private collection of period clothing for the models of the clothes of the main characters of the film. I researched ancient fabrics and I documented how the clothes of the time after being worn for a long time were stained, torn, patched etc. A poor world is what is told in this story, only the fairy and her world belong to an ancient wealth.

"The big job after making the costumes was to make them real through aging. For me, the truth that a costume must transmit across the screen to the viewer is very important. For the costumes I was also very inspired by the first illustrations that came out in the story of Pinocchio written by Carlo Collodi.

"The author of Pinocchio called Enrico Mazzanti and Carlo Chiostri to illustrate the book, one in 1883 and the other in 1901, and I must say that it was fundamental for me to have studied those illustrations. In our work the idea is fundamental, once you have the idea you can always find the right key. The film also talks about a world of anthropomorphic beings, so the study and colors of animals was very important for me. Each character has the colors of the animal represented."

Editing 'Promising Young Woman'

Courtesy of Focus Features

Editor Fred Thoraval on cutting the pharmacy scene in "Promising Young Woman."

"We worked with my assistant/additional editor Emily Freund to cut the beginning of this scene like a classic dialog scene. She worked to stitch together the best improv lines from Bo & Carey from different takes to create a natural flow.

"I focused on the scenes leading up to the Pharmacy. We wanted to make sure that the emotional journey between the scene when Cassie leaves Ryan’s apartment (which seems to be the end of their story) to the moment where they come together to sing the Paris Hilton song felt natural and as seamless as possible.

"I created a small montage with shots from different scenes that we’ve repurposed, to show Cassie back in her monotonous life from the beginning of the movie, showing the sense of the loneliness she was experiencing. This helped us by extending the time before Ryan returns to the coffee shop and re-enforced the moment when he kisses her.

It took us a while, the scene going back and forth between Emily and myself, before finding a good balance but as soon as all the elements were in place, these sequences clicked. The kiss was earned and allowed us to end the beautiful music from Anthony Willis and to transition naturally in the pharmacy until the 'love montage' starts."
