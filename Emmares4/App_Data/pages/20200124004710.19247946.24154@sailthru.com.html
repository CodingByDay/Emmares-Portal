The Quality of Nestle Is Not Cheap….

Some stocks are on a remarkable run. To name just a few: Alphabet Inc., the parent of Google, is now the world’s third trillion-dollar company by market cap, joining Microsoft Corp. and Apple Inc., with Amazon.com Inc. apparently soon to follow; Tesla Inc. is now worth more than $100 billion, making it twice the size of General Motors Co., the next largest U.S. auto-manufacturer; and Netflix Inc., despite spending prodigious amounts of money and facing ever more competition, has just had its best day on the market in more than 12 months.

This sounds like the final victory for the glamorous “FAANG” stocks, the popular acronym for the elite group of internet leaders that has been hogging market gains. These have quite understandably awakened unpleasant memories of the last melt-up of dot-com stocks 20 years ago. All it needed was an assertion at Davos that the boom-bust cycle was over to arouse fresh predictions of an approaching market top.

But for a really intriguing and counter-intuitive tell that all isn’t well in the stock markets, it’s worth looking at some much less glamorous names. The following chart reveals that the branded consumer products groups Nestle SA and Procter & Gamble Co. have both somehow outperformed the NYSE Fang+ index over the last two years:

Some caveats are in order. I am showing Nestle in local currency terms; it is very slightly behind the FAANGs in dollar terms. The two-year time frame captures an early period when the technology favorites were already performing very well. Starting a little earlier would put them back in the lead. And as is clear from the chart, the FAANGs are currently rising much faster.

But even with all these qualifications in place, something remarkable is afoot. The rally of the consumer goods giants can be attributed to a sharp increase in their valuation over the last two years. It isn’t long since they traded for much the same multiple of book value as the S&P 500. Now they have leaped to a wide premium.
 

Some very boring and un-glamorous but very high-quality companies are performing very well at present. Generally when investors will pay up heavily for quality, it is a sign that growth and safety are regarded as scarce. In times of recovery, value companies — which are already cheaply priced — are perceived as offering some safety. Stocks that start with a less demanding valuation are less likely to fall.  But at present, people are not seeing it that way. As the following chart from Societe Generale SA makes clear, the last two years have seen remarkable willingness to pay up for quality, rather than value. Quality has seldom so expensive and value has seldom been so cheap:

Such a wide disparity is normally an outgrowth of bad economic times, as can be seen by comparing the difference between valuations for quality and value stocks with the global manufacturing PMI data:

An environment when quality does this well isn’t generally one where people are prepared to bid up the prices of companies like Tesla and Netflix to the skies. But that is what has happened. Meanwhile, the stock market grows narrower. Generally the size effect means that small-caps should be expected to beat “mega-caps,” most of whose growth is behind them; and that an equally weighted version of the S&P 500, in which every stock counts for 0.2%, should beat a standard market cap-weighted index, in which the FAANGs currently dominate. But the last five years has seen a steady narrowing. The Russell 2000 small-cap index is dropping ever further behind its Top 50 mega-cap index, while the equal-weighted S&P is steadily lagging:

All of this is disquieting. The symptoms of mania and deep economic pessimism are co-existing in the same market. Many stocks are in their own bear market, still far off their highs, but a truly exciting growth company, or a truly reliable high-quality company, will currently be prohibitively expensive.

But there is at least some good reason why “quality” companies are so-called. Over the last quarter-century, in dollar terms, the dependable Nestle has somehow handily beaten the S&P 500, and even the remarkable U.S. tech sector. 

It is a high-quality company indeed. But it is a pity that you now have to pay so much to buy it.

 

Minsky in the Heights

Thank you to all who contributed questions to Thursday’s online discussion about the economist Hyman Minsky and his theory that stability in financial markets leads inevitably to instability. Unfortunately we have had a few technical glitches, so I cannot yet share the transcript.

I can, however, share some extra Minsky trivia. I did not previously know that Minsky was the son of Mensheviks, who fled to the U.S. from Belarus in the wake of the Russian revolution. It was perhaps not surprising that his solution to the ills of capitalism seemed unacceptably left-wing to many.

I already knew that he was the teaching assistant for Joseph Schumpeter — famous for coining the term “creative destruction” — while at Harvard. But I now discover that he had even more illustrious company at his high school. Like many Jewish migrants between the wars, he settled in the Washington Heights neighborhood of uptown Manhattan, and went to George Washington High School.  The emigre community must have made for quite an intellectual atmosphere. While there, he overlapped with Henry Kaufman, the famous “Doctor Doom” of Salomon Brothers who predicted the inflation and rising bond yields of the 1970s; former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger; and Alan Greenspan, the long-time chairman of the Federal Reserve — whose policies were almost the exact reverse of what Minsky would have recommended.

All these men were brought up by migrants, lived through the Depression in their youth, and were privileged to mix in what must have been an extraordinary intellectual milieu in their adolescence. Is there a similar cluster of bright young intellects somewhere now, formed by the world of the financial crisis, and the increasingly polarized society that has followed it? And what ideas might they come up with to try to change the world? 

I doubt that cluster is in Washington Heights, the neighborhood I now call home. It has changed since the war, going into serious decline and then recovery, and George Washington High School became a training ground for baseball players, including Rod Carew, now in the Hall of Fame, and Manny Ramirez, who would almost certainly have joined him by now had he not failed drug tests at the end of his career. And these days my most famous neighbor is Lin-Manuel Miranda, composer of Hamilton. His musical about the neighborhood, In the Heights, comes out this summer. With any luck that will put the neighborhood back on the map. A kind of intellectual creative destruction in the neighborhood continues. But I doubt it will ever again play host to a group of young Minskys, Kaufmans, Kissingers and Greenspans, all coming to very different conclusions about how to change the world.
