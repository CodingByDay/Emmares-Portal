Hi all, it's Eric. Elizabeth Warren is ready to break up Big Tech (and big business writ large). She's behind a new draft bill that, should it ever become law, could bring tech deal-making to a screeching halt.

I reported this week with my colleague Joshua Brustein that Warren’s staff is circulating proposed legislation reviewing “mega mergers” over the past two decades, and banning them going forward.

That would have radical implications for the industry, and has been largely overlooked in the flurry of anti-tech rhetoric on the campaign trail. The draft proposes outright banning mergers when either company has more than $40 billion in revenue, or when both companies have revenue of more than $15 billion. That theoretically puts a freeze on acquisitions by many of the major technology firms, including Alphabet Inc., Facebook Inc., Apple Inc. and Amazon.com Inc. The proposed legislation makes exceptions for buying companies that seem to be imploding, but in sum, it would make it much more difficult for huge companies to buy smaller ones.

There’s a tension in Warren’s antitrust philosophy here. When she first laid out her plan to break up Big Tech, one of the key selling points was that it would help startups compete with big technology giants. Companies like Snap Inc. and Yelp Inc. have struggled to compete with the likes of Facebook and Google respectively. There’s certainly an argument to be made that Facebook’s acquisition of Instagram or WhatsApp derailed a potential competitor before it had time to grow.

On the other hand, much of the startup ecosystem depends on acquisitions. All the time, companies get bought, founders get rich, then sally forth to create new ones. And some promising upstarts won't be long-term successful by themselves–they might only make sense as part of a company that can roll them into a larger, profitable package. For example, Warren has suggested that Nest should be spun out of Google. It’s not clear that what was at the time of acquisition a relatively small hardware business could have succeeded in the long run without a wealthy owner.

Similarly, when Google bought mapping rival Waze, the company didn’t exactly have a clear path to profitability. Warren has proposed unwinding that acquisition as well. In one light, this makes sense. There are three big consumer mapping apps. Google owns two of them and Apple, the world’s most valuable tech company, owns the other one. But would Waze still be a free, widely used service if it was a standalone company?

When it comes to antitrust, Warren is arguing that consumer harm shouldn’t be the only reason to object to a merger. If M&A hurts competition then that should be a serious consideration as well. But there are times when those two values are in conflict. Will voters be okay if more competition translates into fewer free Google services?

Of course, those free services don’t come without a cost. Google sucks up your data and uses its ecosystem of services to reinforce its powerful search-based advertising machine. Still, consumers get high-quality email, search and mapping at no charge.

Even without Warren’s bill it’s become harder for tech giants to buy startups. The Trump administration’s Department of Justice closely examined a relatively tiny acquisition–Google’s $2.6 billion purchase of the data analytics company Looker. And don’t forget that the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States has already been taking a harder look at international tie-ups. (A housekeeping note: Michael Bloomberg is also seeking the Democratic presidential nomination. Bloomberg is the founder and majority owner of Bloomberg LP, the parent company of Bloomberg News.)

Democratic voters will ultimately get to weigh in on whether the antitrust fight is a winning issue. The dominance of giant tech companies has real benefits for consumers, despite the sometimes disturbing consequences. As people mull the downsides of the Big Tech era, they'll also have to ask: How much do I like this Nest? –Eric Newcomer
