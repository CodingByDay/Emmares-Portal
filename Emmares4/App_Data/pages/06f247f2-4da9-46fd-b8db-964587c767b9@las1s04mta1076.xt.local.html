HERE, THERE AND RANSOMWARE — The ransomware epidemic ravaging the country will remain a key area of focus in Washington this week. On Wednesday, DHS Secretary Alejandro Mayorkas — who last week reiterated that ransomware is a national security threat — will speak at a U.S. Chamber of Commerce event devoted to the topic. His remarks will wrap up just as the House Homeland Security Committee gavels in for a virtual hearing on ransomware. The events are the latest in a broader push by the U.S. government to highlight the dangers of ransomware and signal that the criminals responsible will be pursued, according to experts and officials.

“Washington, like most places, is not a monolith. Some parts of Washington have really understood the threat of ransomware and have been at the forefront of trying to stop it for years,” said Allan Liska, Intelligence Analyst at Recorded Future, citing the work done by the FBI and CISA. “What is different now is that ransomware has been identified as a priority by leaders in the government. So, rather than pockets of concern, ransomware will be a priority across multiple agencies. Hopefully, this will lead to swift and effective action,” he added before stresing the government must find ways to tackle current and future ransomware threats.

The Biden administration is “just getting started,” according to Philip Reiner, the CEO of the Institute for Security and Technology, which created a task force of experts to examine the issue and last week published recommendations for policy-makers . “As they continue to fill senior level roles, I would expect to see much more than what we are even seeing now — to include on ransomware.” Cybersecurity has been “left to languish in silos in recent years, without the necessary top-down centralized coordination that is required for the scale of threats we face, and I would assert no administration at this point could do anything but ramp up on this front if they expect to be seen as good stewards of national security and broader public health and safety,” he added.

Of course, as Washington steps up its game, so do foreign adversaries. “We have seen nation-state activity rise in the ransomware and digital extortion arena,” said Principal Associate Deputy Attorney General John Carlin. “This blended threat—one both criminal and national security in nature—creates an additional dimension of risk,” he explained. For example, a nation-state actor can use ransomware as a “cyber weapon of mass destruction” to target critical sectors and infrastructure or tap hackers using ransomware as a way to generate income that can be funneled into other illicit activities. “There is certainly more that can be done on all levels of government to address the ransomware and digital extortion threat,” according to Carlin.

Department of Justice

ANOTHER DOJ CYBER INITIATIVE — The Justice Department is launching a 120-day review of its approach to combating cyber threats, hoping to identify innovative new ways to reduce the volume and impact of ransomware, botnets and other digital attacks, DOJ’s No. 2 official said on Friday. “We want to bring forth actionable recommendations … on, what can we be doing better, working with our partners across borders, to address these threats?” Deputy Attorney General Lisa Monaco said at the Munich Cybersecurity Conference. Monaco said the U.S. and its allies “need to rethink … and really assess, are we using the most effective strategies” to fight cyber crime.

The 120-day review will look at how cryptocurrencies facilitate cyber crime, how supply chain attacks such as SolarWinds can metastasize from the weakest link to sensitive national security institutions, and how to address “the blended threat of nation-states and criminal enterprises sometimes working together to exploit our own infrastructure against us.” On ransomware, Monaco said the review and the task force would both look at how to “go after the entire ecosystem that is being used.”

But DOJ isn’t just concerned about the threats of today. It’s trying to get ahead of the threats of tomorrow. “What is the next ransomware that we’re going to have to deal with?” Monaco asked. “What is the next exploitation by bad actors of other technologies? … We have got to move at the same speed that our adversaries are.” That philosophy informed the government’s decision to seek a court order to expunge malware from hacked Microsoft Exchange servers. “We have got to get innovative and aggressive,” Monaco said, “if we are going to keep pace with what the malicious actors are doing.”

Monaco is no stranger to cybersecurity issues, having served as President Barack Obama’s homeland security adviser and one of the first leaders of DOJ’s National Security Division. During the interview, she recalled how cyber challenges increasingly dominated the briefings that she would give to Obama. The cyber threat landscape “has become more diffuse, more sophisticated, more dangerous than ever before,” she said. “I think we’re at a pivot point yet again.”
 

 
CISA

THE MORE YOU KNOW — CISA on Friday urged all Codecov users to review the company’s latest update to determine if they were a victim of a supply chain attack that began back in January. The DHS cyber wing “recommends giving special attention to Codecov’s guidance on changing (‘re-rolling’) potentially affected credentials, tokens, and keys. CISA also recommends revoking and reissuing any potentially affected certificates.”

The San Francisco-based company, which provides code and software testing, discovered the unauthorized intrusion and manipulation of its Bash Uploader script on April 1. Investigators probing the breach told Reuters last month that the attackers responsible managed to exploit not only Codecov software, but potentially used the organization to leapfrog into a number of customer networks.
Research Corner

MAKING FRIENDS LEFT AND RIGHT — An advanced cyber threat actor with possible ties to the Chinese government is utilizing a previously unknown malware, named PortDoor, to target the Russian defense sector, researchers at Cybereason said in a report published on Friday . The firm’s Nocturnus Team observed the malign actor focusing on the Rubin Design Bureau, which designs submarines for the Russian Federation’s Navy. Igor Vladimirovich, the bureau’s chief, was the initial target of the digital attack when he received a phishing email.

Cybereaon said the attack began with the RoyalRoad Weaponizer — a popular tool among several Chinese cybercriminals — which exploits vulnerabilities in Microsoft’s Equation Editor. “The accumulated evidence such as the infection vector, social engineering style, use of RoyalRoad against similar targets, and other similarities between the newly discovered backdoor sample and other known Chinese APT malware all bear the hallmarks of a threat actor operating on behalf of Chinese state-sponsored interests,” researchers said.

The report does not name the specific threat actor but notes that a file weaponized by RoyalRoad was previously observed being used by previously identified actors. “However, we are also aware that there could be other groups, known or yet unknown, that could be behind the attack and the development of the PortDoor backdoor,” according to researchers. “We hope that as time goes by, and with more evidence gathered, the attribution could be more concrete.”
