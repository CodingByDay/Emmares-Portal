Sociologists categorize everyday interactions among people into five types: exchange, competition, conflict, cooperation and accommodation. Even though our social interactions are curtailed by the coronavirus outbreak, we still see those types in play.

1. Exchange: The Fed cut interest rates to help a flagging economy.
2. Competition: "Hamster buying" hits Europe.
3. Conflict: Companies jack up prices on masks and hand gel.
4. Cooperation: Wuhan doctors share observations on the disease.
5: Accommodation: Japan says postponing the Olympics is possible.

If you've been too busy working from home or homeschooling your kids to even think about social interactions, here are some stories you may have missed.

The Virus Hunter

Ralph Baric has studied coronaviruses since the 1990s, and now he's trying to find the right drug to treat the latest global outbreak. We go inside the secretive North Carolina lab where Baric and his team burn the midnight oil trying to crack the case.

Freeing Fannie and Freddie

Mark Calabria is weighing how to pay off what likely is the biggest mortgage ever. He's the regulator overseeing Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, and his decision on how to free the companies from government control will ripple through a $5 trillion market.

Belt and Road to Nowhere

One of China's most ambitious projects, a planned $62 billion initiative in Pakistan, still isn't ready after seven years. And that may not change anytime soon as China tightens its spending belt because of a stalling economy.

Road Rage

Speaking of conflict and competition, we look at how infighting over a proposed $7 billion deal to run Malaysia's toll highways fractured the coalition government and prompted the shock resignation of Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad.

A Real Page-Turner

There's a new bibliophile in town, and he's looking to transform a creaky bookseller into Amazon's chief competitor. James Daunt is rewriting Barnes & Noble's business plan by using some of the same strategies that turned around U.K. chain Waterstones.

He Needs a $17 Billion Plan

It's good to set the pace, but putting your $17 billion fortune on the line to do so can be daunting. Russian tycoon Alexey Mordashov is trying to find the best way to pass that wealth to his children in a country where this doesn't happen very often.

The Amazon of Africa?

The startup Copia says it may have found the secret sauce for making e-commerce profitable in Africa: free deliveries within two days. The secret behind that secret? Thousands of local shops serving as pickup points for customers.
