After jumping in April, inflation rose again in May. And it doesn’t look like price rises are going to ease anytime soon, according to this week’s forthcoming issue of The Kiplinger Letter. In fact, the Letter’s staff expect consumer inflation for 2021 as a whole to clock in at 5%. Consumers are flush with cash after Congress approved trillions of dollars in stimulus checks and other forms of financial assistance. Many unemployed workers are receiving regular state unemployment benefits plus extra federal money each week. Some employers are complaining that they can’t fill open jobs because would-be job applicants are earning more on unemployment. Beyond worker shortages, there are also shortages of all sorts of materials and components needed to keep up with demand for everything from houses to washing machines. And shipping delays just add to businesses’ costs. Strong demand plus supply chain kinks: It’s a recipe for higher inflation.

If you’re shopping for a car these days, you’re going to have fewer options and you’re going to pay more than you normally would. Why? Inventories of new cars on dealer lots are at their lowest level relative to sales in decades. Automakers that idled factories a year ago when COVID-19 shut down much of the economy were already struggling to ramp up production again when buyers started returning to showrooms during the summer of 2020. Then, a shortage of semiconductors started slowing or halting assembly lines this year. Meanwhile, consumers are flush with cash and they are bidding up the prices of the limited number of new cars for sale. That in turn has driven up the price of used cars. In fact, rising car prices – for both new and used models – were a major reason for overall consumer inflation jumping by 5% in May.
 
The situation isn’t going to get better soon, unfortunately. Ivan Drury, senior manager, insights for car shopping website Edmunds.com, expects it will take six months for car supplies to rise enough to alleviate the worst shortages. To get to the point where inventories are healthy enough that shoppers start seeing the sort of deals they normally expect, “wait a year” is his advice. 
 
But if you need a new car now, there are still some ways you can limit the financial pain. For one, suggests Drury, consider leasing rather than buying. Dealers aren’t offering much in the way of cash rebates or juicy financing for buyers, but you can still find leases with low monthly payments. For shoppers who normally would never consider leasing, now might be the time to try it. You can drive home a new car that won’t cost you too much per month, and you aren’t locked into it for the long term. If you’re worried about exceeding the mileage you can drive the car before steep penalties kick in, you can buy extra miles upfront at a lower cost. If you’re shopping used, check out certified preowned models. Drury says that they often come with attractive financing offers that cost you less over the life of the loan than you would pay if you bought a cheaper, non-CPO model with a higher-rate loan.
