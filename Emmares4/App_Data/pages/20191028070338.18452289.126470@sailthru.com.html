Hey y’all, it’s Austin. Last Tuesday, during a wild week of executive reshuffling at major U.S. companies, Nike Inc. announced that John Donahoe will soon take the CEO reins from Mark Parker. It’s a telling choice: Parker, a former college runner, brought his famed design savvy to the “Just Do It” brand during his four decades at the sports apparel icon. Donahoe, a former chief executive officer of EBay Inc. and corporate software provider ServiceNow Inc., comes with a more technical skillset.

In recent years, Nike, like all retail giants, has been grappling with radical changes in how consumers shop in the internet era. Apropos for a company that counts LeBron James and Michael Jordan as personifications of its brand, Nike has coalesced its digital strategy around what it calls the "Consumer Direct Offense," a major push into web sales, mobile apps and other e-retail channels. Parker talked up how his successor will “help us accelerate our whole digital transformation.” But Donahoe’s focus will likely be on the more boring aspects of that evolution—think data mining and predictive analytics—than the flashier digital endeavors that captivated the company in the past.

A few years ago, Nike’s digital transformation wasn’t going well. As the wearables market was heating up, the company centered much of its strategy around the 2012 launch of its high-design FuelBand, a digital-fitness tracker competing with startup rivals Fitbit Inc. and Jawbone Inc. When I once visited Parker at Nike’s well-manicured campus in Beaverton, Oregon, he was wearing a FuelBand on each wrist, as if to demonstrate how pivotal the gizmos would prove to Nike’s retail future. But several product generations later, shortly before Apple released its watch, Parker killed the FuelBand division altogether, a surprising move some observers saw as a sign of the failure of his digital ambitions.

More recently, Parker has had better luck increasing Nike’s digital reach. The company rolled out a slew of mobile initiatives that helped send online sales soaring 42% last quarter. The Nike+ fitness app system now has more than 170 million members; there’s a premium shopping app for the Air Max obsessed; and the shoe maker has acquired a string of A.I. and data analytics startups such as Celect and Zodiac Inc. to boost its understanding of consumers’ shopping behavior. Parker has estimated that e-commerce will represent a third of Nike’s business by 2023.

In retrospect, even FuelBand looks semi-prescient. Parker seemed to have realized the software powering these systems—and the data it collects in the process—was more valuable to Nike than developing the hardware itself. While Jawbone is now defunct and Fitbit struggles to compete with Apple, Nike is using the digital assets that it’s built up in its day-to-day business. That’s become a trend for the company: This past April, it bought computer vision startup Invertex Ltd., which makes technology that could eventually enable customers to accurately measure their feet at home. That may not have the panache of a wearable, but it will arguably do more for Nike’s bottom line.

Donahoe’s appointment heralds the sneaker giant’s shift from viewing technology as a whiz-bang consumer-facing advantage, to using it as a tool that undergirds the entire operation. To be sure, Nike’s stores are still filled with gimmicks like QR codes, and they’ve certainly pursued some questionable tech (looking at you, Siri-powered shoelaces). But with Donahoe at the helm, expect Nike to start taking a smarter approach on its digital offensive—from new to additive manufacturing to data science—even if it’s a quieter one. —Austin Carr
