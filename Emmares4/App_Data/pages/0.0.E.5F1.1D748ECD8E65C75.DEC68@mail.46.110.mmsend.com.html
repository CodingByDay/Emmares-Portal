AN ENERGY JOLT FOR INVESTORS. Energy has been the best market sector of 2021, and it’s not even close -- with its 36.9% gains nearly quadrupling the S&P 500’s 9.5% advance. (The next-best sector: Financials, +26.5%.) But investors are no doubt looking at the floor, waiting for the rug to be pulled out from underneath them, given a decade of underperformance peppered with several false starts. Fear not, says Raymond James analyst John Freeman: This time, it really is different, specifically for exploration and production companies, who have become much more prudent with their spending. “The stark changes in the E&P industry were on display for all to see this earnings season,” he says in a recent research note. “Instead of using the money from higher oil prices to pursue growth like in years past, E&Ps have instead enhanced returns to shareholders with dividends, buybacks and/or debt reduction.” Valuations on energy stocks still remain below their historical norms. TAX RELIEF FOR UPPER-INCOMERS? The 2017 tax reform law capped the deduction for state and local taxes (SALT) claimed on Schedule A at $10,000. Since then, some lawmakers from high-tax states, such as N.Y., N.J. and Calif., have been pushing to repeal or increase the limit. A group of N.Y. lawmakers has threatened to oppose any forthcoming tax legislation that doesn’t include a full repeal of the SALT limitation and has told President Joe Biden this. Scrapping the $10,000 limit would primarily benefit upper-incomers. The SALT cap repeal isn’t in Biden’s American Families Plan, although administration officials say it will be part of the conversation going forward. For your tax-planning purposes, expect House Democratic leaders to broker some sort of SALT-deduction compromise -- maybe temporarily lifting the cap to $15,000.
