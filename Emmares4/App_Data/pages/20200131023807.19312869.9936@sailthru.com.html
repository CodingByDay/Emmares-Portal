Politicians spend a lot of time talking about building walls, either real or legislative, to keep out unwanted people or goods. But a virus knows no walls, and the interconnectedness of our world is no more evident than when a global health emergency is declared.

That's what the World Health Organization has done as the coronavirus spreads to every corner of the planet. Airlines are eliminating flights to and from China, tourist-dependent businesses are suffering as Chinese stay home during the Lunar New Year holiday, and entire provinces are shutting down for longer periods. There's been one beneficiary, though: face-mask makers.

If you've been busy waiting in line for a mask or trying to straighten out your work-from-home situation, here are some stories you may have missed.  —Michael Tighe

CLO Loopholes

The devil is often in the details, and some buyers of corporate debt don't like what they're finding. Collateralized loan obligations are increasingly coming with loopholes meant to protect managers from the potential effects of a slowing global economy.

Moving a City

What happens to a capital city with more than 30 million people that is sinking into the sea? If you're Indonesia, you move it to the middle of the jungle on a remote island.

Over a Barrel

Sometimes a stiff drink is needed while figuring out the implications of tariffs being slapped on goods around the world. That bottle of bourbon or whiskey probably is on the list, too, but its inclusion is creating some unintended consequences for global trade — and taste buds.

Burn After Reading

To help the environment, please do not print this email. Actually, if you really want to help the planet, you should stop writing emails and start deleting them. Here's why.

On the Prowl for Prosperity

Leaders of 53 African nations are banking on a new trade deal to generate more prosperity for the continent. And frustrated truck drivers hope the African Continental Free Trade Area opens borders so they don't get stuck in traffic with lions prowling around.

The New EV Is an EF

Thailand's Energy Absolute wants to prove that water and electricity can mix. The utility is building a fleet of electric ferries to carry commuters and tourists through the heart of Bangkok. Test cruises are scheduled for this summer, and a ride will cost less than $1.

The Red-Hot Zone

Go ahead and call it a comeback. The National Football League is hot again, and this time it's for the right reasons. TV ratings are surging just the NFL starts negotiating new broadcast and streaming deals. This weekend's Super Bowl won't hurt, either.

Prospecting in the Pacific

A gold rush is shaping up on a spit of land in the southwest Pacific, and the winner could end up with a $60 billion haul. An abandoned mine in Bougainville that's also filled with copper is attracting unlikely prospectors just as the territory moves toward independence.
