Many of China’s factories have reopened after an extended shutdown because of the coronavirus. Unfortunately, there haven’t been enough workers to make it much of a comeback.

Even in more normal times, it was challenging to ramp up production after the Lunar New Year, when millions of Chinese workers go back to their hometowns for the holiday. Now, not only is fear of infection dissuading many from traveling back to their factories, those who do are being forced into quarantine when they arrive.

Photographer: Kevin Frayer/Getty Images

The implications extend well beyond China. Apple warned about its earnings this week partly because iPhone production has been slow to resume. Carmakers including General Motors and Nissan are grappling with a shortage of auto parts, while Nintendo hasn’t been able to get enough components for its Switch game console. One survey of U.S. companies around Shanghai found three-fourths didn’t have enough workers.

That’s not to downplay the human tragedy. With more than 2,000 dead and tens of thousands infected, it’s easy to understand why Beijing’s focus has been on containing the virus. That’s led to some actions, such as quarantining roughly 60 million people in Hubei province, that have helped curb infections but at a substantial toll.

But the economic impact of the outbreak is also not being ignored. A commentary published by the official Xinhua News Agency describes China as having two main task at the moment: controlling the virus and stabilizing the economy. And to that end, China’s central bank, finance ministry and other agencies have already jumped into action.

What remains unclear, however, is how effective those measures will be. As Allianz’s Mohamed A. El-Erian points out, dealing with a virus and a financial crisis are quite different. For one, central bank action is unlikely to be as potent. Goods markets, moreover, respond more slowly than financial markets. Ultimately, the economic recovery could well be harder than it first appears.

HNA’s Troubles

The troubles at HNA, the sprawling Chinese conglomerate, long pre-date the coronavirus, but it looks like it’ll be the outbreak that finally breaks the camel’s back. It was revealed this week that the province of Hainan, where the company is based, is preparing to take it over. HNA first came to prominence in 2016 when it began a global shopping spree that would make it the leading shareholder in such iconic companies as Hilton Worldwide and Deutsche Bank. But the massive amounts of debt it took-on to make those purchases would soon catchup to the company that began as a small regional airline. A fire sale ensured as HNA sought to shed assets and to once again focus on its airlines business. But then came the virus and a tremendous plunge in spending on air tickets during the Lunar New Year period, traditionally a peak season for travel. It’s hard to survive that if you’re a company already struggling to pay its creditors.

Star Power

Not every company in China is doing poorly. Take for example Foresight Fund, founded in 2018 by Chen Guangming, one of China’s most-famous investors. The company this week began taking orders for its second equity mutual fund with a target of raising 6 billion yuan. It closed its books on the first day after receiving 120 billion yuan of orders, setting a new record. It hasn’t hurt that China’s stock markets have rallied recently on expectations Beijing will introduce stimulus measures to cushion the blow of the coronavirus. In fact, 13 different fund products have had to close their subscription periods earlier than scheduled this month.

Hong Kong Shrinking

The outlook for Hong Kong is obviously challenging. Just as the protests that wracked the city for much of last year were calming, the coronavirus struck. Now, we’re starting to get a better sense of the magnitude of that challenge. Data released this week showed the city’s population shrank in the second half of 2019, the first decline since 2002. Also shrinking is the economy. Analysts are forecasting Hong Kong’s GDP will contract by 1% this year, following a 1.2% decline in 2019. Add to that the anecdotal evidence of expatriates choosing to leave the city and banks pausing hiring and the situation begins to look justly daunting.

Trade Commitments

Competition between the U.S. and China was well on display this week. House Speaker Nancy Pelosi stepped up warnings about Huawei during a visit to Brussels. Secretary of State Mike Pompeo took a veiled swipe at Chinese diplomacy when visiting South Africa. But on trade, things seemed to move in a more conciliatory direction. In Washington, President Donald Trump intervened to stop hardliners in his administration from blocking sales of General Electric-made jet engines to China. Beijing, meanwhile, began offering waivers that let American farm goods be imported without having to pay retaliatory tariffs. Chinese buyers were soon bidding for U.S. agricultural commodities. It is quite the shift from a year ago to see trade now become one of the relatively less contentious aspects of the relationship.
