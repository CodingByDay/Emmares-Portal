The economic embrace the U.S. and China have been locked in this century has been so important that it has earned a name: Chimerica. To set about tearing it apart now will necessarily involve substantial pain for all. But that doesn’t mean it won’t happen.

Decoupling is an outcome that looks unlikely on the surface, and yet as the trade war bubbles and tensions flare over everything from Hong Kong to Xinjiang to where we store our cat videos, it is an ever looming threat.

Signs of it starting to take hold are likewise easy to find. Take for example reports this week that Beijing has ordered government departments and state-affiliated agencies to remove all foreign computer equipment and software within three years. It’s possible of course that in the future this push ultimately never materializes, but that doesn’t in the present make this any less chilling of a signal.

In the U.S., Bloomberg’s Peter Waldman and Andre Tartar this week detailed a similarly disquieting trend. By looking through 26,000 security clearance decisions, they illustrated how drastically the U.S. has lost faith in Americans with links to China as conveyed through who is and isn’t allowed to work on sensitive government projects.

A rethink of how Washington and Beijing interact, and what each should expect of the other, seems inevitable. China is too big for it not to be. But whether that process ends in some iteration of the Cold War is still far from being settled.

A Deal in Hand

We've got a deal, mostly. President Donald Trump has signed off on the terms of a phase-one trade agreement between the U.S. and China, though the details have yet to be laid out in a legal text. And while the specifics were unclear as of our publication time, the pact appeared likely to include Chinese agricultural purchases, a rollback of tariffs, plus commitments by Beijing on intellectual property and its currency. It also averted higher tariffs going into effect on Dec. 15, which would have sparked further escalation. But we're not quite across the finish line yet. U.S. officials have been reaching out to congressional allies and those in the business community asking that they issue statements of support. That underlines the need to still sell this deal even if the two administrations feel they've achieved something worthwhile.

Policy Priorities

Meanwhile in Beijing, China's top economic policy makers were holed up for much of the week in meetings to decide what targets to set for GDP growth, inflation and the size of the fiscal deficit in the coming year. Though those numbers won't be publicly announced until March at annual sessions of the parliament, officials did give a read out of their discussions at the conclusion of those meetings. They pledged to avoid systemic financial risks while keeping growth within a "reasonable range," echoing sentiments expressed by an earlier meeting of the Politburo, chaired by President Xi Jinping. It would seem that Beijing plans to continue with its so-call de-risking campaign, as long as growth doesn't crater. A survey of economists showed a general consensus that the world's second-largest economy will be slower in 2020.

Debt Problems

There were also plenty of reminders this week of why Beijing's de-risking campaign is such a priority. First was the value of onshore bond defaults exceeding $17 billion in 2019, setting a new record. A few days later, China suffered its biggest dollar bond default by a state-owned company in two decades. And even when a government investment vehicle was able to avoid default with a late payment, it highlighted concerns about wobbly Chinese borrowers. But these incidents aren't necessarily bad for China. By letting weak companies go under, policy makers are hoping to not only remove risk but to encourage better pricing of that risk, thereby making financial markets more efficient. What authorities do have to avoid, however, is the potential threat of a few defaults sparking some broader unraveling that precipitates a sharp economic slowdown.

Reforming Big Oil

China's massive state-owned oil and gas sector is often referred to by locals as "three barrels of oil." Sometimes used affectionately and sometimes not, this nickname for the trio of giant corporations that control this industry does look increasingly like it may have a limited shelf life. Beijing this week announced the official creation of a new pipeline company that was built by taking away pipelines owned by China National Petroleum, Sinopec and China National Offshore Oil and injecting them into this new entity. Almost all developed markets have separated oil and gas production from transport in order to promote a level playing field and to encourage new entrants into the market. One reason Beijing is keen to spur private investment in domestic exploration and production is that it could greatly enhance national energy security.
