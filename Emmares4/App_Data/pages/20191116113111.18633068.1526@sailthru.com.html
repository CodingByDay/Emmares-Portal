Deal of the week

Convoy gets the deal of the week status, and not just because the round was $400 million. The round also speaks to another trend in mobility.

First, the nuts and bolts. Convoy has created digital freight network that connects truckers with shippers. Convoy’s Series D round of $400 million brings its post-money valuation to $2.75 billion. More importantly, the round closed amid an increasingly competitive market.

Modernizing freight is the new new thing it seems. Uber Freight and Convoy are perhaps the best known. But there seems to be a new one popping up each week. Many are focused on the connecting truckers with shippers angle. Others are aiming for other slices. Why? Trucking is the cornerstone to the U.S. economy as well as many other regions globally. And yet, it has real challenges that promise to be a boon to whomever solves them.

Two newer companies that have popped up on my radar are Comvoy and TruKKer. TruKKer is a digital freight platform that was founded in the UAE in 2016. The startup just raised $23 million in a Series A funding round.

Then there’s Comvoy (not to be mistaken by Convoy) is a new searchable marketplace for work-ready trucks that launched this past week. (Comvoy is under parent company Work Truck Solutions.) The startup pulls from an extensive aggregation of commercial vehicle details and inventory to match customers with the right work truck for the right job.

Micromobbin'

Since Lyft rolled out its new scooters built for the shared economy, the company says its operating costs have decreased and ridership has increased about 20% in markets like Denver and Miami. These Segway-built scooters make up more than 65% of Lyft’s scooter fleet, and the company plans to upgrade the entire fleet by the end of January.

But ridership hasn’t been so great in a handful of markets. This week, Lyft laid off about 20 employees as part of ceasing scooter operations in Nashville, San Antonio, Atlanta, the Phoenix area, Dallas and Columbus.

Meanwhile, we dove into some of the remaining opportunities in the micromobility space. One of those opportunities pertains to the automatic repositioning of bikes and scooters — a task that can save operators money while increasing vehicle availability.

One other note (Kirsten jumping in here). Wheels, the startup founded by Wag founders Jonathan and Joshua Viner, has expanded into the Texas market. The company announced in October that it raised $50 million in a round led by DBL Partners. It seems the company is already putting that capital to use.

Wheels began operating in the Dallas Fort-Worth area in September. It has just received a permit to operate in Austin, where it will be launching operations in the coming weeks.

Zero vs LiveWire

Two very different American companies are squaring off in the race to take electric motorcycles into the mainstream. In one corner is California startup Zero Motorcycles; and in the other is Harley Davidson.

Zero Motorcycles released its all new SR/F this spring, while Harley Davidson began selling its first electric motorcycle the LiveWire in September.

TechCrunch has tested both.

While the companies are different, the bikes have comparable specs. Each one delivers serious power, reasonable range of around 100 miles and charge-times of just over an hour on fast-charging networks. The Harley-Davidson LiveWire has 105 horsepower and the Zero moto has 110 hp.

The big differentiator is price. At $29,799, Harley-Davidson LiveWire is 34% pricier than the SR/F, which costs $19,495.

Neither Zero or HD have provided sales data yet. As a private company, Zero might not release its sale data. But Harley Davidson should start reporting LiveWire sales data in its 2020 financial reporting.

As with many vehicle matchups, the determining factor of which company outsells the other could come down to basic economics.

But both face the same challenge: to close gap with gas bikes on key purchasing factors: such as performance, range, price, and charge times.  SR/F and LiveWire have come closest in all those, particularly performance and range.

The rise of voice

Capgemini, a French multinational consultant and management group, has an interesting report about that shows just how big voice assistants in vehicles will be. It’s been a trend I’ve been following for the past several years, in part because of the lousy experience of voice in cars. But also because I noticed an uptick in voice-related announcements, namely Amazon and Google, at CES and other auto shows the past few years

The report surveyed 7,000 consumers and found that 95% said they will be using in-car voice assistants in the next three years.


A few other key findings:

  • 49% of consumers already use voice assistants in their vehicles
  • 77% use voice to play music and check directions
  • 46% book appointments for their vehicle to be serviced.
  • 45% order specific services such as food

All of these stats are expected to grow, by a third or more in the next few years. That’s important because, there’s money to be made.

Some 37% of people surveyed said they would be willing to pay a premium or monthly subscription price for a voice subscription installed in their car. Younger drivers were most likely to be willing to pay, including 47% of 22 to 31 year-olds compared to 29% of 45 to 59 year-olds and 18% of people over the age of 60.
