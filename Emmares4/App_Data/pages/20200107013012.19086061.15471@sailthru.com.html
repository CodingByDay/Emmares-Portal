Welcome to the Brussels Edition, Bloomberg’s daily briefing on what matters most in the heart of the European Union.

After more than a month of strikes and havoc, French Prime Minister Edouard Philippe starts another round of talks with unions today, which could bring concessions from all sides over the controversial pension reform sought by President Emmanuel Macron. Following unpopular changes to taxes, labor laws and the welfare system, this latest attempt to modernize the economy is proving the most challenging. Despite the risk to growth from prolonged walkouts, Macron has yet to back down. His reformist credentials are on the line. 

—Geraldine Amiel and Nikos Chrysoloras
 

What’s Happening


French Tariffs | A visit to Paris today by European Trade Commissioner Phil Hogan underlines how seriously the EU is taking the U.S.’s threat to hit French goods with tariffs in retaliation for a digital-services tax in France. Hogan, who has vowed a united EU response to any such duties, will hold talks with French officials including Philippe and Finance Minister Bruno Le Maire a week before his first trip to Washington as European trade chief.

Mediterranean Tension | Greek premier Kyriakos Mitsotakis will use a meeting with Donald Trump today to seek U.S. backing against Turkey’s increasingly assertive claims over Mediterranean waters and potential gas reserves. But while a planned pipeline from Israel to Cyprus, Greece and Italy — opposed by Turkey -- enjoys formal U.S. support, Trump has repeatedly let Recep Tayyip Erdogan off the hook, so Athenian expectations better be low. 

Tight Vote | Spain’s Pedro Sanchez looks set to secure a narrow victory in parliament today to form a government and end eight months of political gridlock. If he prevails, Spain will have its first coalition government since its return to democracy more than four decades ago - though the prospect of power-sharing with the far-left populist party Podemos has concerned some investors. 

Czech Conflict | The Czech Republic’s billionaire prime minister is coming under increasing pressure over allegations of conflict of interests with regard to EU funds, which last year triggered the country’s biggest anti-government protests in three decades. The organizers of the rallies are due to announce further protest actions today.

Price Pressures | Euro-area inflation data due today will probably show that consumer prices accelerated 1.3% in December as a result of energy base effects. While that would be the strongest reading since June, it’s still well below the target of the European Central Bank, whose monetary stimulus measures has attracted increased scrutiny for their potentially detrimental side effects.

In Case You Missed It

Europe on Iran | Trump's often-scorned European allies quickly fell in line with U.S rhetoric on Iran, squarely putting the blame on Tehran for the recent escalation, even though they were stunned by Qassem Soleimani’s assassination and are desperate to preserve a landmark nuclear accord. Expect a more concrete position when EU foreign ministers meet in Brussels on Friday.

Pick Up | The euro-zone economy edged away from stagnation at the end of 2019 as services picked up to counter moribund manufacturing. The bloc has increasingly relied on consumer spending to support its expansion, offsetting the slump in manufacturing that’s partly due to the U.S.-China trade war and Brexit. The risk is that factory job losses undermine the economy as a whole.

Turkish Route | Turkey could be courting another controversy with one of the most ambitious projects of Erdogan’s almost two decades in power. The Turkish president said warships will be able to use a planned multi-billion-dollar canal bisecting Istanbul, possibly undercutting a 20th-century agreement meant to ensure stability and security in the Black Sea region.

Milkman Returns | The milk float, a home-delivery service that evolved from horse-drawn carriages to early electric vehicles, belongs firmly in the past. Or does it? Here’s how a little known Amsterdam-based online grocery company revived the concept, but with a modern — and sustainable — flourish.

 

Chart of the Day

The U.S. Chamber of Commerce is warning that American businesses and consumers are bearing the brunt of the trade war and is calling on the administration to change course. Crunching Commerce Department data, it concludes that more than half of U.S. states are facing retaliatory tariffs on at least 25% of their exports to the EU and China.
