Two men who dug up dirt on Biden arrested, Apple's relationship with China, your weekend longreads

THE BIG STORY

Two men who worked with Rudy Giuliani to dig up dirt on Joe Biden have been arrested

 

In his first rally of the impeachment era, President Donald Trump ramped up the personal attacks, calling Joe Biden a “loser,” Rep. Ilhan Omer a “disgrace,” and whipping up a crowd of thousands.


As it so happens, on the same day as Trump’s rally, Lev Parnas and Igor Fruman, two key subjects in House Democrats' impeachment inquiry who worked with Rudy Giuliani to gather damaging information about Biden, were arrested at Dulles airport near Washington, DC. The pair had one-way tickets to fly out of the US.


According to the indictment, the two men were charged with conspiring to skirt federal laws that prohibit foreign nationals from contributing to US campaigns.


Their arrest comes as news arrives that Marie Yovanovitch, Trump’s former ambassador to Ukraine who was recalled from her post early, will testify to Congress. 


A lawyer for Trump says Yovanoitch badmouthed him behind his back, and obstructed his efforts to dig up dirt on Biden — you can imagine Congress is interested in hearing from her.


To make a straight line between these stories, I texted our political reporter Paul McLeod so he can give us a handle on this.

STAYING ON TOP OF THIS

 

Californians are outraged after millions had their power shut off

This week, California's largest utility turned off the lights for more than 2 million people in an attempt to prevent its power lines and equipment from sparking wildfires.


Pacific Gas & Electric, whose equipment has been blamed for starting some of the most destructive and deadly fires in the state's history, preemptively made the unprecedented decision, and Californians are outraged.


Is this about climate change and wildfires? Kind of. Many are wondering why the company didn’t deal with the equipment a long time ago so it wouldn’t come to this. Gov. Gavin Newsom noted that, “This is not from my perspective a climate change story as much as a story about greed and mismanagement.”

SNAPSHOTS

 

The 2019 Nobel Peace Prize was just awarded to Abiy Ahmed, the Prime Minister of Ethiopia. Ahmed was given the honor for his work towards ending a 20-year conflict with neighbor Eritrea.

 

Asylum seekers protesting squalid conditions shut down a border crossing for 15 hours. A group of up to 300 immigrants marched to the middle of the Gateway International Bridge, which connects Brownsville, Texas, and Matamoros, Mexico, and sat down in the roadway, blocking traffic in both directions for nearly 15 hours.


A new ICE training facility is being modeled after American cities, and members of Congress who represent those areas are livid. The government will pay a manufacturer of tactical products to duplicate neighborhoods of Chicago and cities in Arizona for Immigration and Customs Enforcement tactical training. Some Congress reps say this is offensive to the people they represent.


A memory card with videos of a brutal murder was found on the street, and police have arrested a suspect. The SD card found on the street in Anchorage, Alaska contained videos and photos of a man viciously attacking a woman. One man was charged as a result. According to a charging document, the memory card contained 39 images and 12 videos. It was titled “homicide at midtown Marriott.”


Iranian women were allowed to attend a soccer match for the first time in nearly 40 years. Iran’s conservative regime only allowed 4,000 tickets for women in a stadium that seats about 80,000. Rights groups blasted it as “a cynical publicity stunt.”

APPLE'S RELATIONSHIP TO CHINA

 

Apple has removed a mapping app that let protesters in Hong Kong track police

The crowdsourced mapping app, called HKmap.live, let people in Hong Kong track police during the country’s ongoing pro-democracy protests. 


A few days after Apple was criticized in Chinese state media for aiding “rioters” in Hong Kong by keeping the app in its App Store, HKmap.live was removed. Apple issued a statement saying it verified with Hong Kong authorities that “the app has been used to target and ambush police.”


The app’s removal sparked outrage from critics who have accused the company of kowtowing to the Chinese government. There is wider context here: China is Apple’s second-most important market after the US, and for years, Apple has responded to Chinese pressure.


Just this week, the company removed the app of news outlet Quartz from the Chinese App Store over Quartz’s coverage of the Hong Kong protests.

GET COZY

 

Enjoy some tall cups of tea and long sits and longreads

Ellen DeGeneres, Chris Evans, And The Celebrity Fetish For Kindness. The brouhaha over Ellen DeGeneres’ friendship with George W. Bush has pushed a lot of people to reconsider the relationship between positivity and justice, and choosing one over the other. Shannon Keating looked closely at this: “There’s nothing inherently wrong with forgiveness — on an interpersonal level, it can be incredibly powerful and cathartic — but...the problem arrives when acts of forgiveness, kindness, or other more banal niceties are afforded more attention than appeals for due process.”


I Thought Wilderness Was A Place To Find Myself Until I Spent Four Months Living In It. Pack up your bags and head west. At least, so goes the dream and promise of the “wilderness.” For a variety of reasons, Leo Schwartz found himself doing just that, getting a job at the Deschutes National Forest — and he learned some lessons the hard way: “The people of the Forest Service do this work not just because of a spiritual connection with nature, but because our world is burning.”


The Politician Is Here, It’s Queer — But Now We’re Totally Used To It. Are you watching Ryan Murphy’s new Netflix series? I am, and boy is it exactly as dramatic as you think it would be. Michael Blackmon writes about the show’s treatment of queerness: “What’s surprising and welcome in Ryan Murphy’s latest series is that most characters are queer without it being a focal point — and without it being a trait that defines their whole character.”
