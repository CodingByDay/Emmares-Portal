Back in 2005 when I was a reporter in China, I spent several months shuttling between a hospital in Beijing and a remote village to try to understand what happens when “big sickness” strikes a poor Chinese family.

A seven-year-old with leukemia was undergoing treatment at the Beijing Children’s Hospital. His mother was camped out in a crowded waiting room along with scores of other parents, all of them manically preoccupied with settling their bills. These were meticulously itemized—drugs, x-rays, plasma, needles, gauze, tape, tubes—and payable each day.

Back home in Inner Mongolia, the child’s father was desperately selling off the family’s meager possessions to cover the charges until, at last, there was nothing left to sell. Then he rode a bus to Beijing, met up with his wife at the hospital, and the two of them walked into the intensive care ward to scoop up their son—his immune system shattered—and bring him back to an empty house to die.

China’s medical system has made great strides since then. Almost 95% of the population has some form of health coverage, compared with one in five at the turn of the century. Beijing’s health services are now as advanced as those in California.

But in certain respects, little has changed. China still suffers from an acute shortage of general practitioners, the doctors at the front lines of preventative care. Thus, “small sicknesses” grow into “big sicknesses,” and the afflicted run to for-profit urban hospitals that drain their savings, often by massively overprescribing drugs and procedures. 

This system exacerbates income inequality and the rural-urban divide. Public anger can even boil over into physical attacks on doctors, an occurrence so common it has a name: “Yi nao” or “medical disturbance.”

Now comes the coronavirus, which has stretched an already overwhelmed and chaotic hospital system beyond the breaking point. This is why the epidemic isn’t just a medical emergency, but a political one.

Ever since the establishment of the People’s Republic in 1949, health has played an outsized role in its politics, writes the historian Xun Zhou. The Communist Party believed that its prestige at home and abroad would derive in large part from the success of its effort to nurse back to health a nation once derided as the “Sick Man of Asia.” It largely accomplished that goal—perhaps the Party’s proudest boast. Chinese doctors scored impressive victories against infectious diseases. Life expectancy jumped; infant mortality plunged. 

For years, China’s health system was a model for the developing world. Until the 1980s, that is, when China began to dismantle collective farms in the countryside and state factories in the cities—the primary vehicles for healthcare delivery—under Deng Xiaoping’s market reforms. Large parts of China’s medical network collapsed.

Today’s remedial measures are nowhere near enough to meet the health challenges of a modern, rapidly aging population suffering from soaring rates of diabetes, cancer and heart disease. The coronavirus magnifies these systemic failings. The Party is clearly fearful, and the rest of the world should be too. An insecure China could lash out in unpredictable ways.

Knee-jerk expulsions

The story of the boy with leukemia does have a happy ending. A few hours after I accompanied the tearful family down the hospital steps, my story appeared on the front page of The Wall Street Journal. An outpouring of donations from all over the world enabled the boy, Cui Dejie, to continue his treatment, with enough money left over to help dozens of other kids with the often fatal disease. Dejie is now in college, and doing well.

Memories of those days came flooding back this week when I read about the expulsion of three Wall Street Journal reporters, one of whom—Chao Deng—is risking her life to report on the epidemic from Wuhan. The knee-jerk expulsions, over an ill-conceived headline describing China as the “sick man of Asia,” an opinion piece they had nothing to do with, were a terrible mistake. The move highlights the tragedy that’s befallen the U.S.-China relationship.

The battle against a raging epidemic, led by brave Chinese doctors and nurses, should be strengthening bonds of human friendship across the Pacific. Instead, it’s driving these two countries further apart.
