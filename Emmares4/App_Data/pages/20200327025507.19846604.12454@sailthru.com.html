Behind almost every wave of coronavirus infections is a government order for people to go into quarantine. But while Covid-19 moves with an almost predictable uniformity, those governments do not.

China went into quarantine months ago and gradually is coming out. The U.S. just went into one and can't agree on when to come out. And the U.K. stayed out until it realized it had to get in. Now some of the most isolated parts of the world are starting to take action.

It's all making the world's two biggest economies sick, which may spark a different kind of global pandemic. If you've been spending your time wondering when you'll be able to get out of self-isolation, here are some stories you may have missed.

Say "Aaah"

The epicenter of swab manufacturing in the U.S. is a factory in rural Maine, which is gearing up to make a million per week to meet demand for Covid-19 tests. We take you inside the Puritan Medical Products plant.

Food Fights

Relax. The world has enough food, despite images of panicked shoppers emptying shelves. The real issue is how to move all that food to the places where it's needed most. Complicating matters is the fact that some nations are starting to hoard their supplies.

The Virus Hunter

A one-time family doctor from a small town in South Korea has become something of a national hero for her management of the coronavirus epidemic. Jung Eun-kyeong's playbook also makes her a potential role model for virus-fighters elsewhere.

Lonely Planet

What does the global lockdown look like from space? Check out the before-and-after satellite photos of locations typically brimming with people that now appear almost empty.

Turbulent Billionaire

You might think the world's first drone-made billionaire would be flying high, but that's not the case with DJI's Frank Wang. His Shenzhen company dominates global sales, but the reclusive founder is grappling with a trade war, accounting fraud and his own success.

Bust Before Boom?

This is supposed to be the time when oil-rich Guyana lives up to its IMF billing as the world's fastest-growing economy. But a confluence of crises in the South American country threatens to trigger a bust before the boom even gets there.

A Fish Tale

There may not be plenty of fish in the sea. The warmest ocean temperatures ever are reducing oxygen levels and affecting migration patterns, threatening the $42 billion tuna industry. So Chicken of the Sea's owner is taking steps to protect the species.

Gaming Glitch

There's plenty of time for gaming now, but next year there might not be enough games to fill your days. The coronavirus pandemic is canceling conferences and keeping developers at home, squeezing the product pipeline just as new consoles are ready for release.
