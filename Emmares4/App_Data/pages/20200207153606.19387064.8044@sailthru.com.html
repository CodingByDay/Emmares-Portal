Manufacturers have been been plagued by a number of pests this earnings season, from a lingering slowdown and economic uncertainty to Boeing Co.’s 737 Max troubles and the burgeoning coronavirus crisis. So it’s oddly fitting that the best-performing  stock on the S&P 500’s Industrial Index through this reporting season was a pest-control company.

Shares of Rollins Inc., a $13 billion company that makes its money zapping termites, roaches and bed bugs under the Orkin brand among others, were up more than 16% so far this year as of 3 p.m. Friday. That’s just above General Electric Co.’s gain on the back of improving free cash flow and a lack of any new incremental bad news. Even Copart Inc., the auctioneer of junked cars that has been growing like a tech company and was the hottest industrial stock of 2019, couldn’t compete with Rollins’s performance. To a degree, Rollins’s gains are a recovery from an 8.1% slide in 2019 amid unusual margin and earnings growth weakness. But if you’re looking for companies that may tangentially benefit from the quickly spreading coronavirus, Rollins offers an interesting case.

While pest-control companies aren’t directly involved with combating the coronavirus, public-health crises like that tend to boost general hygiene awareness and related spending, says Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Eshan Toorabally, who follows Rollins rivals ServiceMaster Global Holdings Inc. and Rentokil Initial Plc. Rollins’s mosquito services, for example, have been growing aggressively over the past three years in part because of growing concerns about the Zika, West Nile and Eastern Equine Encephalitis viruses . Mosquito-related sales were up more than 30% last year. That contributed to a robust 5.7% increase in organic revenue for the last three months of the year, compared with a virtually flat to modestly down performance on average from the biggest multi-industrial companies.

That kind of growth isn’t unusual for Rollins. Including the impact of a steady stream of acquisitions of smaller pest-control companies, sales haven’t declined once on an annual basis in the last 20 years, according to data compiled by Bloomberg. For one thing, bugs don’t really care whether the economy is moving up or down. And if you have a bug problem, it can’t wait and it can’t be handled in a slipshod way. That’s especially true for businesses given the pervasiveness of cell phones and social media. God forbid a customer posts a video of a rat in a McDonald’s or Starbucks store.

There’s also a natural boost in demand as the global middle class grows and starts to expect more professional services and stricter health regulations. While much has been written about the potential catastrophic declines in insect populations due to climate change, a shift toward warmer and wetter weather in recent years has generally been favorable for certain bugs. The past few years have brought resurgences in bed bugs, Formosan termites and ticks, James Clement of Buckingham Research Group said by phone. “Having mother nature as your marketing partner is a good thing,” he said. “You get free advertising from all the bugs crawling around and it’s not a capital-intensive business.”

That’s why Rollins’s relatively mild stumbles caught investors so off guard in 2019. Net income was weighed down by a variety of charges and expenses. Rollins increased staffing for a spring and summer season that didn’t play out as normal because of unusual weather patterns. The company expanded its 401(k) and stock-grant programs to improve employee retention. Rollins also transitioned its pension plan to an insurance provider, resulting in a $50 million one-time charge. And the company’s aggressive pace of M&A came back to bite it a bit through increased salary and advertising expenses, as well as rising insurance costs due to the incorporation of additional vehicles into the fleet of bug-man cars and industry-wide higher premiums. In its largest acquisition yet, Rollins spent $400 million last year to acquire Clark Pest Control of Stockton.

“This was the most disappointing year we've had for 22 years,” CEO Gary Rollins said on a January earnings call. “So we think that it was an anomaly.” That’s likely true, but there was also a lull in big margin improvement programs last year. The next big boost will come from investments in new technology meant to help drivers get to their bug-killing destinations more efficiently and reliably – similar to some of the programs pushed by the likes of United Parcel Service Inc., where Rollins CFO Eddie Northen used to work. Rollins expects a 150 to 250 basis-point margin increase from the corresponding reduction in mileage over the next two to three years. Until that kicks in, the margin story is really more about pinching “pennies and nickels and dimes,” said Clement, who like all but one analyst covering the stock recommends holding or selling the shares. “It’s expensive. Whether it’s worth it or not, that’s for individual investors to decide.”

YOUR WEEKLY BOEING UPDATE
Federal Aviation Administration chief Steve Dickson let drop this week that there’s a new software problem on Boeing’s 737 Max. This involves a light that indicates whether the so-called stabilizer trim system that pushes the plane’s nose up and down is working properly. Boeing said it disclosed the issue to the FAA the week of Jan. 20 and doesn’t expect it to affect the current best estimate of a mid-2020 return for the plane, which has been grounded since last March in the wake of two fatal crashes. Either way, it’s not great that Boeing is still finding new problems this late in the process. Dickson said the certification flight for the Max – a key step toward getting it flying again – could take place in a few weeks, but appeared to indicate this warning-light issue needs to be resolved first. Given the prolonged grounding, major Max supplier Spirit AeroSystems Holdings Inc. this week cut its quarterly dividend to just a nominal penny a share to try to save cash, with Moody’s Investors Service having already cut its credit rating to junk. And in case you weren’t tired of hearing of Boeing software problems, NASA had some choice words for the company over the aborted test flight of its Starliner spaceship in December. Boeing failed to identify two “critical software defects” beforehand because “breakdowns” in the design phase led to the company missing problems “despite their detectability,” NASA said. Sound familiar?

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Textron Inc. is reportedly in talks to acquire Bombardier Inc.’s business-jet division as the struggling Canadian manufacturer grapples with a deepening cash burn and a hefty debt load. Bombardier had been in talks with Alstom SA about combining their rail businesses, and the business-jet operations were meant to be the foundation of the company’s future. But talks with Alstom have stalled over questions about the valuation of a business that’s struggled to deliver the trains its customers have ordered, according to the Wall Street Journal. It’s unclear what the structure or terms would be, but a deal would help consolidate a crowded private-jet industry and the resulting price stabilization could prove a boon for all manufacturers, including General Dynamics Corp.’s Gulfstream. Jefferies analyst Sheila Kahyaoglu estimates that a $6.7 billion deal for the Bombardier division would boost Textron’s 2021 earnings per share by 21% and give it access to larger, longer-range models. But she also notes that some investors aren’t too keen to see Textron double down on a market where growth has been subdued in recent years. Regulators may be a hurdle, with the combined company accounting for nearly half of global business-jet sales by volume, says Bloomberg Intelligence’s Douglas Rothacker.   

Stericycle Inc. agreed to sell a hazardous-waste management business to Harsco Corp. for $462.5 million. Stericycle will continue to offer waste services to health-care customers, including unused consumer pharmaceutical take-backs. Harsco has been aggressively buying and divesting businesses (including an air-cooled heat-exchanger unit and a commercial-boiler and water-heater operation) over the past year in an effort to recast itself as a higher-growth, less cyclical company focused on environmental services. Harsco investors weren’t impressed with this latest deal. That could have to do with the fact that the price implies a lofty valuation of 13.2 times the Stericycle business’s expected Ebitda in 2020. That profit works out to a measly 6% of projected revenue this year. A target of $15 million in cost savings by the third year of ownership does little to offset the expensiveness and the deal will bloat Harsco’s net leverage ratio to 3.5. But it’s a positive step for Stericyle, which has been struggling after its own M&A activities pushed its debt so high that S&P cut its rating to junk last year.    
 

Science Applications International Corp. agreed to buy Unisys Corp.’s unit that provides IT services to the federal government for $1.2 billion. It’s the latest in a string of deals for government technology contractor SAIC, including the $2.5 billion purchase of rival Engility Holdings Inc. The company is paying about 10.5 times the Unisys’s business’s expected Ebitda in calendar year 2020, which seems reasonable. SAIC said the purchase will enhance its capabilities in government priority areas including modernization and cloud migration. But it will be a game changer for Unisys, which intends to use the proceeds to fund its pension commitments and pay down debt. Clearly those obligations had been an overhang on the stock because shares of Unisys shot up 46% on the day of the deal’s announcement.
