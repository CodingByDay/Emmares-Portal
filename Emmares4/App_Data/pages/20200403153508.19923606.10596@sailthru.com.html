With about 10 million Americans filing for unemployment benefits in the past two weeks, restaurants and gyms now shuttered and more than half of the world’s aircraft fleet parked, it feels like the economy as we know it has come to a complete stop. But the underlying plumbing is largely still operational. While delivery drivers and grocery-store clerks have been appropriately lionized, much of the country’s factory workforce is also still reporting for duty. In the age of the coronavirus, wide swathes of the U.S. manufacturing sector fall under the umbrella of “critical infrastructure,” meaning facilities can stay open even in the face of restrictive shelter-in-place orders.

I’ve talked a lot recently about the ways in which manufacturers are stepping up to make products outside their normal purview to help the nation combat the coronavirus, whether that’s LVMH’s hand-sanitizer production, clothing companies’ pivot to face masks or General Motors Co.’s foray into ventilators. The White House continues to needlessly point fingers at corporations that are trying to help, with 3M Co. becoming the latest to earn President Donald Trump’s Twitter ire despite having aggressively stepped up face-mask production. The administration finally took on some responsibility for orchestrating the production push for key medical gear: it invoked the Defense Production Act this week to help ventilator manufacturers procure needed supplies and to prioritize the U.S. for shipments of 3M’s N95 respirators. This remains a slipshod effort and there are risks of more protectionist attitudes backfiring. But those ventilator supplies have to come from somewhere, as do the raw materials for the respirators. Another company has to make sure those products get from point A to point B.

“When you start thinking through all of this, it actually becomes a pretty big chain that’s needed to make sure everyone has what they need,” Rachel Jones, vice president of energy and resources policy at the National Association of Manufacturers, said in a phone interview. The nature of some manufacturing work means employees will need to be in relatively close contact with each other and the onus is on the companies to make sure they're taking adequate precautions.

Guidance from the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, a division within the Department of Homeland Security, identifies essential workers as those who ensure “continued critical infrastructure viability” through repair and maintenance work, call-center support, supply chains, construction and other operational functions. The industries that fall into this group range from agriculture and transportation to financial services, chemicals and defense. That obviously isn’t everything, but it’s still an incredibly broad definition. The CISA guidance is meant to be advisory; each state can still set its own specific rules and even companies deemed essential can decide to shutter their factories out of concern for the health of their workers. Boeing Co., for example, shut down on-site work in the Puget Sound area for two weeks for both its commercial jets and the KC-46 military tanker and P-8 maritime patrol aircraft after a worker died of coronavirus complications. But there is “a surprisingly limited degree of business disruptions across the multi-industry universe, with plant shutdowns generally isolated and sporadic,” Wolfe Research analyst Nigel Coe wrote in a report this week.

For every high profile closure such as Ford Motor Co.’s decision this week to keep its North American factories on an indefinite lockdown, there are a handful of counter-examples. Office buildings may not be too worried about heating and ventilation right now, but hospitals, supermarkets and laboratories are. Trane Technologies Plc, the former climate business of Ingersoll-Rand Plc, continues to make and service products for those purposes. The refrigerated transportation business of Carrier Global Corp. has been deemed essential because that’s how food and medicine are ferried across the nation. The pharmaceutical and biotechnology industries, among others, use equipment from Rockwell Automation Inc. to keep their production lines moving smoothly. Caterpillar Inc.’s machines are used to mine essential commodities and its engines help keep the power on in data centers.

It’s a bit more of a stretch to understand why the nation is so desperately in need of F-35 fighter jets or nuclear submarines at the current moment. But “modern warfare can be a great deal more lethal than coronavirus will ever be,” Loren Thompson, chief operating officer for the Lexington Institute, a think-tank that receives contributions from the aerospace and defense industry, said in an interview. “The military exists to deter war. The view from Washington is if you can’t do that, then all the other infrastructure is at risk.” The defense industry also offers a fairly direct means of providing stimulus to the U.S. economy. Contract awards have continued to flow and the Department of Defense has accelerated progress payments, with the apparent aim of flushing cash flow into the hands of suppliers, many of which also serve the beleaguered commercial industry. To that end, the U.S. Air Force said this week it would release $882 million withheld from Boeing Co. over deficiencies in its military tanker program.

One takeaway from this is that with factories still largely open and deemed essential, companies may be more inclined to reduce discretionary spending and trim salaries (including for the CEO), rather than attacking fixed costs by outright firing employees. It’s notable that most of the public layoff announcements so far have come from the beaten-up commercial aerospace industry, where the post-coronavirus recovery is likely to take the longest given the fear factor associated with flying. Boeing supplier TransDigm Group Inc. announced this week it would reduce its workforce by up to 15% and General Electric Co. will furlough 50% of its U.S engine manufacturing workforce for a month, adding to cuts announced the week prior. In contrast, truck-engine maker Cummins Inc. announced temporary across-the-board salary reductions for its U.S workforce but didn’t specify any fresh layoffs.  Others are even hiring: Lockheed Martin Corp. said Friday it had hired 1,000 employees over the past two weeks and is advertising for 5,000 open positions. It’s also extending awards of up to $500 to employees in appreciation of how none of the “critical infrastructure” we’ve talked about stays viable without these workers being willing to take the risk of showing up.

ASK NOT FOR WHOM THE BELL TOLLS
Factories may generally still be open, but orders declined at the fastest pace since 2009 in March, according to data from the Institute for Supply Management released this week. A gauge of employment also dropped to recession-era levels, and that likely doesn’t reflect job cuts and furloughs announced in the past week. As I said, in the industrial world, most of these have been linked to commercial aerospace. Travel-industry data analytics firm Cirium estimates more than half of the world’s active airplane fleet is now grounded, eliminating the need for most maintenance work. Carriers such as American Airlines Group Inc. have moved to speed up retirement of older jets, while Boeing and Airbus SE are reportedly bracing for production cuts amid an expected drop in demand for new planes. Boeing will offer voluntary buyouts to its 161,000 employees, with the expectation that several thousand will take that option or look to retire, according to Bloomberg News. The decision to hold off on forced layoffs leaves the door open for Boeing to receive some form of government bailout money, despite CEO Dave Calhoun’s pushback on the idea of a government equity stake. The company’s continuing 737 Max woes aren't making things any easier on the company: Jet lessor Avolon Holdings Ltd. scrapped an $8 billion deal for 75 of the jets, and a targeted mid-2020 return to service is at risk because the pandemic is keeping regulators from being in the same room. 

Meanwhile, both American Airlines and Southwest Airlines Co. have now said they will apply for an industry-specific relief package included in the $2 trillion stimulus bill. The Treasury Department has urged airlines to submit their requests for a $25 billion pot of grant money to cover payroll expenses by Friday to avoid processing delays. Interestingly, congressional Democrats are criticizing the Treasury’s efforts to secure equity stakes in exchange for grant money, calling the conditions too onerous. That is sort of the point, though, when you are dealing with a lender of last resort, and the strings attached to this money don’t appear to have deterred American and Southwest. Not one to be left out, Ford is calling for a revival of the government's “Cash for Clunkers” program to provide stimulus to the auto industry. 

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
United Technologies Corp. closed on its merger with defense contractor Raytheon Co. on Friday and completed the spinoffs of its Carrier Global Corp. climate business and Otis Worldwide Corp. elevator division the same day. This is hardly the environment United Technologies had in mind when it announced the breakup plan in November 2018 and the Raytheon merger in June of last year, but here we are. The coronavirus outbreak has rendered the 2020 guidance provided by Carrier and Otis in February essentially useless, but it’s safe to say demand for new elevators and HVAC systems isn’t going to be great this year. Rival elevator company Schindler warned last week that organic sales may decline as much as 10% in 2020, signaling that not even the typically more stable maintenance side of the business will offer much cover with so much of the economy on lockdown. Meanwhile, the commercial aerospace businesses at the remaining parent company — newly named Raytheon Technologies Corp. — are facing a “two-year tough spell,” CEO Greg Hayes said in an interview with Bloomberg Television. With that in mind, Bernstein analyst Douglas Harned this week cut his forecast for the combined company’s 2022 cash flow to $6.1 billion from $8.6 billion. He’s concerned about the international bent of the newly acquired defense business because of the plunge in crude prices and the pinch to countries’ budgets as they combat the coronavirus, but that’s certainly one of the more stable industries right now. 

There’s more: Elsewhere in closed deals, General Electric Co. completed the sale of its biopharmaceutical business to Danaher Corp. for $21.4 billion and SPX Flow Inc. completed the sale of its power and energy segment to affiliates of Apollo Global Management for $400 million in net proceeds, giving both companies a much-needed cash injection to help pay down debt. Arconic Inc. followed through with the spinoff of its aluminum sheet business, leaving the Howmet Aerospace Inc. plane-parts company to brave the markets alone. The metals operations — now dubbed Arconic Corp. — were unlikely to be a hot investment in the best of times. The aerospace side looks likes a risky proposition as well in the current market. 

HD Supply Holdings Inc., on the other hand, was less sanguine about market conditions and decided to delay the separation of its facilities maintenance business from its construction and industrial product distribution operations. This is not a huge surprise, and the split will probably still happen eventually. Elsewhere in canceled breakups, earlier this month, XPO Logistics Inc. yanked its strategic review of possible spinoffs and asset sales. I had missed the news amid the deluge of coronavirus updates, but the plan had struck me as extreme financial engineering and suffice it to say, that kind of thing is tricky even in the best of times, let alone when credit and equity markets are rocky. 

BorgWarner Inc., meanwhile, warned that Delphi Technologies Plc’s decision to tap out its credit line put a planned $1.5 billion takeover of the auto-parts supplier at risk. Delphi didn’t have BorgWarner’s permission for the move, which the latter company says constitutes a breach of their merger agreement. Delphi’s credit-line drawdown is “prudent, but underscores the operating challenges it faces and potential covenant issues that might arise without the merger,” Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Joel Levington wrote in a note. Analysts expect the deal to be negotiated on more favorable terms for BorgWarner, rather than completely scrapped.
