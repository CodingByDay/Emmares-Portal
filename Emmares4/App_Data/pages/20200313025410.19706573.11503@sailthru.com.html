Chicken Little's warning that "the sky is falling" has taken on new importance in the maelstrom of the coronavirus pandemic.

Airlines are facing the biggest drop in global demand ever, including post-September 11. The U.S. barring travel from Europe was the latest punch to the gut.

Industry group IATA says airlines could lose $113 billion in passenger revenue. Profit warnings, forecast revisions and service cuts are backing up at terminals everywhere.

And there are ripples beyond the runway. Fewer flights mean fewer sales for Boeing and Airbus, which in turn mean fewer purchases of whiskey and cigarettes at duty-free shops. Even the actor who played Sully isn't immune.

If you were glued to the all the "sky is falling"-esque warnings this week, here are some stories you may have missed.

Seattle's Patient Zero

The man at the center of the deadly coronavirus outbreak in Seattle, Washington, appeared to do everything right after coming down with symptoms, but everything still went wrong. Authorities couldn't track the virus even as Patient Zero was isolated in a unit developed for Ebola cases.

High Drama

If isolation is what you crave, consider the job of Renoj Thayyen, sitting in a hut 15,500 feet up in the Himalayas. The hydrologist monitors the melting of glaciers, the source of a battle between three nations over an increasingly weaponized resource: water.

Tesla's Fast Track

The home of the autobahn has a notoriously poky bureaucracy, so Elon Musk is pressuring German officials to step on the accelerator when reviewing plans for a Tesla factory near Berlin. His effort is testing the nation's adaptability to a changing global economy.

The H in WFH Stands for Hell

You can take Chinese tech workers out of their "996" offices, but it seems you can't take the "996" away. (That's referring, of course, to working 9 a.m. to 9 p.m. six days a week, plus overtime.) Some in China say working from home is even worse because the line between professional and personal is becoming more blurred.

No Longer a Rockstar

Dan Houser, co-founder of Rockstar Games and creative mastermind behind Grand Theft Auto V, is leaving the video game maker after employees claimed they were overworked and underpaid. A byproduct of his accomplishments in the industry was a corporate culture described as all-consuming and destructive.

Fowl'd Up

It seems like a no-brainer of a business plan: sell hamburgers and coffee to Americans. But Philippine tycoon Tony Tan Caktiong, the man behind fried-chicken juggernaut Jollibee, is seeing his U.S. gambit falter.

Nuclear Fallout

Remember Fukushima? Japan suffered its worst nuclear-power disaster nine years ago, an accident that turned an environmental leader into a coal addict. Here's why one of the most advanced economies pursues a Third-World energy policy.

The Rarest Unicorn

Every gearhead has seen a Ferrari, but very few have seen a real Bizzarrini. The rare Italian marque was designed by Giotto Bizzarrini after he left Ferrari in the 1960s, and he made fewer than 200, including the one admired above by French icon Johnny Hallyday.
