The spread of the so-called Delta variant of COVID-19 in the U.K. is a worrisome development. Infections there have risen to 15,000 per day, up from from 2,000 a month ago. While great progress against COVID-19 has been made in the U.S., with average daily infections falling below 12,000, a spread of the Delta variant here could create another mini-peak of infections. It is unlikely that government restrictions will be reimposed in such an event, but it could slow the rush of consumers into spending on services such as travel and eating out.

Delta and Delta Plus are the newest, and most transmissible, of the COVID-19 variants identified so far. The good news: They are still susceptible to certain vaccines. A Public Health England study found that two doses of Pfizer’s vaccine were 88% effective against Delta, compared to 93% against the original version. Results for the AstraZeneca vaccine were lower at 60%. But a single dose of either vaccine was only 33% effective, showing the need to push vaccination campaigns to their completion. Beyond vaccines, the Department of Health and Human Services recently suspended the distribution of antibody treatments bamlanivimab and etesevimab because of evidence that they are not effective against the new COVID-19 variants. Both drugs are made by Eli Lilly (LLY).

June is almost up, which means it’s time for fund providers and analyst outfits alike to provide their midyear outlooks for the stock and bond markets. This week, we’ll dive into a few of those insights, starting with Capital Group -- the name behind the American Funds. A running theme for months both here and in our Closing Bell newsletter has been weighing the greater opportunity: value or growth? Capital Group’s answer: “Balance them both.” The firm points to Nov. 9, 2020, as a turning point for cyclically sensitive stocks in the financial, energy and travel. “Those companies that used the crisis to innovate and improve operations should be well-positioned as the economy reopens,” Capital Group’s strategists say. But growth is hardly dead in the water. “Long-term growth trends are alive and well. Even after the acceleration in all things digital during the pandemic, there are still long runways for growth in cloud services, digital payments, streaming entertainment and more -- all powered by semiconductors.”
