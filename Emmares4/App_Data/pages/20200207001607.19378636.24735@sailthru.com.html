Charting the Decline of Value

It seems a fact of life in the post-crisis era. Value isn’t as valuable a tool for investors as it used to be. Why? And will it finally recover? The debate among investors and financial academics is growing intense.

This is a deep and complicated debate, which I am going to try to summarize with few words and a lot of charts. I recommend the source material. I will be taking a lot of painstaking math and statistics as read; if you have the time and care about investing, you might want to check the numbers in the originals.

By “value” we mean the tendency for the prices of stocks that are cheap according to their fundamentals to outperform in the long run. The idea is associated with the great investor and academic Benjamin Graham, who published his ideas in the wake of the Great Crash in the early 1930s.  It meant much careful valuation of companies. In recent decades, value is regarded as a quantitative factor. Using modern computers, academics led by Eugene Fama and Kenneth French were able to show that the cheapest stocks (measured by their price-to-book ratio) persistently performed better than the most expensive. This insight underpins much modern quantitative investing, as well as the many exchange-traded funds branded as “smart beta” — based on indexes that include only the cheapest stocks.

The following chart, from a massive new piece of research from Research Affiliates LLC shows how a value strategy, as defined by Fama and French, performed compared to growth stocks (which are chosen because their profits are growing). The value factor was obviously very real and very powerful for half a century — and has become much less so since the financial crisis:  

That run of underperformance is taking on historic proportions, and is awakening questions as to whether something has permanently changed. The Russell indexes are among the most popular benchmarks for U.S. stock-pickers. This is how value has performed compared to growth according to Russell, over the last two decades:

We know why value did so well in the early aughts. There had just been a historic bubble in internet stocks, none of which would ever have shown up in a value investor’s portfolio. Value naturally outperformed as that bubble deflated. But somehow it has now given up almost all of that outperformance. That is mostly because of valuation. Value stocks are by definition cheaper than growth stocks. But the gap varies. Using simple price-to-sales ratios, one of the hardest metrics to manipulate, value stocks are roughly as cheap now as they have been for much of the time over the last 20 years; while growth stocks are twice as expensive. The gap hasn’t been so wide since 2001.

Generally, when such a divergence happens, value investors should just sit back and wait for differences to regress to the mean. As the following chart from Capital Economics Ltd. of London shows, the gap in price-earnings valuations is very wide, and on historical experience implies that growth will underperform value by some 10% over the next two years. 

The issue now becomes whether we can rely on regression to the mean, or whether something has changed. The next chart, from Research Affiliates, builds on the first. From 1963 to the crisis we see steady gains for value, occasionally interrupted by outside events, and then reliably snapping back to the mean. Since the crisis, things look different. Value has steadily weakened, and relative valuations have widened. Regression to the mean has been delayed far longer than ever before. 

So. Has something changed? One possible answer is that intangible assets — brand valuations, patents and so on — matter far more than in the past. As this Research Affiliates chart shows, intangibles have always been more important for growth stocks, but their rising influence is common to both:

When the Research Affiliates team recalculated the data including intangibles in companies’ book values  to create new price-to-book multiples, they found value stocks had performed much better, and hadn’t peaked until 2014. 

What does this imply? In brief, this suggests that value as formerly calculated may never revert to the previous mean, because tangible book value is a less important measure these days. Meanwhile, if we use book values including intangibles, we have a big divergence that looks quite a lot like the dot-com boom, and there is no great reason why that shouldn’t regress to the mean. Therefore (and I am excluding an immense amount of mathematics that helped reach this conclusion), it is reasonable to expect that value will at some time before much longer start to beat growth.

If there is an important corollary to this, it comes from Joseph Mezrich, quantitative strategist for Instinet LLC. He points out that the widening valuation gap is entirely down to growth stocks getting expensive. Value stocks’ valuations look stable in relation to their previous norms. 

 In a world of professional investors, where the critical aim is to do better than your peers, buying value while others are stuck with growth should work out fine. But there is no particular reason to expect value stocks’ valuations to rise. Reversion to the mean can happen just by growth stocks getting cheaper, which is very easy to imagine. Quants who take a long position in value and short growth can make money; those of us who just buy stocks have no particular reason to expect value stocks to go up.

What might cause value stocks to get more expensive? In general, over history, they have risen when the economy is growing fast, all stocks look attractive and the bargain they offer grows more attractive. It might require a quick recovery from the coronavirus, and faster global economic growth than many now expect, before value investors’ portfolios start to look good in absolute terms.

The news is good as far as it goes, but it still looks a little too early to declare that the panic can end.
 


Eyes Down for NFP Day

We are about to hear new data about the U.S. jobs market. Traditionally the single most important economic data download each month, it isn’t clear how crucial the non-farm payrolls number is this time. Interest rates aren’t rising any time soon, whatever the jobs headline — even if the numbers show signs of inflationary pressure.

We get a welter of information on NFP Fridays, of which the headline figure, frequently revised, is among the less interesting. This chart from John Velis, foreign exchange analyst at Bank of New York Mellon Corp. in New York, offers a complete pattern, built around two separate measures the bank has developed to aggregate NFP day data. One shows the momentum, and one the level. 

The level, as President Trump accurately informed us on Tuesday, is historically impressive. But it has stabilized. If worried about politics, then we might note that momentum slowed badly in the run-up to the 2016 election, and this probably helped Trump win. His arrival did spark a pickup in momentum, but that is now slowing markedly. The pattern is beginning to look ominously similar to the months before the last election. 

If employment data start to weaken in a meaningful way, that could have a big effect on political expectations. At this point, the market is probably more worried about this data’s effect on the electorate than about its effect on the Federal Reserve.
