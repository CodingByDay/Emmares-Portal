After months of investors hand-wringing over the manufacturing malaise in the U.S., all it took was a half-baked trade truce to give the sector its best annual performance since 2013. Friday brought a few reminders that risks still abound, even if the market seems bent on ignoring them.

The Institute for Supply Management’s gauge of U.S. factory activity fell to 47.2 in December, marking the fifth straight month of contraction and the worst reading since June 2009, according to data released on Friday. A gauge of new orders also declined for a second straight month. It’s important to note that those numbers shouldn’t yet reflect Boeing Co.’s planned halt to production of the troubled 737 Max, which was set to begin in January. Elsewhere, Toyota Motor Corp. and Honda Motor Co. reported surprise U.S. sales declines for December, and signs point to the industry as a whole needing to rely on incentives to close deals. A U.S. airstrike targeting one of Iran’s most powerful generals could actually prove a boon to demand for American oil and the equipment that helps extract it, while wreaking havoc on other businesses such as the airline industry. But it also was a reminder that even with the trade war and Brexit fears in the rearview mirror, geopolitical flash points remain.

The optimistic read on those ISM numbers is that manufacturers are still working through the trade war hangover and that better days are around the corner. The IHS Markit gauge of factory activity, which has never dipped below the 50 point mark that divides expansions and contractions this year, showed only a modest decline in December from the month earlier and economists have been divided over which benchmark is more accurate. But if the corner has in fact been turned, stocks are already pricing that in and then some. “The fact is the market isn’t pricing in really any negative news,” Kathryn Rooney Vera, head of research and strategy at Bulltick LLC, said in an interview on Bloomberg TV this week with me and David Westin. “The market is pricing in a complete resolution on the trade front, no U.S. recession, no undesired consequences from the monetary expansionary exercise that we’ve seen on a global scale.” For industrials specifically, she recommends investors pocket their gains. “I just think we’re tapped out,” she said.  

For valuations to hold, earnings have to catch up. The worst does seem to be over for the slide in growth, but the trajectory of a recovery remains an open question. The general consensus is that the slowdown that had the biggest impact on those industrial companies on the front lines of economic swings has bottomed out and that growth could start to perk back up in the latter half of 2020. But U.S manufacturing executives were also expecting 2020 could bring the first annual decline in capital expenditures in 11 years, according to a semiannual survey conducted by ISM and released Dec. 9. The trade truce was inked several days after that, and a bottoming out in China would certainly help. Longer term, however, investors may be underappreciating the lessons that China has learned from the trade war – mainly, that the country needs to be less dependent on the U.S. for goods and services. Despite commitments for $50 billion in U.S. agricultural purchases, “China got the message that they need to diversify away,” Kirk Hartman, chief investment officer at Wells Fargo Asset Management, said in a separate Bloomberg TV interview with me and Westin. “This is a trend that’s not going away.”

On the Iran front, the overall market reaction wasn’t nearly as bad as might have been expected, given the difficulty of sustaining oil price increases in the shale age and the prospect of a weaker dollar, as my Bloomberg Opinion colleague John Authers notes. The exception is the airline industry, with the S&P 500 Airlines Index dropping as much as 3.4%. A spike in the price of jet fuel – should it last – would come at a particularly inopportune time for the airlines, many of which are set to negotiate new labor contracts in 2020 and likely take on more expenses in that department amid a tight jobs market. The way to counter those rising costs is through higher fares, but the Boeing Max is sabotaging them there. Whenever regulators do agree to lift a grounding order put in place following two fatal crashes, that will unleash a significant number of available seats.

NEW YEAR, NEW COUNTRY
One-time auto industry titan Carlos Ghosn shocked the world this week when he announced he’d fled to Lebanon in order to escape the “rigged” justice system in Japan where he was facing charges of financial misconduct. Ghosn, who rescued Nissan Motor Co. from financial distress and helped craft a global alliance with Renault SA and Mitsubishi Motors Corp., has claimed he was framed by Nissan executives and protested the harsh conditions of his detainment. Lebanon, where Ghosn was raised, has no extradition treaty with Japan and is highly unlikely to hand him over, while a government minister in France – where Ghosn also has citizenship and where Renault is based – has said the country wouldn’t extradite him, either, should he land on its shores. It’s still unclear how Ghosn managed to sneak out under the noses of Japanese prosecutors who were monitoring his movements via camera as part of a two-part, $14 million bail agreement and without passports that were in the possession of his lawyer. The Financial Times reported that Lebanon pressed for Ghosn’s return a week before and that Ghosn had hired a force of private-security operatives who helped stage an escape plan that – according to separate reports — included a musical instrument box and a stopover in Turkey.

It’s all very Hollywood-esque and certainly helped break up what had until then been a slow news week. But once the drama has died down, there remain legitimate questions about the alleged conduct that landed Ghosn in hot water in the first place, including under-reporting compensation and misappropriating funds for personal use. In September, Ghosn struck an agreement with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission over allegations that he failed to disclose $140 million of pay at Nissan. He neither admitted nor denied the charges, but the $1 million fine he paid and the 10-year prohibition from serving as a company officer or director seem to suggest that not all was above board. Lebanon authorities have indicated they will refer the allegations to the local judiciary, but it’s unclear how probing a trial would be in a country where Ghosn is hailed as a national hero. If Ghosn truly isn’t fleeing justice but rather “injustice” as he claims, then the right thing to do would be to submit himself to a trial in France, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Chris Bryant writes. As for Nissan, the risk of Ghosn using a press conference scheduled for next week to dish out damaging details about current executives is ultimately a distraction from a turnaround effort that’s going nowhere fast, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Anjani Trivedi.

NEW YEAR, SAME BOEING
As Boeing enters 2020, the troubled 737 Max is still grounded, production has been suspended and there’s no definitive indication of when either of those things will change. Boeing will have a new CEO after Dennis Muilenburg's pre-Christmas ouster, but it’s replacing him with the ultimate insider in David Calhoun, who has been on the company’s board since 2009 and blessed many of the poor decisions that got Boeing to this point. I don’t want to understate the mistakes that Muilenburg made with regard to the 737 Max; there were many of them and a few were so ill-advised, it was staggering. But he’s also in some ways just the latest casualty in the broader comeuppance for a profit-first mentality and corporate arrogance that traces its roots back to Jack Welch’s tenure at General Electric Co. In light of that, I’m not really sure why Boeing thinks throwing another Welch disciple at this deep-rooted cultural problem is going to fix it. Calhoun will become CEO on Jan. 13, giving him a few weeks to exit non-Boeing commitments. He held an executive position at Blackstone Group Inc. and the lead director role at Caterpillar Inc. Before all of that, Calhoun worked at GE for nearly three decades. He was in the running to become Boeing’s CEO, but the job went instead to another Welch protege, Jim McNerney. It was McNerney who made the call to rejigger the 737 with new engines rather than start from scratch, requiring Boeing to build in the anti-stall flight-control software system that ultimately would send two planes and 346 people plunging to their demise.

The hope is that Calhoun, with all of his connections, will be able to ease the palpable tension between Boeing and the Federal Aviation Administration while making good on the company’s commitment to transparency and reform. The risk is that the parallels to GE go a step further and that Calhoun’s tenure resembles that of John Flannery, another consummate insider who replaced Welch’s hand-picked successor, Jeff Immelt, and then spent his brief 14-month stint as CEO unearthing mountainous liabilities and profit shortfalls. Already, we know that until very recently, Boeing amazingly was still sitting on documents that should have been given to regulators months ago, with the company handing over a fresh batch of messages between Boeing employees on the same day Muilenburg was ousted. The contents haven’t been disclosed, but a House committee aide has called them “very disturbing.” A Boeing statement issued the day after Christmas announced the “long-considered” retirement at the end of 2019 by Michael Luttig, Boeing’s former general counsel who had been reassigned to focus on the Max crisis. Airbus SE, meanwhile, surpassed Boeing as the world’s biggest planemaker for the first time since 2011, according to Reuters. Airbus hit its full-year delivery target after reportedly canceling holidays and keeping its plants running until just hours before midnight on New Year’s Eve. This wasn’t unexpected given the Max grounding, but it’s a reminder of the risk of a longer-term market-share shift and a potential realignment of supplier resources and loyalties as companies like GE try to make up for the lost work on the Max. Be wary of more bad news from Boeing in 2020.  

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Anixter International Inc. has had a very busy holiday season. When we last left the bidding war over this industrial distributor of specialty wire and cable products, Anixter had disclosed that a mystery company had been competing with Clayton, Dubilier & Rice to buy it and analysts suspected that this rival might be rival Wesco International Inc. Turns out they were right and this has turned into one doozy of a bidding war. CD&R raised its offer for Anixter on Dec. 23 to about $4 billion including debt. The offer consisted of $86 a share in cash plus a $2.50 contingent value right that would reward shareholders should the private equity firm also buy Wesco within one year of closing. Filings later showed CD&R made a fully financed proposal to buy Wesco for $68 a share, or about $4.3 billion, but was turned down. Needless to say, this is an unusual twist. You don’t typically see a buyer attempt to end a bidding war by just acquiring the competition, too. Wesco isn’t very keen on this plan and improved its own offer for Anixter on Dec. 26 to $93.50 in a mix of cash and stock. Not to be outdone, CD&R matched Wesco’s bid on Jan. 2 but is still offering all cash. And then Wesco on Friday offered a whopping $97 a share, padding its bid with more stock but keeping the cash component the same. Everything about this screams late-cycle risk-taking. For CD&R, it shows the lengths that private equity is willing to go to put massive piles of cash to work. For Wesco, the opportunity to consolidate a fragmented industry is significant, and Jefferies analyst Hamzah Mazari estimates the company could offer up to the low $100 a share range before the math starts falling apart. But there’s a significant amount of execution and balance sheet risk entailed in this deal, particularly if the industrial economy doesn’t cooperate.
