Still a Loser’s Game

Active equity management has long been regarded as a “loser’s game.” That is how it was described in the title of a seminal book by Charles Ellis in 1973, and the description has seemed more and more apt as active managers have watched money flow out and into rival passive vehicles. The arithmetic is overwhelming — as more money in the stock market comes to be controlled by fund managers, so active management becomes a zero-sum game. On average they will match the market’s returns, minus fees. As the weakest drop out, the survivors will attempt to win a zero-sum game against ever more skilled opponents. 

Not all money is controlled by institutions, so active managers will occasionally beat the market. When this happens, it generally prompts some excitement about an active resurgence, which will soon be dashed. What is ironic is that active managers have been doing exactly what they needed to do to fight back, and it still hasn’t helped much. It might even be hurting. 

The first thing active managers can do to bolster competitiveness is to cut fees. This hurts them in the short run but may help longer term. According to Morningstar Inc., investors now pay half as much to own U.S. mutual funds as they did two decades ago. But with zero commissions on share dealing now common, fee reductions haven’t helped much, yet.

Secondly, they needed to avoid “closet-indexing” or staying too close to the benchmark. This protected against embarrassment, but also tended to ensure that they couldn’t beat their benchmark by much in the longer run. Now, active managers are steadily moving toward more concentrated portfolios less closely linked to the index (with high “active share” in the jargon). This works when their bets are on the right stocks.  

Meanwhile, another sin of active managers was excessive churn. Rather than pick a reasonably concentrated portfolio of stocks and stick with them, funds tended to trade. That brought extra fees and often showed inconsistency and indiscipline. Trading within passive funds is, of course, minimal. But for a long time now, active funds have reduced their turnover.

The Investment Company Institute, the trade association for mutual funds, expresses turnover as a proportion of the total assets held by shareholders — giving greater weight to the larger funds in which investors have the most money. This suggests a more dramatic improvement that started more recently:

All this should be good. Investors are paying less for trading, and active managers are showing conviction. But then we come to the problem. Holding on to a few big positions tends naturally to expose funds to the momentum factor — the tendency for winners to keep on winning and losers to keep losing. Over time, momentum has made a lot of money for traders, but it is subject to occasional and dramatic crashes. As time goes by, the stocks that have won the most will take up a larger proportion of the portfolio, making the fund more exposed to momentum, and therefore more vulnerable to a crash. In recent years, Mezrich shows that brief periods when a majority of U.S. active equity funds outperform the market have overlapped with the strongest periods of relative performance for momentum. Reversals of momentum invariably bring a majority of funds back below their benchmarks:

The latest brief period when active funds appeared to be beating the market petered out halfway through last year. This coincided with the end of a spell of dramatic outperformance by the “FAANGs” — Facebook Inc., Amazon.com Inc, Apple Inc., Netflix Inc. and Google (now Alphabet Inc.). Managers who bought these stocks early in their run and held them looked very good for a while, until they didn’t.

So the recent strong performance may have been nothing more than the consequence of a brief but successful bet on the dominant internet names. Mezrich suggests it may also have had much to do with an unsuccessful bet on interest rates. In the post-crisis era, the impact of rates has been all-pervasive. The critical bet that fund managers had to get right was that rates would stay lower for longer than many expected. That implied investing in “bond substitutes”  — boring companies that pay a high and well-supported dividend yield.

But in aggregate, investment managers have made the opposite wager, and eschewed bond substitutes. This may have been because they thought rates would rise. It might also have been a desire to make the kind of big bets that could let them beat the market convincingly, such as the FAANGs (which as a group aren’t high dividend-payers). Whatever the reasons, the strategy only worked during the brief periods since the crisis when long-term rates were rising. The pattern is clear:

The problem won’t go away. Mezrich shows that rising rates have always been better for stockpickers, because they tend to come at a time of economic growth, when previously neglected smaller companies come into their own. With more choices, this is a classic time for stockpickers to do better, if they make the right ones.

But now we have returned to a falling rates environment. And active managers have returned to playing a loser’s game. This isn’t all the fault of passive funds. Active managers could at least review their portfolios regularly to check that they aren't over-exposed to momentum, and to hedge against falling interest rates. But the bottom line is that funds really are reducing fees, making more concentrated bets, and cutting down their turnover, just as they should be doing, and it still isn’t working. Loser’s game indeed.

 

Credit: There May Be Trouble Ahead

There are plenty of arguments over whether we have reached the end of the long expansionary cycle in the U.S., and whether the rest of the world can stay out of recession. One alarming new data point released Monday comes from Credit Benchmark, which crunches credit upgrades and downgrades by banks. It claims more than 30,000 different bank analysts’ observations are collated into its monthly survey.

Credit deterioration is a typical symptom of the end of a cycle — and that is exactly what Credit Benchmark is finding, particularly in the industrial sector. To start with the least surprising name, the direction has been negative for industrials in the U.K. with only brief interruptions since the Brexit referendum in June 2016. Many companies have business models that will be badly affected by exit from the EU. The extent of the problem, however, is alarming and dispiriting:

With the Conservatives looking well-placed to take an overall majority in next month’s general election, uncertainty should die down. The country is likely to exit on terms roughly in line with those already on the table. But turning around credit sentiment promises to be difficult. 

Meanwhile Brexit isn’t helping the rest of the EU. Last week’s pleasant surprise that Germany had avoided a recession in the third quarter didn’t stop a continuing deterioration in credit quality. Two years ago. Germany appeared to be leading the world in a “synchronized global recovery.” All such hopes are gone, and the return of negative interest rates hasn’t assuaged credit concerns:

In the U.S., where manufacturing is struggling while the rest of the economy seems far healthier, there have now been two successive months of perceived deteriorating credit quality for industrials, even as stock markets regained their highs.

The U.S.-China trade dispute, and to a lesser extent uncertainty over the new trade deal with Canada and Mexico, plainly affect U.S. manufacturing, and probably will contribute to credit downgrades. Some clarification in the political scene might resolve these issues, but it would be unwise to stake too many hopes on it. The campaign for next year’s presidential election is still too fluid for anyone to be basing credit decisions on it.

There is no way to ignore this data. It looks bad.



Germany
 

The German economy isn’t officially in recession. That is good news up to a point, although it has disappointed some because it may persuade politicians that they don’t need to stimulate the economy using fiscal policy. For the longer term, however, Germany’s problems continue to look profound.

Last month I published this column, on how Harvard research suggests that Germany has pursued virtually all of its industrial strategies as far as they can go. That means that to grow further it needs to foster much innovation.
