Toward the end of a long life that will be remembered in part for his diplomatic breakthrough with China, former U.S. Secretary of State Henry Kissinger told the Bloomberg New Economy Forum in Beijing that the two countries are now in the “foothills of a Cold War.”

The resulting conflict could be worse than World War I, Kissinger warned. At age 96, the architect of rapprochement—the secret negotiations with China that set up the historic visit by President Richard Nixon—is now watching as that project unravels. Kissinger said he believes the U.S. and China can salvage their relationship, but both sides must understand the political causes of the standoff and commit to overcoming them. “It is far from being too late for that,” he declared. —Andy Browne

Today’s Top Stories

China ‘Cautiously Optimistic’ On U.S. Trade Deal

China’s chief trade negotiator indicated he was “cautiously optimistic” about reaching a partial deal with the Trump administration. Vice Premier Liu He (below) made the comments in a speech in Beijing ahead of the New Economy Forum. He has also reportedly asked the top U.S. trade negotiator to travel to China to continue talks this month, an invitation that so far hasn’t been accepted.

Here’s what the top delegates at the forum were saying about trade.

Hong Kong’s Rich Are Seeking a Backup Plan

As the forum got underway, it was impossible to ignore the protests raging in Hong Kong. Kissinger said he hoped for a negotiated solution on the principles of the 1997 handover from Britain, in which China promised the territory a “high degree” of autonomy. Protesters are demanding the democracy China pledged at the time. Bankers meanwhile weighed in on the financial fallout: Piyush Gupta, head of Singapore’s DBS Bank, said high-net worth clients from Hong Kong are moving money to Singapore, the U.K and elsewhere as “an insurance policy.” Standard Chartered CEO Bill Winters said clients have set up accounts outside Hong Kong “just in case.” But Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon said he doesn’t “see evidence” of Hong Kong losing its status as a hub of Asia.

Barshefsky Blames Protectionism On Both Sides

Charlene Barshefsky, the former U.S. trade representative who negotiated China’s entry into the World Trade Organization in 2001, decried Chinese state subsidies for industry, protectionism and mercantilism “all turbo-charged by scale.” In a speech at the forum, Barshefsky also took aim at U.S. tariffs slapped on China, criticizing America for neglecting its own development by failing to invest, and then blaming China for its woes.

Hank Paulson Calls Delisting a ‘Terrible Idea’

Former U.S. Treasury Secretary Hank Paulson (above) said calls to oust Chinese companies from American stock indexes are contrary to the foundations of capitalism, as he warned against the dangers of decoupling the world’s two largest economies. Chinese Vice President Wang Qishan, delivering the keynote speech at the forum, reaffirmed China’s commitment to market-based reforms while warning that the international order is “under attack.”

Tech Sectors Already Under Strain by Trade War

Trade war tensions are exposing growing rifts between China and Silicon Valley. Bill Gates weighed in, as did leading venture capitalists and startup founders who expressed concern on the sidelines of the forum. Signs of trouble are emerging in everything from venture capital to movie-making. Fundraising for dollar-based venture capital funds in China is down 75%, estimates Qiming Venture Partners’ founding partner Gary Rieschel. Olivia Hao, an executive at Beijing-based film production startup Baozou, said it is increasingly hard to make investments or buy other companies in the U.S.
