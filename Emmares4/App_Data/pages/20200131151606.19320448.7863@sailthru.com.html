Boeing Co.’s 737 Max and the coronavirus are the earnings bogeymen that industrial companies didn’t need.

Sales growth was already set for a muted trajectory in 2020 amid lingering economic uncertainty and still-weak business investment, as evidenced by results this week from bellwether companies including Caterpillar Inc., 3M Co. and United Parcel Service Inc. Most are expecting the growth environment to remain lackluster – and for some markets, to get a bit worse – in the first half of the year before improving in the back end. But as I wrote this week, for many companies, that “improvement” has more to do with easier comparisons than any true demand spike. We’ve also heard this line before, and forecasts for a back-end-weighted recovery rarely play out as hoped. The prospect of a three-month-plus halt to Max production and fears the burgeoning coronavirus crisis will nip a Chinese economy recovery in the bud are adding particularly unwelcome wrinkles to that dynamic.

More than 200 people have died from coronavirus, with nearly 10,000 cases confirmed, as of Friday midday. No company was willing to put a hard number on the economic impact, with most simply calling out the hit to Chinese demand as something they are watching. Among the more drastic moves, Delta Air Lines Inc., American Airlines Group Inc. and United Airlines Holdings Inc. said Friday they would halt all flights to mainland China after the U.S. government warned Americans not to travel to the country in an effort to contain the spread of the respiratory disease. That will deepen the disruption in an aviation sector already reeling from the loss of the Max, which has been grounded globally for nearly a year in the wake of two fatal crashes. The major U.S. carriers have pulled the Max from their schedules through June, but Boeing’s latest projection for a mid-2020 return to service clearly implies more rejiggering will have to be done. The duration of the grounding forced Boeing to take the drastic step of halting production on the Max in January to try to stem some of its cash bleed. The company reported earnings this week and said it burned $4.3 billion in cash in 2019 and expects that number to grow in 2020.  

Boeing executives gave little detail as to the future pace of production, saying only that it will start slowly warming up its factories around April and then set the manufacturing rate “one airplane at a time.” It is making assumptions about that pace to get to its estimate of $2.6 billion in increased costs over the life of the Max program, but after previous projections ended up being wildly optimistic, it was understandably not too keen to share those. Suppliers, however, also have to make assumptions on the Max and they were more forthcoming. United Technologies Corp. is assuming a 90-day delay to production and planning on a 21-jet-per-month pace for the second half of the year. Spirit AeroSystems Holdings Inc., which gets more than half its revenue from the Max, said it expects to ship a total of 216 sets of Max equipment in 2020, implying an average production pace of 24 per month. Spirit doesn’t expect to achieve the pre-Max production rate of 52 jets per month until late 2022. General Electric Co. said it will ship half as many engines in 2020 as it did in 2019.

The net of this is, there will be some pretty significant financial pain for Boeing’s supply chain. United Technologies estimated the Max production woes will shave $350 million to $375 million off its operating profit this year. While Honeywell International Inc. saw minimal impact in 2019, the production stoppage and updated timeline will cause a significant reduction in deliveries, enough to reduce growth in its aerospace division by low-to-mid single digits, or hundreds of millions of dollars. Some of that will be offset as it redirects production to other parts of its backlog; it also will see increased demand for maintenance and repair work as airlines fly older planes for longer to make up for some of the lost capacity. Parker-Hannifin Corp. is assuming it sells no Max-related products for the rest of its fiscal year ending in June, forcing it to lower its revenue guidance this week. GE will provide more details of the impact to its cash flow and earnings from the Max production halt at an outlook meeting in March, but cited the plane as the biggest variable to its 2020 forecast.  

“While there are perhaps fewer indications of a broader-based recession today, there remain several challenging and potentially fast-changing dynamics that create uncertainty,” Honeywell Chief Financial Officer Greg Lewis said on the company’s earnings call this week, citing tensions in the Middle East, Brexit, U.S.-China trade relations, coronavirus and the Max. When you add it all up, 2020 is shaping up to be “an equally and perhaps more uncertain year than the one we just completed,” Lewis said.

MORE BOEING BLUES
Executives tried (hard) to paint this week’s earnings release as a purging of all remaining bad news and to evoke a more transparent, conservative tone. But a current projection for $8.3 billion in compensation (net of insurance) for customers affected by the grounding falls well short of what analysts were expecting, and it seems logical that the number may yet rise as negotiations with airlines and lessors progress. While new CEO David Calhoun was more candid and frank than his predecessor in his assessment of the Max crisis, a few choice comments signal the company still has a ways to go on the accountability front. Calhoun said he didn’t think culture contributed to the missteps in the design of the MCAS software system blamed for triggering nosedives in the two Max crashes. He also took issue with being described as an insider, despite the fact that he has been on the board for over a decade. “I watched the same movie you did – I think I was in the front-row seat,” Calhoun said on a call with reporters. Perhaps it wasn’t spelled out clearly, but part of the reason he received those six-figure checks each full year of his tenure was because he was meant to be a director of said movie, not a bystander. It’s all very eerily reminiscent of GE’s failed first attempt at a turnaround under insider John Flannery, and it’s hard to see how Boeing makes real progress toward change without following that company down the path of overhauling its board and management team with outsiders.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
General Electric Co. will reportedly gauge buyer interest for its steam-power unit in the coming months. CEO Larry Culp carved out GE’s steam, nuclear and conversion businesses into a separate unit within the larger power division, a move that many interpreted as a prelude to eventually selling anything not specifically related to gas turbines. With GE’s fourth-quarter results this week adding to signs of stabilization and improved operations for the power unit, Culp may feel more comfortable exploring his options. It’s not clear what kind of price the steam business would command. Largely built through the ill-fated acquisition of Alstom SA’s energy operations in 2015, it can be volatile: Bookings for the so-called “power portfolio” division fell 55% on an organic basis in the fourth quarter from a year earlier when GE had registered a large steam-power order. But on the whole, GE’s fourth-quarter report was markedly placid, with no fresh negative surprises. Industrial free cash flow for 2019 of $2.3 billion was clearly better than the negative number GE had warned might be possible in March, although technically, it was at best in line with what analysts had been anticipating before Culp sounded the (apparently conservative) fog horn of gloom and doom. A forecast of up to $4 billion in free cash flow for 2020 seems doable based on unit outlooks but isn’t a low bar and it’s still not clear how GE gets to the levels implied by its current valuation.   

Stanley Black & Decker Inc. agreed to buy Consolidated Aerospace Manufacturing (or CAM) for as much as $1.5 billion. CAM was cobbled together by private equity firm Tinicum through a series of transactions; it sells fasteners, self-locking nuts and other manufacturing odds and ends for the aerospace industry. Interestingly, about $200 million of the purchase price is being held back and is contingent on the “timely” return of the Max jet and Boeing reaching certain production levels. For Stanley, the deal caps an at least $5 billion acquisition spree over the past five years and should help it further diversify its business mix with higher-margin products. Adjusted for tax benefits, the purchase price works out to a reasonable three times sales. CAM had been thought of as a potential partner for TriMas Corp.’s aerospace operations, which increasingly stands out as undersized in a consolidating sector. To that end, TriMas agreed this week to buy RSA Engineered Products, a manufacturer of components for air-management systems used in aerospace and defense applications. No price was disclosed for that deal. 

Kone Oyj reportedly has made the highest offer for Thyssenkrupp AG’s elevator unit with a bid of about 17 billion euros ($18.7 billion). This isn’t that surprising, considering Kone has more opportunities for cost savings than its private equity rivals and also has to throw enough money at Thyssenkrupp to convince the company to go through what is likely to be a grueling antitrust review. Kone plans to divest Thyssenkrupp’s operations in Europe to bidding partner CVC Capital Partners to help win regulators’ blessing for a deal. To make a lengthier closing process more palatable, the company is reportedly prepared to pay a multi-billion euro down payment that Thyssenkrupp would get to keep even if the deal is blocked by regulators. Kone is also willing to pay either all in cash or a mix of stock and cash, depending on Thyssenkrupp’s preferences. The private equity consortiums also in the running have bid under 16 billion euros, but offered sweeteners of their own, including the opportunity for cash-strapped Thyssenkrupp to retain a minority stake in the elevator business and the promise of fewer job cuts. The price is likely to climb as the would-be buyers jockey for advantage, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Chris Hughes.

BorgWarner Inc. agreed to acquire Delphi Technologies Plc. – a maker of auto parts including engine-ignition systems, brakes and fuel pumps – for about $3.3 billion including debt. Delphi Technologies was created when Delphi Automotive (the General Motors Co. spinoff) split in two in 2017, carving out the Aptiv Plc business to focus on autonomous driving systems. BorgWarner’s offer implies a more than 60% premium for Delphi, based on the companies’ average trading values in the 20 days leading up to the bid announcement. It’s the latest in a string of deals meant to help auto suppliers weather a shift to electric vehicles and a slowdown in demand. (Robert Bosch GmbH CEO Volkmar Denner separately warned that global car production will likely to stagnate until at least 2025.) Delphi does bring some electrification technology, but was also trading at a depressed valuation for a reason and analysts say the acquisition will dilute BorgWarner’s margins.
