With the number of confirmed coronavirus cases zooming past 75,000, it's increasingly likely that the victims will include the global economy.

Across China, companies are telling workers there’s no money to pay them — or that they shouldn’t have to pay full salaries to quarantined employees. Bloomberg Economics says the health emergency could drag China's GDP growth for the year down to 5.6%. Nomura, Goldman Sachs, UBS and Macquarie Group also cut their growth forecasts.

A slowing China means a slowing just-about-everywhere-else because of the interlocking supply chain. Hong Kong, Japan, South Korea and Vietnam are most vulnerable.

On the corporate front, Apple will miss its quarterly revenue targets,  
Maersk's yearly outlook faces "considerable uncertainties" and Cathay Pacific Airways warns of a significant drop in earnings. China may bail out its airlines.

As for a potential vaccine, that's going to take a while. While you wait, here are some stories worth reading. — Michael Tighe

Life on the Inside

Where do you go when public health officials won't let you go anywhere? Bloomberg's Stella Ko takes you inside her coronavirus quarantine in South Korea after testers flagged her temperature as being higher than normal. Spoiler alert: She's fine.

Rough Trade

The U.K. left the European Union's band last month to resume a solo career, but it still wants to collaborate with the bloc on new material. Boris Johnson needs to trade old trade deals for new ones, and he's got 570 billion reasons to write a hit with his former partners.

Flipping Out Over Burgers

If discovering fire is one of the most important events in human history, then grilling beef patties over the flame can't be far behind. Michelin-starred chefs name their favorite burgers from around the world, and some don't even have meat. Want fries with that?

A Biblical Plague

One of the 10 plagues of biblical Egypt is descending on East Africa, and the culprit may be climate change. Swarms of desert locusts are invading the region, ravaging crops and pastures, and further threatening food security for millions of people.

Masayoshi Son faces a repeat case of overpromising and underdelivering as another of his star investments fails to meet lofty expectations. India's Oyo Hotels & Lodging, run by 26-year-old Ritesh Agarwal, is losing money and missing inventory targets.
