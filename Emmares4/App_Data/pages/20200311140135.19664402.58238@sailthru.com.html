The fresh smell of breakfast tacos and the sound of festival-goers' converse on concrete can mean only one thing: South by Southwest 2020 is on the horizon. Or, well, it was. As the coronavirus (aka COVID-19) continues to incite panic, pose some legitimate health threats and complicate day-to-day life, SXSW recently joined other big events like Google I/O and Mobile World Congress in postponing the 2020 edition.

Before the news, Ars was again planning to be on site, and the 2020 schedule did boast many obviously Ars-y panels and speakers to check out. Headliners included the heads of the NSA, the US Space Force (srsly), Marvel, and Twitter. And some recent pop culture and gaming favorites from the Orbital HQ were also scheduled to make an appearance: the Google Stadia dev team, the writers and cast of Better Call Saul, the composers of Watchmen's synth-y soundtrack. We thought we might even convince Steve Ballmer to catch some music and dance with us. 

But with SXSW 2020's cancellation, this week's Orbital Transmission is focusing elsewhere. Losing a major event like this hurts many different groups of people beyond the schedule of a headliner: from the local hospitality economy to the small filmmakers, panel proposers, musicians, and media members depending on these events in various ways. In light of that, Ars is still hoping to highlight some of the most interesting stuff that would've been found at SXSW 2020—look for a slew of film reviews on site soon, #SXScreeners—and these were the smaller SXSW panels and presenters we had highlighted on our schedule. As usual, there was enough cool stuff that we didn't even have room to include the developers of Exploding Kittens.

 

—@NathanMattise

Orbital Transmission 03.11.2020
 

Hey—we recognize that sci-fi author name!

You may have heard that former Ars Culture Editor Annalee Newitz left this site a few years back to focus on some other work—namely, her now burgeoning sci-fi writing career. Newitz dropped Autonomous back in 2017 and it's now being turned into a TV pilot. She's got multiple novels on the way through Tor beginning this year, and at SXSW she was ready to discuss how sci-fi imagines cultivating new galactic worlds since humanity's trying to get to Mars and all.

 

Modern animation's coolest studio has its own version of a hackathon

We were fans of Titmouse, Inc. long before we met founder Chris Prynoski in 2018—you've seen The Venture Bros., right? But Prynoski and his colleagues have nimbly navigated the ways technology has changed animation workflows and how the entertainment business at large has shifted with streaming services (and animation work showing up everywhere from ads to VFX games). At SXSW, he was set to share the details of Titmouse's versions of a hackathon, a 5 Second Day and Smash Party. (We'll have to see if we can get the scoop soon.)

 

Linguistics on the Internet is always fascinating

We genuinely adore hearing from the folks behind things like Dictionary.com—whether they're discussing why emoji should now be in the dictionary or if the peanut butter brand should get to define our collective pronunciation of GIF. So of course another Dictionary.com SXSW session caught our eye. A pair of lexicographers from Dictionary.com were set to outline the process of how old words gain new meanings because of the Internet (see "viral," "woke," etc.) and what the threshold is for a word to enter the dictionary in 2020 (c'mon "graybeard!").

 

Our must-see film centers on centers like Hakeem Olajuwon

SXSW film always has a handful of upcoming theatrical releases worth braving lines for (see things like Get Out and Stuber in recent years), but we find the most value for crowd-battling with documentaries. 2020 may no longer have crowds, but luckily many documentarians are still happy to share screeners of their projects in the hopes of getting them in front of audiences and attracting distributors or streaming homes (again, look for #SXScreeners on site soon). Among the SXSW-announced titles, two sat squarely in our sweet spot: Console Wars will outline the early 90s battle between Nintendo and SEGA while Insert Coin chronicles the rise and fall of the arcade icons at Midway.
