There are many pastimes that can sooth an anxious mind: meditation, gardening, painting, running. Biotech investing, however, isn't one of them.

Consider what happened over the span of just a few hours on Thursday. Early in the morning, Sage Therapeutics saw more than half its market value melt away after its experimental treatment for major depressive disorder failed to reach its primary target in a key study. Sage had been a Wall Street sweetheart this year thanks to a run-up in its shares of more than 50%. But its study data changed the story in the blink of an eye.

Meanwhile, Acadia Pharmaceuticals was enjoying the sort of upswing that investors dream about. Its shares jumped more than 21% after new data from its antipsychotic drug were better than anticipated in elderly patients with dementia. If the FDA grants the drug a new designation, it could open up a vast new group of patients who currently are treated with older drugs that can come with significant risks.

And then there was Biogen. The biotech giant's year has encapsulated the agony and the ecstasy of investing in innovative medical companies; in March it lost more than a quarter of its market value when it said it was giving up on its aducanumab Alzheimer's therapy. Then it soared in the fall after reviving the treatment -- and the hopes of millions of patients.

On Thursday, Biogen said at a big meeting of doctors and brain scientists in San Diego that it is resuming its studies of the drug, and it laid out a previously undisclosed tranche of trial data. That sent its shares up sharply -- after an intraday dip, of course. But big questions remain, including whether regulators might make the company run yet another trial of the medicine. That could add significantly more cost and delay its entry to the market for several more years.

All that excitement in so few hours might have some investors longing to ride their bikes or trim their bonsai trees. But in biotech, any respites are likely to be brief—Tim Annett

Here's what else we're watching:

Global genes. Foreign drugmakers are joining the gene-therapy gold rush, as Japan's Astellas agreed to buy U.S.-based Audentes, the maker of a treatment for a life-threatening neuromuscular disease, for $3 billion.

Carcinogen questions. Global health regulators are looking into whether the widely used diabetes drug metformin may contain unsafe levels of a carcinogen previously been found in heart and gastric medications.

Optimizing Optum. UnitedHealth is best known for its giant insurance business, but the company said this week it expects Optum, which houses its PBM and physician practices, will soon make up about half its profits.
