Since the 1970s, scientists have been trying to connect our brains to computers. They started by drilling holes in the human skull to attach electrodes, which would then translate brain activity into data that could be used to control a computer. These days, scientists are still chasing the seamless brain-computer interface, though the electrodes have gotten much smaller and the implanting technology has gotten far less invasive. The ideal outcome for everyone in the field is a BCI that doesn't require any implants or surgeries at all—a simple device that slips over the head or around the neck that can obviate the need for keyboards, mice, touchscreens, and even steering wheels.

Arielle Pardes tests out some of the latest noninvasive neural interfaces being designed by ambitious Silicon Valley startups. She finds that the race to plug humans directly into computers may not be won for a while, but that it's already yielding promising results.

Also this week, Brian Barrett argues that if you want a sense of everything that's wrong with consumer technology, just look at the history of 3D TV. And we have some surprising tips for anyone who got a new robot vacuum or a Nintendo Switch Lite for the holidays.
