Shortages and supply chain issues have been in the news a lot lately. They’re also the top story in the latest issue of The Kiplinger Letter. Here are a few highlights: Labor shortages will start to moderate this summer, starting in states where extra unemployment aid has already been canceled. But wages are likely to stay high, as employers shell out more to get the workers they need. Supplies of key inputs such as steel and semiconductors will likely improve in the second half of the year, but will take time to catch up with demand. That will continue to hinder production and elevate prices of certain consumer goods, such as cars.

The most intractable shortage of all: Freight transportation. There simply aren’t enough ships, trains, planes and trucks carrying cargo fast enough to where it’s needed. Expect ocean shipping delays for the rest of the year. Both trucking and rail are currently overburdened and likely to stay that way until autumn. The current shortfall in air freight capacity won’t improve until international travel recovers, adding more space on commercial flights for cargo. That’s not likely to happen until 2022, at the earliest. All modes of freight transport are currently expensive, and some may get even more so before the year is over.

The Biden Administration is committing $3.2 billion from March’s stimulus legislation to help fund the
development of oral anti-viral pills to lessen severe symptoms of COVID-19. These medicines would be taken at home when symptoms appear. Pfizer (PFE) expects to have such a drug available for
distribution by the end of the year. Merck (MRK) is also working on a drug called molnupiravir.
The only approved oral antiviral is Gilead Sciences’ (GILD) remdesivir, which has had only modest effects. Other anti-virals include Regeneron (REGN), which must be given intravenously. This would likely delay treatment.
