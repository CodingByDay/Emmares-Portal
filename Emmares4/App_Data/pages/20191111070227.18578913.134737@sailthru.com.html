Hey y’all, it’s Austin. Have you been binging Apple TV+ content like me? The company’s first slate of original programming, which has been rolling out this past week, is getting mixed reviews, but for $4.99 per month, I couldn’t resist checking out what all the noise is about as Apple Inc. goes up against Netflix Inc., Walt Disney Co. and other media giants in the great streaming wars.

The meh response to its shows so far puts Apple in an unusual position. Just 62% of critics were positive on the much-touted Jennifer Aniston series, The Morning Show, and only 41% liked Jason Momoa’s See, according to review-aggregator Rotten Tomatoes. Historically the company's products are hailed for their design perfection or category-redefining innovation. But this early batch of Apple originals ought to remind the company and its shareholders that TV, unlike hardware, is a hit-or-miss business. It exists in a fickle marketplace of never-ending eyeball competition, where cancellations are common and renewing another season involves far more creative risk than another incremental MacBook upgrade.

It’s a tough lesson that another technology player, Amazon.com Inc., had to learn when it initially entered the streaming space more than a decade ago. Back then, Amazon's video-on-demand service was clunky, and critics noticed. Also problematic was the 2013 premiere of its original TV programs, developed under its Amazon Studios arm. The company debuted 14 pilots hoping customers would help them decide which series to extend. The experience felt more like cringe-watching than binge-watching. Amazon quickly killed nine pilots, including the animated space series, Dark Minions, and a musical comedy about interns called Browsers. It picked up Alpha House and Betas, but both series were dead by 2014.

The following year, however, Amazon won a Golden Globe for Transparent, and it has since gone on to roll out huge critical hits such as The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel and Catastrophe. The success has given consumers another reason to pay $119 annually for Prime membership and helped bolster Amazon’s hardware efforts, including its Fire tablets, the Alexa-enabled Echo Show and its TV-connected portfolio of set-top boxes. It’s also given the company an entry point into live television.

What are the takeaways here for Apple? Amazon Chief Executive Officer Jeff Bezos once attributed the company’s strides in video to its data-based content approach, but it’s just as likely that Bezos’ willingness to spend billions of dollars on original programming—despite dozens of now-cancelled duds—deserves most of the credit. Creative failures are inevitable, Bezos said in an interview in the early days of this initiative, which could be an adjustment for a tech company accustomed to putting out chart-topping products and refining every millimeter of the user experience.

“You can say, ‘I want to make something that millions and millions of people are going to watch,’” he said. But “if that's your starting point, you paint yourself into a corner and you often end up with homogenized, uninteresting content.”

Bezos also stressed that, when it comes to TV, he tries to get out of the way. While working with Transparent showrunner Jill Soloway, Bezos said it would be “horrific” if he started editing “early drafts of Transparent season-two scripts,” adding, “I can help Jill by leaving her alone.”

Amazon has a long way yet to go to catch up to the content libraries of Netflix and HBO. Bezos is now said to be in search of his own Game of Thrones. Still, the ethos of both patience and distance might prove valuable for Apple. The company is grappling with potential flops as well as charges that executives, including CEO Tim Cook, have been interfering with showrunners’ creative processes.

But Apple will have time to learn the ways of Hollywood: It’s apparently already renewed all its debut series for a second season. —Austin Carr
