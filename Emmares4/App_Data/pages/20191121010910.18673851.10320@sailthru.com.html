Trade War: What Is It Good For?

Ups and downs in the trade negotiations between the U.S. and China continue to drive ups and downs in markets. That is the narrative with which we are all now comfortable, and it has circumstantial evidence on its side. 

As I write, the last 24 hours have seen the worst sell-off for U.S. stocks in a month after a Reuters report that a “phase one” deal may not be reached this year. Stocks in Asia then recouped some of their losses thanks to a Bloomberg report that Liu He, China’s chief negotiator, was “cautiously optimistic” about reaching a deal.

The issue is of course important, and certainly provides reasons, or at least excuses, to buy or sell stocks. But it isn’t as central as many try to make it appear, while a phase-one deal is harder to achieve than many suggest.

Does it really matter?

First, note that since Donald Trump was elected, the 100 companies in the MSCI World index with the greatest exposure to China have performed in alignment with the index as a whole. That is illustrated in this chart of the relative performance of the most prominent exchange-traded funds for each asset class. Stocks’ big sell-off came after last year’s Buenos Aires summit had appeared to lower the temperature on trade. Their recovery in the last few weeks has come despite the lack of any clear progress:

Although there have been plenty of occasions when trade headlines have moved markets intra-day, it is highly questionable that the topic is as salient as people tend to think. As I argued last week, changes in liquidity, particularly from central banks, are far more helpful in explaining market trends in the last three years. 

 

Is a “phase one” deal really close?

Why are risk assets doing so well despite the obviously troubled negotiations? Primarily because of the arguments based on game theory that a resolution is in the interests of both sides, and therefore likely. That argument is somewhat flawed. Meanwhile, the chances of a longer-term agreement on the most important issues can be overstated.

Arthur Kroeber of Gavekal Dragonomics presented a fascinating take on China’s “decoupling” from the rest of the world in New York this week. He points out that a phase-one deal is harder to reach than it should be because of the framing that both sides have made in advance. Trump wants to be able to declare victory, and wants an excuse not to raise tariffs.  His opening demand for greater Chinese agricultural purchases of $50 billion was far too high, and unrealistic. This would be more than double China’s greatest ever purchase of American farm goods, which currently stand at about $10 billion.

Meanwhile, China wants all tariffs rolled back, or at least a schedule for their rollback, and the scale and the breadth of those now in place makes this very hard. Having levied so many tariffs, removing them will look too much like a U.S. defeat. 

Thus, Kroeber suggests that only another market sell-off might force the two leaders into making a deal.

 

What else can China do?

Meanwhile, China has more power than is at first apparent in the negotiations. First, note the way the yuan has moved under Trump. It is still a largely managed currency, and China has allowed it to depreciate whenever trade tensions rise. The last few days have, not coincidentally, seen the yuan cross the barrier of 7 to the dollar once again.

And for the long term?

Discussions about the long-term relationship with China over the last few days have involved emotive phrases such as a “Second Cold War” or a “Silicon Curtain.” There is alarm at the world being bifurcated into two economic systems again, as it was during the era of the Soviet Union. 

That would certainly be something to fear, but how likely is it? Kroeber offered this chart, showing that China is vastly more interconnected with the global economy than the Soviet Union ever was. Going back to Soviet levels would be almost unimaginably horrific — which is why it is unlikely to happen.

It seems far more plausible that China will suffer the rather more bearable fate of Japan, which is far less central to the global economy now than it was 30 years ago, but remains far more intensely integrated than the Soviet Union ever was. 

If we dial down the overheated comparisons with the Cold War, the fact remains that the U.S. and China are deeply entwined and that a lot of pain is possible once the thorniest problems, over technology transfer, come up for discussion. As Kroeber points out, U.S. technology has already been virtually closed off to Chinese money, by prompt political action, while U.S. investment in China is still quite constant. That could yet change. A growing proportion of the components in iPhones are made in China, making disentanglement more painful for all concerned:

So, in short, the U.S.-China conflict is tougher to resolve in both the short and the long term than many currently seem to assume. And while it is undoubtedly very important, it perhaps isn’t as important as many currently believe.
