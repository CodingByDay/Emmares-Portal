Bad News, Good News, and Averting a Credit Crisis

For stock market investors, the bad news is that the news has been so bad of late that a big decline in corporate earnings is inevitable. That will bring a bear market with it.

The good news is that the impact will be temporary. Earnings should be increasing by the end of the year, as should the stock market. 

The really horrible, terrifying news is that recovery is dependent on what a group of politicians can thrash out between them, in what will be an election year in the U.S.The extremely negative reaction in Asian trading to President Trump’s televised address to the nation, making his first ambitious proposals on dealing with the crisis, underline that the next months will be hazardous.

First of all, one point to get across quickly is that we have a new world. Data from even a few weeks ago no longer matter. For my favorite illustration of this, here is a chart showing core inflation for the U.S., excluding food and fuel, and the expected average inflation rate for the next 10 years, as generated by the bond market. Wednesday brought data showing core inflation was growing surprisingly fast when it was last measured in mid-February — and 10-year expectations immediately dropped below 1% for the first time since 2009:

Inflation forecasts have been thrown out by the weird and hectic conditions in the bond market. Instead we are now seeing a wave of attempts to adjust earnings forecasts for the changed conditions. Within 24 hours I received documents from Credit Suisse Group AG, Citigroup Inc. and Goldman Sachs Group Inc. making big cuts to their projections. As this chart from Citi’s chief global equity strategist Rob Buckland shows, the necessary adjustments are huge:

Those numbers are for the U.S. Citi reckons that the downgrade for emerging markets, originally expected to log better earnings growth, will be even worse, taking them down to a 16% decline. Meanwhile, analysts are all over the place. It is easy enough for top-down strategists and economists to make sweeping downgrades for the entire corporate sector, but the individual broker analysts who cover single stocks take a while to catch up. No wonder stock markets are gyrating wildly. But there could be hope. Goldman Sachs is predicting an earnings recession in the U.S., which will be most severe in the second quarter of this year. But it is also expecting a swift rebound. This is the performance of S&P 500 earnings during post-war recessions:

This leads Goldman Sachs’s chief U.S. equity strategist, David Kostin, to predict a huge V-shape for the stock market over the course of the year. The S&P 500 is now at 2,741 (though June futures plunged a further 4.7% to 2,600 in Asian trading after President Trump announced restrictions on travel from Europe). Kostin predicts a mid-year level of 2,450, making this a real bear market; and then a recovery to 3,200 by the end of the year. That would be a 32% increase from the trough.

What could stop this recovery? Let’s exclude public health risk for now, as it is impossible to gauge with precision. If the U.S. suffers a worse economic hit than the Chinese province of Hubei did, then stock markets aren’t going to like it.

What matters critically, and what was roiling markets Wednesday, is credit. Particularly in the U.S., companies are over-leveraged after years of minimal interest rates. Goldman’s own data show that in the recent sell-off, companies with weak balance sheets have suffered the worst:

If we look at the performance of the “quality” style, which generally involves investing in conservative companies with strong balance sheets, we can see angst about credit steadily increase over the last few years, before taking off in style in the last few weeks. As this chart from MSCI indexes shows, this trend is even more marked in the developed world outside the U.S.:

Worries about balance sheets make sense just because of the virus. The earnings slump may last only a matter of months, but that may be longer than a heavily indebted company can survive. The V-shaped recovery for profits and share prices could be imperiled by a wave of bankruptcies. Then there is the impact of the oil price decline, which will make it hard for many high-yielding borrowers in the energy sector. If they default, that will mean losses for their creditors, which could affect the entire credit market. Even the spread on investment-grade corporate bonds, as rated by Moody’s, has shot up in recent weeks:

Spreads aren’t as high as they were in 2016, and actual rates are still quite low thanks to the phenomenally low yields on Treasuries. But credit is now under severe pressure, after a long period when it seemed insulated from problems elsewhere. 

Then there is the issue of forced sales. This chart comes from Vincent Deluard of INTL FCStone Financial Inc., and shows Saudi Arabia’s reserves, compared to the price of oil. It has a tendency to eat into its reserves when the oil price goes down — so we can assume it will sell a lot of reserves in the coming months. That is one of the theories doing the rounds of Wall Street for the rise in Treasury yields Wednesday, a day when stock prices tumbled. Someone somewhere had no choice but to sell Treasury bonds:

As my colleague Cameron Crise puts it, this crisis has until now been an exogenous one. Markets have had to process two huge external shocks. Now it is endogenizing, if that is a word. The internal workings of the market could yet drive a fresh credit crisis. That would nix any recovery in earnings or share prices this year, and turn a V into a U or even an L.
