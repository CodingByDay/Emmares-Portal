There’s good news on vaccinations, but also a concern. They appear to be having an effect in pulling down new COVID-19 infection rates to their lowest level in the U.S. since last October. 54% of the adult population has received at least one dose by now. (The first dose of a two-dose regimen has been shown to be mostly effective by itself.) However, first doses given peaked in mid-April and have plummeted by more than a third over the past two weeks. That indicates that the initial rush of people eager to get a vaccine is passing, and now the more difficult part of convincing holdouts to get inoculated begins. The hope is that the number of holdouts is not so great that herd immunity for the entire population cannot be achieved.

A $2 trillion Microsoft? Not quite yet. Microsoft’s (MSFT) bid to become the second $2 trillion company (Apple (AAPL) was first) took a minor detour Wednesday morning -- the latest example for investors that not all great earnings reports are always rewarded with gains. MSFT shares are sliding today after Tuesday night’s fiscal Q2 report. Microsoft posted 19% year-over-year revenue growth, its fastest rate since 2018, and beat both sales and earnings guidance. Yet that performance didn’t wow investors. Why? UBS analyst Karl Keirstead (who has a Buy rating on the company’s shares) says constant-currency revenues “only” came in at the high end of guidance, not above. But regardless of what MSFT shares are doing today, analysts continue to strongly favor the company (35 rate it Buy, with three Holds and zero Sells, per S&P Global Market Intelligence) and broadly lauded the report. Says Wedbush analyst Daniel Ives (who has an Outperform rating on the stock): “Last night was another cloud masterpiece delivered by Redmond as MSFT is continuing to see massive cloud momentum that is still in the early days of playing out.”
