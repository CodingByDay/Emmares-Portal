Hi readers,

Several of my friends have been asking me why the UK isn’t offering the covid-19 vaccine to children and teenagers. The UK decided this week that 16 and 17-year-olds can now be routinely offered the Pfizer/BioNTech vaccine. But that still means most secondary school children will remain unvaccinated, unlike in countries such as the US, where all teens have been able to get the jab for some time and that may soon be extended to under-12s. 

With UK schools now set to abandon several anti-coronavirus measures in the coming autumn term, many parents fear that the UK is about to unleash a Great Barrington declaration-style “let it rip” experiment on its unvaccinated children.
 
The UK’s Joint Committee on Vaccination and Immunisation (JCVI) has previously said it is waiting for more results to come in from clinical trials in children, and the main factor weighing against jabs for this group is concern over safety.
 
Specifically, the JCVI was concerned about the rare incidence of a side effect called myocarditis, or heart inflammation that can cause breathlessness and chest pain, after vaccination with the Pfizer/BioNTech and Moderna jabs. The US has found this happens most often in younger males, at a rate of 1 per 13,000 in boys from ages 12 to 17.

Any heart condition sounds alarming, but myocarditis is also sometimes seen after receiving the flu vaccine or flu itself, when it is similarly more common in younger men and boys. It tends to be mild and temporary and is treated with over-the-counter anti-inflammatory drugs such as ibuprofen.

PROS AND CONS
 
Myocarditis can be a consequence of covid-19 too. In fact, a study put online last week found it happened six times as often after a natural infection with the coronavirus than after vaccines against the disease, as I wrote about here.
 
Weighing against vaccination, on the other hand, it seems rare for under-18s to get seriously ill from covid-19. Recent studies have found that in the first year of the pandemic in England, only 25 under-18s died from the infection. Another study out this week suggests that long covid may be less common than has been previously reported, with 1 in 50 under-18s having symptoms for more than eight weeks.
 
Clearly there are both pros and cons to letting younger teens get the vaccine. This doctor explains on Twitter here why he thinks the JCVI is being too cautious, while another thinks the right decision has been made, for now.

Nevertheless, as long as no new safety concerns emerge from the ongoing trials or real-world experience with vaccinating teenagers, it seems likely that in the next few months the JCVI will start offering the vaccine to 12-year-olds and up in the UK. I suspect that for many parents, that decision cannot come soon enough.
