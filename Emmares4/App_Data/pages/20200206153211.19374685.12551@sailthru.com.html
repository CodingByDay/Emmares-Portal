The bugs are making a comeback.

That's one of the most important lessons now emerging from the novel coronavirus outbreak—that for all of humanity's scientific success stories, new and evolved diseases remain a persistent threat to our well-being. Since 1970, more than 1,500 new pathogens have been discovered, and “epidemics in the 21st century are spreading faster and farther than ever."

From SARS to MERS to the illness that now has millions locked down in major Chinese cities, newer threats are cropping up even as old scourges such as cholera and plague resurface with disturbing regularity. Chillingly, our most reliable ally in the fight against infectious disease, antibiotics, are losing their power. That means that a protective net that once helped keep people around the world healthy is increasingly a sieve that allows some of the most pernicious threats to slip through.

The world's wealthiest countries have the capacity to protect themselves. We're better at sharing information and science is racing ahead all the time, finding new tools and methods to identify and contain outbreaks. Still, a true pandemic would strain our resources and test our resolve. It will take political will, and a reshaping of incentives for drug companies, to improve our defenses.—Tim Annett
