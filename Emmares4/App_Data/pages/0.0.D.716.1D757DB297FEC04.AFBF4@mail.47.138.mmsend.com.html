The messenger RNA technology at the heart of Pfizer’s (PFE) and Moderna’s (MRNA) successful COVID-19 vaccines has many other potential uses as well, and could revolutionize disease treatment. (mRNA carries a message to cells to do something, such as produce antibodies. Once delivered, it dissolves.) Researchers will be starting Phase 1 trials next year for a universal flu vaccine, and also for HIV and herpes vaccines. The tech could also create a universal coronavirus vaccine, including for the common cold. Pfizer and GlaxoSmithKline (GSK) are working on a vaccine against respiratory syncytial virus, or RSV, which can be deadly for infants and young children.

Cancer is another target. Moderna and BioNTech (BNTX) are working on that. Also, autoimmune diseases such as lupus, MS, and arthritis. BioNTech has found the treatment to be effective in mice. mRNA tech could be helpful in gene therapy by replacing a defective gene with a new one. That would help combat sickle cell anemia. Translate Bio (TBIO) has started early stage trials in people for its application of mRNA tech to cystic fibrosis. Finally, there are potential applications for malaria and tickborne diseases, which would be huge breakthroughs for large parts of the world.

If you own a home or want to buy one, you probably already know just how frothy the housing market is. Home prices rose 19% in April from a year earlier, a pace that is putting houses out of reach for many would-be buyers. But there are some faint signs that the market is starting to cool off, according to last week’s issue of The Kiplinger Letter. Home sales declined in April for the third consecutive month, because there simply haven’t been enough houses for sale to meet demand. However, the inventory of homes for sale is starting to creep higher, finally. And more consumers are rethinking their desire to buy; a survey conducted by Fannie Mae showed that, for the first time since the survey started asking the question, more people say that now is a bad time to buy a home than a good time. If these trends of more houses hitting the market and fewer people looking to buy continue, that should eventually slow the pace of price increases. And in the long run, a market with a better match between supply and demand will probably be a healthier market.
