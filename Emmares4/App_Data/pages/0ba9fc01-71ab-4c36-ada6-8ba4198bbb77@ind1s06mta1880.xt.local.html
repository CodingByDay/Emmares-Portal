Navigating Type 2 Diabetes
 
Long-Term Complications of Diabetes
 
Issue 12 of a 15 Part Series
Having type 2 diabetes increases your risk for many health issues, from dental problems to heart disease. The good news is that controlling your diabetes can help prevent these complications or keep them from getting worse.
 
 
KNOW THE WARNING SIGNS
• A type of nerve damage known as peripheral neuropathy is one of the most common complications of type 2 diabetes. It can feel like a burning or stabbing pain or a numbness in your feet, hands, arms, or legs. Talk to your doctor if you have these symptoms.
• Eye problems, such as diabetic retinopathy, also are common. Tell your doctor right away if you notice blurred vision, trouble seeing at night, dark spots in your vision, halos around lights, or you're sensitive to bright light. You can have eye damage even if you don’t notice any vision changes, so it’s important to get an eye exam every year.
• Red, swollen, or bleeding gums are a symptom of gum disease, another health problem that is more common in people with diabetes. Make sure you see your dentist at least twice a year.
• Erectile dysfunction is more common in men who have diabetes. There are many possible causes, from nerve damage to depression. Your doctor can help pinpoint the problem and recommend treatment.
 
 
HEART DISEASE AND STROKE
Some serious health problems may have no symptoms, including kidney disease and heart disease. Be aware that a “silent” heart attack, which could feel like heartburn or nothing at all, is more likely in people with diabetes.
Strokes also happen more often to people who have diabetes, but keeping your blood sugar and blood pressure under control and sticking with a healthy lifestyle can keep your risk low. Learn the warning signs of a stroke. Call 911 immediately if you suspect you have had one. Timely treatment is critical.
 
 
THE TAKEAWAY
The list of potential diabetes complications is long, and the worst-case scenarios are scary. But there is encouraging news: People with diabetes today are living longer, healthier lives with fewer complications than they did just 20 years ago, the CDC says. The main reasons: greater awareness and better control of risk factors.
