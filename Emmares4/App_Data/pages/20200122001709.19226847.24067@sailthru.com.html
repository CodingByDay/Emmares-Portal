Love in a Sub-Zero Climate

The president of the U.S. told the World Economic Forum at Davos that negative rates were “something I could get used to very quickly.” He added: “Love that!” Then he complained about the Federal Reserve’s still-positive rates back at home, which he believes put the U.S. at a serious disadvantage. 

Others are inclined to disagree. European banks are already all too used to negative rates, and could heartily do without them. And Larry Kudlow, the president’s senior economic adviser, who also argues for lower rates from the Fed, made plain that he wanted them to stay positive: “Negative rates are not a good idea. They’re really bad for banks, they’re really bad for savers and they’re not great for investors.”

We have established that zero is not the absolute lower bound for interest rates, but we also appear to be in the process of showing that they cannot go far into negative territory. As the European Central Bank and the Bank of England gear up for their first meetings of 2020 this week and next, the issue won’t go away.

The best demonstration I have seen of the problem that negative rates causes for banks comes from the German-based credit aggregator Deposit Solutions. It used the ECB’s figures to calculate what European lenders have paid thus far in negative interest charges on excess liquidity parked at central banks. The total comes to 25 billion euros ($28 billion). 

The previous installment of the report, produced six months ago, can be found here. The impact is serious. The eurozone’s banking system remains stricken, and the single-currency area remains particularly dependent on banking, rather than credit markets, for supplies of debt. The healthiest way for banks to rebuild their balance sheets and start lending again would be for them to make higher profits. But the payments in negative rates are on their own equivalent to 12% of pretax earnings for German banks, and 23% for Greek banks. 

Nothing that hurts European banking profits is good news. As the chart shows, the slump in profitability is severe, and eurozone banks’ latest earnings are barely above their level from the worst of the crisis of 2009:

Negative interest is attractive for borrowers, as the president made clear. For lenders, it is utterly toxic. Negative rates also create acute problems for savers. The economics of financing a retirement annuity are transformed for the worse, for example. As Europe’s system is more bank-dependent than the U.S., it should arguably have prioritized exiting negative rates over exiting quantitative easing bond purchases — or at least this is the argument by Alberto Gallo of Algebris Investments in London.

In a further sign that Christine Lagarde’s inheritance at the ECB is difficult, its latest survey of the eurozone’s senior lending officers showed that demand for loans was also falling from businesses, for the first time since 2014. Borrowers are precisely the people who should be stimulated by low or negative rates, so this is concerning.

Companies, like consumers, may be deciding that if rates are negative they should adjust their expectations — and save more and spend less. Lagarde, who is conducting a strategic review to create time to study the options, has a lot to think about. Prompting her old friends in Europe’s finance ministries to embark on a more expansive fiscal policy would be the most obvious way to deal with sagging loan demand, but normalizing rates will also help.

Meanwhile, as Trump rightly said, it’s harder for a central bank to be hawkish when others are dovish. This has helped to keep the dollar strong, and now makes it harder for the ECB to raise rates. Any move by the Fed this year is far more likely to be dovish than hawkish. The Bank of England could also create ripples. With British ministers again stoking up rhetoric for an aggressive stance in negotiations with the European Union for a post-Brexit trading relationship, uncertainty is increasing again, while deeply disappointing retail sales figures suggest weak consumer confidence — and a case for the BOE to cut rates. As this month’s meeting will be the last for Mark Carney before he is succeeded by Andrew Bailey, the theory among many analysts is that it might be bad manners not to cut this month, and force the new governor to start his tenure with a reduction. 

In what will also be the last act of the BOE while the U.K. is still technically a member of the EU, it would also be an appropriate parting gift.

 

How Markets Go Viral

Epidemics are back in the news. I am not a doctor, and have no ability to gauge the potential seriousness of the coronavirus that has just claimed its first confirmed case in the U.S. after taking a number of lives in mainland China. All of us should be concerned about the advent of a new disease, and have compassion for its victims. Beyond that, nobody can answer how severe the problem could become, and nobody beyond a few experts in epidemiology and public health has much ability even to try.

I can, however, offer some perspective on how much the latest potential epidemic could affect markets. It could create quite a lot of damage. 

SARS, which terrified Asia in 2003, is the closest medical analogy, I gather, but the case that might be most similar, of a scary disease arriving in the U.S., is the Ebola epidemic of western Africa in 2014, which briefly became the biggest news story in the U.S. in October of that year after a few Ebola patients arrived in the country. It was also a huge political issue in the mid-term elections, driven by the Twitter account of an outspoken private citizen named Donald J. Trump. This is Google Trends’ track of searches for Ebola over the year:

And this is a chart of the S&P 500 for that year. The pullbacks of August and October (when the drawdown came close to a 10% sell-off on an intraday basis) overlap directly with the spikes in concern about Ebola. 

Looking at coverage from the time, we find that executives were asked about Ebola in earnings calls, while this piece shows Regions Financial Corp., a bank based in Birmingham, Alabama, blamed the epidemic for small businesses’ reluctance to take out loans. Semiconductor manufacturers and makers of scissors were asked their opinion.

For some context, there were only ever 11 confirmed cases of Ebola in the U.S., all but two of which were contracted outside the country. The two nurses who contracted the virus on American soil both recovered; only two people died of it in the U.S., both of whom had fallen victim to the disease in West Africa.

This was a dreadful ordeal for those affected and their families. The notion that a disease that only took two lives in the U.S. could contribute in a big way to a correction of almost 10% in the stock market, however, seems almost unfathomable. This is in part due to crowd psychology and the way that fears and panics can take hold. It is also because of shameful exploitation of the issue by politicians. Because a new epidemic is a classic “black swan,” we are aware that risks have risen, but cannot measure how great they are, or how high the cost could be if our worst fears are confirmed. Dealing with such problems, markets naturally tend to sell off violently, but not — unless the very worst is confirmed — for very long.

As warm-blooded humans we should pray for the health of the victims and hope that the epidemic makes little extra progress. As cold-blooded investors, the coronavirus might provide a convenient excuse to take some profits. And judging by the Ebola precedent, it might yet provide a great buying opportunity.

 

Minerd-sky Moment

And finally: Guggenheim Partners’ Scott Minerd has warned delegates at Davos that world markets are a Ponzi scheme. This is quite a claim, and suggests that we are in the final stage of the progression that the late Hyman Minsky diagnosed, in which increasing debt-fueled speculation leads ultimately to collapse.

Could he be right? Handily, we are discussing Minsky in a book club live chat on Thursday at 11 a.m. in New York, after we have heard from Lagarde at the ECB. Minsky’s views both on the markets, and on the choices facing Lagarde, would have been very interesting.
