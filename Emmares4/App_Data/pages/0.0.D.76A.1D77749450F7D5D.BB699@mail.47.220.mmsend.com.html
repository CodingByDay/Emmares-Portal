It appears a spike in COVID-19 infections in the U.S. due to spreading of the Delta variant is inevitable. Based on India and the U.K.’s recent experiences with Delta, the U.S. can expect a period of rising infections for the next couple months as it gradually spreads around the country, but only small increases in hospitalizations and deaths. Most of these severe outcomes will take place among the unvaccinated and be concentrated in states with lower levels of vaccinated residents. So far, infection rates have risen significantly in Missouri and Arkansas, and appear to be on the upswing in Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Florida, Kansas and Oklahoma.

The rising infection rate will not likely slow the economy much, as governments will not reimpose business restrictions, but fear and uncertainty could temporarily slow the strong recovery in services a bit. The spread of the Delta variant will also put greater emphasis on the debate about whether booster shots are needed by those who have already been vaccinated. Data is lacking about whether booster shots would help or not; however, Israel has decided to go ahead and give booster shots to those with weak immune systems.

Next, let’s talk taxation of capital gains. As the Kiplinger Tax Letter recently reported, President Biden wants higher taxes for the wealthy on such gains—specifically, a 39.6% rate for taxpayers reporting $1 million or more of adjusted gross income. The same AGI threshold would apply to single filers, heads of household and couples filing a joint return. Biden’s changes would also be retroactive, applying to gains after April 28, 2021, the date the White House released its American Families Plan.

Ron Wyden (D-OR), the top Senate taxwriter, also backs capital gains reform, but supports a different approach: A mark-to-market policy on tradeable assets. Wyden’s proposal would require certain individuals to recognize unrealized gains and losses each year on their publicly traded assets and pay income tax as if they sold their holdings for a fair market value on the last day of each tax year—namely, those who meet either a $1-million income threshold or a $10-million asset threshold for each of the preceding three years. Worried readers should note that neither of these proposals is likely to become law, though it’s possible Democrats could ultimately agree on a smaller increase in the top capital gains tax for the wealthy.
