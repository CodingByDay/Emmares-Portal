Hi, this is Saritha in Bangalore. Earlier this week, in the back of my Uber cab, I came face-to-face with India’s streaming mania.

When I jumped into the car, I found Shoibuddin, my driver, avidly watching a Bollywood comedy on his dashboard-mounted smartphone. Enjoying such on-screen humorous escapades helps ease the stress brought on by chaotic roads and surly riders, the 29-year-old cabbie says, as he switches between video and Uber's ride-hailing software.

India is turning out to be one of the world’s most exciting online video markets, estimated to have half a billion subscribers by 2023. That's second only to China. Ushering these new fans along is expanded access to tech infrastructure, inexpensive data rates and cheap smartphones.   

Until a couple of years ago, streaming video was unheard-of in India’s deepest corners where cable ruled. Today millions living in even the smallest towns have lots of options. Shoibuddin, for example, pays among the world’s lowest rates for the wireless broadband that flows through his low-price but sturdy smartphone.

Movies and sport capture initial users who later form broader viewing habits and tastes. Recently, there’s been a step up in demand for localized content – stories with greater appeal to Indian audiences increasingly told in languages other than English.

The boom is enticing the unlikeliest of players to get into the streaming game. This week, food delivery app startup Zomato unveiled a new kind of binge – more than 2,000 videos in English and Hindi including 18 original shows around food with a comedy, reality or celebrity twist. It joins a crowded field where the likes of Netflix, Amazon Prime, Disney-owned Hotstar and a swarm of local players already offer a surfeit of viewing riches.

Indian users are not exactly willing to pay big bucks for streamed content so companies are forced to improvise. Reliance, the country’s oil-to-telecom conglomerate, already offers a plethora of shows and movies free to the users of its Jio wireless telecom service. It’ll start charging for a premium service next year where subscribers will get to watch the latest Bollywood movies on the same day they are released in theaters.

Walmart-owned Flipkart announced that it’ll soon start offering free streamed video. Both Amazon and Netflix have cut-rate, India-only plans. Netflix’s mobile-only monthly plan is 199 rupees (less than $3).

Recent India entrant Apple’s custom plan for its local TV service in India costs 99 rupees a month compared with $5 in the U.S. Apple will add to its limited library a series based on the bestselling novel Shantaram about a convicted Australian bank robber and heroin addict who escapes from prison and lands in the slums of Mumbai.

In short, the Indian viewer is today spoiled for choice. Shoibuddin has no plans to be an Apple TV customer and certainly won’t be watching Shantaram. He’s sticking with slapstick, saying he gets enough real-life drama driving the streets and alleys of this sprawling tech boom town.
