Hey everyone, it's Nat in Brussels.

Bill Gates, Steve Jobs and Sundar Pichai all reportedly limited their kids’ access to technology as they grew up, with time-suck and addiction the primary concern in common. The companies they created have all come under fire over privacy, taxation or antitrust issues, and now increasingly for designing products almost impossible to put down.

My chiropractor told me he's seeing more physical injuries occurring too, such as bruised noses -- a result of people falling asleep with their laptops on their chest, which inevitably fall on their faces in the night. It’s also a common sight worldwide to see people walking down the street hunched over their smartphones, paying little attention to oncoming traffic.

Such habits have at least one notable regulator personally concerned. Margrethe Vestager, the European Union’s top tech watchdog, said in an interview with me and my colleague Aoife White on Friday, that she sometimes struggles to keep her phone in her handbag instead of answering emails or sending messages.

“What a number of these services do very well is to make you make choices and to make you stay as long as possible,” encouraging users to pay with their time and data, Vestager said. “I think in the years to come, we will be so much wiser as to what it does to us. We'll say maybe we shouldn't have jumped in so easily.”

As keen as the European Union can sometimes be to regulate big tech, there’s no obvious solution in this case. People have the freedom to consume what they wish in a free society as long as this doesn’t impinge on others’ rights.

France has made some efforts, however. Last year, it implemented a law banning cell phones and other electronic devices from schools, while a previous French law enshrines the right for workers to disconnect from the internet during off-hours or while on vacation.

Some companies are also making changes of their own volition, such as Apple, which last year implemented new controls to limit screen time aimed at combating smartphone and app addiction.

But maybe it’s also “a you problem,” to quote a recent New York Times headline. Nir Eyal, author of a book that offered Silicon Valley guidance on how to build habit-forming products, has now published a follow-up advising users on how to reclaim their attention. That involves turning off notifications and learning to be comfortable with not always staring at a screen when waiting in line somewhere.

“If people want a society where no one looks each other in the eyes, where no one shares a comment about the weather waiting for the bus because they're all playing Candy Crush,” Vestager said, “that's the society we get.”
