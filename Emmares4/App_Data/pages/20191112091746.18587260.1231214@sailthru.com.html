In our latest issue: the woman forcing Amazon to treat its workers with dignity, a little bit at a time.

How do you get the attention of a trillion-dollar company? If you are Nimo Omar, and that trillion-dollar company is Amazon, you start at the bus stop.

Omar is 23, the daughter of East African refugees, and a relentless organizer. She lives in Minneapolis where an Amazon warehouse opened in 2016. About 30 percent of the workers there are East African and many complained about grueling quotas, physical strains, and, of particular concern to Muslim Somalis, no place to answer the call to prayer.

Omar got to work to improve conditions in the warehouse. A former union organizer, she went on to cofound the Awood Center to organize within the local Somali community. As Jessica Bruder reports in WIRED’s cover story this month, Omar would head to an Amazon shuttle bus before dawn, “greeting bleary-eyed workers as they headed off to the warehouse. ‘At first, people didn’t want to talk to me,’ she says. Some were downright rude. But gradually people started offering up their phone numbers, saying they’d be willing to meet up later.”

Bruder has spent a lot of time with Amazon warehouse workers. To write her book, Nomadland, Bruder spoke to retirees who made ends meet working the holiday season for Amazon and got a temp job in a Texas warehouse herself. (She lasted a week.) Her reporting in Minneapolis tracked the efforts of Omar and Amazon workers, their walkouts and demonstrations. Amazon made changes. Prayer rugs were purchased. Fans were brought in to cool the warehouse. These were small concessions, and the push and pull between Amazon and the Minneapolis workers continues. But the warehouse workers had commanded the attention of a trillion-dollar company, and that itself was a remarkable feat.

Vera Titunik | Features Editor
