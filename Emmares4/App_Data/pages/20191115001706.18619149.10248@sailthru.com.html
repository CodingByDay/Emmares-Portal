Let’s Get Liquid

You might already have read a column of mine entitled “The Only Chart You Need to Understand 2019’s Global Markets.” For reference, this is the chart in question, drawn up by CrossBorder Capital of London. It shows a dramatic turning point in the liquidity produced by central banks this year — upward in the case of developed markets, and downward for emerging markets. 

Ultimately, these huge changes in the availability of money do far more to explain the tectonic shifts in markets over the last year than such things as the trade war, or the latest economic prognosis. If you want to explain 2019’s markets in one chart, I am happy to keep to the claim that this is the one. 

Now, for those who need some convincing, and also need to understand just why liquidity has moved in this way, I will offer you another 10 charts. 

Most of us know the story of how the Federal Reserve and the other big developed markets came to turn on the taps this year. But in the emerging world all the main sources of liquidity have seen a decline this year. This is led by central banks, but there have also been declines in foreign inflows of cash, and in private sector flows.

The source of tighter liquidity in the emerging world is China. Since the financial crisis, the country has survived by pumping out prodigious amounts of debt. Figures released Thursday by the Institute of International Finance show that China’s outstanding debt is now more than triple its GDP:

It is two years since the outgoing president of the People’s Bank of China saw fit to warn that the country wanted to avoid a “Minsky Moment” — or in other words the kind of sudden and crashing loss of confidence in credit that the western world witnessed when Lehman Brothers went bankrupt in 2008. Since then the PBoC has been active, but it has been driven by a desire to clean up balance sheets and minimize the risk of a Chinese Minsky Moment. Entering this year, hopes were widespread that the PBoC would turn on the tap and try to stimulate China’s economy. The exact opposite has happened, as illustrated by CrossBorder Capital:

As a result, China’s economy is unambiguously slowing down. It is growing faster than the U.S. or Europe still, but Bloomberg’s own gauge of real economic activity makes clear that China is ticking over more slowly than it has done in decades:

China’s central bankers are consciously emphasizing macro-prudential risks over macroeconomic ones, and averting a crisis at the potential cost of weaker growth. That lack of liquidity, however, and lack of Chinese demand for goods, has had a negative effect on the entire emerging markets world. JPMorgan Chase & Co.’s widely used index of emerging market foreign exchange is close to plumbing a new low, even though no major country in the asset class is currently thought to be in crisis.

The latest IIF data show why this is such a concern. The amount of dollar-denominated debt issued in emerging markets has shot up in recent years, in an understandable attempt to take advantage of low rates. Dollar-denominated debt swamps euro- and yen-denominated debt.

Outside China, non-bank borrowers in other emerging markets have racked up foreign currency debt (mostly in dollars) equivalent to more than 20% of GDP. This is higher than on the eve of the Asian crisis of the late 1990s. 

Put in a different perspective, emerging markets now take a far bigger share of total global debt than they did even before the Asian crisis. Tightening liquidity in emerging markets, along with a strong dollar and weak emerging currencies, therefore combine to spell potential serious problems, particularly if the trade war cannot be steered to a safe conclusion. (And it hasn’t gone totally according to plan so far).

There should be a natural counterbalance to this. The dollar was strong last year precisely because U.S. liquidity was drying up. As international investors suffered a shortage of dollars, so their scarcity drove up the price of the currency. Now, the combination of plentiful dollars in the U.S. and tighter liquidity in the emerging world should help emerging currencies recover. The dollar remains strong — and it maintains its strength against the developed market currencies as well — but demand for greenbacks should be about to slacken. That would cause some relief.

Against that, there is the problem that the effects of tighter liquidity in China have a wider impact. As this chart from John Velis of Bank of New York Mellon Corp. shows, the euro has tended to weaken or strengthen against the dollar in line with Chinese activity. If China slows, all else equal, we can expect the euro to weaken. The eurozone is correctly perceived as far more exposed to China than the U.S. is. So tighter Chinese liquidity is tending to increase the upward pressure on the dollar:

For the time being, the dollar remains strong despite the Fed’s U-turn toward easier money. Everything perversely seems to conspire to keep it elevated. Most recently, news that Germany had narrowly avoided falling into a technical recession in the third quarter was taken as reason to fear that there wouldn’t therefore be a fiscal stimulus from the government — and so the good economic news still managed to drive the dollar higher.

The resolute dollar and tight liquidity in the emerging world remain the greatest concerns for world markets — and a deterioration in the trade war would exacerbate both of these issues. For now, the huge flow of extra liquidity in the developed world has been more than enough to act as a counterweight. It really does come down to liquidity.
