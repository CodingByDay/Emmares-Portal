Hi, this is Vlad in Tokyo, a place where dinosaur flip phones still roam. Rendered obsolete by iPhone-like devices in most places around the globe, flip phones have retained their popularity in Japan and South Korea as simple tools for the increasingly unfashionable task of making calls.

In 2020, however, they look set to stage a comeback powered by the latest invention in smartphone tech: foldable screens. Motorola announced its reboot of the iconic Razr last week with a flip phone that’s all display on the inside. It's got the familiar and tactile clamshell form factor -- even featuring the OG Razr's signature chin at the bottom -- but it also runs modern Android apps and has a large screen for gaming and video streaming.

Samsung will join Motorola in launching its own square-shaped clamshell device with a flexible interior screen in the spring. Others will inevitably follow. Unlike in 2019, when Samsung aborted the Galaxy Fold's initial launch due to durability issues and Huawei’s Mate X got pushed back many months, next year’s crop of foldables looks much more promising and this flip form factor is the key.

Flip devices remain popular in Japan because of their simplicity. They make answering and ending calls an intuitive, unambiguous and frankly satisfying task. Instead of hunting around for an icon to tap on a touchscreen, you need only flex your device open or slam it shut. Big carriers like NTT Docomo persist in offering a range of cheap and uncomplicated flip phones, and most people I see carrying such a phone tend to have a smartphone for more advanced tasks in their other pocket.

The basic, subconscious appeal of the flip phone may be hard to grasp from a YouTube hands-on video. But walk into a Verizon or Walmart store in the U.S. this January and you’ll probably experience it while trying the new Razr. The promise of this phone and its ilk coming in the new year is that they'll bring back the familiar tactile experience and portability while retaining the modern niceties of big, all-screen devices.

There are still many technical and prosaic challenges ahead for this type of handset: how do you make the super-thin display layer robust to the daily grind of careless use, how do you integrate batteries that don’t bend, and how do you build a hinge that is both strong and smooth in operation? The question for the vast majority of people will also be, how do you justify a $1,500 expense -- the new and improved Razr’s price tag -- when cheaper and better-specced smartphones abound?

Motorola is an unlikely candidate to perfect the foldable-display flip phone on its first outing, having been in a malaise pretty much since the original Razr more than a decade ago. And yet, the new Razr is a promising and instantly enticing device: it marries a nostalgic appeal with a futuristic form, and it draws the outline of what I expect to be the next major form factor to establish itself in the consumer market.

Trust Japan to always be ahead of the tech trends. Even when it is behind.
