The coronavirus looks ever more like a subprime mortgage.

To a man with a hammer, everything looks like a nail. And to a man (or woman) who lived through the credit crisis, every other financial disturbance looks like 2008.

I, like many readers, lived through those events. They have largely defined my career, and it has been hard to stop them from dominating my interpretations of all financial upheavals I have witnessed since. But there’s no escaping it: The past 24 hours look a lot like the events surrounding the Lehman collapse. It is obvious that plenty of traders and — crucially — politicians are aware of what went wrong then, understand the traps, and are trying to avoid them. Nevertheless, I think the rest of the coronavirus saga will follow outlines recognizable from 12 years ago.

Let’s start with the day’s most remarkable market event, which was a rise of 34 basis points in the 10-year Treasury yield, probably the world’s most important financial benchmark. That was its biggest daily move since the summer of 1987 (when the yield started as high as 8%). This was a historic move out of bonds.

Treasury bonds have just reversed their recent trend. Meanwhile the dollar has also reversed, staging what it might be fashionable to call a V-shaped recovery. Its sharp decline started at the same time as the dive in stock markets, but it has since rallied as stocks have kept falling. Indeed, its shape for the year so far is more of an “N.”

Two of the dollar index’s three biggest daily moves of the last eight years have happened in the last week. (The other was the day after the British electorate voted to take the pound out to the woodshed and shoot it). 

How can we explain this? Lehman taught us not to look just at news headlines. Some of the biggest moves in 2008 simply reflected market players adjusting their own books. The rise in the dollar probably reflects a desperate shortage of dollars needed by borrowers. (Note that emerging market credit, currencies and equities generally didn’t join in Tuesday’s rebound.)

As to the rally in bond yields, possibilities doing the rounds include that some big hedge funds have been forced to sell Treasuries (Bridgewater Associates has admitted a 20% loss and many others follow similar strategies); sovereign wealth funds selling bonds to meet demands for cash from governments in the wake of the oil price decline; and China selling some of its hoard of bonds, either because it needs to or because it has a sinister plot to crash the American economy. Most reasonably of all, bond yields only reached historically low levels because of an epic squeeze in the market, so it shouldn’t be surprising to see some retreat. All these factors make some sense. Some people may simply have decided to bank profits. There are worse ideas. 

But the main reason for the rebound really is the news. On Tuesday, the Federal Reserve said it was reactivating a 2008-era program to support commercial paper issued by companies — essentially to ensure that scarcity of short-term funding doesn’t force some large company to fail to meet its payroll, and take the crisis to another level. The day also brought headlines that the administration was looking for a fiscal stimulus package with a total price label of $850 billion. By weird coincidence, that was almost the exact amount that Congress eventually voted for the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, or ARRA, in 2009. By the end of the day, news came that the Treasury secretary was looking for more like $1.25 trillion. (Former Fed governor Narayana Kocherlakota then wrote a Bloomberg Opinion column calling for that amount to be doubled, to $2.5 trillion.)

By taking yields so low, the bond market was effectively inviting the federal government to borrow like there was no tomorrow. That is what the government is now girding to do, and the huge extra supply of bonds is bound to put upward pressure on yields. 

Meanwhile, disconcertingly, it didn’t have a great effect on areas of the market that stand to benefit the most. Stocks rebounded, unsurprisingly after Monday’s fall. But high-yield bonds barely improved, while banks, which have sold off massively, did no more than rise with the market. Far more still needs to be done to alleviate the pressure on credit:

The news that the U.K., a much smaller country, would be spending 360 billion pounds (more than $435 billion, even after a dreadful run for Britain’s currency), suggests that the final number will indeed need to be big.

What next? I suspect we will follow the political template of 2008. Back then, also in a presidential election year, both candidates needed to be involved in negotiations with members of Congress deeply worried about their chance of re-election. The Troubled Asset Relief Program, or TARP, fell victim to an unholy alliance of left and the right (both of whom had principled objections to spending such an enormous sum of taxpayers’ money on the banks). When it was voted down, the stock market tanked. Then it was rammed through, with the aid of much more “pork” to bribe dubious representatives. The market carried on tanking anyway, and the money was used wholly differently from the purpose originally advertised. Yet in the final analysis, the TARP turned out to be a successful program that cost American taxpayers very little. 

Prepare for something similar this time. The center in Congress has been further hollowed out over the last decade. There are now a lot of libertarians staring down a growing group of self-described democratic socialists, who will both dislike the deal. While George W. Bush was a minor factor in 2008, Donald Trump adds an extra layer of uncertainty. And the scope of what is required will be much wider. Direct checks to individual Americans looks more likely — a huge change in policy. Helicopter money could be here at last. And who exactly should be bailed out? The airlines will be politically problematic. So will Boeing Co. There is a need to prop up the shale industry, buffeted by a blow that had almost nothing to do with the coronavirus. And nurturing the financial sector through this mess will be no easier than it was in 2008. 

Getting Congress to agree on measures to reconcile all of this, in a hurry, and President Trump to sign off on the package, is going to be challenging. We won’t see an exact repeat of 2008, but we will recognize the pattern. 

Meanwhile 2008 also provides a template for what we can expect from markets. My colleague Cameron Crise drew an analogy with the Kubler-Ross stages of grief: 

  • Denial: the classic initial refusal to believe that anything is different from the last 20 times you successfully bought a little dip
  • Anger: As losses intensify, phrases like “this market is so stupid” are heard more and more often
  • Panic: liquidity evaporates, risk premia and volatility soar, and even favorite trades are sacrificed in the name of risk reduction
  • Response: Policymakers offer up a response to solve the underlying issues, and despite initial hopes markets keep falling...BUT WITH LOWER VOLATILITY
  • Resignation: When it feels like the terrible trend will go on forever, at last a reversal is in sight [and when all hope is lost, the smallest bit of good news can make people money]

Cameron says, and I agree, that we are on the cusp between panic and response. Even if news on the virus gets worse, we are accustoming ourselves to the new reality. In 2008, the period when policy makers threw something new at the problem every week while the market fell and volatility gradually reduced lasted about five months. A successful policy response might help to speed that up, but there is the added imponderable that we don’t know how long measures to combat the virus will last. This paper by academics at Imperial College, London, has frightened many politicians with the suggestion that social distancing may need to carry on for 18 months.

So this is your base case. American politicians inflict more thrills and spills on markets, which finally begin to see a brighter fortune some time much later in the year. Let us hope we can improve on it.  

 

Meanwhile, in Europe….

Developments in the EU are even more extraordinary.  In their case, it is the sovereign debt crisis that has come back into focus, only this time the script will change. Germany implacably opposed anything like bailout bonds to rescue the banking system during the crisis early last decade. But now, Angela Merkel, still in power and attempting to navigate her third major international financial crisis, is prepared to countenance coronavirus bonds, with borrowing done in common. The euro zone has been perpetually unstable because of its bloated banking system, and because of the deep structural flaw that it has a common monetary policy but a mess of different fiscal policies.

The virus (and the U.K.’s decision to leave, which will make the process of coming up with a new policy far easier) seems finally to have wrought a vital change. But if the U.S. Congress is dysfunctional, it has nothing on the workings of the euro zone. There, as in the U.S., investors who only recently started studying epidemiology will have to brush off the political science textbooks again.
