Hey everyone, it’s Kurt. On Wednesday, I published a story with colleague Sarah Frier about how TikTok is on the hunt for a new U.S.-based CEO. When I first heard about the job, I figured it would be the kind of sexy role tech executives would be willing to swap their bitcoin fortunes for. The app is growing like crazy, and has the kind of popularity with teenagers that makes Mark Zuckerberg want to clone it relentlessly. Who wouldn’t want that gig?

But the more I thought about it, the more I realized how difficult the job will be. The most obvious issue involves potential regulation. Because TikTok is owned by Chinese internet behemoth ByteDance Inc., the U.S. government is worried the app might pose a security threat. The Committee on Foreign Investment in the U.S. is even reviewing ByteDance’s 2017 purchase of Musical.ly, the app that ultimately became TikTok. This new CEO will have to put those concerns to bed, a daunting task given the current state of international relations with China.

Perhaps worse: This person may simply be hired to be a body on the ground—living, breathing proof that TikTok does indeed have a serious presence in the U.S. In that case, this person won’t have much power.

Then when I then heard about the job’s responsibilities, I became even more skeptical. In one scenario described by a source, this new CEO would help run the company’s business and operations, most likely from Los Angeles or New York—an important position as TikTok tries to turn its massive U.S. popularity into a similarly massive advertising business. But product and engineering would still be run from China, presumably under existing TikTok CEO Alex Zhu.

Most successful tech CEOs, especially those running consumer internet or social-media companies, tightly control product development. At Snap Inc., Evan Spiegel calls the shots. At Facebook Inc., Zuckerberg decides the future. They both make mistakes, sure, but part of their success comes from the freedom to fail by taking bold swings. It’s hard to imagine that a job without these responsibilities will entice the experienced leaders the tech industry most celebrates.

Whoever takes the spot may find it difficult to succeed. Then again, with more than a billion installations and hundreds of millions of users, it might be hard to mess up, too. —Kurt Wagner
