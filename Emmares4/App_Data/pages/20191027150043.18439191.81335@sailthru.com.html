There has always been something a little crazy about Neil Young—in a good way. His band is even named Crazy Horse. As Jesse Jarnow writes this week on Backchannel, the lunacy takes the form of a purity of vision that seems to lead Young to obsessiveness in everything he has pursued in his long career as a rock star, writer, filmmaker, car mechanic, model train enthusiast, entrepreneur, and—most centrally—maker of hi-res digital artifacts. He hates MP3s and is on a decades-long quest to bring the world high-quality streaming audio.

Jarnow spent some quality time with the polymath and came back with fresh insights on everything from Mark Zuckerberg (“I am putting down Mark [Zuckerberg],” he quotes Young. “He is in over his head”) to his longtime backing band (“Crazy Horse is not a great band and does not have a great feeling all the time,” Young says. “And yet Crazy Horse is able to go places that I can’t go with anybody else. I know I can go bipolar, I can go cosmic”).

Young’s new book, To Feel the Music: A Songwriter’s Mission to Save High-Quality Audio, documents his quest to bring listeners the best sound possible. And it seems to be working: Both Apple Music and Amazon Music are newly offering hi-res streaming. “The world is gonna get it in a big way, and it’s all gonna be there,” Young tells Jarnow. “Everything from the record companies will get to be heard through streaming in a very big way, and I think it’s gonna change Earth.” Crazy, yes, but maybe crazy amazing.

Mark Robinson | Features Editor, WIRED
