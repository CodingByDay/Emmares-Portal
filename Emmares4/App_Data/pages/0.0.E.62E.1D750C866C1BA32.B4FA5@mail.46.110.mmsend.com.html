Value investing is beating growth investing. So far this year, stocks in the S&P 500 index with a value tilt have returned an aggregate 18%, compared with 9% for their growth-focused counterparts. When economic growth is scarce, investors pay dearly for fast-growing stocks. But when growth is abundant, bargain hunting for undervalued stocks tends to pay off. As the economy comes roaring back, value stocks are going to be able to generate a lot of earnings, says John Rogers, the longtime value aficionado who leads ARIEL FUND (ARGFX). “Those earnings will be much more valuable in a higher-interest-rate environment than the earnings of growth stocks that will be coming years and years in the future,” he says.

That doesn’t mean you should abandon growth stocks—in fact, you might be able to pick up bargains in some tech stocks. But it’s a good time now to explore funds with a knack for value. Besides Ariel Fund, DODGE & COX STOCK (DODGX), a member of the Kiplinger 25 list of our favorite funds, is a value stalwart. VANGUARD VALUE INDEX ETF (VTV), a diversified exchange-traded fund with a large- company value tilt, charges just 0.04% in expenses. Among value-oriented stocks, Rogers likes carpet manufacturer MOHAWK INDUSTRIES (MHK), the Ariel Fund’s second-largest holding. Another Ariel favorite is MADISON SQUARE GARDEN ENTERTAINMENT (MSGE), which owns valuable midtown New York real estate surrounding the arena.

The electric pickup truck market just got a jolt. Ford unveiled an EV version of the best-selling F-150 on May 19 (with some free publicity courtesy of President Joe Biden, who took it for a spin the day before). The F-150 Lightning, which will go into production in spring 2022, joins a few other EV pickups coming soon: The 2022 GMC Hummer EV pickup will be available this fall, the California startup Rivian will start selling the 2021 Rivian R1T in June, and Tesla is developing its Cybertruck. But the EV F-150, with a starting price of $41,669 (and federal and state tax incentives), could be a game changer for electrics. According to Motortrend.com, “on a high-volume vehicle such as the F-150, it potentially represents a seismic shift—not just for Ford but also for the industry and its overall adoption of EVs.” The Lightning is an EV Swiss army knife: It can tow up to 10,000 pounds. Its battery can be used as a backup power source, which could power a house for up to three days, according to Ford, or charge power tools on a work site. Its standard-range battery is expected to travel 230 miles per full charge, while the extended-range battery reaches 300 miles. Oh, it can also can go from zero to 60 miles per hour 4.4 seconds.
