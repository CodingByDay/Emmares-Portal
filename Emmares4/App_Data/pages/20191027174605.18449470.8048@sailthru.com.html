Good afternoon from Los Angeles, wherever you may be. I'll be up at about 4 a.m. for Spotify earnings, an early start to a big week in Hollywood; the Streaming Wars are here.

On Tuesday, AT&T will host an investor day to tout its upcoming streaming service, HBO Max. On Friday, Apple will being selling its streaming service, Apple TV+. And less than two weeks after that, Disney will take the bubble wrap off Disney+.

Streaming TV is not a new concept. Netflix and Hulu have been doing it for 12 years. YouTube has been doing it for 14 years. But after years of downplaying these services’ effect on the cable business, stodgy old media companies are finally going all-in on Internet TV, spending billions of dollars to finance new apps and new shows.

I wrote about the frantic 12 months we are now entering for Businessweek’s Year Ahead Issue, and I wanted to devote this newsletter to handicapping four new ones (Apple TV+, Peacock, HBO Max and Disney ).

While Netflix chief Reed Hastings insists there’s room for everyone. Most surveys suggest the average customer will pay for three to five streaming services. Netflix, Amazon, and Hulu are all well-entrenched and unlikely to hemorrhage customers. That leaves room for one or two more winners—and some also-rans.  -- Lucas Shaw

 
Meet the players

Disney+: The favorite.

Analysts, investors and rivals all expect Disney+ to succeed, and Verizon just gave the service a big boost by offering a year for free to its customers.

Why it will work: At $6.99 a month, it costs about half as much as Netflix and offers a library of movies and TV shows that appeals to every kid — everything from "The Avengers" and "Star Wars" to "The Simpsons" and "Toy Story." Name a parent who isn’t going to buy the service just to stop renting "Frozen" over and over.

Why it won’t: Disney screws up the technology? None of the original series is any good? It’s hard to see Disney+ failing, but it’s easy to see the service hitting a wall in three years to five years. Disney will limit the programming to family-friendly entertainment, which will also limit the prospective audience. Cable and Netflix offer something for everyone; Disney doesn’t want to do that.

Apple TV+: The funnel.

Chief Executive Officer Tim Cook has staked Apple’s future growth on subscription services, including Apple TV+. Hollywood is fascinated by the company, but nobody is quite sure what to expect.

Why it will work: Simple math. Apple is giving away a year for free to anyone who buys a new Apple device. Ap ple sells about 200 million devices annually; if even a quarter of those people keep Apple TV+ after their yearlong freebie, Apple will have one of the most-watched TV networks in the world.  That was what happened with Apple Music, which attracted more than 60 million customers in less than four years.

Why it won’t: Apple’s existing TV app, which combines shows from other apps in one place, has yet to catch on. The new paid app has no library to speak of, so Apple is asking people to pay for a handful of original series with nothing to watch in between. What happens if people don’t like the first few batches of shows? 

HBO Max: The big gamble.

No other service is spending as much on original programming, or offering as broad a selection of TV shows, movies, documentaries and specials. This is the one service going right at Netflix, but also the one with the toughest sell to consumers.

Why it will work: An unparalleled library that includes:

  • All of the HBO original hits (“Game of Thrones,” “The Sopranos," "Sex and the City") and all new HBO programs.
  • Warner Bros. TV and movie catalog (“Friends,” “The Big Bang Theory,” “Harry Potter,” DC Comics)
  • CNN documentaries and animation from Cartoon Network and Adult Swim.
  • More original series than anywhere but Netflix, from a competition series about florists to a revival of “The Boondocks” to a movie from Melissa McCarthy and her husband.

Why it won’t: It is expected to be the most expensive of these services, and needs to walk a delicate balance. It wants to use the HBO brand to convey quality, and build from a base of some 35 million customers. But it wants to convince all the people who don’t pay for HBO that they need this too. To get a sense of how expensive this will get for HBO Max, just check out this chart of Netflix's spending over the years.

Peacock: The question mark.

Comcast is the biggest cable company in the U.S. Is it ready to build a service that will help kill the bundle?

Why it will work: Like HBO Max, Peacock will offer an appealing array of library programs (“The Office,” “Frasier”) and new originals (a new “Battlestar Galactica”). Unlike HBO Max, it will be free (to existing pay-TV customers).

Why it won’t work: This is a more conservative play than Disney+ or HBO Max. Comcast isn’t ready to forsake the pay-TV business for a model that isn’t very profitable. (For all Netflix’s success, it still needs to borrow money every year.) So why is someone going to pay for Peacock over any of these other, more appealing offerings?

Apple’s Hollywood plan

Businessweek just published Mark Gurman and my look into Apple’s new streaming service. I encourage you to read the whole piece – it’s not that long – but here are some tidbits you may not have known:

Agents have been trying to convince Apple to fund original TV for years. Apple dabbled in original series with “Planet of the Apps” and “Carpool Karaoke,” and also had a plan to fund scripted series, including one with Keanu Reeves. But those efforts were half-hearted. 

Apple pursued  Imagine Entertainment , the production company led by Brian Grazer and Ron Howard. The two producers, whose work includes "A Beautiful Mind," "Friday Night Lights," and "Empire," flew to Cupertino to present Apple’s Eddy Cue with a term sheet. But Grazer and Howard eventually decided they didn’t want to be employees of a large company.

Apple started to spend real money after hiring Zack Van Amburg and Jamie Erlicht from Sony. Their first big splash for “The Morning Show,” a TV series starring Reese Witherspoon and Jennifer Aniston. Van Amburg and Erlicht bought the project in their first few weeks on the job, before they had much of a staff or plan. But they had their reputation, Apple’s check book and Hollywood’s affinity for the company.

Apple’s first couple years making TV have had all the bumps you’d expect of a giant tech company trying to make it in Hollywood. The company overspent and then dialed it back. It imposed Silicon Valley practices like non-disclosure agreements and project code names, only to scale that back a bit as well.

Apple’s March presentation for TV+ was a disaster. The company didn't provide much detail about the service, or show any footage. Many celebrities were upset that they’d been asked to attend but not told they weren’t going to appear or being mentioned on stage. After the presentation, agents and managers complained to Van Amburg and Erlicht, who run the company’s TV efforts, with complaints.

But this all may be irrelevant in the end. As discussed above, Apple has a huge funnel for potential customers. All it needs is a couple critical hits, and it will be seen as a success. Think Amazon Prime. Sure, many Prime customers don’t use the video service. They might not even know it exists. But their credit card clears, and the critics love “Fleabag.”

By the numbers

The No. 1 movie in the world is "Maleficent: Mistress of Evil." The Disney sequel is about to cross $300 million worldwide. But "Joker" is the top movie at home, and the DC Comics film just  surpassed “Deadpool” as the highest-grossing R-rated film of all time.

The No. 1 album in the U.S. is YoungBoy Never Broke Again’s “AI YoungBoy 2.” The rapper sold just 3,000 albums, generating almost all of his money from streaming. Six of the 10 best-selling albums this past week were from rappers.

Also: Lizzo’s “Truth Hurts” topped the Billboard Hot 100 for the seventh week, tying the longest reign of any female rapper ever. This song is now the subject of a legal spat over songwriting credits.

Kelly Clarkson’s new daytime talk show is a hit.  The show is averaging 1.9 million viewers per episode, fourth among all daytime talkers. She trails only "Ellen," "Live with Kelly and Ryan" and "Dr. Phil.”

 

The week that was

  1. Filmmakers Martin Scorsese, Francis Ford Coppola, Ken Loach and Fernando Meirelles all criticized Marvel movies, and Coppola went so far as to label them “despicable.” Disney CEO Bob Iger responded by acknowledging their rights to “bitch about movies,” but saying he reserves the word despicable for mass murder.
  2. Greta Gerwig’s “Little Women” jumped into the Oscar race. “ Little Women is a film with humor, heart and — particularly notable in a year of such dark Oscar contenders — a happy ending,” writes Scott Feinberg.
  3. Facebook rolled out a news section within its app, an effort to combat misinformation and improve its relationship with news organizations. The company will pay some of the participating periodicals in order to use their stories. The section will include articles from about 200 publishers, per Gerry Smith and Sarah Frier.
  4. Stephen A Smith is signing a new deal with ESPN that will pay him $10 million a year. That is more than 200 times the average salary for a reporter, but less than the pay for broadcast news hosts Matt Lauer and Savannah Guthrie.
  5. Earnings galore!
    • Twitter stock fell as much as 20 percent after the company reported sales well short of forecasts.
    • Verizon reported more new customers than analysts expected.
    • Comcast added a bunch of internet users and lost of a bunch of cable customers.
    • Snap boosted its daily active users by 13 percent to 210 million, and sales by 50 percent. But investors soured on a weak forecast.
  6. Netflix is selling a cheaper, mobile-only plan in Malaysia, which is similar to a plan the company already offers in India. The world’s largest paid online TV network is having to cut prices to sign up customers in poorer countries outside of its strongholds in the U.S., Europe and Latin America.
  7. The Players’ Tribune, a sports news website started by Derek Jeter, is on the block. The site has posted first-person accounts from athletes Kobe Bryant and Kevin Durant, but it still faces the same headwinds as every other digital publisher.
