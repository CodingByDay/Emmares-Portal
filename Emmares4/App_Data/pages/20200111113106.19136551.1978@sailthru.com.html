Welcome back

Welcome back to The Station, your hub for transportation news and analysis. I’m your host Kirsten Korosec, senior transportation reporter at TechCrunch.

I spent the past week in Las Vegas for CES 2020. Yes, a week. While CES officially started on Tuesday, like every year,  events can be held as early as Sunday. The newsletter might feel a bit abbreviated and single-minded this week, and well, that’s because I’ve been in the CES bubble.

So was the rest of the automotive and transportation industry. Yes, there were hundreds of booths — from the giant installations from companies like Hyundai, Toyota and Mercedes-Benz as well as hundreds of smaller displays showcasing companies in the smart cities, robotics, and sensors categories.

Beyond that though, I ran into the top executives, engineers and founders from companies like Aurora, Argo AI, Waymo and Zoox. Everyone was there even if they weren’t on the show floor.

CES musings

The flurry of official CES announcements had ended by Tuesday evening. Those news announcements are important, but I go to CES to talk to people.

And so I spent the rest of the week interviewing the C-suite executives, engineers and founders of companies like Aptiv, Mercedes-Benz, Mobileye and lots of smaller startups. News from those meetups will continue to trickle out over the next few days. In the meantime, here’s a few insights from my interviews and just walking the floor that I found particularly interesting and notable.

Amazon had a big visible footprint this year and much of that was focused on Alexa and integrating the voice assistant in the car. But what really struck me was how busy their booth in North Hall was. What was the attraction? Rivian. Even on the last day of the show, when the crowds had dropped off, the masses surrounded the electric vehicle company’s R1T.

Lidar seemed to be everywhere this year. These companies weren’t necessarily brand new. But their presence was far more visible this year with bigger booths and a near constant flow of demos outside the convention center. Two differences from last year: companies have expanded their focus (or at least their marketing) to include ADAS and active safety systems in passenger vehicles and some were talking a lot more about folding in perception software. The mood felt more desperate this year; everyone was jockeying for position.

Aptiv has had a big display the past few years outside of the convention center. In the past, the company’s partnership with Lyft and its self-driving cars (with a human safety operator behind the wheel) has received a lot of attention.

This year, the supplier unveiled to customers something that didn’t get a lot of attention, but should. Aptiv unveiled a smart vehicle architecture that it has been working on for nearly three years. Vehicles have become so complex, especially as connectivity as come into car, that OEMs are starting to run out of room for all the copper wire needed. Aptiv sees its SVA as a solution that automakers will eventually have to move towards. Stay tuned for my interviews with Aptiv CTO Glen De Vos and CEO Kevin Clark as well as my tour of Aptiv’s giant technical center with president Karl Iagnemma.

Mercedes-Benz got a lot of attention for its Avatar-inspired concept car. I had a chance to ask Ola Källenius, the head of Mercedes and Daimler Chairman, a few questions and he had a number of interesting comments around electrification, automation and data. Here are some nuggets.

On the decision to delay the EQC in the U.S.: “We had to make a bit of a tough choice. We had such a demand in Europe that demand far outstrips supply even though we’re ramping up and adding additional battery allowance in production.” He later said there will be “tens of thousands” sold this next year.

On data and digital ecosystem: “You’ve got to own your own operator system and that digital marketplace in your car, that needs to be Mercedes real estate.”

“The most important for us is the digital soul of the car — the electric and electronic architecture — and that has to be controlled by us.”

Micromobbin'

Lime laid off about 14% of its workforce and will cease operations in 12 markets, an effort the company says will improve its profitability. Lime is shutting down in Atlanta, Phoenix, San Diego, San Antonio, Linz, Bogotá, Buenos Aires, Montevideo, Lima, Puerto Vallarta, Rio de Janeiro and São Paulo.

Last year, both Bird and Lyft laid off employees working on micromobility. In March, Bird laid off up to 5% of its workforce and then cut up to a dozen Scoot employees in December. Lyft, similarly, also laid off up to 50 people on its bikes and scooters team in March.

I feel this trend will continue this year. Movement towards profitability is becoming more important and that could mean — in the view of some investors I spoke with at CES — a move to more scooter friendly markets like Europe. By “friendly” I mean conducive to profits.

“European cities for geographical and political reasons are much better suited for that kind of mobility,” Matthieu Lattes, a general partner at White Star Capital, told me.

That might seem like a simplification. Setting up operations in Europe doesn’t mean success. But Lattes’ point is that the culture, regulations, infrastructure and size of these cities make operations a lot easier to reach profitability.

On other news: shared electric vehicle company Revel is expanding to Oakland. The company said it will operate a fleet of 1,000 vehicles, which are currently being placed on Oakland streets, under an OakDOT permit program. The mopeds will be available to rent for licensed drivers over the age of 21 who pass an initial safe driving record check.

Oakland will be Revel’s first market on the West Coast, and Revel’s service area will cover the entire city.

Revel will offer free lessons seven days a week for Oakland residents interested in additional riding or safety instruction. Lessons will meet outside Revel’s Oakland warehouse, located at 1960 Mandela Parkway (Bay 9).
