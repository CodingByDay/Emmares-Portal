The Big Picture: Living With Coronavirus

The latest jobs report put in stark relief the real-life effect that the coronavirus pandemic has had on Americans and the economy.

Unemployment hit 14.7% Friday, the highest since the aftermath of the Great Depression. Economists think the number is actually worse – and it all happened more quickly than other downturns.

At the same time, U.S. deaths from coronavirus have now passed 76,000. There is a flattening of daily new cases, mostly because of declines in the New York-New Jersey area, but the plateau and tail look to be pretty long because other places are on the rise. Experts believe that tail will become even longer, now that a majority of states are starting to reopen. And if people aren’t careful and don’t strictly social distance, there will be spikes.

COVID-19 is now also in President Trump’s inner circle. It was revealed this week that a military aide who worked in the West Wing, as well as Katie Miller, Vice President Pence’s press secretary and wife of White House adviser Stephen Miller, both tested positive. Trump and Pence continue to be tested routinely and have both tested negative.

“This is why the whole concept of tests aren’t necessarily great,” Trump said Friday. “The tests are perfect, but something can happen.”

It’s actually the opposite. The new White House cases demonstrate why testing needs to be more widespread, not that testing doesn’t matter. Miller is being quarantined, but imagine if the White House wasn't routinely testing – the president would likely inevitably get it.

And routine testing is not even close to the case for the normal Americans, who are now – out of fear for their livelihoods – going to be venturing out of their homes as states reopen.
— Domenico Montanaro, NPR’s senior political editor/correspondent

Politics Roundup: The Coronavirus Crisis

Presidential mask attire: While the Trump administration's guidelines recommend Americans wear a face covering in public spaces to slow the spread of the coronavirus, President Trump and Vice President Pence have made appearances sans masks. Speaker of the House Nancy Pelosi has called on the president to wear a face covering in public (as she does) to set an example for the public.  

White House rejects CDC guidance: The CDC drafted detailed rules for schools, restaurants, child care centers, churches and mass transit as the country looks to reopen. But the White House coronavirus task force rejected the guidance, calling it “overly prescriptive.” An administration official said the task force would welcome a new set of recommendations, but that never happened.

Task force status: After initially saying he planned to wind down the White House coronavirus task force, President Trump has changed his mind and plans to keep the force running “indefinitely.” The shift came after the president received “calls from very respected people.” Trump now plans to add two or three new members to the team by next week. 

Trump dismisses ousted scientist: Rick Bright, a high-ranking scientist focused on vaccine development, filed a whistleblower complaint after being transferred to a “less impactful position” after he was reluctant to promote the use of certain drugs as a treatment for the coronavirus. The president dismissed the complaint, telling reporters Bright "seems like a disgruntled employee who's trying to help the Democrats win an election." On Friday, Bright’s attorneys said the Office of Special Counsel recommended he keep his post during the investigation.

More relief money proposals: With the overall unemployment rate nearing 15%, Democrats and some Republicans are considering additional ways to provide relief to Americans. A trio of Senate Democrats wants to give $2,000 per month to individuals through the end of the coronavirus health crisis. One Senate Republican has suggested covering payroll for companies who rehire workers.

ICYMI: Top Stories

DOJ drops Flynn charges: The Justice Department moved this week to drop the criminal charges against former Trump national security adviser Michael Flynn. Flynn at one point pleaded guilty to lying to the FBI about his contacts with Russia’s then-ambassador to the U.S., but the DOJ’s move to give Flynn a break brings a close to the case. 

Supreme Court streams arguments: For the first time in history, the Supreme Court offered livestreaming audio of its oral arguments in cases this week, which included a controversial birth control case. Here are the top moments of the week. Arguments in several high-profile cases resume on Monday at 10:00 a.m. ET.

Trump and black voters: President Trump's campaign was trying to earn support from African American voters by promoting the low black unemployment rate. But now, the economy is in a tailspin — and the campaign is being forced to change its approach to black voters, a group Trump has never performed well with.

Biden’s VP search: Joe Biden has the unique perspective of choosing a running mate after having served as vice president himself. And as NPR’s Asma Khalid reports, his choice will likely be the standard bearer for the Democratic Party within a few years.

New postmaster general: Louis DeJoy is a businessman who has given millions of dollars to the Republican Party and President Trump's campaign. Now, he's been named the new postmaster general, a position placing him in charge of an agency Trump has repeatedly criticized.
