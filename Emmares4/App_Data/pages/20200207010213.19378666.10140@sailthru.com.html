It's clear that the coronavirus is having a major impact on life across Asia: Millions are under quarantine, thousands have been stranded by an unprecedented travel ban, and many are confined by what's been dubbed "the world's biggest work-from-home experiment." That's all happening as the death toll and the number of cases continues to climb.

It seems fair to say that the political ramifications of the authorities' handling of the crisis will likely be felt for years to come, while intense economic disruption in China will impact supply chains around the world.

If you're one of the many stuck at home, here are some stories to keep you busy.  —Tracy Alloway

Wuhan Sacrificed

Photographer: Barcroft Media

Photographer: Barcroft Media

The human tragedy in the city of Wuhan, which has now been in lockdown for two weeks as Chinese authorities attempt to stem the spread of the coronavirus, continues to unfold. Unsurprisingly, there are moral, ethical and legal questions posed by the quarantine of some 60 million people. This story walks you through some of the thorniest.

 

Dinner for 1.4 Billion

Photographer: Getty Images

Photographer: Getty Images

There's a huge logistics challenge embedded in China's unprecedented containment efforts: How to move vital food and medical supplies around the country when transport links are effectively closed. To keep food flowing, authorities have opened up "Green Passages." Meanwhile, those stuck at home are eschewing food delivery services out of fears that delivery drivers could be spreading the deadly disease.

 

Man vs Microbe

Photographer: AFP via Getty Images

Photographer: AFP via Getty Images

While 2019-nCoV is morphing into an existential crisis for China's authorities, the sudden outbreak is clearly of concern for the entire world. Governments and health authorities are constantly battling the threat of the next big superbug. In the meantime, a top medical adviser for commercial airlines tells you how to protect yourself from viruses.

 

Cabin Fever

Photographer: AFP

Photographer: AFP

Literally. Thousands of people are stuck on two cruise ships after some onboard tested positive for the virus. Many more are now working from home, in a grand experiment that could have implications for the world economy for years to come. In Hong Kong, which recorded its first coronavirus-related death this week, outrage continues to grow over a lack of face masks and the government's overall handling of the crisis, while toilet paper is also in short supply.

 

Not-So-Cool-Runnings

Photographer: Getty Images

Photographer: Getty Images

Something weird is going on with climate change models. Last year, many of them suddenly started forecasting much hotter future temperatures than previously. The reason for the models running hot en masse isn't exactly clear. But the worrying thing, as this story notes, is that the same models have a history of successfully projecting global warming for the past 50 years.
