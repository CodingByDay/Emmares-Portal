DRIVING THE DAY

OVER THE WEEKEND, the U.S. military launched airstrikes in Iraq in response to rocket attacks that killed a U.S. contractor and wounded four American soldiers. The target: Ketaib Hezbollah, an Iraqi militia tied to Iran. Today, the group's commander is warning of a "very tough" response. Given the breadth of Iran's proxy forces in Iraq, it's a threat the U.S. is taking seriously.

The strikes didn't make the front pages of most papers this morning, but the Trump administration saw the gravity of the moment and gave a rare triple briefing on Sunday to reporters camped out at Mar-a-Lago -- featuring Secretary of State Mike Pompeo, Defense Secretary Mark Esper and Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Gen. Mark Milley.

Pompeo squarely blamed Iran for the American contractor's death, and issued a warning: "Today what we did is take a decisive response that makes clear what President Trump has said for months and months and months, which is that we will not stand for the Islamic Republic of Iran to take actions that put American men and women in jeopardy. We will always honor that commitment to take decisive actions when that takes place." (h/t the Daily Mail's David Martosko for the transcription.)

THIS INCIDENT will soon be forgotten, lost in the swirl of campaign news, impeachment jousting and White House folderol. But there are still thousands of U.S. troops, contractors and other personnel in Iraq and around the region -- and they're likely vulnerable to attacks by Iran's allies. It's not hard to imagine a scenario in which Tehran, crushed by sanctions and feeling the heat of a protest movement back home, gambles that an old-fashioned Middle East dustup might help break its isolation.

SHOULD THAT HAPPEN, not only would U.S. forces face new dangers, but the Middle East would also immediately rocket to the top of the political agenda in Washington and on the campaign trail. President DONALD TRUMP would face the choice of whether to escalate or calm the situation. He might benefit from the usual commander-in-chief's halo, unless and until things were to go badly. And Democrats would race to remind voters of what happened the last time the U.S. got into a shooting war in the desert.

WE'RE ALREADY SEEING how Iraq could hurt JOE BIDEN in the 2020 Democratic primary, with PETE BUTTIGIEG ripping the former senator and vice president for his vote to authorize the second Gulf War. But voters might decide they want an experienced pair of hands in the Oval Office, rather than someone untested on the international stage.

SAD NEWS ON CAPITOL HILL -- "Rep. John Lewis battling Stage 4 pancreatic cancer," by Rishika Dugyala and John Bresnahan: "Rep. John Lewis said on Sunday that he is suffering from Stage 4 pancreatic cancer and will undergo treatment, though he plans to keep serving his district.

"Doctors discovered the Georgia Democrat's condition in a routine December medical visit and subsequent tests. The diagnosis has been reconfirmed, Lewis said in a statement.

"'I have been in some kind of fight — for freedom, equality, basic human rights — for nearly my entire life,' he said. "I have never faced a fight quite like the one I have now."

"Lewis said that he is 'clear-eyed' about the prognosis, but that his doctors believe recent medical advances have made this type of cancer treatable. Lewis said has 'a fighting chance' and will return to Congress in the coming days to get back to work — though the Georgia Democrat said he might miss a few votes." POLITICO

MEDIAWATCH -- The New York Times issued a lengthy correction on a recent column by Bret Stephens: "An earlier version of this Bret Stephens column quoted statistics from a 2005 paper that advanced a genetic hypothesis for the basis of intelligence among Ashkenazi Jews. After publication Mr. Stephens and his editors learned that one of the paper's authors, who died in 2016, promoted racist views.

"Mr. Stephens was not endorsing the study or its authors' views, but it was a mistake to cite it uncritically. The effect was to leave an impression with many readers that Mr. Stephens was arguing that Jews are genetically superior. That was not his intent. He went on instead to argue that culture and history are crucial factors in Jewish achievements and that, as he put it, 'At its best, the West can honor the principle of racial, religious and ethnic pluralism not as a grudging accommodation to strangers but as an affirmation of its own diverse identity. In that sense, what makes Jews special is that they aren't. They are representational.' We have removed reference to the study from the column."
