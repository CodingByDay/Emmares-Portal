Hi all, it’s Eric, with a public service announcement: Think twice before you trade messages with Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman.

That’s certainly a lesson for the Davos set, given what we know now about how Saudi Arabia seemed to have infiltrated Amazon.com Inc. Chief Executive Officer Jeff Bezos’s phone.

Bezos reportedly met Prince Mohammed at a dinner in April 2018. The following month, the prince sent Bezos a WhatsApp message with a link to a video that contained a string of malicious code, according to a forensic investigation by FTI Consulting, an advisory firm. Soon after, Bezos's phone began transmitting an unusual amount of data. Later, Prince Mohammed seemed to taunt Bezos about his extramarital affair before it was public knowledge. 

There are still many things to learn about exactly how the hack went down. In the meantime, the incident also brings up questions of geopolitics, international business and cybersecurity. First off, it’s worth wondering why the U.S. remains so closely allied with someone who may have played a personal role in such a brazen hack—and one who has been accused on involvement in the murder and dismemberment of a dissident journalist who was also a U.S. resident. The same goes for the power brokers of Silicon Valley, many of whom have rubbed elbows and taken checks from the Saudi regime over the past several years. 

The Bezos affair also has me feeling particularly worried about the security of my digital data. If literally the wealthiest man in the world isn’t safe online, what does that say about the rest of us? What should we be doing about our own digital security?

In part, the Bezos hacking illustrates the unique perils of being in the top 0.00001 percent, where life is apparently not all private jets and Oscar parties. As the owner of the Washington Post and the CEO of Amazon, Bezos is a prime target. Even if he employs the top security personnel, he's likely to face particularly novel threats. After all, the people carrying out a plot that involves the direct participation of a world leader can presumably spring for the best hacking tools. Compared to Bezos, the rest of us certainly benefit from relative obscurity.

As with many victims of hacking, Bezos seems to have fallen victim not just to technical wizardry but also to social engineering. In retrospect, texting with Prince Mohammed was a big risk. It’s no secret that the Saudi government has an aggressive intelligence-gathering apparatus. At least one person who interacted with Prince Mohammed when he visited the U.S. brought a burner phone to his meeting with the prince, according to a person familiar with the matter.

But the Bezos incident isn’t just a message to elites. It’s also a reminder of how insecure we all are online. In light of the news, I’ve been locking down my own accounts, changing my passwords and making sure that I have two-factor authentication on any service that offers it. I’ve also become more paranoid. Just this week, I texted a friend to make sure she actually sent me a link on Instagram. She confirmed she had sent a video of my favorite San Francisco coffee shop. (I’d tell you which one but I don’t want to accidentally give up an answer to my password security questions.)

I’ve ordered Google’s Titan Security Key to improve my Gmail security. At work—thankfully—Bloomberg already provides a dedicated piece of hardware for two-factor authentication on my work email.

Sometimes I wonder if I really do have any great secrets for anyone to find. As a journalist, I want to protect the confidentiality of anonymous sources. Other than that, I’m not sure I’d be worth the trouble. Still, I can’t help but worry, mostly about phishing attempts. I received a strange phone call the other day that purported to be from Apple, warning me that my iCloud account had been compromised. I suspected the recorded message was itself a trap. When I reached out to Apple directly, they couldn’t really tell me anything definitively either way.

Don’t we all get strange calls from spam marketing numbers, trying to get us to settle up debts on cars that we don't own? I wonder if it will always be like this online. Will we always have to keep our guards up and question the authenticity of every link? How long will we have to worry about people making prank phone calls to trick police officers into raiding our houses?

And if you can’t feel safe swapping messages with princes you meet at dinner parties, who can you trust? —Eric Newcomer
