4-Day Workweeks Can Boost Happiness and Productivity?

From The Atlantic:

People who work a four-day week generally report that they’re healthier, happier, and less crunched for time; their employers report that they’re more efficient and more focused. These companies’ success points to a tantalizing possibility: that the conventional approach to work and productivity is fundamentally misguided.

We’re fans of work-life balance around here, and we try to make sure that our employees take vacation time. It’s extremely important so you don’t get burned out.

In order to facilitate a 4-day workweek, the company in this story reduced meetings and social events and convinced employees to get their work done more quickly. If you’ve ever tried to cram a bunch of work in at the last minute, you know just how effectively you can get work done if you’re really trying, instead of doing 15 minutes of actual work. And if an employer systematically gets rid of useless nonsense like having standing meetings for no reason you’re going to have more productive employees.

In our experience, however, the best employees are going to get 7 or 8 days’ worth of work done in a normal 5-day workweek. They will usually do things on a daily basis that you won’t get out of the lowest performers in a month, or a year… or ever. You may have heard of the legend of the 10x programmer, who gets more done than 10 other programmers combined. The trick is to make your company culture revolve around getting things done, and then hire people that fit your company culture.

It’s also worth noting that Buffer, the company in this story, has a product that isn’t all that great. Maybe they should go back to 5 days a week.
