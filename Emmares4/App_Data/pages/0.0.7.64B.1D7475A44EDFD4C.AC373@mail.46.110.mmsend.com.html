A reopening economy, government stimulus, and various shortages led to a big boost in inflation in April. Prices rose 0.8% from March, the largest one-month jump in 12 years. Shortages of new cars and trucks caused by a lack of computer chips, plus government stimulus checks, fueled a run on used vehicles, whose prices jumped 10%. That alone accounted for more than a third of April’s 4.2% increase in the Consumer Price Index. Shortages of rental vehicles pushed rates up nearly 40%. Shortages of computer chips caused computer prices to pick up by 5% and TV’s to rise 3%. Normally, their prices both would have declined. Shipping difficulties from China helped boost toy prices by 3%. Now that many vaccinations have been completed, people are starting to travel again. As a result, airfares surged 10%, hotel/motel rates jumped 9%, and car insurance rates have risen 6% over the past two months as people have started driving more.

This surge in inflation is going to create a quandary for the Federal Reserve, since one of the Fed’s goals is to fight inflation. But Chair Jerome Powell has previously indicated a commitment to keeping short-term interest rates low in order to push down the unemployment rate, and his analysts tell him that the inflation surge is temporary. So, it is likely that the Fed will stand pat, but if workers and investors start to think that the higher inflation is permanent, then they will demand higher wages and yields, perhaps creating a self-fulfilling prophecy and leaving the Fed to play catch-up.

One way to protect your portfolio from inflation: Real estate. Today’s CPI print might have been a surprise, but investors haven’t exactly been caught off-guard, given 2021’s ample cash flows into sectors that benefit from increasing consumer spending. The threat of inflation clearly isn’t over, however, nor is the need for a portfolio defense against it. Investment management firm Neuberger Berman points out two ways in which the real estate sector has historically helped people stay cool when the economy heats up. One is pure performance. “In the 12 calendar years since 1991 when inflation was less than 2%, the average return of the REITs Index was 7.4%,” say Neuberger portfolio managers, noting that the average is 11.5% if you back out the extreme year of 2008. “But the average return for the 18 calendar years when inflation was above 2% was 16.5%.” They add that over the past 30 years, REITs have outperformed the S&P 500 58% of the time during low and moderate inflation, but 71% of the time in years of high inflation. The other way REITs help beat inflation is income growth. The dividend growth of the FTSE NAREIT All Equity REITs Index of U.S. real estate securities has outpaced inflation in 19 of the past 20 years, and it has really kicked into high gear over the past decade or so. “Even if we start with the slump in 2009, REIT dividends have grown by almost 250%, whereas those nine years of inflation amounted to less than 25%,” say Neuberger managers.
