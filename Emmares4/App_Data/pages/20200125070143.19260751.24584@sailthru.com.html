Chinese provincial officials, a surprisingly independent bunch given the country’s top-down governance model, have always lived by the adage: “The mountains are high, and the emperor is far away.” Indeed, by the time the modern emperor discovered the true extent of the coronavirus now sweeping through central China, it was too late.

Already, the disease has spread beyond China to Singapore, Japan and the U.S., half a world away from the now-closed Wuhan market where wildlife delicacies like wolf cubs, bats and snakes could be had. It is here where the virus may have jumped from snakes to humans. 

President Xi Jinping faces a crucial test of his rule. He has amassed more power than any leader since Mao, so the response to the health crisis is all on him, just as he alone must ultimately take responsibility for the simultaneous crisis in Hong Kong and the related debacle in Taiwan, where Tsai Ing-wen, the head of an independence-leaning party, just won re-election as president on the strength of anti-mainland sentiment.

Xi is also chief steward of a sputtering economy. And he’s making the big calls in a relationship with the U.S. that’s headed off a cliff. As Chinese tourist destinations and even a Disney resort close their doors to the spreading virus, public pressure is bearing down on him to fix the worst public health crisis since SARS arrived in the early 1990s.

Some speculate that the initial delay in raising the alarm may have been less about oblivious local officials than their fear of doing anything without explicit instructions from Beijing. The real problem, according to this analysis, is that Xi’s personality-driven rule is breeding a generation of bureaucrats who don’t feel empowered to make decisions.

“Xi’s lead­er­ship style has ef­fec­tively in­stilled a ‘wait and see’ at­ti­tude within the bu­reau­cracy,” Jude Blanchette, a China spe­cial­ist at the Cen­ter for Strate­gic and In­ternational Stud­ies, told The Wall Street Journal.

In any event, after Xi stepped in, the bureaucracy snapped into action. We’re now witnessing the awesome capacity of the Chinese state. To take the pressure off an overwhelmed medical system in Wuhan, a swarm of backhoes immediately started clearing land for a new hospital, with the improbable goal of opening its doors to patients in six days.

At the same time, we’re watching the exercise of untrammeled authority that harks back to China’s dynastic past. The government declared a quarantine of Wuhan and its urban satellites (the word quarantine derives from the Italian “quaranta giorni,” meaning the 40 days ships were required to stand off Venice before passengers and crew could disembark during the Black Death of the 17th century).

Worse for Xi, this is all happening on the eve of the Lunar New Year, when tens of millions of Chinese travelers are on the move.

Wuhan isn’t Venice, of course. There are few parallels for sealing off a transport mega-hub of 11 million people. And when you add up all those confined in nearby cities, some 35 million Chinese—the population of Canada—are now cut off from the world.

Is this a necessary move that demonstrates the advantages of a command-and-control government? Or is it a wild overreaction by Xi (below), made possible by the absence of checks and balances, public debate and media scrutiny? It’s too early to judge the effectiveness of XI’s draconian actions. Surely, though, a free press would have alerted everybody sooner to the public health danger.

Rui Zhong and James Palmer, writing in Foreign Policy, say the poor will be the hardest hit. Panicked residents are stripping store shelves of food supplies, and beleaguered hospitals are pleading for medical supplies. (For perspective, they call Chinese measures the equivalent of shutting down Chicago two days before Thanksgiving).

All of this represents just the start of what is likely to be a months-long battle tobring the virus under control, assuming it can eventually be cured or even treated. Xi’s standing at home and abroad is at stake as China grapples with an emergency that could theoretically spawn a global pandemic. By the time this virus burns out, Xi will have further cemented his power or added to his vulnerabilities. Either way, it may shape the remainder of his rule over one fifth of humanity.
