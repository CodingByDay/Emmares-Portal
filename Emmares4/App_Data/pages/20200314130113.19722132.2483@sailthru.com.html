Hello there

Hello folks, welcome back (or hi for the first time) to The Station, a weekly newsletter dedicated to the all the ways people and packages move around this world.

It seems that everywhere you turn, COVID-19, the disease caused by coronavirus, is affecting our lives. Stock markets have been roiled, supply chains have been disrupted, public transit usage has declined, airlines have slashed capacity, events have been canceled and companies are asking employees — who can— to work from home.

In this issue of The Station, we’re going to take a closer look at how COVID-19 is affecting transportation. And it is.

On Friday afternoon, President Donald Trump declared a national state of emergency, which gave markets a last-minute boost. That might have eased concerns on Wall Street for the moment, but it hasn’t trickled down to change other public behavior.

I hit up the grocery store on Friday night to grab an ingredient or two for a stew; I was met by a crush of shoppers and empty shelves in some very specific areas. There was plenty of fresh fruit and vegetables, but canned soups, frozen pizza, disinfectant and toilet paper had been overrun.

What does toilet paper and canned food have to do with transportation? Trucks. They’re the backbone of the U.S. economy and moved more than 71% of all U.S. freight — that includes food — and generated more than $700 billion in 2017, according to the most up-to-date statistics available from the American Trucking Associations (ATA). Trucking has been affected by COVID-19 as well. The president’s emergency declaration lifted some rules around trucking, specifically exempting certain types of loads from hours of service rules.

Micromobbin'

“The micromobility industry is entering a difficult point in its history,” Ride Report CEO Williams Henderson told TechCrunch.

Indeed. And COVID-19 won’t make things an easier as people reduce their use of shared scooters and bikes over concerns about the disease.

Still, there was some non-COVID-19 news this week, including some new funding for Ride Report. The startup just closed a $10 million round led by Unusual Ventures to better facilitate relationships between cities and operators. The Portland-based company monitors micromobility vehicles across cities — currently 70 worldwide — to enable legislators to better understand the impact micromobility has on their respective cities. Henderson said he is “bullish” on the needs that citizens and residents have around more efficient modes of transportation. He also acknowledged that there are “some growing pains.”

A couple of other micromobility items caught my eye this week.

  • A study that examined the relationship between Uber’s ride-hailing network and its effect on bicycle-sharing system ridership came to an interesting conclusion. Researchers used Budapest as its focal point and found that “banning Uber caused a significant decrease in usage among frequent users especially on weekdays, suggesting a complementary relationship between these services.” Researchers also said their findings indicate that ad hoc users mainly use BSS (bicycle-sharing system) and Uber as substitutes.
  • Uber said in a blog post that it has deepened its investment in R&D to develop a new generation of scooters. Parisians will be among the first to see what the company has come up with. Uber’s JUMP revealed a new model of scooter with two mechanical drum brakes, anti-theft technology, puncture-resistant tires and a swappable battery system. The scooters will be available beginning this summer in Paris.

Ride-hailing tries to adjust

Shares of Uber and Lyft fell this week (granted, along with virtually every other stock) as the market reacted to their vulnerability to the coronavirus.

In an effort to get ahead of the spreading virus, Uber announced Wednesday that it may suspend accounts of drivers and passengers who have been exposed to or contract COVID-19. The company also has said it will work to provide drivers with disinfectants to help keep their vehicles clean.

Those efforts weren’t enough to keep shares out of the red though. Uber and Lyft are dependent on drivers and passengers to use the ride-hailing app, as well as their other shared products, like scooters and e-bikes.

Uber and Lyft will likely see usage drop off as the coronavirus spreads. Just take a look at China’s ride-hailing company Didi Chuxing. Daily active users on the app fell by more than half in the month after the government there acknowledged the outbreak, the Financial Times reported.

WFH

The spread of COVID-19 has caused some of the world’s biggest companies, including Amazon and Microsoft, to ask their employees (who can) to work from home. That directive has rippled through our economy. It has also changed traffic.

I asked Inrix to pull some data for me. I’ll be taking a deeper dive into the data next week, but wanted to share the initial findings with my readers now.

The company looked at the percentage increase in travel speeds on March 11, March 12 and March 13 versus a normal Wednesday, Thursday and Friday morning. Every major U.S. city that Inrix provided data for saw speeds increase compared to the same days last year; that means traffic was lighter. For instance, Seattle saw speeds increase by as much as 28% during the morning commute compared to the same period last year.

Public transit has also seen a drop off in activity as people opt to work from home or avoid taking buses and subways to minimize their exposure to COVID-19. The Verge looked at ridership data in major transit agencies. New York City’s subway system was down 18.65% on Wednesday, March 11th, compared to the same day last year. That’s about 948,000 fewer trips than an average weekday in January. You can read the rest of their story here.

Robotaxis and coronavirus

Robotaxis are not saturating our streets just yet. But there are a couple of examples out there that give us an idea of the challenges autonomous vehicle companies will face.

Let’s start with WeRide, an AV startup founded in 2017 and headquartered in Guangzhou, China. The company has a fleet of 20 autonomous vehicles that are used in a ride-hailing service. The company still has human safety drivers behind the wheel who can take over if needed. And that means potential exposure between driver and rider.

WeRide has resumed its robotaxi service, but it’s taking a number of measures to lessen the spread of CVOID-19. All the safety drivers have taken the nucleic acid tests and confirmed negative before returning back to work, according to WeRide. Safety drivers must wear masks throughout the day and disinfect their hands and the company has installed protective film in the vehicles to avoid cross-infection.

Here in the U.S., Waymo is trying to navigate this outbreak. The company issued a statement this week that its taking extra steps to sanitize the autonomous vehicles operating on its Waymo One ride-hailing app in Phoenix. Still, there have been hiccups. One of its safety drivers refused to pickup a rider at Intel’s campus after hearing news reports that an employee had tested positive for COVID-19. Waymo confirmed the incident.

In other AV news, which is not related to COVID-19, Uber Advanced Technologies Group resumed testing its autonomous vehicle technology on public roads in San Francisco. These public tests are starting small. The company only has a couple of vehicles on the road. It’s still a milestone for the company. Two years ago, the company scaled back its testing program following a fatal crash in Arizona that killed a pedestrian. It didn’t seem likely that Uber ATG would recover. It’s not in the clear yet, but the relaunching of public testing signals some progress.
