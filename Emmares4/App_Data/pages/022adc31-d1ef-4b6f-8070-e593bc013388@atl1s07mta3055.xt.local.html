The Production Design of 'The Queen's Gambit'

As Beth Harmon (played by Anya Taylor-Joy) in"The Queen's Gambit" comes of age in the 1960s, she learns to play chess, becoming a child prodigy who soon travels across America from Las Vegas, Mexico City, Paris, and Moscow.

Production designer Uli Hanisch winner of the Art Directors Guild Award for Television Movie or Limited Series created sets in Berlin recreating Hotels, lobbies and rooms and shares sketches from the show.

This sketch is drawn by concept artist Hannah Brückmann and shows the establishing shot of the Aztec Palace Hotel in Mexico City.

The building is purely invented and an architecturally over the top vision of a mid-century modern Mexican Hotel from this period.

It extends the Berlin interior location of the Friedrichstadtpalast and uses some references of its beautiful colored window designs.




This sketch is done by our concept artist Thomas Möhring, using the lower part of the Berlin location of the Palais am Funkturm, 
where we have shot both exterior and the interior lobby and ballroom level for the tournament at the Mariposa Hotel.

The upper part of the building and the surrounding just suggest a glimpse of Beth´s view of the strange world of Las Vegas.



With this image, concept artist Thomas Möhring has taken the street location in Berlin at Karl Marx Allee and embedded the Moscow Hotel entrance.

The buildings are all real and existing, but Möhring has added both soviet-style bas-reliefs, the giant Cyrillic Hotel letters and cleared out all contemporary Berlin background elements.

The person with the umbrella in the foreground might be himself on his way home.

Crafting Looks for 'The Mosquito Coast' 

Costume designer Justine Seymour's concept for Apple TV Plus' "The Mosquito Coast" was based on Allie Fox’s philosophy of anti-consumerism and living on discarded, yet useful items.

For Melissa Georges' Margot, Seymour wanted a well-used look and feel to her character. " I wanted all the clothing to be second-hand  to suggest that her rather soft and well-used clothing was a style choice."
 
The Fox family survives a three-day crossing of the traitorous desert into Mexico, arriving at an extraordinary Hacienda.

Seymour says, "The family is given clean clothing, presumably owed by other travelers that for some reason, no longer need them. As an homage to Gary Jones, the costume designer of 1986, 'The Mosquito Coast,' directed by Peter Weir. I took this opportunity in Episode 4, to introduce the yellow Hawaiian worn by Harrison Ford. The family is given an assortment of clothing, slightly humorous and out of character. Dina receives a Back Street Boys t-shirt, Margot is given a striped linen dress and Charlie has some old clothes from Hugo, and Allie gets the yellow Hawaiian shirt. "

As the time at the Hacienda comes to an end, a formal dinner takes place where this time the Fox's have to dress up. "Here I get to play with a dark seersucker suit, coupled with a linen Guevara shirt for Allie, a vibrant red dress for Margot, that I imagined was once worn by the Matriarch and a simple traditional Mexican sundress for Dina. While Charlie has been tempted away by the prospect of some hunting with real guns."

The Mighty VFX of 'Godzilla vs. Kong'

For “Godzilla vs. Kong,” VFX house Scanline delivered just under 400 shots across several sequences including Godzilla’s initial attack on Pensacola, Kong on the transport ship including his first confrontation with Godzilla at sea, Mecha Godzilla’s training in the arena with the skull crawler, Godzilla’s fight against Mecha Godzilla, and the three-way fight between Godzilla, Kong and Mecha Godzilla.
 
VFX supervisor Bryan Hirota says, “Coming up with the right look for Kong was critical for the film as his character was one of the primary drivers of the narrative.” 

The film takes place 50 years after we last saw Kong in “Kong: Skull Island” so he needed to be aged quite a bit. Says Hirota, “We started our work based on provided concept art and combining that with real-world references, we developed a final “old man” look for Kong who was more aged, muscular and bigger while still maintaining continuity from Skull Island.”

The biggest challenge was the size of the two creatures and creating believable interactions as they battle it out.  He says, “Creating interactions that are believable often required multiple levels of simulations as their sheer size and strength often meant they were constantly destroying the environments they came in contact with. Whether it was the ocean or Hong Kong having believable destruction and fluid simulations at this scale requires a lot of computational horsepowers.”
