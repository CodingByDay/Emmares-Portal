Talking about death doesn't have to be gloomy - but there are things to avoid
“Mummy, can we still think about things when we’re dead?” my 4-year-old asked me, apropos of nothing, as we were walking to the shops.

The question caught me off guard. It’s not like we never mention death, and even my 2-year-old enjoys a bit of doctor role play where she pretends to be the patient, lying on the floor proclaiming: “I am died.” But my son’s question was so specific, and I knew what the implication was. He’d moved from thinking about death as a bodily experience (lie down, close your eyes and try not to move!) to a question of consciousness – or lack of it.  If we can’t think when we are dead, then – to put it bluntly – we are nothing. Game over. I wasn’t sure I was ready to go there. 

I didn't know whether he’d started asking about death because it’s natural for children of his age, or whether it has something to do with the pandemic. There’s certainly been a lot more talk of death around. All I know is, I was totally unprepared. How should we be talking to young children about death without giving them anxiety, especially at a time when anxiety is already an issue? What’s appropriate? And how can we support them if they have lost someone close to them during the pandemic? 

I decided to ask some experts, and what I discovered was quite uplifting, rather than the gloomy subject I was anticipating. In many ways, this is not as sombre a subject as it sounds. It’s a bit like talking about sex – it might make you feel uncomfortable, but really it’s about preparing children for their life experiences. It’s about honesty, and getting it right can prevent an awful lot of confusion and heartache later on (or some of it, at least). 


The death of a pet can be a really good way for children to get used to talking about the subject. Photo: Michael Lingberg/Alamy
 

When do children normally start asking about death (and is it the pandemic freaking my son out)?

The age at which children tend to start asking about death depends on the family circumstances, says clinical psychologist Chantal Basson at the Pennine Care NHS Foundation Trust, UK. Neither of her parents are alive, and her daughter started asking about what happened to them when she was about 3 years old.

By four years old it is perfectly common for children to become aware of the concept of death, says Basson. However, the pandemic will have increased the amount of "death talk" we tend to have, especially if the news is on a lot, or if lockdowns have forced you to have more adult conversations in front of children than you normally would. It’s so easy to assume they aren’t listening, but “they absolutely are”, she says. “They are way more astute than we generally give them credit for.”

Is it ever too early to broach the subject? “When the opportunity arises, we should be talking about death to children as soon and as naturally as we would talk about anything else,” says John Wilson, a bereavement specialist at York St John University, UK. “The deceased pet, a dead bird in the garden or the mouse the cat brought in are all golden moments.”
 

what should I say?

Don’t try and fluff up your explanation with metaphors like “went to sleep” or “gone away”. “To say that grandma ‘fell asleep’ can terrify a child into wakeful hours,” says Wilson. Instead, use direct language: death, died, dying. “Those are the easiest words for children to understand. And whilst we can be a little bit frightened about talking about death as adults, actually using those words makes children feel safer,” says Basson. “So, if we can remain calm when we're talking about this and use the words ‘death’ and ‘died’ in a very kind and compassionate way when we're talking to children, then they will know what we're talking about.” 

Enjoying activities that remind you of the person your child is missing gives them permission to talk about their feelings . Photo: Shutterstock/Odua Images
 

Should we be worried that these kinds of conversations are going to cause anxiety, especially in light of the pandemic?

We should be more concerned about getting it right than about getting it wrong, says Wilson. “When my son Paul died aged 14 months, his big sister Emily, then aged 3, was told that the ambulance was taking him to hospital, which was true,” he says, but after that, Emily had a fear of hospitals and doctors for several years. “Looking back, we could have handled it better in being proactive in talking about the experience. We can’t protect children from the distress of broken attachments when they lose somebody close.” But we can protect them by answering the questions honestly, he says. If children don’t have the information they need, they can end up fabricating stories, which is more problematic. 
 

In what way?

Very young children have an innate egocentricity – they think the world revolves around them, so if they are trying to make sense of a death they might blame themselves. Wilson suggests the following scenario: Imagine a grandma is babysitting and she has a heart attack. If a child doesn’t understand what actually happened, they might begin to worry that it’s something they did – because they were naughty and wouldn’t go to bed when they were told, they killed grandma. During this magical stage, anything is possible as far as kids are concerned, and they fill in the blanks. 

At around the age of 6, children start to have a much more concrete world view, and take things more literally. At this age, metaphors can be especially problematic. A classic example is a child overhearing their parents saying they are going to view grandma’s body in the chapel of rest. The child gets very upset because it considers “body” to mean the trunk of a body, with no arms or legs – a particularly upsetting image.  

It’s only around adolescence that a more symbolic understanding of death is truly possible.
 

So how do we get it right?

A rule of thumb is to follow their lead. Children probably won’t ask questions they can’t handle, says Wilson. Give a bit of information, then give it some time to percolate and see if the child comes back with follow-up questions once they have processed it. In the same was as talking about sex, “answer the question as honestly and openly as you can without giving any further detail until they ask for it”, Basson says.  

It's also alright to focus on people normally dying when they are very old, or very sick, says Basson, so children don't overly worry about the death of their parents or even their own. 

What about teenagers, who already have so much going on emotionally? If they experience the death of a loved one, how can we help them?

“It’s always about honesty,” says Wilson. Holding back information might feel like we are protecting them, but it can lead to anger later. Teenagers can also try to protect other family members by concealing their own grief , says Wilson. To get around this, give everyone permission to keep the loved one in the frame, he says. “One of the big things with teenagers as well is just reminiscing.” Visit places the deceased person would have liked, for instance. It’s also important to remember that teenagers might be more comfortable talking to their friends – as long as they have someone to talk to, that’s the main thing. 

For younger kids, too, mentioning the person that has died is important – don’t worry that you’ll upset them, because chances are they are already thinking about it. You might want to encourage them to draw the person, write how we feel about them, or create a memory box, says Basson. 

Normalising their feelings is also important: for instance, telling them it’s OK to miss someone or that’s it’s OK to feel sad. “I think one of the things that sometimes we want as grown-ups is for people to grieve in a certain way that makes us comfortable,” says Basson. “And I guess just trying to move away from that expectation is really helpful, so that somebody’s grief is their own grief, and allowing them to grieve in their own way is really important.”


 

helpful resources

Winston’s Wish is a UK childhood bereavement charity, and has many resources available, including covid-specific advice such as how to tell a child that someone has died from coronavirus. There are also many great books to help children with their feelings and to get the conversation going around death. The Scottish Book Trust has put together a list of 15 books for under-5s dealing with themes of grief and loss. I also recently asked Twitter for people's personal favourites. Some other books that came up are Ida, Always by Caron Levis and Charles Santoso, The Paper Dolls, by Julia Donaldson and Rebecca Cobb, and for slightly older children, Gilbert the Great by Jane Clarke and Charles Fuge. 

Need a listening ear? UK Samaritans: 116123 (samaritans.org). Visit bit.ly/SuicideHelplines for hotlines and websites for other countries.

I feel reassured that my son’s morbid curiosity is totally appropriate for his age, and is just as likely to have been fuelled by our newfound love for the Disney film Coco (try explaining skeletons returning from the afterlife to visit their living relatives) than the pandemic.

It’s only by talking about it with the other adults in our family that I’ve discovered I’m not the only one on the receiving end of these questions, so the next step will be to make sure the other adults in our son’s life are on the same page, so he isn’t receiving mixed messages.
