Short passing
 
Passing the ball over a short distance

Though a basic skill, drilling your team on their short passing is not something that should ever be overlooked. Key to all footballing play, short passing requires intelligence, timing and vision in order to retain possession and make progress up the pitch. Often, it's the teams that are most creative, consistent and dominant with their passing that pose the biggest threat to you on match day. This is not only because this allows them to progress their attacks and play their style of football, but also because it keeps the ball out of your possession, forcing you to chase and press.

Today we have a set of sessions that will help drill your players on a range of short passing scenarios, helping to keep their short passing fresh and ready for your next match. These sessions will encourage your players to be creative and positive in their passing, helping to ensure they pose a threat to opposition teams.
Today's Sessions
 
 
Short and sharp passing
 
This session is great for improving passing quality – in technique, accuracy and weight. It also helps perfect the skill of disguising a pass.

Although the core of this session is uncomplicated, these basics are vital for players because a reliance on good passing forms the bedrock onto which every other skill is added. For that reason, I view this as one of the most important sessions that we run.
 
 
Passing patterns
 
This session works on players’ ability to play one-two passing moves in order to create space and to get in behind the opposition’s defence.

The practice is comprised of initial unopposed passing drills which familiarise players with movement, awareness and link-up play, before progressing onto a competitive 8v8 game.

The entire session lasts 75 minutes and is broken down into 20 minutes of passing patterns and a 55-minute game.
 
 
Playing short and long
 
This session examines when to play short and long. It’s a set-up that, despite its simplicity, addresses a key question that a player needs to ask himself every time he gets the ball, so is actually a mental principle that it’s vital to practise. And whilst it’s not an original question, the way we search out the answer in this session certain has the intention of making the challenge engaging and lively, with conditions that can be changed to suit the challenge or group.

We would typically use this once or twice a week in the lead-up to a main session or as part of an active recovery day.
 
 
Quick play and finishing
 
This session is about quick combination play in and around the box with the emphasis on a positive end product, be that a cross, a shot or a goal.

The session does its best to recreate a game situation, with sharp, neat touches of the ball at all times. Pressure is applied by defensive players with attackers invited to score in either goal, allowing quick play away from pressure and a constant search for goalscoring opportunities.

We can progress further into an 8v8 game (2x8min periods), in an area the size of two penalty boxes.
