The story in our genes

For over 100 years, the study of human evolution was driven almost entirely by archaeology: by digging up bones, stone tools and other artefacts. From the first Neanderthal bones found in the 19th century to the Australopithecus “Lucy” in the 1970s, it was all about excavation.

Biochemists started reading genes in the 1970s, but at first it was a laborious process. Despite rapid advances, it still took a decade to produce the first drafts of the human genome – all 3 billion letters of it. Those drafts were published in 2001. They were incomplete in various ways, but they represented a huge advance: the proverbial treasure trove of data that researchers could explore almost endlessly.

Almost immediately, some researchers began using the genome to explore human evolution. They compared our DNA to that of chimpanzees to see which bits are different. They found ways to test whether a gene had undergone natural selection: that is, if one variant had been favoured by evolution. Some claimed to have evidence that genes had been selected for within the past few thousand years, suggesting that humans are still evolving.

Some of the most intriguing studies focused on a gene called FOXP2, which was rather misleadingly dubbed the “language gene”. FOXP2 absolutely is involved with language – if it’s faulty, the result is severe speech and language problems, and mice given the human version of FOXP2 call differently – but it’s one of many such genes.

The real explosion in genetics, though, came in 2010 with the sequencing of the Neanderthal genome.
 

LOST COUSINS

Neanderthals were another species of human (well, depending on how you define a species ) that lived in Europe and western Asia for hundreds of thousands of years. They died out about 40,000 years ago, meaning we shared the planet with them for tens of thousands of years. Furthermore, they were closely related to us, with a shared ancestor that lived within the past million years – so they represent a very recent stage of hominin evolution.

A team led by Svante Pääbo at the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology in Leipzig, Germany, managed to get a draft of the Neanderthal genome from bones found in Vindija cave in Croatia. The DNA had degraded over the millennia, but the team found ways to compensate for this. Furthermore, sequencing had become much faster.

The big finding was that humans and Neanderthals interbred. Today, people with mainly Eurasian ancestry carry more Neanderthal-style genes than people with mainly sub-Saharan African ancestry. The implication is that when modern humans first ventured outside Africa, they met Neanderthals and had sex with them. At least some of the resulting babies survived and passed on their genes, so today billions of people have some Neanderthal DNA.

In the decade since, there has been an explosion of ancient DNA work. We now know that modern humans also interbred with a second group of ancient humans: the Denisovans, who lived in east Asia. Researchers have found traces of other interbreeding events in our genomes, which cannot yet be traced to specific hominin groups.

The message is simple: humans are a mishmash. We haven’t been evolving in splendid genetic isolation for hundreds of thousands of years, maintaining a “pure” lineage. Instead, our ancestors sometimes mated with members of distinct groups, including those we now label as different species. There were hints of this in the archaeology – but it took genetics to reveal it unambiguously.
 

The future of archaeogenetics

The discoveries have surely only just begun. Last month, 20 years after the first drafts of the human genome were released, researchers published a far more complete version . This one fills in the roughly 8 per cent of the genome that was still either missing, or probably wrong, in published sequences. In particular, it includes most of the sections in which the same sequence is repeated over and over.

This means we now have a much better picture of so-called segmental duplications: long stretches of DNA that have been copied, sometimes several times. The stretches are so long, they can contain entire genes.

It turns out that segmental duplications make up 7 per cent of the genome. Such duplications may have had important impacts on our evolution. For one thing, if a person has two copies of a gene instead of one, that may mean their body makes more of the protein coded for by that gene. What’s more, having two copies means that one of them can mutate freely, without causing harm – and this can lead to the evolution of new traits and abilities.

A study published in 2019 found that some people carry long stretches of DNA from either Neanderthals or Denisovans. We don’t know what these bits of DNA do, but they show signs of having been favoured by natural selection. Clearly, big duplications like these have a lot to tell us about our origins.

It’s also an open question whether early hominins were more evolvable than other groups, with a greater capacity for large-scale genetic changes like segmental duplications – but it’s a question researchers can now tackle.

Integrating genetics into the story of human evolution is going to require a touch of delicacy. Relations between geneticists and archaeologists have sometimes become strained. Depending on your point of view, either the geneticists have made some unwarranted claims that go beyond the evidence, and have misused key terminology , or the archaeologists have a serious case of get-off-my-lawn and don’t like having their established ideas challenged. My view is that there has been a bit of both, and we will learn more if the two groups cooperate – as seems to be increasingly happening.

I hope this doesn’t make me look stupid

Finally, I’ll do something I don’t often do, and make a confident prediction.

Last week, researchers published the most detailed map to date of connections within the human brain, tracing how 50,000 cells link up within one cubic millimetre of brain tissue. The data set is ridiculously large, spanning 1.4 petabytes. We have a long way to go before we can map an entire human brain, but the technology is improving at exponential speeds. The prospect of mapping an entire mouse brain, a data set 1000 times larger than the one derived from those 50,000 cells, is now within sight. I won’t be surprised if it’s done before this decade is out.

That’s not the prediction, though. The prediction is that within the next few decades, brain mapping will become as fast, cheap and commonplace as genetic sequencing is now. We will have maps of the brains of other primates, from monkeys to chimpanzees. Those data sets will be just as transformative as genetics has been – and just as disruptive to established ideas about our origins.
 

HOMININ HYPOTHESIS

On several mornings over the years, I’ve woken wondering why on earth I drink alcohol. Edward Slingerland’s new book Drunk offers the intriguing suggestion that alcohol may have been crucial to the rise of complex human societies . His contention is that when we drink in groups, we become both more tolerant of each other and more creative. So while drinking alone is just a way to poison yourself slowly, drinking in groups may be the motor of civilisation.

I’m increasingly wary of single-factor explanations for anything in biology, especially in human evolution, and that scepticism certainly extends to this. But I do think there’s likely to be a grain (thank you, I’m here all week) of truth to Slingerland’s idea.
 

ESSENTIAL READING

One of the great mysteries of human evolution is why women go through menopause. The vast majority of female animals of other species don’t experience anything of the sort. I suspect part of the reason we don’t understand this yet is that the menopause itself is under-discussed and often misunderstood. Reading Helen Thomson’s review of gynaecologist Jen Gunter’s new book The Menopause Manifesto, I was taken aback by how little I knew: for instance, that the menopause is linked to osteoporosis. Clearly, I have some reading up to do.
