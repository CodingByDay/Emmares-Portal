The Big Picture: Looking For Guidance

The death toll from the novel coronavirus surpassed 50,000 in the U.S. Friday.
 
“It could maybe go away,” Trump had said at the end of February, when there were only about 60 cases and no known deaths yet at that point from it. “We’ll see what happens. Nobody really knows."
 
Now, we know.
 
The national curve for deaths and cases has still not flattened, though the White House sees some hopeful signs in cities and states that have been hotspots. But states are starting to open back up, even those that aren’t seeing those signs.
 
Georgia, which opened up many businesses on Friday, is one of those places where cases and deaths have continued to climb. And it’s now America’s risky coronavirus reopening experiment.
 
This is all brand-new territory, and when Americans have been looking for guidance from their president, they’ve instead been getting misinformation and misleading claims, whether it’s about potential treatments or disinfectants in long and often meandering briefings.
 
That may be about to change. With the president facing criticism for seemingly thinking out loud behind the lectern at these briefings, floating untested ideas, Friday’s was cut short to just over 20 minutes. Axios reported that the president may reduce the length of them or not appear daily.
 
Fox News polls in Michigan and Pennsylvania show Trump losing to presumptive Democratic nominee Joe Biden by 8 points, and he's down 3 in Florida. It's very early, and all that can change, like it did in 2016. But advisers to the president, in and out of the White House, reportedly think these briefings are hurting his image.
— Domenico Montanaro, NPR’s senior political editor/correspondent

Politics Roundup: The Coronavirus Crisis

More relief: President Trump signed the latest coronavirus relief bill this week, a $484 billion package that includes an additional $321 billion for a small business lending program that ran out of money in less than two weeks. The bill also includes more money for hospitals and coronavirus testing efforts. Read the full bill here. 

Immigration executive order: President Trump signed an executive order temporarily suspending immigration for people seeking green cards for 60 days in response to the coronavirus. However, the order contains exceptions for permanent residents of the U.S., along with spouses and the children of U.S. citizens.

Social distancing poll: A large majority of Americans — 80% — say that strict social distancing and shelter-in-place measures are worth it to keep people safe from the coronavirus, according to a new Kaiser Family Foundation poll. The poll also found that 84% of Americans say their lives have been disrupted by the coronavirus.

Barr threatens legal action: Attorney General William Barr said the Justice Department would support legal action against states that go "too far" on imposing strict coronavirus social distancing requirements. Barr called some current stay-at-home orders "burdens on civil liberties" and said that if they continued and lawsuits were brought, the DOJ would side against the state.

ICYMI: Top Stories

"A time machine to the future"​​​​​: Every big national crisis in America, from the Civil War to the Great Recession, has brought with it a political transformation. The same may be true for the coronavirus pandemic, which could result in a new, expanded role for the federal government.

The risk of voting: Several people may have become infected with the coronavirus as a result of Wisconsin's controversial decision to move forward with in-person voting. Approximately 400,000 people voted in person after courts and GOP lawmakers rebuffed proposals to postpone the election or conduct it by mail.

"People vote when it's personal": Americans under 30, mobilized in part by strong disapproval of President Trump, are slightly more likely to vote in November, when compared with polls taken in spring 2016.

SCOTUS' election role: Chief Justice Roberts has worked hard to show that justices are not politicians in robes. But questions were raised when the conservative court majority shot down accommodations for the coronavirus that would have allowed six more days for absentee ballots to be received in Wisconsin's election. With November inching closer, how will court decisions affect who votes and when?
