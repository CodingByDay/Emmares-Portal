The world’s average temperature is rising faster than previously thought and 

may increase by as much as 5 degrees Celsius by the end of the century, according to new findings by the World Meteorological Organization. That’s almost triple the goal set by the Paris Agreement. Such a change would have vast consequences: rain forests would turn to desert, rising seas would flood the interior of continents, ice would vanish from both poles and there would be an irreversible loss of diversity among plants and animals.  —Josh Petri
 
“There is no doubt that increases in fossil fuel usage and decreases in forest cover are aggravating the potential problem of increased CO2 in the atmosphere.”

—Excerpt from a 1980 Imperial Oil internal report. The Exxon subsidiary concluded as far back as 1991 that curbing global warming would require a high carbon tax.
 
 
Top stories

A trade court handed U.S. solar developers a victory by temporarily blocking the Trump administration’s attempt to kill a loophole that’s helped the industry weather tariffs on imported panels. 

Rather than embrace cleaner energy, Russia is introducing bigger tax breaks for oil exploration and boosting coal production. For President Vladimir Putin, the best low-carbon alternative is nuclear power—provided by Russia’s state-controlled Rosatom Corp.

Fighting climate change will cost companies worldwide almost $2.5 trillion over the next 10 years, according to an estimate by Allianz SE. The German insurer went on to warn that most companies are “insufficiently prepared” for the regulatory wave coming toward them. 

Wood and water play a critical role in the making of spirits. As the climate crisis accelerates, whiskey and gin distillers are putting a green spin on their methods to attract the dollars of environmentally conscious drinkers.

A record August 2018 heat wave in her home country inspired Greta Thunberg to protest outside Sweden’s parliament, urging her leaders to live up to promises they made when they signed the Paris accord. A year later, she’s still in front of parliament every Friday—unless she’s boating around the world to give political leaders a dressing-down. Her dedication to fighting against the climate crisis has turned her into a leader of a growing grassroots movement. She is one of Bloomberg’s 50 people who shaped 2019.
 
What we’ve been reading

In the wake of ever-more-powerful, climate-fueled disasters, people often say the same thing: “We will rebuild.” It’s an attitude that’s been encouraged by a profit-hungry home-building industry, but one that some experts say should be discouraged in favor of managed retreat.

Speaking of the “R” word, officials in the Florida Keys announced they had decided against elevating a three-mile section of road to protect it from rising tides. It would cost up to $161 million to protect access to about two dozen homes. “We can’t protect every single house,” a city official explained.

Jalopnik noticed something odd on Subaru’s website.

The automaker was touting as a selling point the advantages of all-wheel-drive when it comes to coping with climate change. When confronted, the automaker pledged to remove the language, saying something was lost in translation.
