Re: Bonds

The story of bonds this year can be fully described with words starting with re. The Federal Reserve’s desire to do no harm and rehabilitate markets and the economy after the near-bear market in the fourth quarter of 2018 fostered a rally in Treasuries and flattening of the yield curve. Then acute concerns about slowing growth outside the U.S. raised recession concerns, sparking an inversion of the Treasury curve that led to the Federal Reserve starting an easing cycle. Then global data – especially in manufacturing – started to become less bad, stoking reflationary hopes that pushed sovereign bond yields higher (and reoriented winners and losers in the equity market in the process). Data lately have shown the world isn't in such a bad situation as feared, though isn't on a strong upswing either, producing a retracement of the recent selloff in core bonds.

To strategists, it’s all about the bond market being realistic.

“When price action is largely justified by sentiment surrounding opaque processes and/or technical developments, there’s a notable risk of a retrenchment once the momentum works itself out,” writes Ian Lyngen at BMO Capital Markets. “We would argue that, on net, this dynamic is what has been the guiding force for Treasuries this week as the market comes to terms with a more realistic optimism surrounding the outlook.”

The 10-year Treasury yield is poised for its ninth weekly decline of about 10 basis points in 2019, the most for any year since 2015. Bonds became bid again amid signs that a phase-one trade deal might not be as set in stone as investors had thought and some mixed economic data.

Chinese economic indicators were underwhelming; Fed Chair Jerome Powell said it was “very hard” to understand the world’s second-largest economy. One key challenge is figuring out how much of its slowdown is part of a natural maturing of its catch-up from poverty, and how much is cyclical and policy-induced.

German growth data showed the countryavoided back-to-back quarters of contraction. Europe’s economic engine avoiding recession may ultimately end up being a case of “good news is bad news” for bond bears: the ink on the release was barely dry before Finance Minister Olaf Scholz suggested that the news means Germany doesn’t need fiscal stimulus.

While there’s a lot of focus on the back end of the curve as a gauge of sentiment regarding the outlook for growth, the front-to-belly deserves some love, too.

At this juncture, there’s very little separating the three-month yield from the two-year yield from the five-year yield: that section is about as flat as a pancake. That is, there’s little sign the market is pushing the Fed to ease in the near term, and doesn’t see an acute need to ease over the medium term. And all the while, U.S. stocks are at records, managing to grind higher even as bonds rallied.

It’s hard to imagine a more ringing market endorsement for Powell’s diagnosis that the U.S. economy and monetary policy are in a “good place” than this confluence of cross-asset pricing suggesting the 2019 discord between stocks and bonds has finally been resolved.

Carry That Weight

During his two days of testimony before the Joint Economic Committee of Congress, Powell emphasized that the U.S. fiscal trajectory is unsustainable. He judges fiscal sustainability to be determined by whether debt is growing faster than GDP. If it is, then it's definitionally unsustainable.

(Japan, over in the corner, is saying, “Hold my sake.”)

It’s debatable, given Powell’s definition, if the U.S. really is on an unsustainable footing. According to the U.S. Office of Management and Budget, the ratio of debt-to-GDP has been flat for the last four years.

Powell’s comments were also accompanied with a concern about business investment in the present (because of slow global growth, demographics, and the trade war) as well as the long run, because of elevated government debt. This speaks to the potential for a “crowding out” dynamic: the idea that government borrowing both utilizes real resources and potentially pushes up interest rates in a manner that reduces private capital expenditures. However, he didn’t seem to allow for or endorse the possibility of “crowding in” – government spending supporting higher aggregate demand and business investment – to improve the current situation.

During Thursday’s testimony, Powell somewhat softened some of the fears surrounding debt that he had fanned. Any day of reckoning on the U.S. debt load “could be quite far off” – more than a decade, at least, even on the current trajectory – he said, due to the U.S. dollar’s role as the world’s reserve currency and the depth and breadth of the U.S. economy and financial markets. That means the U.S. retains fiscal space to support growth in a downturn, he added.

Bank of Canada Follows the Fed

On Thursday night, Bank of Canada Governor Stephen Poloz delivered a speech in San Francisco – on the heels of a wine tasting and having Reserved his preferred vintage for after his professional duties – that could be described as Jerome Powell’s 2018 Jackson Hole speech on steroids. He built upon Powell’s approval of Alan Greenspan’s decision to keep policy more accommodative than otherwise because of the emergence of technology-driven curbs on inflation, by deeming the Fourth Industrial Revolution warranting a similar thought process.

The speech text wasn't immediately available but the paper that informed the remarks is available here.

The speech and Q&A were structurally dovish but tactically and practically more neutral. On the one hand, Poloz acknowledged that Greenspan’s decisions in the 1990s to limit policy tightening “really did let things grow” as “creative destruction was able to dominate.” On the other hand, he indicated that because Canada's inflation is on target, the BOC has the opportunity to take a more symmetric approach in dealing with risks to the outlook than some peers that have been lowering interest rates. 

The BOC's Governing Council at its October meeting managed to push back against the idea that keeping rates on hold for a prolonged period would result in passive, de facto tightening for the real economy. Such de facto tightening could in theory happen as homeowners face higher mortgage payments at the end of the typical Canadian five-year fixed rate loan. 

David Doyle at Macquarie Capital Markets, who has often warned about the dangers of rate resets, largely agrees with the central bank – to a point.

“In its October Monetary Policy Report, the BOC indicated micro level data from chartered Canadian banks suggests the headwind is not nearly as severe at just ~10 basis points (using August mortgage rates),” he writes. “Our analysis of the underlying data supports the BOC’s conclusion with the difference from the estimate based on the five-year Canada yield resulting from spread compression since 2015.”

However, he cautions that this is a function of a slim gap between mortgage rates and the five-year sovereign yield, which could revert – especially if the central bank doesn’t deliver on the 27 basis points of easing priced in over the next year. As such, these spreads and market-implied odds warrant close monitoring for those trying to assess how Canadian activity will unfold in the coming quarters. These are variables with a wide range of potential outcomes and implications.
