The Big Picture: Mixed Messages

The White House had a rough day Friday.

President Biden and his team sowed confusion about what exactly his policy is on admitting refugees to the United States — and it’s still unclear what it will eventually be.

Biden signed an emergency declaration on Friday, shifting the allotment of slots for countries from which the U.S. will accept refugees. Refugees from Somalia, Syria and Yemen, for example, will once again be allowed after former President Donald Trump barred them.

The administration also said, however, it would not in this fiscal year be raising the refugee cap set by Trump — 15,000, the lowest number in U.S. history.

That drew a massive backlash from fellow Democrats, many of whom called the decision “unacceptable.” By the end of the day, the White House issued another change. It said the cap would not get to a previous goal of 62,500 by October, the end of the fiscal year, because of a “decimated refugee admissions program we inherited,” but that it would be higher than 15,000.

How high? They don’t know, but “expect” to have an answer within a month. 

It was a day of whiplash and an unforced error from a White House that had been rolling out policy in a fairly smooth and methodical fashion to this point.

So what’s going on? This whole issue is tied up with immigration, which has been a sore spot for Biden. While refugees are handled separately than traditional immigration, White House press secretary Jen Psaki admitted that the surge at the southern U.S. border is a “factor” in not raising the cap. She said there’s overlap between personnel needed to work on refugees and immigration. 

While Biden is receiving high marks for the overall job he’s doing and for his handling of the coronavirus and the economy, an NPR/PBS NewsHour/Marist poll last month found just 34% of U.S. adults approved of how he’s handling immigration. The administration is scrambling to respond, asking for 1,000 government workers to “volunteer” to relocate to the border for months-long stints to work with unaccompanied children. 

While running for president, Biden had promised to raise the yearly refugee cap to 125,000, and he even signed an executive order shortly after he took office, reflecting that. Notably, the policy was found to be the least-popular of more than two dozen executive actions tested in a February survey, with more Americans opposed to it than supporting it.
— Domenico Montanaro, NPR’s senior political editor/correspondent

ICYMI: Top Stories

U.S. troops leaving Afghanistan: President Biden announced this week that all U.S. troops would leave Afghanistan without conditions by Sept. 11, 2021 — the 20th anniversary of the 9/11 terrorist attacks. Biden said it would not be a "hasty rush to the exit,” adding the U.S. will leave Afghanistan "responsibly, deliberately and safely.”

Infrastructure poll: While 56% of American adults support President Biden's infrastructure plan, including 9 in 10 Democrats and half of independents, Republicans overwhelmingly do not, according to a new NPR/PBS NewsHour/Marist poll. Republicans also disagree with how Biden wants to pay for the plan, with a majority opposing both raising taxes on those who make more than $400,000 a year and taxing overseas profits at the same rate of U.S. earnings.

Insurrection latest: The U.S. Capitol Police's internal watchdog found that some advance intelligence offered a "more alarming" warning ahead of the Jan. 6 insurrection and said leadership decisions hindered officers on the ground during the riot. The report also said Capitol Police did not place enough emphasis on threat assessments.

Capitol Police officer honored: U.S. Capitol Police Officer William "Billy" Evans this week lay in honor at the Capitol, where he served for 18 years and died during an attack at the Capitol complex earlier this month. President Biden, House Speaker Nancy Pelosi and Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer all delivered remarks at the ceremony honoring Evans. 

Anti-Asian hate crimes: Congressional Democrats called on their Republican colleagues this week to show public support for a bill to address the rising number of violence and hate crimes aimed at Asian Americans. And after weeks of pressure, the Biden administration appointed Erika Moritsugu as a White House senior liaison to Asian Americans. 

Vaccine conspiracies: After federal health officials announced a pause on Johnson & Johnson's coronavirus vaccine, the most popular link post on Facebook about the move was shared by a conspiracy theorist who thinks the pandemic is a cover for government control. The post was just one in a deluge of conspiracies and misinformation that flooded social media in the days following the move.

Officer charged with Wright killing: Kim Potter, the former Brooklyn Center, Minn., police officer who fatally shot Daunte Wright during a traffic stop, was charged with second-degree manslaughter this week. President Biden called Wright's death "tragic" but said of the protests that followed, “There is absolutely no justification — none — for looting. No justification for violence."

Going Deeper

GOP working class: Since former President Donald Trump garnered support from working-class voters during the 2020 election, the Republican Party has sought to rebrand as the party of that voting bloc. The working class, which is hard to define in the U.S., is also being targeted by Democrats who hold that Biden’s economic plan is targeted specifically toward uplifting those Americans.

Reparations bill markup: H.R. 40 has passed through the House Judiciary Committee after decades of waiting. The bill, which was originally introduced in 1989, seeks to convene a panel of 13 experts to determine the legacy of slavery and “appropriate remedies,” but it has been rejected by leading Republicans. 

Infrastructure as a gender issue: The debate in Washington over what does and doesn’t qualify as infrastructure has expanded to include gender issues. In the past, infrastructure has traditionally been about men's jobs — with 9 in 10 infrastructure jobs being held by men. Biden’s infrastructure plan also swings in favor of men but is expected to be evened out by the upcoming Family Plan.
— Claire Oby, NPR Politics intern
