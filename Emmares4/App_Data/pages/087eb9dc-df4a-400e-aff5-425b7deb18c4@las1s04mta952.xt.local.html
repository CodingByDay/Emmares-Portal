Russia

REDACTIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS — The Senate Intelligence Committee's bipartisan report on Moscow's influence effort to aid the Trump campaign in 2016 mostly confirmed what previous investigations already detailed — how Kremlin operatives and troll farms linked to Russian President Vladimir Putin executed a vast campaign to disparage Hillary Clinton and the U.S. democratic process along the way. But there were a few new details, key takeaways and context within the report — the second in the intel panel's series of investigations into Russia's election meddling in 2016. Here's what stood out to us and others:

— The committee wants the executive branch to warn the public about the dangers of foreign interference in the 2020 election. The timing of that recommendation is interesting to say the least given the impeachment inquiry surrounding President Donald Trump's request that Ukraine investigate 2020 contender Joe Biden. Additionally, Trump has regularly dismissed the notion that Russia carried out the interference campaign to bolster his candidacy, suggesting instead that it was Ukraine that sought to meddle in the election.

— It's clear from the report, however, that committee Republicans don't share the president's misgivings. All members of the Intel committee signed the document. After its release, Sen. Richard Burr (R-N.C.) tweeted : "Russia is waging an information warfare campaign against the U.S. that didn't start and didn't end with the 2016 election. Their goal is to sow societal discord and erode confidence in the machinery of government."

— The committee also called for greater information sharing between social media companies and for the executive branch to set up a task force to monitor foreign interference via social and develop a framework to counter it.

— There were redactions galore. Johns Hopkins University professor and cybersecurity expert Thomas Rid pointed out on Twitter that seven pages about the Russia's GRU intelligence unit were blacked out.

— Rid also highlighted one new anecdote about the Internet Research Agency, the troll farm believed to have carried out much of the disinformation effort: "One communication obtained by the Committee details an IRA employee's description of Election Day 2016, from the vantage of an information warfare operative: 'On November 9, 2016, a sleepless night was ahead of us. And when around 8 a.m. the most important result of our work arrived, we uncorked a tiny bottle of champagne ... took one gulp each and looked into each other's eyes .... We uttered almost in unison: 'We made America great.'"

— The report spends a lengthy 20 pages breaking down the IRA's activity by platform, illustrating just how far it extended beyond Facebook, Google's YouTube and Twitter, which have faced the brunt of public and political scrutiny for serving as vehicles for Russian meddling. "IRA activity and engagement with Americans through Instagram accounts dramatically eclipsed the comparable interaction achieved through Facebook pages." Instagram meddling by the IRA began as early as 2015, according to the report; the agency accumulated 3.3 million followers and 187 million engagements on the platform.

Surveillance

NEW 702 CONTROVERSY — The FBI improperly searched an NSA database for information about Americans, the U.S. intelligence community revealed on Tuesday. A newly declassified opinion by the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court — made available by ODNI and DOJ — found that the agency's so-called backdoor searches for information of Americans hoovered up by warrantless surveillance efforts may have broken the very law authorizing the program, in addition to violating Fourth Amendment protections against unreasonable searches.

The revelations are sure to renew concerns about the powerful spying program allowed under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, which intercepts the digital traffic of foreign targets but also incidentally pulls in personal information on an unknown number of Americans. Last year, Congress voted to extend the life of the tools for several additional years.

The court's opinion "highlights Congress' constitutional obligation to act independently and strengthen the checks and balances on government surveillance," Sen. Ron Wyden (D-Ore.), a leading privacy hawk and a member of the Senate Intelligence Committee, said in a statement. Additionally, he said, it underscores the "need for the government to seek a warrant before searching through mountains of private data on Americans." Edward Snowden also felt the need to weigh in on the disclosures.
 

 

THE CAVALRY HAS ARRIVED — FireEye will start providing special cybersecurity services to think tanks, NGOs, news organizations and certain nonprofit groups, the firm said Tuesday. "It is very common for the most technically-sophisticated and well-organized actors to prey on individuals and organizations who struggle to mount an effective defense," John Hultquist, director of intelligence analysis at FireEye, wrote in a blog post published during the group's Cyber Defense Summit in Washington. "The consequences of this disparity could not be more serious." The FireEye Outreach program is the latest example of a tech or security company offering free or low-cost help to organizations that face a disproportionate share of cyberattacks due to their work in the public interest.

It's unclear how exactly FireEye will structure the program or how it will tailor its assistance to these customers. Hultquist wrote that the outreach effort would provide "support through training, intelligence, and technology."
Hacked

WATCH WHAT YOU CLICK — As companies embrace the cloud, hackers are increasingly deploying phishing campaigns and trying new tactics that attempt to dupe the growing number of people using those services, especially Microsoft Azure, FireEye also noted on Tuesday during its summit. Some tactics the company detected include hackers hosting phishing pages on Azure or planting malicious URLs on documents hosted on cloud file-sharing services. And when it comes to overall email threats, Microsoft continues to be the most popular company for cyber crooks to impersonate.

GOTCHA — And if you needed one more reason to hate email: Business Email Compromise attacks jumped 269 percent compared to the previous quarter, according to a Mimecast report that analyzed nearly 500,000 email users and 260 million messages. In total, the firm discovered more than 29 million pieces of spam, about 29,000 malware attachments, about 29,000 dangerous file types and more than 60,000 impersonation attacks.
