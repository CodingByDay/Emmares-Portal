What's harder, making new drugs, or pricing them?

Biogen might say it's the former. After scrapping its aducanumab therapy for Alzheimer's in March, the company reversed course this week and said it will seek approval on the back of some new, more encouraging trial data. A number of drugmakers have been burned in seeking a treatment for the memory-robbing disease, and Biogen was sheared of about a quarter of its market value earlier this year when it looked as though it might join their ranks. The clouds that gathered around Biogen have parted, for now.  

Also tough: figuring out how much to charge for a promising new therapy. Amgen, which currently sells the innovative cholesterol treatment Repatha at two different prices, plans to pull the $14,000 version off the market and only offer it for $5,850 a year beginning in 2020. The costlier iteration had struggled to gain traction because patients didn't want to shoulder high co-pays. And the two-price structure faltered because prescription plans didn't like the rebates they got from the discounted one. Amgen thinks one low price will boost sales and help Repatha realize its unmet potential. 

Vertex is hoping it has found a sweet spot. This week the biotech company celebrated for developing several groundbreaking drugs got FDA approval—five months early—for a three-drug combo for cystic fibrosis. The news means nearly every patient who suffers from the deadly disease now will have a treatment option. But at $311,503 a year, about $24,000 a month, many will need all the help they can get to pay for it. —Tim Annett

Here's what else we're watching:

Opioid brawl. About $50 billion in settlement offers have sparked a fight between states and local governments over how much the pharmaceutical industry should pay for its role in creating the U.S. opioid epidemic.

Powder problems. Johnson & Johnson recalled one lot of baby powder after tiny amounts of asbestos contamination were found in samples from a single bottle. The company is battling lawsuits over cancer claims.

Vaping crackdown. The FDA said a New Jersey company used illegal marketing tactics to push its products. Eonsmoke's wares, which the company says work with Juul, were touted by social-media influencers.

Cancer confrontation. Merck came up with the short end this week in its ongoing immunotherapy face-off with Bristol-Myers, after strong data on the latter's Opdivo raised a threat to Keytruda's lung-cancer dominance.
