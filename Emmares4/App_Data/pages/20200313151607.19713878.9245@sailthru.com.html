The question is no longer which industrial companies will suffer from the coronavirus fallout; they all will.

The markets whipsawed this week as the pandemic continued to spread, forcing governments around the Western world to transition from containment efforts to draconian attempts at mitigating the worst possible public health impact. The result was a U.S. travel ban on foreign nationals from 26 European countries; a moratorium on college and professional sporting events; closed schools and museums around Europe; a ban on bars and restaurants in Italy; and a ban on large gatherings in New York and Los Angeles, among many other things. The worst day for the stock market since Black Monday 1987 finally pushed governments into action, with Germany pledging to spend whatever is needed to help its affected workers and companies weather a sustained economic standstill and the European Commission vowing to relax fiscal and state-aid rules to give countries leeway for stimulus measures. In the U.S., the Trump administration and Congress were still haggling over a package to respond to this crisis as of midday Friday, but did appear to be working toward a deal.Separately, the administration is working on financial assistance for airlines.

Even with the wheelbarrows of money now on offer, a V-shaped recovery looks unlikely. While there are encouraging signs of a return to life in China, many factories are still operating at reduced capacity, according to the New York Times. Nearly 75% of U.S. companies have experienced supply-chain disruptions because of virus-related shutdowns, according to an Institute for Supply Managment survey. It’s unclear if a massive wave of financial stimulus will convince virus-stricken consumers and businesses to spend again, particularly amid elevated levels of household and corporate debt. It’s telling that even with the stimulus measures providing a modicum of light at the end of the tunnel, literally the only stock still in the green on the S&P 500 Industrials Index this year is bed-bug and cockroach-killer Rollins Inc. People are still going to want their homes and restaurants to be bug-free, but how long until they’re willing to take a flight across the ocean again? Or maybe a better question is, how long will it be before governments will let them? Other questions that come to mind: When do companies feel comfortable buying factory-automation equipment again? Or mammography machines for that matter, with most health-care dollars likely to go toward treatments for the coronavirus for the foreseeable future?

No one knows the answers, but it seems likely that things will continue to get worse before they get better. Assuming that's the case, it feels prudent to focus on those companies in the manufacturing world that will have the hardest time in the difficult period to come. The key characteristic is going to be debt, whether accumulated through ill-advised share repurchases or acquisitions. A few companies come to mind.

It makes sense to start this conversation with Boeing Co., a company for whom all of its speculative decisions are coming home to roost. Air Canada is cutting 18% of its order for Boeing’s 737 Max and more cancellations will likely follow as the global grounding of the jet reaches a year. That leaves Boeing perilously dependent on cash flow from wide-body models that are bound to fall further out of favor with the reduction in overseas travel demand. The company spooked investors by shifting into cash-preservation mode this week. It’s limiting discretionary spending and overtime pay and freezing hiring, with Reuters reporting that layoffs or furloughs are also a possibility; at the same time, Boeing plans to fully draw down a $13.8 billion loan as a precaution amid the coronavirus turmoil. JPMorgan Chase & Co. analyst Seth Seifman said a dividend cut is possible, noting that bringing the annual payout to less than $5 a share would free up $2 billion in cash. The cost to insure the company’s debt against default for five years widened to as much as 400 basis points this week, the highest ever, according to ICE Data Services.

It sure would be nice to have some of the more than $40 billion Boeing spent on share buybacks since deciding to go ahead with the Max in 2011. And how’s that 2017 decision to fund the pension with $3.5 billion of its own stock working out? A portion of Boeing’s pension may be recoverable given its defense work for the U.S. government, but even factoring in that adjustment based on ratings firm standards, the unfunded balance was about $13 billion at the end of 2019, according to Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Matthew Geudtner. That’s higher than any company on the S&P 500 Index apart from General Electric Co. and Exxon Mobil Corp.(assuming similar adjustments for Lockheed Martin). And every 25-basis-point decrease to the 3.3% discount rate assumption at the end of 2019 adds about $2.8 billion to that obligation. GE, to its credit, has been more proactive on its pension, with a plan to contribute $4 billion to $5 billion after it closes the sale of its biopharma business to Danaher Corp. CEO Larry Culp has been racing against a recession clock since he started the job in late 2018 and has put the business in a better position to weather the blow, but the company’s pension and insurance liabilities are subject to the whims of the markets to a large degree and may require additional funding. 

While Boeing may be the most extreme example of blind optimism, it was hardly the only one. I also think about Parker-Hannifin Corp.’s acquisition spree. The company spent $1.7 billion last year to buy Exotic Metals Forming Co., which makes high-temperature engine components and exhaust-management systems for aircraft including Boeing’s Max. It also paid $3.675 billion to buy Lord Corp., which makes aerospace parts as well as adhesives and coatings. The idea was to make the company less vulnerable to economic swings. But both deals were expensive, with valuations based on arguably over-optimistic estimates of cost savings, and amounted to debt-fueled bets on an aerospace sector that was already looking like it was due for a breather. Parker-Hannifin is now in the upper quartile for indebtedness among S&P 500 industrial companies. 

There’s much that can be learned from the counterexample of Honeywell International Inc., which faced calls from analysts to fill the revenue void left by two 2018 spinoffs with a sizable acquisition. CEO Darius Adamczyk didn’t take the bait, instead spending a measly 425 euros ($492 million) on warehouse-automation company Transnorm that year and almost $9 billion on share repurchases in the past two. The company ended the year with more than $10 billion in cash. Yes, it’s heavily exposed to the aerospace sector as well as the oil and gas sector through its UOP and process-automation businesses. But it has ample firepower to buy back a load of stock or take advantage of low valuations to make a significant acquisition, and that’s exactly where you want to be as an industrial company right now.

HOW BAD CAN IT GET FOR AIRLINES?
Airlines absorbed the brunt of the pain from the sell-off this week, with very good reason. United Airlines Holdings Inc. President Scott Kirby laid out a worst-case scenario at the beginning of the week in which revenue drops by 70% in April and May and is still down 20% come November and December. It’s mind-blowing to think about, but with each passing day that kind of scenario looks less and less extreme. Breathtaking capacity cuts announced by U.S. airlines on Tuesday were stale by the following day when the Trump administration announced its EU travel restrictions. American Airlines Group Inc. said late Thursday it’s cutting its international capacity by 34% this summer from its original plan, including a 50% reduction in transatlantic flights for the month of April. That compares with guidance earlier this week for what now seems like a modest 10% reduction in international capacity. The earliest the now-shuttered routes to European destinations such as Frankfurt, Munich and Paris will come back online is May 7. Separately on Thursday, American said one of its pilots had tested positive for the coronavirus. “However bad you thought it was, it’s worse,” Cowen & Co. analyst Helane Becker wrote in a note on Thursday. The U.K., which is currently exempt from the U.S. travel restrictions, doesn’t expect the peak of the coronavirus outbreak in that country for at least another 10 weeks. President Donald Trump said Thursday he may extend travel restrictions to parts of the U.S., describing New Rochelle, New York, as a “hot spot.”

It should be some comfort to investors that airlines are thinking about this in dire terms and proactively reining in capital expenditures and share repurchases. Some European airlines are teetering on the edge; highly indebted Norwegian Air Shuttle, for one, parked 40% of its long-haul fleet through May, canceled 25% of short-haul flights and laid off 50% of its staff. But leverage levels have come down remarkably over the past decade at the biggest U.S. carriers, with the one glaring exception being American Airlines. In October, American had projected robust free cash flow in 2020 after years of heavy investment in new planes. “We do believe the time has arrived that American can more succinctly shine a light on approaching improvements – assuming the wheels don’t come off the economy,” JPMorgan Chase & Co. analyst Jamie Baker said in a note at the time. Well, here we are. The good news is that American doesn’t have a significant debt maturity until 2022 and it has $10 billion of unencumbered assets against which it can take out loans. American for now prefers to accelerate the retirement of older Boeing 757s and 767s rather than defer new deliveries, but other carriers may not feel the same way, especially with insanely low oil prices eroding some of the impetus to switch to more fuel-efficient models.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
United Parcel Service Inc. said it would hire the first female CEO in its history. Might have been nice if the company gave this occasion its own moment, rather than, you know, announcing it in the middle of a global crisis, but progress I suppose. Former Home Depot Inc. CFO Carol Tome will replace David Abney, effective June 1, and Abney will remain executive chairman through September. At 63, it’s not clear how long Tome will be in the top role, but this is part of a strategic effort on UPS’s part to bring in more executives from the retail world to help it cope with the surge in e-commerce volume on its network. This is in sharp contrast to rival FedEx Corp., which is still run by founder Fred Smith and which this week named a 15-year veteran as its next CFO. 

Bombardier Inc.,  in a surprise announcement, said it is replacing CEO Alain Bellemare with Eric Martel, head of Canada’s biggest electric utility, Hydro-Quebec, effective April 6. Bellemare oversaw the great unraveling of Bombardier that culminated earlier this year with deals to sell the company’s transportation business to Alstom SA and cede control of its A220 commercial jet program to Airbus SE. It’s unusual to turn the page on a CEO so soon after he signed a major transaction in the Alstom divestiture that could be contentious from a regulatory perspective. Martel was previously the head of Bombardier’s business jet division and he will need to draw on that expertise, seeing as that’s all the company makes now.
