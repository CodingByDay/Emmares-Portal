Surveillance

FISA FUMBLE — The House was expected to vote today to reauthorize a handful of domestic surveillance powers that lapsed in March — but Minority Leader Kevin McCarthy asked Democratic leaders late Tuesday night to pull the bill after the president said Republicans should vote “no.” “I hope all Republican House Members vote NO on FISA until such time as our Country is able to determine how and why the greatest political, criminal, and subversive scandal in USA history took place!” President Donald Trump tweeted on Tuesday. Attorney General William Barr and Trump’s top conservative allies helped craft the bill, H.R. 6172 , to extend until December 2023 a set of surveillance authorities that are based in part on the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act.

Speaker Nancy Pelosi and House Majority Leader Steny Hoyer did not announce this morning by press time whether they'd hold off on putting the bill on the floor. The vote was set to be one of the first tests of the new proxy voting system.

Lawmakers were also expected to vote on a bipartisan amendment from Reps. Zoe Lofgren (D-Calif.) and Warren Davidson (R-Ohio) that would bar the collection of Americans’ internet search history and web browsing data without a warrant — an idea that closely mirrors one that came one vote shy of being adopted when the Senate considered the bill. “Protecting Americans’ internet browser searches from warrantless surveillance is a modest, though important first step,” said Davidson, adding he would support the entire renewal bill if the amendment is adopted. But ACLU Senior Legislative Counsel Neema Singh Guliani called the amendment "far weaker than the original" and said "its sponsors should fix its massive deficiencies.”

Trump has often railed against FISA. He alleges the FBI abused the FISA process during its investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 election, claiming without evidence that the Obama administration was involved in an illegal effort to “spy” on his campaign.

THE PANDEMIC AND CLOUD THREATS — As adoption of cloud services has increased during the pandemic, so have cloud threats, McAfee said in a report out today. Threats from outside attackers grew 630 percent, compared to use of cloud collaboration tools increasing by 600 percent, McAfee found. Notably, insider threats saw no noticeable change, suggesting to the company that working from home hasn’t affected employee loyalty.

“While we are seeing a tremendous amount of courage and global goodwill to overcome the COVID-19 pandemic, we also are unfortunately seeing an increase in bad actors looking to exploit the sudden uptick in cloud adoption created by an increase in working from home,” said Rajiv Gupta, senior vice president for cloud security at McAfee. “The risk of threat actors targeting the cloud far outweighs the risk brought on by changes in employee behavior.”

WFH WTF — More than eight in 10 organizations consider it likely that they will continue support for working from home, but most aren't equipped to do it in a secure way, Bitglass found from a survey out today. Among the IT professionals polled, 41 percent said they hadn't taken steps to expand secure access for remote work, with half citing the lack of proper equipment. Sixty-five percent said they allow access from personal, unmanaged devices, although 55 percent said they consider it a significant security risk. Malware was the most worrisome threat, with 72 percent citing it as their leading concern.

A CALL TO STOP THE HACKS — From our friends at POLITICO Europe’s Cyber Insights: A group of eminent lawmakers, academics and executives from around the world on Tuesday launched a call to stop cyberattacks on health care institutions. The call from the CyberPeace Institute said “governments should work together, including at the United Nations, to reaffirm and recommit to international rules that prohibit” cyberattacks on hospitals, health care and medical research facilities.

“For now and for the future, governments should assert in unequivocal terms: cyber operations against health care facilities are unlawful and unacceptable,” the call stated. Signatories include former U.N. chief Ban Ki-moon, former U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright, and seven Nobel Peace Prize laureates.

APP PROBLEMS — Qatar’s Covid-19 contact tracing app had security vulnerabilities that exposed the sensitive personal information of more than a million people, Amnesty International said Tuesday following an investigation. After Amnesty International alerted Qatar to the issue, authorities fixed it within a day.

“While the Qatari authorities were quick to fix this issue, it was a huge security weakness and a fundamental flaw in Qatar’s contact tracing app that malicious attackers could have easily exploited,” said Claudio Guarnieri, head of Amnesty International’s Security Lab. Qatar’s contact tracing app isn’t the only one to encounter security woes.

BRINGING UP THE REAR — The Senate Armed Services Committee announced that it plans to mark up the fiscal 2021 defense authorization bill in the second week of June. The Cybersecurity Subcommittee is up last, on June 9. Members of the Cyberspace Solarium Commission have targeted the bill as a landing place for many of their ideas.

FINISH THEM — Authorities arrested a Ukrainian national that they allege is a member of the notorious Fin7 hacking gang, Motherboard reported on Tuesday based on court records unsealed last week. Denys Iarmak was extradited from Thailand and charged with 26 hacking-related charges. The group's victims include Trump Hotels, Whole Foods and Chipotle, and could have gains on hand estimated at $1 billion. The arrest follows three more arrests of people accused of Fin7 affiliation in 2018.
