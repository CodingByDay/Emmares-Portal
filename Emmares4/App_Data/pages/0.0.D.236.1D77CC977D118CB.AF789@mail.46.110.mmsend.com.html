One big worry weighing on stocks: COVID-19…again. American investors were treated to a dour open Monday, with the major indexes down well over 1% at the open -- and they weren’t alone. Global markets also sagged this morning, and one of the biggest reasons is a worldwide resurgence in COVID fears. BMO Capital Markets Economist Jennifer Lee provides a laundry list of concerns hovering over the equity markets today. In Australia, the state of Victoria’s lockdown, which was scheduled to end tomorrow, has been extended. The U.K., which today celebrates “Freedom Day” – a dropping of all COVID restrictions – has seen a jump in cases over the weekend, while a new viral variant has prompted the U.K. to force French tourists to quarantine themselves upon arrival. Meanwhile, the Olympics are slated to begin on Friday, and Japan’s Olympic Village already has two reported COVID cases, threatening the Games. And don’t forget, the U.S. has its own issues: Last Friday, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention said the seven-day average for COVID-19 infections had risen 70% in a week, with hospitalizations up 36% in that time, amid outbreaks in unvaccinated areas of the U.S. as the more contagious Delta variant becomes the dominant strain here.

As the markets fall today because of fresh fears about rising infection rates, it’s important to remember that infection surges always end at some point. COVID variants are frequently more transmissible than the original virus, which accounts for the surge, but they are mostly transmissible to the unvaccinated. As both vaccination rates and those with antibodies from a previous infection continue to climb, this leaves fewer easy targets for subsequent COVID variants. Eventually, surges are likely to stop altogether, even if COVID itself is not eliminated.

The surge in India, a mostly unvaccinated country, peaked two months after its surge began. Infections in the U.K. are still rising, but will hit the two-month mark in about a week, so they may peak soon. If the US follows the path of the U.K., it could be September before the current surge dies out. While renewed lockdowns are off the table in the U.S., certain states and cities may reimpose mask requirements, and some consumers may temporarily stop doing things like traveling, dining out or attending sporting events and conferences.

Even if the COVID-19 virus is not eliminated, its ability to spread should be greatly slowed by next year. The number of new vaccinations in the U.S. has gradually slipped to 270,000 per day, but if this lower rate can hold up, about two-thirds of the population will have been vaccinated by the end of the year. That is not too far away from the roughly 70% that is thought to be needed for a population to achieve herd immunity, when viruses stop circulating easily.
