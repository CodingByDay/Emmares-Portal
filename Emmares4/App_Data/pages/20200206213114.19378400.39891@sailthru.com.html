The coronavirus has presented a sharp test for China’s health care system, and so far, the results are concerning.

Already, the outbreak has killed more than 600 people. The number of infections, at more than 31,000, has exceeded the global total for Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome, or SARS.

It should be noted that much of the toll has been in Hubei province and its capital city of Wuhan, where the virus first emerged. Hubei has accounted for over 90% of deaths and two-thirds of all infections.

That’s partly because authorities have quarantined roughly 50 million people who live in Wuhan and its neighboring cities to contain the disease’s spread. While the ultimate effectiveness of that effort is still unclear, no other region in China or beyond has had anywhere near as many infections as Hubei.

The province’s higher mortality rate highlights another issue. A surge in infections has exerted an enormous strain on local medical facilities, especially in Wuhan. The result has been a shortage of supplies, doctors and capacity at hospitals, which have been forced to turn many away so that they can focus on the most-seriously ill.

While the situation has improved as Beijing prioritizes those shortages, it won’t obscure that Hubei appears to have been unprepared for this emergency.

Crises of this magnitude are of course rare, but they are also not unprecedented, particularly in China. The obvious example is SARS. There was also avian flu, which still occasionally rears its head. And even African swine fever, which while not infecting humans, did show how quickly disease can spread province-to-province.

To be sure, China’s response to the coronavirus has been widely commended. It’s been significantly more open than when SARS first emerged. That said, this new virus also shows that Beijing has a lot to do before the next outbreak.

Economic Blow

The human suffering caused by this outbreak has been tremendous. Its economic impact looks like it will also be far reaching. Fear of infections is keeping much of China’s workforce at home, either by choice or government order. That means they’re not working and not shopping, sending reverberations across the global economy. Companies including Nike, Starbucks and Hyundai are already feeling the pain. Economists at Goldman Sachs, UBS and Macquarie are among those cutting their growth forecasts. Bloomberg Economics estimates first-quarter GDP could slow to 4.5% from 6% in the previous quarter. And while Chinese policymakers have taken a number of steps to cushion the blow, they’re also reviewing whether to lower their expectations for this year.

Trade Obligations

A weaker economy will also make it harder for China to meet some of the obligations it signed on to as part of the phase-one trade deal with America. It was revealed this week that Beijing is planning to speak with the Trump Administration about allowing greater flexibility when it comes to those commitments. Commentary from White House economic adviser Larry Kudlow, who said the boom in exports orders from China would be delayed by the virus, suggests that’s a request Washington is expecting. Whether that translates into a quick agreement between the two sides is something we’ll have to wait and see.

Hong Kong Protests

Hong Kong was wracked by protests for much of last year largely because of one question: how integrated should it be with China. The coronavirus appears to have brought it even more to the fore. Medical workers in the semi-autonomous city went on strike this week to try and force Chief Executive Carrie Lam to close the border with the mainland. They argue it’s the best way to prevent infections and cite bans imposed by countries such as the U.S. and Australia. While Lam has tightened controls on entry from China, she’s so far refused a wholesale closure.

Carbon Emissions

There’s not much about the current situation to be optimistic about, but here’s a shot. China is the world’s biggest polluter, annually emitting more carbon than the U.S. and Europe combined. Concern about the coronavirus, however, has resulted in fewer of the country’s factories being in operation and a significant reduction in the number of cars on the road. Chinese demand for oil has already pulled back sharply. That should translate into fewer emissions at least in the early months of 2020. Still, we’d all still have been better if this outbreak hadn’t happened.
