Driving the Day

DEF CON VILLAGES GO VIRTUAL — One of the things that makes DEF CON stand out among standard conferences is the numerous “villages” where hackers get hands-on experience probing voting machines, medical devices and much more for vulnerabilities. While DEF CON’s villages will be hampered by the move to a virtual event this August, many of the villages are paving the way to simulate that community vibe online and adjust to the lack of physical access to equipment.

Sam Levin, one of the founders of the IoT Village, said it wasn’t initially clear how a “capture the flag” competition could work, since usually participants would compete in the same network and send updates to a scoreboard. “Our solution: We just send one person to the office and they're going to plug into the devices, and we’ll give everyone OpenVPN credentials,” Levin told MC. For demos on hacking into devices, screen-sharing in forums like Discord is promising, he said. Still, the villages will suffer some because people can’t just wander in and start hacking into a voting machine. “That's something we worry about losing,” Levin said.

Speaking of the Voting Village, they’re still sorting things out, but plan to at least have speakers webcasting, co-founder Jake Braun told MC. However, one innovation from last year — a “speed dating” scenario where dozens of election officials connected in person with technologists — could continue. “We were really shocked by the number of election officials who came out on their own dime last year,” said Braun. “We planned on scaling that up this year. We started thinking about how we could do something like that virtually.”

Beau Woods is involved in organizing a number of the villages and said he’s seeing some innovation. The Car Hacking Village, for instance, “is writing software code and interfaces to be able to hook up to the cars” stationed in someone’s garage and make sure that a group of hackers can keep track of what worked or didn’t and whose actions triggered what outcomes, Woods told MC. Some villages hired a professional event planner to work through logistics. One positive: “We don't have to be constrained by the eight-hour day. We don't have to be constrained by the number of people who might be in South Africa or somewhere and can't afford the flight ticket and hotel.”

Some events won’t be possible, however. Skytalks, which forbids all recording, isn’t an option.

By the way: DEF CON founder Jeff Moss tweeted this week about a fundraiser for Democratic presidential candidate Joe Biden next week where he’s hosting a Q&A with Biden foreign policy adviser Tony Blinken. In 2016, DEF CON featured a fundraiser for then-candidate Hillary Clinton.

CYBER EYES IN THE SKY — The Trump administration is withdrawing from the Open Skies Treaty, which permits unarmed reconnaissance flights over participating nations, and The New York Times reported Thursday that cybersecurity is a factor . “In classified reports, the Pentagon and American intelligence agencies have contended that the Russians are also using flights over the United States to map out critical American infrastructure that could be hit by conventional weapons or cyberattacks,” the Times reports. Bill Evanina, director of the National Counterintelligence and Security Center, mentioned Russia’s alleged intelligence collection on “civilian infrastructure and sensitive sites” in a statement on the withdrawal.

Some cybersecurity experts found the cyberspace explanation questionable. “I mean, what can they gather with Open Skies overflights compared to satellites?” tweeted Jason Healey, a senior research scholar at Columbia University's School for International and Public Affairs. “And what would that teach them which would be useful for cyber targeting?”

SURGE IN PHISHING A-TAX — Malicious actors are trying to steal people’s Microsoft account credentials using fake IRS tax forms, the security firm Check Point said in a report published today. The spearphishing campaign encourages victims to log into a fake Microsoft OneDrive page to download their completed 1040 and W-2 tax forms. The campaign was created through a one-click phishing kit that lets hackers simply plug in an email address where they want to receive victims’ unwittingly provided credentials. Tax-themed phishing attacks have surged since January and have averaged 630 per week since the end of March, according to Check Point. The IRS extended the tax filing deadline to July 15 due to the coronavirus pandemic.

BROUILLETTE: 'SLIGHT INCREASE' IN CYBERATTACKS ON NATIONAL LABS — Energy Secretary Dan Brouillette said the national labs, which have been dedicating much of their resources toward Covid-19 research, have seen an increase in cyberattacks recently. "We are seeing increased activities around our national laboratories in particular, with regard to cyberactivity, slight increases in the number of hits that we see there," Brouillette told members of his Secretary of Energy Advisory Board on Thursday, via our friends at Morning Energy.

"We know that there are nation-states around the world interested in some of the research being done in the laboratories ... they are very interested in the specific research into Covid-19. We are very very protective of that type of information and that type of work." Brouillette said he was satisfied with the defenses DOE has in place.

TIN OR ALUMINUM IS ACCEPTABLE — More enemies and an emphasis on election security are two ways online operations have changed in the last decade, U.S. Cyber Command and NSA chief Gen. Paul Nakasone said in an interview. “The number of bad actors and their sophistication have increased. The return of great power competition means we should expect to see adversary cyber activity not just in terms of espionage, but also in terms of disruption, destruction and, certainly, influence,” said Nakasone, marking ten years since Cyber Command’s founding.

"Perhaps the mission that was least predictable a decade ago was election security, which is itself only one aspect of a broader revolution in influence operations. Keeping the 2020 elections safe, secure and legitimate is my top priority,” according to Nakasone, who, as one of the so-called Four Horsemen, was instrumental in creating Cyber Command.

ICAM FOR FIRST RESPONDERS — CISA this week released a paper on two pilot projects to test the concept of federated identity, credential and access management (ICAM) for public safety agencies. “While public safety ICAM has not yet seen wide adoption, the combination of shorter technology lifecycles, edge computing, and emerging cybersecurity threats will heighten the risk profiles of legacy information technology systems and challenge current identity management practices,” CISA’s summary states. “In the coming years, public safety agencies will need to explore options for making access control both secure and user friendly.”

Last year, CISA — along with the Office of the Director of National Intelligence, SAFECOM and and the National Council of Statewide Interoperability Coordinators ICAM Working Group — conducted demos in Texas and Tennessee using Personal Identity Verification-Interoperable cards and Fast Identity Online (FIDO) credentials. It concluded that users wanted stronger authentication; liked both kinds of credentials but found the FIDO credentials better for mobile devices; that both technologies were easy to use; that they were affordable; and that more work would be needed to expand use of the framework that was explored.
