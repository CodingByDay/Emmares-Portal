Micromobbin'

Welcome to micromobbin’ — a weekly dive into the tiny but mighty chaotic world of micromobility. This also happens to be a place where Megan Rose Dickey reigns. Follow her @meganrosedickey.

Uber, Lime and Spin each deployed 500 electric scooters in San Francisco as part of the city’s permitting program. This means residents in SF can now choose from Uber-owned JUMP, Lime, Spin or Scoot scooters. Unfortunately, for Skip, the company did not receive a permit to continue operating in the city, which means layoffs at the local level are afoot, Skip CEO Sanjay Dastoor said earlier this week.

Meanwhile, former Uber executive Dmitry Shevelenko unveiled Tortoise, an autonomous repositioning software for micromobility operators. The idea is to help make it easier for these companies to more strategically deploy their respective vehicles and reposition them when needed.

Let’s close this section with the obligatory funding round. Wheels, a pedal-less electric bike-share startup, raised a $50 million round led by DBL Partners. That brought its total funding to $87 million.

Oh, but wait, TC reporter Romain Dillet reminded us that micromobbin’ happens outside of the U.S. too. Uber also announced this past week that it has integrated its app with French startup Cityscoot, which has a fleet of free-floating moped-style scooters.

This is the latest example of Uber’s plan to become a super mobility app that goes well beyond its own network of ride-hailing vehicles.

Snapshot: Touchscreen tech

We’ve seen a lot of different approaches when it comes to engaging with connected car services: head-up displays on the windshield, small screens perched on the dashboard, interactive voice and of course connections and mounts for smartphones.

But how about if your whole car becomes the touchscreen? A startup called Sentons is working on technology that could make that happen. The company uses a technique involving processors and AI that emit and read ultrasound to detect physical movement on a surface, such as touch, force or gestures, and users can create “virtual controls” on the fly that work on these surfaces.

This week, it released SurfaceWave, a software and hardware stack that works on glass, metal and plastic surfaces of smartphones.

CEO Jess Lee says that the next iterations are going to be the kinds of materials that are used to make car dashboards and other interior surfaces you find inside the vehicle, including leather, thicker plastic and other materials. The company is already engaging with automotive companies, Lee told TechCrunch.

I can see a lot of possibilities for this in the human-driven vehicles of today. We’ve already seen how Tesla has changed how we think about infotainment systems in cars. And then there’s electric vehicle startup Byton, which plans to bring a vehicle to market with a touchscreen that extends along the entire dashboard.

The real opportunity for Sentons will be with autonomous vehicles, a product that will afford its passengers more leisure time.

— Ingrid Lunden

Made in China


Earlier this week, Tesla was given the OK to begin producing vehicles at its $2 billion factory in Shanghai. Tesla was added to the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology’s list of approved automotive manufacturers.

Now we’ll watch and wait to see if production starts this month. Expect the topic of China and this factory to come up during Tesla’s earnings call with analysts October 23.

In other China factory news, we hear that electric vehicle startup Byton plans to host a splashy opening ceremony in early November for its new plant. The event will include lots of Chinese officials, company executives and maybe a preview of a near-final production version of its M-Byte vehicle.

Byton’s factory in Nanjing covers some 800,000 square meters (8.6 million square feet) funded with a total investment of more than $1.5 billion. Over the summer, the walls and roof went up, equipment was installed and commissioning began in five major workshops: stamping, welding, paint, battery and assembly.

The plant will begin trial production in late 2019.

This all sounds great, but there have been challenges and the constant requirement for capital is one of them. Byton has delayed the launch of the production version of the M-Byte by two quarters. It’s now looking like commercial production will begin by the end of the second quarter of 2020.

Here are a couple of interesting tidbits for those manufacturing geeks out there:

  • The stamping shop completes one panel every 3 seconds on average. Byton claims it will be one of the fastest stamping production lines in China.
  • The welding shop incorporates 335 welding robots supplied by KUKA and boosts the automation rate to 99%, according to Byton.
  • The paint shop is equipped with a “3 Coating 2 Baking” system and thin film pre-treatment as well as the transverse “Eco-Incure” oven technology.
  • The battery shop will produce and assemble battery packs, designed independently by Byton. Battery maker CATL provides the battery cells and modules and aluminum maker Constellium supplies the aluminum battery tray.

A little bird

We hear a lot. But we’re not selfish. Let’s share. A little bird is where we pass along insider tips and what we’re hearing or finding from reliable, informed sources in the industry. This isn’t a place for unfounded gossip.

Is it me or is everyone in the autonomous vehicle industry talking about system integration these days? I’m so old I remember when the only thing anyone wanted to talk about, when it came to automated driving, was software. OK, with the exception of lidar.

There has always been a requirement to integrate software with hardware to create a self-driving stack that can take the place of a human driver. We know that.

What’s new is that AV companies are talking more openly about the importance system integration. The phrase has popped up enough in the past several months for me to wonder, why now?

One company that is talking more about the integration of hardware and software is Cruise. Brendan Hermalyn, the company’s director of autonomous vehicle hardware systems, wrote a blog post about its approach to designing and developing hardware as well as system integration.

What might not be known is just how much they’re doing in house. The company is becoming more vertically integrated and its hardware team has grown substantially. Among what is likely a long list of hardware projects, we know the company is working on its own compute and sensors, including lidar (oh trigger word!). The lidar play makes sense considering the company acquired Strobe in 2017.

A carmaker's evolution

Volvo has been making moves towards electrification and automated driving for awhile now. But several announcements and an unveiling this week gives us a much better understanding of the company’s evolution.

Volvo introduced October 16 its first all-electric vehicle, the XC40 Recharge and launched the Recharge brand — the overarching name for all chargeable Volvos with a fully electric and plug-in hybrid powertrain. The automaker also announced it would introduce a new EV every year through 2025 and slash by 40% the carbon footprint of the lifecycle of every car and SUV it builds over the same time frame.

On top of that, Volvo Cars and its Chinese parent company Geely Holdings will merge their existing combustion engine operations into a standalone business. The move will “clear the way for Volvo Cars to focus on the development of its all-electric range of premium cars,” Volvo Car Group President and CEO Håkan Samuelsson said at the October 16 XC40 reveal.

And then, a couple of days later, Volvo went ahead and announced it has established a new dedicated business group focused on autonomous transportation. This new business has a mandate worth noting: it’s focusing on mining, ports and other transportation hubs that require the movement of goods.

What does systems engineering in AVs mean?

 

I recently spoke to Randol Aikin, the head of systems engineering at self-driving trucks startup Ike Robotics, about company’s approach, which is based on a methodology developed at MIT called Systems Theoretic Process Analysis. STPA is the foundation for Ike’s product development. Yes, this gets deep in the weeds and it’s a big long. But it’s ok. Dig in.

The company also released a wickedly long safety report (it’s halfway down that landing page in the link provided).

I have some excerpts (edited for clarity and space) here from an interview with Aiken. 

Explain STPA.

“It’s a way of representing a complex system through the interaction of a bunch of different elements. The power of it is that when you analyze a complex system that involves human operators and training and complex software, the way it interacts is really difficult to analyze on a line-by-line basis. And so it requires abstraction; it requires a hierarchical and abstract way of representing the system.

It’s a fundamentally different approach to analyzing a complex system as compared to automotive safety standards that have been used in the past.

How? 

OEMs don’t have to think about the training of the drivers of their vehicles, they don’t have to consider whether the vehicle should be out on the road in the first place; but STPA considers all those elements.

Where STPA is headed?

There’s some adoption within the industry. There’s a wide appreciation that something else is needed, or figuring out a way to analyze the systems and then eventually come up with the yardsticks by which we measure ourselves.

But why is it special?

What’s interesting about the STPA approach is that it allows an engineering viewpoint into the effects of, for instance, bad incentives provided by leadership or poor training or unsafe practices that allow you to actually treat it as an engineering model.

It sounds like what you’re saying is it’s stripping the emotion and the financial incentives out and placing a lot more value on an engineering approach to make sure that progress and development follows those tenants. 

It’s along the right lines. One of the elements is that we treat safety as an engineering problem and not only as a moral aspiration.

We’re not looking at this from a resistors and connected wires and software lens, we’re looking at this from a holistic lens that involves humans and involves decision making, design, leadership, and that’s really why we want to treat all those things together.

Ok. So how does this allow you to make rapid progress without testing your trucks (in autonomous mode) on public roads?

We asked the question, what do we have to prove to ourselves and demonstrate in order to be on a public road safely? It’s the same question that we’re going to have to answer for the product as well, which is, what do we need to prove to assure that we’re safe to operate without a human in the cab?

It’s one of the huge unproven hypotheses. Anybody in this space that doesn’t consider that to be a huge technical challenges is ignoring a really thorny and important question.

By going through this process we’re proving out hypotheses of how we’re going to take a systems approach to build a body of evidence that one day will be able to pull the driver from from the cab.

Why is it so unusual to take this approach?

Note: CEO Alden Woodrow jumped in to answer this.

When you’re a venture backed startup, with limited runway there are all sorts of incentives to try to demonstrate as much progress as quickly as possible. This is true of larger companies as well. That can cause the temptation to take shortcuts and try to do things that are really visible and accessible to the public, in terms of their tangibility. Driving on public roads is a good example of that. That can be a very compelling announcement to make.

We decided when we started the company to try to resist those temptations, even though we’re subject to all the same incentives as a venture-funded company.
