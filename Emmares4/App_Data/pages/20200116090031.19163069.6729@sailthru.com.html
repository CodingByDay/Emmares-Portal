Oh annual tech conferences—you're not quite what you used to be. Just this week Sony announced it won't be attending E3 2020, and companies like Microsoft have long since abandoned their marquee annual keynote at CES.

 

But even as major tech companies increasingly decide to eschew trade shows in favor of holding their own events, we continue to find value and news of interest whenever we're on the ground. CES 2020 proved no different. Yes, there were smart toilets and other BS. But we saw laptops to love from Dell and Samsung among others. The annual barrage of drool-worthy TVs continued. Lenovo kept the foldable, bendable tech train going for those awaiting the Westworld dream. And the gaming peripherals market acknowledged it might be time to rework big ol' bulky VR headsets.


 

So for this week's Orbital Transmission, we're kicking it old school and sharing the news that stood out to us from last week's CES. Over-the-top insanity and vaporware will always exist (and maybe that's partially why we're seeing a gradual shift away from big trade conferences), but even a grizzled Ars heart was able to see some things to love in Las Vegas. What happened there is sometimes genuinely good enough not to stay there.

CES' slow evolution into a car show continued in 2020

We'll always be interested in the new 2-in-1s or TVs, but CES' most interesting tech increasingly comes in one particular form factor: cars. This year, we saw awareness tech that would let cars leverage pedestrian cell phones in order to prevent accidents. We saw concepts that were both way far-out (Mercedes' car moves like a crab and has bionic flaps) and nearly ready for the streets (Nissan's Ariya electric SUV might be crucial for the carmaker in light of some international news). But the car tech that stayed with us the most came from a non-manufacturer. Sony's Vision S is a stunner that looks a bit like a cross between a Porsche Taycan and a Lucid Air and packs in Sony's industry leading tech from entertainment to AI. The company probably won't be challenging Tesla anytime soon, but after this we'd definitely be curious to drive something with a Sony badge.

 

Bring on PC-gaming, but in a Switch-like form factor

Gaming has traditionally been a CES stronghold, though it often comes in the form of new controllers or various laptops and PC peripherals. At CES 2020, however, we saw a gaming offering that genuinely caught our eye—Dell’s new Concept UFO. It's a rectangular handheld gaming console with an eight-inch display and removable controllers on both ends. Working models we saw all ran Windows and were able to render playable games in handheld mode; docked mode, in which the console was connected to an external display and its controllers were detached; and another portable mode in which the controllers were detached but connected by a center "bridge." So... Switch-like form factors, but for PC gaming? How soon can we get one, Dell?

 

Do you enjoy cores? AMD revealed its mobile Zen 2—and the 64 core Threadripper 3990X

AMD has really been bringing the heat to Intel recently, and now the company's conquest gets mobile. AMD revealed the Ryzen 4000 at CES 2020, and it has the company's signature high core and thread counts (plus the low TDPs). AMD also announced the Threadripper 3990x at this year's event, and we actually got a chance to build and hands-on review a test system using the 3970x. It was pretty nuts—although it drastically outperforms both Intel's HEDT parts and its Xeon Scalable parts for most workloads, the sheer thread count of the thing meant the 140mm fans on our NZXT Kraken liquid cooler were in leaf-blower mode every moment the system was on, and it raised the temperature in the office its workbench was in significantly when running under load.

 

It wouldn't be CES without a bonkers keynote, so thanks Samsung

The tradition of over-the-top keynotes at CES is long and decorated. The Rock and Bill Gates were best buddies launching the Xbox way back at CES 2001, and who can forget that we're all #BornMobile thanks to Qualcomm (and Big Bird, Guillermo Del Toro, Steve Ballmer, Maroon 5, and more) at CES 2013. So we appreciate Samsung carrying the mantle in 2020 with its (presumably vaporware-filled) keynote last week. It had everything you could want: small BB-8-like robots that will follow you around the house, an entire exoskeleton for AR/VR fitness-usage, and signs of life that Samsung still insists on its own smart assistant with news on the Bixby speaker. Stay weird in 2021, CES.
