Teen e-cigarette use is surging after years of persuading kids not to smoke. Regulators blamed sweet flavors and promised to sweep the market of products they say lure teens to vaping. They missed some.  

Walk into any vape shop or convenience store and you can see them: A crop of new all-in-one products that are exempt from the policy. One of the most popular brands, Puff Bar, sells about two dozen flavors, including Blue Razz and O.M.G. (that's orange, mango, guava). The company behind Puff Bar this week said it has “ceased all distribution” and plans to file an application with regulators to reintroduce its products.

That doesn't mean the fakes are throwing in the towel. Puff Bar is already battling counterfeits and knockoffs, something even vaping behemoth Juul struggles to combat. A thriving market of impostors means regulators are likely to continue finding themselves one step behind.

Both the public-health and vaping communities blame regulators for allowing upstart brands like Puff Bar to hit shelves. The e-cigarette market was essentially frozen in August 2016, but that hasn’t stopped companies from jumping into the booming industry.

What's more, the pace of innovation around vaping is picking up (and consumers are eager to give new products a try). Puff Bar’s website features a silicone cap that consumers can snap on to USB-shaped devices and release flavors like peach ice or sour apple. A number of Puff-related trademarks have been recently filed, including Puff CBD. 

A shake out may be on the horizon. Companies must submit applications to the Food and Drug Administration by May 12 if they want to stay on the market. Big questions loom for the agency, including how it plans to catch and remove illegal products in the seemingly endless game of public health whack-a-mole.—Angelica LaVito
