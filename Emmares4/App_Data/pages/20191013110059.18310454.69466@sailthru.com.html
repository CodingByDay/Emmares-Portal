Two decades ago, videogames were supposedly about to start looking like movies. As digital technologies revolutionized the way films were made, the thinking went, games themselves would become interactive movies, complete with blockbuster film icons who would draw ever-larger audiences.

It was, as Lisa Wood Shapiro writes this week on Backchannel, a heady time. It also turned out to be a dead end. Games have certainly evolved since the 1990s, but not in the way they were predicted to. Which makes it especially fascinating to revisit the making of Ripper, a 1996 cyberpunk-noir videogame starring, among others, Christopher Walken, Karen Allen, Ossie Davis, Paul Giamatti, and Burgess Meredith. Wood Shapiro was a production coordinator for the game, her first real job out of college.

The gaming artifacts from that era live on, though, mostly on YouTube (“the de facto purgatory for abandoned games,” Shapiro writes) and in a few gaming museums that maintain the antique hardware needed to play them. “Adventure games weren’t a great genre, but the Silliwood games are like the sci-fi films from the ’50s,” says Alex Handy, director and founder of Oakland’s Museum of Art and Digital Entertainment. “There’s joy in watching them.”

Mark Robinson | Features Editor, WIRED
