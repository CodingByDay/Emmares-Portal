Driving the Day

IOWA: THE FIRST 2020 ELECTION SECURITY TEST — For Iowans participating in the caucuses, the process is decidedly low tech. It involves people gathering in rooms and writing their favored candidate's name on paper. But that doesn't mean there are no cybersecurity risks, nor that Iowa isn't an important test for election officials' preparedness ahead of 2020.

In addition to paper, there will be apps and websites and smartphones involved in the process, giving hackers plenty of opportunities to disrupt the democratic process, Eric reports in a story out this morning. "If there is value, there will be an attack," Duncan Buell, a computer science professor and voting security expert at the University of South Carolina, told Eric. "And whether to change the results or just to disrupt the process, there is value in attacking the Iowa caucus."

WE CAN WORK IT OUT — The Trump administration will work with the U.K. to develop a system for safely sharing sensitive intelligence once Huawei begins building part of the British 5G network, Secretary of State Mike Pompeo said Thursday. "All the elements of the Five Eyes will work together on this to ensure that these systems are sufficiently secure, that they're going to deliver the outcomes that we need them to deliver, and that we have ... only a level of risk that we find tolerable inside of those systems," Pompeo told reporters in a joint press conference with U.K. Foreign Secretary Dominic Raab.

Pompeo's comments about a safe level of risk reflect the Trump administration's conciliatory approach to the U.K.'s decision, announced Tuesday, to give Huawei access to a limited part of its new 5G network. A U.S. official previously told POLITICO that the U.S. would try to convince the U.K. to scale back Huawei's role even further, but Raab indicated that the U.K. wouldn't budge. "We think we have a targeted approach [that] protects our security [and] protects our relationship with our closest intelligence allies, [the] Five Eyes," he said. Pompeo later said, "We think putting Huawei technology anywhere in your system is very, very difficult to mitigate."

Pompeo on Thursday also welcomed the European Union's recently published 5G risk mitigation measures and said they validated the U.S.'s approach to Huawei. "All parts of future 5G networks should be considered critical infrastructure," he said in a statement, "and each country should have measures in place to protect the safety, security, and privacy of citizens who rely on these networks."

HUAWEI SITS DOWN WITH POLITICO — As recounted by our friends at MT: Just days after the U.K. riled Washington by not purging Huawei from its 5G buildout, Andy Purdy, Huawei's chief security officer, made the case in a meeting with POLITICO that the Trump administration's anti-Huawei campaign has nothing to do with any security risk.

"The U.S. believes that China wants to take over the world, and anything ... that they think directly or indirectly helps China, they're against," said Purdy, who worked on cybersecurity at both DHS and the White House. "And it's a lot harder to create a technology [sector] industrial policy that helps drive America forward than it is to say, 'OK, we're going to draw a line in the sand here.'"

AVAST APOLOGIZES — Avast swiftly shuttered its data marketing analytics subsidiary Jumpshot following a joint Motherboard and PC Mag investigation that revealed it collected and sold user website browsing histories to major companies. "Avast's core mission is to keep people around the world safe and secure, and I realize the recent news about Jumpshot has hurt the feelings of many of you, and rightfully raised a number of questions — including the fundamental question of trust," Avast CEO Ondrej Vlcek wrote on Thursday, adding an apology.

Vlcek said he made the decision following a review months ago when he became CEO. By ending both Jumpshot and its data collection "with immediate effect," hundreds of employees are in line to lose their jobs, Vlcek said. Avast stock absorbed a big hit after the data collection practice became news. Jumpshot took in $36 million in revenues for Avast in the last fiscal year.

TRADITION — Russian cyber criminals are returning to their roots, sponsoring hacker forum competitions and offering cash prizes, according to a report on Thursday from cybersecurity firm Digital Shadows. In December, hackers were asked to submit articles on topics like searching for zero days and APT attacks, with the winner receiving $5,000. "If competition entries do increase for this event, it may be that in the coming months more cybercriminals will come to realize the advantageous cost-benefit calculation associated with organizing a forum competition and that similar competitions will become more frequent," the report concluded.

SURVEY SAYS — Iranian hackers are trying to get into U.S. government networks by tricking federal employees into clicking on a survey from a well-known contractor, Intezer said in research released Thursday. The hacking group, known as APT34, Oilrig and by other names, cloaked its malware in a fake Westat employee satisfaction survey tailored either to Westat workers or its customers.

Notably, the campaign relies on upgraded versions of malware that FireEye first identified in July. "The last APT34 operation was exposed only a few months ago by FireEye, and judging by our current findings we can confidently state that the group has since evolved its operations," Intezer wrote. "The technical analysis of the new malware variants reveals this Iranian government-backed group has invested substantial efforts into upgrading its toolset in an attempt to evade future detection." The company told ZDNet the campaign might have additional targets in mind, too.

LET'S GO HUNTING — Microsoft announced on Thursday the launch of the Xbox Bounty program. The program welcomes gamers, security researchers and technologists to help hunt down security vulnerabilities in the Xbox ecosystem and report them. Microsoft will offer monetary prizes up to $20,000 for eligible submissions. In 2017, 2.5 million Xbox and PlayStation gamers had their details hacked.
