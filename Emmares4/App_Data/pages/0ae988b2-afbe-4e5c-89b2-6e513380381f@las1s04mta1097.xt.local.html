Driving the Day

GRENELL HEARTS TRUMP, HATES HUAWEI — President Donald Trump is selecting Richard Grenell as his next acting director of national intelligence , and it's inspired a big reaction: from his status as the first openly gay Cabinet member, to his hyper-aggressive Twitter presence, to his lack of intelligence experience, to Trump using the "acting" label to avoid his Senate vetting, to his allegiance with Trump in a position that's supposed to have some degree of objectivity. For cyber folk specifically, his most notable stance is as a staunch critic of Huawei, one that he's advanced as ambassador to Germany.

He has threatened that the U.S. will cut off intelligence cooperation with Germany if it doesn't keep Huawei out of its 5G network, and has responded with his trademark aggressiveness to those who have criticized the U.S. position on Huawei. "According to this guy, the US doesn't get to react to policies we find dangerous," he tweeted just this week. "I find it offensive that you think the US cooperation must stay the same no matter what you do. We call that taking us for granted."

As Martin has reported, intelligence officials fear presidential retaliation for contradicting him in the annual worldwide threat briefing to Congress to such a degree that they've sought not to hold public hearings. Grenell's allegiance to Trump may raise questions about whether he'll be interested in "speaking truth to power," as intelligence officials have done thus far in response to the Russian election interference conclusions the president has resisted.

GOT TO ADMIT IT'S GETTING BETTER — Democratic presidential candidates are showing signs of getting wise to campaign cybersecurity , according to ratings out today from SecurityScorecard. Every candidate in the race, and some who have since departed, had a rating of "B" or above, an improvement from the "C" rating the company gave the DNC last year. The company bases its grade on things it can detect from outside an organization, such as Domain Name System configurations.

Among remaining candidates, Joe Biden ranked highest at 97 out of 100. The most common problem: some bad cyber hygiene practices. "A few of the hygenic issues are likely attributed to campaigns leaning toward accessibility of their platforms over very strict security controls, something observed by many corporations and entities," SecurityScorecard said. The company also rated DNC-recommended third-party vendors, a practice that has both good and bad elements. "This does exhibit strong supply chain analysis and vendor standards, however, it certainly puts a target on these third parties," it wrote.

ICS ASSAULT STATS AND TRENDS — More than three-quarters of the 438 ICS vulnerabilities reported in 2019 were considered '"deep within' a control systems network, requiring some existing access to a control systems network to exploit," Dragos found in its 2019 year-in-review. Overall, ICS-targeting attacks continued to grow, with third-party and supply chain threats increasing, and hackers are increasingly targeting remote connectivity, such as VPNs, Dragos said.

"Escalating geopolitical tensions increase the chance that offensive cyber effects operations against ICS will be employed more regularly, putting critical infrastructure and human life at higher risk," the company wrote.

ALSO, MORE ON THE NATURAL GAS RANSOMWARE ATTACK — Signs point to the ransomware attack on a natural gas facility that CISA alerted about this week being the same incident the Coast Guard reported late last year. Dragos on Wednesday said that was its conclusion based on information it received, which also jibed with a news story. "Based on a variety of factors, Dragos concludes with high confidence the events in the CISA alert represent well-known ransomware behavior and is not an ICS-specific or ICS targeted event," Dragos wrote. The Coast Guard identified the ransomware Ryuk as the culprit , something CISA didn't specify.

TEACH A MAN TO PHISH — Pre-made infrastructure, templates and tutorials sold on cybercriminal marketplaces are making it a cinch to launch phishing attacks, Digital Shadows concluded in an analysis out today, with the tutorials averaging less than $25 and the tools needed for an attack costing less than $20 in some cases. "It has never been easier for a phisher to set themselves up in business," said Harrison Van Riper, a senior analyst at the company. "Many of the templates and fake clone sites we discovered are extremely convincing and impersonating hundreds of brands."

Election Security

SEE SOMETHING, SAY SOMETHING — Five of the nation's top national security officials on Wednesday urged the public to help watch for election interference in 2020 . "We have yet to identify any activity designed to prevent voting or change votes," Attorney General William Barr, FBI Director Christopher Wray, outgoing acting DNI Joseph Maguire, acting Homeland Security Secretary Chad Wolf and CISA Director Christopher Krebs wrote in an op-ed. "However, we remain watchful of any malicious activities from cybercriminals and from foreign actors like Russia, China and Iran."

The quintet called for a "whole-of-society" approach to combat meddling. "We cannot prevent all disinformation, foreign propaganda or cyberattacks on our infrastructure," they warned. "However ... we can all help to mitigate these threats by exercising care when we share information and by maintaining good cyber hygiene to reduce the risks that malicious cyberattacks will succeed."

PARDON THIS HACK — Trump's high-profile pardons this week included a lesser-noticed name : Ariel Friedler, the former CEO of a tech company who pleaded guilty to hacking into the computer systems of competitors to his firm, Symplicity. Trump ally Chris Christie requested the pardon for his friend Friedler, who served two months in prison. "As a result of this incident, he was forced to sell the company that he had dedicated his life to building," the White House said in a statement on the clemencies Trump granted . "During the investigation, his time in prison, and after his release, Mr. Friedler expressed deep remorse for his actions."
