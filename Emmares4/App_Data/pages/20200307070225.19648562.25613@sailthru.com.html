In the rich world, public fury is building over the initial, dangerously lethargic response by governments to the new coronavirus. Doctors in the U.S., which spends more on healthcare than any other country, are outraged over the lack of test kits after the Trump administration fumbled emergency planning. Missteps by public health authorities in Japan have become a political crisis for Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, with the fate of the summer Olympics hanging in the balance. In Britain, the epidemic has revealed a chronic shortage of intensive care beds, the result of years of under-investment in the National Health Service. Says Yorkshire doctor George Priestley: “It’s going to end in death.”

Meanwhile, after some major miscues, China is earning plaudits from the World Health Organization for its decisive action to contain the virus. Bruce Aylward, the WHO doctor who led a fact-finding mission there, was clearly awed by what he saw: Pop-up hospitals, free rapid swab tests, a profusion of CT machines, hands-on crisis management by top officials and the widespread use of social media to spread life-saving public health messages.

Still, the anger of some Chinese remains palpable, especially given that the vast majority of the 3,454 dead are their countrymen. Those in Wuhan who screamed “Fake, fake! Everything is fake!” at a visiting minister this week remind that public pushback over early efforts to hide the outbreak’s extent hasn’t subsided. Indeed, the renowned Sinologist Geremie Barmé writes that the coronavirus has reignited Chinese political dissent.

Dr. Aylward’s account though is a counterbalance to Western criticism of China that focuses on its draconian quarantine measures. The mass lockdowns Beijing imposed may have bought precious time for the rest of the world to prepare for the contagion’s onslaught.

If the ultimate test of government is its ability to keep citizens safe, it’s not at all clear that the democratic model has delivered superior results.

“From the worldwide financial crisis of 2008 to climate change, ordinary people have concluded that the authorities cannot be trusted to keep them secure,” writes Peter S. Goodman in the New York Times. “That has allowed politicians to attack legitimate problems with simplistic solutions, like trade protectionism and armored borders.”

Trust and Resilience

The coronavirus adds to the already gathering risk climate change and trade wars pose to the new economy. In much of the West, these problems are exacerbated by a crisis of confidence in politicians and the economies they manage. 

“This is not a drill,” proclaims Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus (below), the WHO’s director general. “This is not a time for excuses. This is a time for pulling out all the stops.”

And yet governments lack the basic tools to manage this crisis. Looser monetary policy, the usual prescription for economic shocks, is a classic case of “pushing on a string”: More money in the pockets of consumers doesn’t help much if malls are closed and travelers balk at flying. In any case, central bankers aren’t equipped to handle a healthcare disaster. Once those realities sank in, investors who initially cheered the Fed’s rate cut this week began frantically dumping stock again.

Time is running out: Covid-19 is accelerating existing retrograde trends of xenophobia, nationalism and protectionism. New economy business leaders have months, not years, to prepare for the future.

As a result, Bloomberg New Economy will soon open a global conversation on how businesses should respond. We’ll examine the obstacles facing governments and industry, as well as healthcare, education and finance. We suspect trust and resilience will figure prominently in any solution, but getting there will be the real challenge. Watch this space!
