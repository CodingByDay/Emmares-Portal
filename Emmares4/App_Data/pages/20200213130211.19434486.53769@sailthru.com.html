Smartphone 2020 season officially started this week. Samsung, the world's biggest smartphone vendor, took the wraps off two flagship-y phones on Tuesday. The Samsung Galaxy S20 iterates on the company's longstanding phone line, offering 5G compatibility, a bump up to the Snapdragon 965, and (of course) MOAR CAMERAS. And perhaps more interesting, the Samsung Galaxy Z Flip takes the company's foldable work and applies it to a more svelte form factor (less phablet, more modernized flip phone).

 

Of course, if you're not an iOS fan, these announcements might still leave you a bit cold. Each phone pushes past $1,000, and if recent history has shown us anything, it's that the best Android phones (certainly in terms of value, if not overall usage) remain in the budget category. Has a 2020 Android smartphone announcement seemed more lucrative than, say, the Pixel 3a yet? Heck, have any of these upcoming devices appeared more enticing than something like the Nokia 2.3, which will cost a tenth of the price?

 

With these latest entrants from the market leader, this week's Orbital Transmission calls for surveying the (possibly sorry) landscape of 2020 flagship smartphones. Expect reviews of these new Samsung devices soon enough, and at first glance they feel fine. But from the Fold to the Razr to the Librem 5, it's clear 2020 hype has so far led to dashed hopes—not happiness.

Orbital Transmission 02.13.2020
 

Samsung could use a win because... have you seen the Fold?

If you thought Ars' Reviews Editor Ron Amadeo was harsh on the Pixel 4, phew, did you read about his time with the Samsung Galaxy Fold? This was supposed to be The Future™, but it turned into a bit of a catastrophe. Other companies latched onto the tech and produced their own foldable to arrive pretty soon after the Fold. And the Fold itself ran into numerous technical delays and difficulties, including early review units bricking within days. The result? "The Galaxy Fold is an exercise in how not to build a foldable smartphone," Amadeo wrote. "Developing a totally new form factor like this is something that requires careful consideration of how the hardware and software would work together, but Samsung seemed more concerned with being first to market than making a good product."

 

Speaking of foldable, the Surface Duo exists

If Samsung thinks foldable competition is tough now, wait until later in 2020. Microsoft's first-ever Android phone, and its first self-branded phone, is slowly becoming a reality. Twitter user Israel Rodriguez spotted a person with a prototype of this foldable on the Vancouver subway and snuck a spy video out into the world. This encouraging-but-limited footage shows off the possible form factors, but other than the fact that the Surface Duo runs Android, comes with two 5.6-inch displays, and will be out Holiday 2020, we know next to nothing about the device for now.

 

It's quickly gone from "Hello Moto" to "Oh no, Moto"

In the year 2020 when nostalgic names like BlackBerry are literally coming to an end, it was quite a surprise that one of the most anticipated smartphones for the year was... the Moto Razr? It brought some of the old (the flip) with the new (bendable OLED displays that could span the entire device) and appeared to be more ready-for-primetime than the Galaxy Fold, and then... the phone came out. Within days, footage of broken in-store demos and folding failures started to pile up, reminding all of us that any smartphone that folds, bends, twists, or whatever is definitively a first-get device (with first-gen issues). We're still curious to put hands on this thing, but be warned if you're in the buying market right now.

 

Unfortunately, Linux smartphones still aren't quite ready yet

Maybe you don't like iOS, but you don't like Android either. Years after the demise of Windows Phone, that truly leaves you with minimal options. So the fact that a smartphone newcomer, Purism, actually shipped a genuine Linux phone called the Librem 5 should be celebrated. The company somehow scraped together a new supply chain of mostly open source components, it came up with a smartphone design from scratch, and it is building its own smartphone distribution of Linux—in just a year or two. For the time being, we found this to very much be a work-in-progress after a few days hands-on. But know while most of the smartphone stuff isn't quite done yet, the real power of this being actual Linux is already apparent. You easily open a terminal, punch in "sudo apt-get install [app name]," and you'll install an actual desktop Linux app.
