Angelina Jolie on the VFX and ‘Extraordinary Detail and Care’ Behind Disney’s ‘The One and Only Ivan’

Courtesy of Disney

“My child, Shiloh brought the book to me, she had read it and she loved it,” Angelina Jolie says recalling the first time she had seen “The One and Only Ivan,” written by Katherine Applegate.

Exploring why her child was affected by the book, why she loved Ivan, a silverback gorilla, and why he spoke to her led to Jolie stepping in, not just as a voice actress, but as a producer on Disney’s adaptation of the novel.

Not only was Ivan based on a real gorilla, Jolie thought it was a great way for her child to learn about “what activism can do and what sanctuaries are.” But more importantly, “There’s family and self-discovery,” she says of the story.

"The One and Only Ivan" centers around a gorilla who lives in a cage at the Exit 8 Big Top Mall. Having spent his life in captivity with other animals, he starts piecing together his past with the help of Stella, an elephant voiced by Jolie, and together they devise a plan to escape from captivity.

The bigger question was how was director Thea Sharrock going to bring Stella and the animals to life when the film’s principal cast consisted of animals. That’s where the VFX team at MPC stepped in to bring Stella, Ivan and the other animals to life.

“Any time you see a shot that has no humans in it, that’s CGI; it’s not just the animals. They also created the environment,” Sharrock notes of the meticulous detail that combines CGI, virtual production, live-action and VFX.

Oscar Contender: A Look at the Concept Art for 'Trial of the Chicago 7'

Courtesy of Netflix

 


Director Aaron Sorkin was adamant that Oscar contender “Trial of the Chicago 7” needed to be shot in Chicago.

With that in mind, cinematographer Phedon Papamichael set out to visually recreate the protests of 1968. Much of the main exterior locations hadn’t changed much.




However, Sorkin and the crew traveled to New Jersey for the interior courtroom scenes.




It was up to production designer Shane Valentino to build the contrasting world of the riots and the courtroom. The heaviness of the wood and its imposing style was designed to show history and its resistance to change.

Even more intimidating were the framed paintings that Valentino hung of judges, gazing down on the accused. “I think it helps our judge come into this space and feel like this is something very grand, something that he oversees and has power over,” he says.

Colin Wilkes on the Costumes of 'Palm Springs'

Courtesy of NEON

 

I built my concept for Sarah and Nyles from a few self-imposed guidelines:

Whatever they wore had to stand the test of time and wouldn't date easily. This made the costumes believable in a world where time pretty much doesn't exist -- especially for Nyles, who we find out has been in the loop for some time.

Sarah and Nyles needed to both compliment and contrast each other visually -- when next to each other, they create a yin-yang of sorts.

I wanted to use bright colors so they would pop against the various landscapes. I wanted to keep the concept of "play" alive as we go on adventure with them. I assembled a treasure chest of wild a la carte pieces that they could throw on at any time. You'll notice this throughout their escapades as there's a pith helmet here, a tinsel cape there, even those coordinated outfits for their dance sequence.

For Nyles, I wanted him to be the quintessential everyman of leisure. I looked to Andy Sweet's photography of South Beach throughout the ‘70s to capture the essence of Nyles. For color, I took a lot of cues from early 2000s palette, namely the mustard-ketchup color combo of his look. Leonardo DiCaprio in Baz Luhrmann's “Romeo & Juliet” was, of course, a touchstone. Andy Samberg and I spent a lot of time trying on combinations of Hawaiian shirts and swim trunks to finally find Nyles.

With Sarah, I wanted her to feel effortlessly thrown together, but still owning an impressionable style. I wanted a one-of-a-kind shirt that she had worn to the bones. We found that perfect burnout seafoam t-shirt in the fitting and I cut off the sleeves. I had the fictional band tee "The Static Femmes" designed by graphic artist Shaina Hedlund. We honed the design to land somewhere between an ‘80s Cyndi Lauper and ‘90s Riot grrrl band. The denim shorts were originally a light blue, which we overdyed to a black to edge the look.

The red snakeskin boots were my nod to “The Wizard of Oz” as Dorothy tries to find her way home.
