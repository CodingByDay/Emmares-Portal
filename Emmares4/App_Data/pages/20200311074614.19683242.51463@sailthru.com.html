Our take on the latest developments

As coronavirus grips America and Europe, China could be just days away from declaring it has no new cases.

The country reported 24 additional infections for Tuesday, a stunning turnaround when you think it was recording more than 3,000 cases a day in early February.

Cautious signs of activity are appearing in Wuhan, the central Chinese city where the pathogen first emerged and which has been under lockdown for more than six weeks. There are some cars on the streets and the government is laying the groundwork for business to resume. But the sidewalks remain empty with people still barred from leaving their apartment buildings, according to one resident we spoke to.

Meanwhile, eerily familiar stories—from the stresses in Italy’s health care system to the U.S.’s issues with testing—are emerging as the virus takes hold globally. The Chinese government faced an enormous backlash domestically over its delayed response early on in the epidemic, and is now almost gleefully playing up these struggles in state media and offering assistance to other countries.

While criticized as extreme by some public health experts, it does appear that China’s decision to quarantine about 60 million people in and around Wuhan helped slow the spread of the virus. But debate rages over whether the price paid in human suffering and the hit to the economy was worth it, and whether less draconian measures could have achieved the same outcome.

Perils still abound for China. Doctors treating patients in Wuhan say the virus remains enigmatic: symptoms can start out mild, perhaps with a low fever, before suddenly escalating. There are still doubts about the reliability of its data. And epidemics can have several peaks, which means new clusters could emerge as travel resumes and people get back to work.

President Xi Jinping also warned last week that “many people living in isolation at home will develop psychological problems of one kind or another.” And the trauma of the virus could be compounded by an economic slump—many analysts are now worried that a drop off in demand from an increasingly infected world could cause GDP this quarter to contract for the first time in decades.

For China, the ground may finally be steadying, but the aftershocks are just beginning.—Sharon Chen
