Another existential moment for Uber ATG

Uber Advanced Technologies Group, the self-driving vehicle unit of Uber, appeared (at least to outsiders) to finally have its house in order after a series of controversies, including being sued by Waymo for stealing trade secrets and a fatality caused by one of its self-driving test vehicles.

The company settled with Waymo in February 2018. By December, Uber resumed on-road testing of its self-driving vehicles in Pittsburgh, nine months after halting its entire autonomous vehicle operation after one of its vehicles struck and killed pedestrian Elaine Herzberg in the Phoenix suburb of Tempe.

But nope, it’s not over. Uber’s settlement with Waymo, which was connected to its acquisition of Ottomotto, included the joint retention of an independent software expert to “ensure that our autonomous vehicle hardware and software do not misappropriate Waymo intellectual property.”

Here comes the important piece that will have implications for Uber ATG no matter how you slice it. In a securities filing this week, Uber said the independent software expert found it was still using some of Waymo’s technology. Here’s the snippet.

The independent software expert recently made adverse findings as to certain functions in our autonomous vehicle software. These findings, which are final, will likely result in a license fee or in design changes that could require substantial time and resources to implement, and could limit or delay our production of autonomous vehicle technologies.

In addition, in August 2019, a federal grand jury indicted Anthony Levandowski, a cofounder of Ottomotto LLC and our former employee, on charges of theft of trade secrets from Waymo.

This discovery could require Uber to seek a license for the right to use intellectual property held by Waymo, according to the filing. Even if such a license is available, Uber might be forced to pay “significant royalties,” which could increase operating expenses. Another likely outcome is that Uber will need to develop alternative non-infringing technology.

Either way, the company is looking at added expenses.

Micromobbin'

TechCrunch reporter Megan Rose Dickey is somewhere scootin’ around, and so this week I’m taking the reins, er, wheel, um handlebars? of micromobbin’.

The big dramatic news award of the week goes to Los Angeles and Uber subsidiary Jump. The dispute, which came to a head earlier this week, centers on Los Angeles’ controversial MDS data-sharing policy that requires companies in its one-year pilot program to transmit data, in real time, on all scooter trips made within the city. The data includes travel time and the start and end points of every trip. (David Zipper has a good explainer on this policy over at Slate)

Los Angeles temporarily suspended Uber’s permit to rent electric scooters and bicycles for failure to comply with its rules and gave the company until Friday to appeal the decision or leave. Jump initially responded by threatening (in a letter) to sue the city for an “unfair” and “improper” suspension.

Here’s the latest (as of Friday evening): Uber/Jump sent its official request for a hearing on the temporary suspension of its scooter permit. The company can still operate during the process.

“This does not negate or void our previous intentions toward a lawsuit, it is merely a step in the process before we file,” an Uber spokesperson told me an email, adding that the company still believes LADOT’s demands for real-time location data of its riders raises concerns of legality, government surveillance and consumer privacy.

Uber said it is still complying with the remaining aspects of MDS data requirements that enable LADOT to enforce regulatory compliance on things like caps. “The only thing we’re contesting is the requirement for real-time tracking of user trips,” the spokesperson said.

Meanwhile, Lyft has brought back its Ditch Your Car program. This time Lyft is partnering with online used car company Carvana. Starting Nov. 7, select riders began to receive an email invitation to participate by selling their personal vehicles with Carvana through Dec. 16. In return, participants receive $250 in Lyft credit, 3 free months of Lyft Pink and of course, whatever Carvana paid for your used car.

Hitch is another interesting ride-hailing startup we’re watching that focuses on long distance routes between cities.

The startup launched earlier this year with a route between Austin, where it’s based, and Houston. Hitch recently added a second route between Austin and Dallas. Hitch’s CEO tells me the company aims to scale quickly in Texas and plans to add a third route between Austin and  San Antonio next month.

Snapshot: Chinese mobility startups in the U.S.

The presence of Chinese mobility companies in the U.S. isn’t new. But the scale and scope might surprise you.

Strategic advisory company ZoZo Go mapped Chinese auto-related companies in the United States and published its findings in Automotive News. What ZoZo Go found was 105 Chinese-auto related companies heavily concentrated in California and the Midwest.

Michael Dunne, CEO of ZoZo Go, told me that what struck him was that despite trade and tech wars several Chinese AV startups still operating under most people’s radar.

TechCrunch has written about some of the Chinese-backed autonomous vehicle startups, including AutoX, Deeproute.ai, Pony.ai and TuSimple. But there are dozens more related to batteries, hybrid technology, mapping, even chassis systems.

Sixty of these companies are in Michigan and about 34 are in California.

A new AV company

Ghost Locomotion, a fascinating autonomous vehicle company emerged from stealth this week with with $63.7 million in investment from Keith Rabois at Founders Fund, Vinod Khosla at Khosla Ventures and Mike Speiser at Sutter Hill Ventures.

The company is not aiming for robotaxis or delivery bots. It’s targeting passenger cars. And it’s an area where the government has not yet chosen to regulate, CEO and co-founder John Hayes told me.

Ghost is developing a kit that will allow privately owned passenger vehicles to drive autonomously on highways. The kit involves hardware like sensors and a computer that is installed in the trunk and connected to the controller area network (CAN) of the vehicle.

Cameras are integrated into what looks like a license plate holder at the back of the vehicle, as well as another set that are embedded behind the rearview mirror.

A third device with cameras is attached to the frame around the window of the door

The company says it will deliver this product in 2020. A price has not been set, but the company says it will be less than what Tesla charges for its Autopilot package that includes “full self-driving” or FSD. FSD currently costs $7,000.

The response I got from my article was engaging. Some are highly skeptical, while others made comparisons to Comma.ai. Still, others wanted to know if Ghost would be installing redundant steering and braking as part of the kit. Hayes’ response on Twitter was interesting.

Making simpler mechanical systems is a better choice than redundant systems for increasing reliability.

Electric steering is simpler than hydraulic steering, electric motor in a hybrid is simpler than the IC motor, we’ll see electric braking emerging.

Notable reads

It’s been awhile since I dumped a ridiculous amount of studies, surveys and reports on you. Well, this is your lucky week. Here are three.

SAE International recently released the results of its SAE Demo Days Survey. SAE polled nearly 1,400 participants at four SAE Demo Days events. Keep in mind that the survey might not reflect actual sentiment in the U.S. because the folks who were surveyed expressed enough interest in self-driving cars to attend one of these events. Of those polled:

  • 82% were initially enthusiastic for self-driving cars, with nearly 10% of participants reporting higher enthusiasm post-ride.
  • 76% think a self-driving car experience is similar or superior to a human-driven experience.
  • 92% want to have control shared between human and self-driving.

Abode Analytics surveyed 1,040 U.S. adults about connected car features and self-driving cars. All respondents were required to have a U.S. license and own a smartphone. The “Future of Driving” report was released in October.

Key takeaways:

  • 40% of drivers are in favor of self-driving cars being available for purchase
  • 44% of drivers say having a car with features such as lane assist and parking assist is a factor in their car-buying decision
  • 60% of electric/hybrid car owners are willing to share their car data in exchange for personalized experiences
  • 1 in 4 drivers say they’re driving a car with some type of built-in voice control functionality, and 39% of drivers say they use their voice assistants daily

Panjiva, the supply chain research unit at S&P Global Market Intelligence, released some automotive trade and tariffs-related data. A few of the interesting items, include:

  • Imports of completed vehicles climbed 5.4% year over year in September, including a 6.6% surge in shipments from Japan and an 18.8% rise in shipments from South Korea.
  • The improvements are not universal though. Shipments from the EU fell by 2.5%. Imports from Canada and Mexico were unchanged.
  • Meanwhile, there’s a widespread downturn in auto components imports. GM’s imports fell 38% year over year in the three months ended Oct. 31. That likely reflected the strike that disrupted the company’s operations for most of the month.
  • Shipments linked to Ford declined 19.8%. Panjiva said it may reflect a shift of sourcing within its North American supply chain.
  • Japanese manufacturers also saw declines despite a rise in shipments of vehicles, with imports linked to Toyota down 4.1%, while Nissan’s fell 6%, and Honda’s dropped 8.8%.
  • There were few signs of expansion. Imports linked to Jaguar Land Rover rose 3.5%, likely due in support of increasing sales of vehicles produced overseas, Panjiva said.
