Hi it’s Shelly in Hong Kong. I just returned from a trip to Silicon Valley, where a red scare has washed over the San Francisco Bay Area.

At a dinner party, an executive from a big tech firm admitted to a table of a dozen people that he had barely thought about China before but now worries its approach to censorship and authoritarianism is bleeding into the U.S., posing a severe threat to America. The table nodded along.

A banker spoke of the confusion around working on financing for Chinese tech upstarts that last week got added to America's blacklist and can no longer do business with U.S companies. A venture capitalist starting a new fund told me they now check general partners' passports and only allow U.S. citizens to participate, excluding even Chinese citizens with U.S. green cards for fear of tripping up U.S. foreign investment prohibitions. 

It was in stark contrast to the last decade, where Silicon Valley spoke of China as the future for technology companies and clamored for more cooperation.

During my stay, I spoke to startup founders about how the Silicon Valley outposts that China's Tencent, Alibaba and Baidu use to make investments in U.S. upstarts have gone dark. According to them, these outfits often mask the names of Chinese participants in announcing the few investments they do make, to avoid drawing too much attention. Harmless inquiries to folks who had built their businesses on the U.S.-China tech trade about when their next trip to Beijing might be were met with blank stares. 

While the whole world was enraptured by the NBA's decision to kowtow to China andexpress regret for an executive's tweet supporting the Hong Kong protests, there's a bigger, more lasting shift going on in Silicon Valley that has decidedly turned against China.

In recent years, American tech companies contemplating what to do about the world's second largest economy seemed to view their strategic choices as: a) Continue making futile attempts to engage with China, like Facebook did, putting aside the country's history of shutting out Western companies that threaten to eat into the profits of homegrown companies; b) The Samsung strategy, which is to pretty much give up on the market; or c) Wait. Stay out of the fracas. Don't tweet. Don't engage. Keep quiet. 

While Samsung has still managed to hold on to the title of largest global smartphone seller despite its China market share going from 20% in 2014 to 1% by 2017, most other folks seem to have stuck with option C -- the wait and see approach. The rationale is that having a presence in China is key to being a global company and capturing the remaining growth in the world, and "Hey, we want to be ready if things eventually change." The thing is, waiting on China is a bit like meeting a guy at a nightclub and giving him your number, hoping that because he said he'd call that he actually will. 

But as my visit to Silicon Valley showed, it seems that waiting around is no longer a viable strategy. The real choice for American tech companies, venture capitalists and startups seems to have narrowed to just two options: Either take China at face value and decide that as a rational business, profits matter more than any kind of moral high ground, or make a conscious decision to stop pursuing business in a country that will require you to adhere to its viewpoints inside -- and outside -- its borders.

Otherwise, as the NBA found out, you'll end up alienating your customers on both sides of the world. In fact, American basketball star LeBron James put it quite eloquently on Monday: "Yes we do have freedom of speech, but there can be a lot of negative that comes with that, too." Especially if your freedom of speech, say, coincides with something China doesn't want you to say.
