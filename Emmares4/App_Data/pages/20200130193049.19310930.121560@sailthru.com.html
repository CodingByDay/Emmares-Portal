Footwear companies are always trying to convince us that if we slip on their newest technology-packed shoe, we can run faster, go longer, and beat all of our personal records. Of course, a lot of factors go into quickening a runner's pace—training, nutrition, weather, the right playlist—but our choice of shoes is admittedly pretty high on the list.

Nike made news a couple of years ago when some runners wearing its Vaporfly shoes posted better marathon times than runners with boring, non-Vaporfly running shoes. Just this week, Brooks Running gave WIRED an exclusive look at its newest long-distance racing shoe, the Hyperion Elite. The $250 marathon shoe has a carbon fiber plate along the bottom, and it's cushioned by some newly formulated foam that Brooks says increases the stability of the runner's feet on the road. With the added ability to maintain their form even after fatigue sets in, Brooks says, a marathoner should be able to keep trotting at a faster pace for longer.

If all of this is making you feel out of breath (as it is for me), then you should know we also wrote some stories this week that don't involve as much physical exertion. Louise Matsakis wrote about Byte, Lauren Goode looked back at 10 years of the iPad, and Arielle Pardes dug into Tinder's new anti-harassment tools.
