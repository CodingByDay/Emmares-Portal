The terrible events of September 11, 2001, happened 18 years ago. But when you look at how we communicated at the time, it can seem like much more time has passed. On that day, people left voice messages on tape recorders saying goodbye to loved ones. The president’s top advisers monitored developments on their Blackberrys and pagers. Air Force One had no cable or satellite TV.

As Garrett Graff writes this week on Backchannel, the attacks on that sunny late-summer morning marked a seismic shift in our times. But, he says, “the deeper I got into studying 9/11, the more I felt that it was less the beginning of the 21st century and more the ending of the 20th century—a relic of the analog age rather than the dawn of the digital.”

How would such an attack play out in today’s world of maximal social media communication? Graff fears that we might all retreat to our silos. “Rather than uniting together in a national moment,” he worries, “we would all put ourselves at the center of the story instead.”

Mark Robinson | Features Editor, WIRED
