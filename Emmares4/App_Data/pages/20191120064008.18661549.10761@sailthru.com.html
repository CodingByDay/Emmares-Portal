Could U.S.-China relations get any worse? You bet. On the eve of the New Economy Forum in Beijing, the U.S. Senate unanimously backed legislation requiring an annual review of Hong Kong’s special trading status. This followed remarks by U.S. Vice President Mike Pence warning that it would be hard to reach a trade deal if authorities in Hong Kong used violence against demonstrators.  As if the quest to end the U.S.-China trade war wasn’t hard enough, a new battlefront has opened up. 

Let’s stipulate that the basic problem between the world’s two largest economies is lack of trust—and there’s not much that can be done about that in the short-term. But these two economies are deeply entwined and have a host of mutual interests, like avoiding climate change. In the absence of trust, we need new institutions to hold the relationship together. Figuring that out will be the focus of a U.S.-China Task Force launching at the New Economy Forum this week. —Andy Browne

Today’s Top Stories

Sovereign Investors Turn Their Backs on Coal

Our planet is burning up. We need to get to zero on net carbon emissions. On that front, there’s some good news: Bloomberg Opinion columnist David Fickling says that sovereign investors are turning their backs on fossil fuels. Bill Gates, who appears at the forum this week, doesn’t think divestment makes much difference to carbon emissions. But maybe that’s because he’s a software guy, Fickling speculates. “Coal power plant financing is very challenging,” Dharma Djojonegoro, deputy chief executive of Indonesian generator PT Adaro Power, told Reuters in June.

China Is Building More Coal Power Stations

Unfortunately, China counters the trend away from coal. Its Belt & Road Initiative is dotting unspoiled stretches of Central Asia with the most polluting kind of coal-fired power stations.

U.S. Senate Backs Hong Kong Demonstrators

The U.S. Senate unanimously passed a bill Tuesday aimed at supporting democracy protesters in Hong Kong and warning China against a violent suppression of the demonstrations, drawing a rebuke from Beijing.

Pence Pins Trade Deal to a Peaceful Resolution

Vice President Mike Pence said Tuesday that it would be difficult for the U.S. to sign a trade agreement with China if demonstrations in Hong Kong are met with violence.
