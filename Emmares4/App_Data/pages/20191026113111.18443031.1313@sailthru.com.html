A SoftBank-backed company cuts back

Less than a year ago, vehicle subscription startup Fair raised a huge Series B funding round of $385 million led by SoftBank, to take its business global.

The company has made some acquisitions in its short life, including the unprofitable leasing business of Uber and Canvas, which was previously owned by Ford. Fair also made three high-profile hires just a few months ago as part of its plan to scale its business model internationally and try to make a name for itself on the global stage.

Now, the company that was founded by automotive, retail and banking executives, including Scott Painter, former founder and CEO of TrueCar, is cutting back. The company is laying off 40% of its staff and removing its CFO, Tyler Painter, the brother of Scott Painter. He’s being replaced in the interim by Kirk Shryoc.

Scott Painter told TechCrunch this is a proactive move and not made because SoftBank or any other investor has leaned on it to do so.

Whether or not this is a “proactive” move, here’s the upshot: the car subscription business isn’t the slam dunk some companies thought it would be. Startups and automakers, including Audi, Porsche, Cadillac and Ford have tried to launch car subscriptions with varying success. Ford sold its business to Fair and Cadillac shuttered its service, although it has said a reboot is coming.

Have these cuts extended Fair’s runway enough to allow it to scale and stay a viable business?

Micromobbin'

Welcome back to micromobbin’ — a weekly dive into the tiny but mighty chaotic world of micromobility.

Bird deployed its long-awaited cruiser in the form of the Scoot Moped. But there are a couple of caveats. It’s just a one-seater and is only currently available in Los Angeles.

Meanwhile, because congestion and scooters with dead batteries are two big problems plaguing the micromobility industry, Swiftmile convinced Austin to let the company deploy its parking and charging systems in the city.

Swiftmile’s move into Austin isn’t surprising (this is Kirsten weighing in). Austin mayor Steve Adler, and a few others, told me earlier this year during SXSW that when the scooters came to the city, the bike-share industry took a dive. Scooters were everywhere and it was clear that scooter management was going to be the next big opportunity.

Swiftmile CEO Colin Roche, who I also met during SXSW, told me at the time that a number of cities were interested in deploying these charging systems. The key to Swiftmile’s system is that it also can provide scooter companies with diagnostics and keep the device locked in the dock if it is malfunctioning. Systems like these could help scooter companies like Bird and Lime extend the life of their scooters and prevent local governments from banning them altogether.

Deal of the week

Autonomous vehicle startup Zoox has earned the “deal of the week” label in part because there is still so much more left to learn about what’s going on over there. Tips? Send them to me. Email me at kirsten.korosec@techcrunch.com or send a direct message to @kirstenkorosec.

Here’s what we know. TechCrunch has been chasing down a few leads about Zoox’s efforts to raise funds. Axios broke the initial story that Zoox raised $200 million in a new convertible note funding that will be folded into a Series C round. This round is expected to close later this year or at the beginning of 2020.

We don’t know the investors or post-money valuation. After its Series B round of $465 million, Zoox was valued at $3.2 billion.

Word on the VC street (with folks who have direct knowledge) is that some Zoox investors are approaching other VC firms and trying to sell their shares on the secondary market. It’s not unusual for early investors to take some money off the table once a company reaches a valuation of that size. However, it also suggests that these investors don’t have the rosiest of outlooks on whatever deal or deals might be coming down the line. It doesn’t help that the latest capital came in as a convertible note, not equity.

Zoox isn’t just developing a self-driving system. It’s also building a vehicle from the ground up. Yes, it does have suppliers and partners, but it is more of a vertically integrated company than others in the industry. That takes a lot of capital and will likely require some kind of partnership with a manufacturer, unless Zoox makes the highly unusual and even more capitally intense move to produce its own vehicles.

The rise of robotaxi pilots

Companies vying for a stake in the autonomous ride-hailing business have largely focused on the technical challenge of teaching computers how to drive. As the industry gets closer to solving that problem, companies are beginning to realize that a successful and profitable mobility service requires more than just the ability to safely bring people from Point A to Point B.

Think of all the seemingly small moments of dialogue with an Uber, Lyft or even traditional taxi driver. “Drop me at the end of the block,” or “oh I forgot my bag in the house, will you wait while I grab it?” These simple interactions become much harder when a human driver is replaced with a robotaxi.

It’s why you’ll see a lot more small-scale autonomous ride-hailing pilot programs like the one announced Friday by Hyundai, Pony.ai and Via. Hyundai is launching November 4 a robotaxi service called BotRide that will operate on public roads in Irvine, Calif. The fleet of electric, autonomous Hyundai Kona crossovers will be equipped with a self-driving system from Chinese autonomous startup Pony.ai and Via’s ride-hailing platform.

This isn’t a driverless service; there will be a human safety driver behind the wheel at all times. But it is one of the few ride-hailing pilots on California roads. Only four companies, AutoX, Pony.ai, Waymo and Zoox have permission to operate a ride-hailing service using autonomous vehicles in the state of the California. And under CPUC rules, the rides must be free.

Waymo also operates a much larger ride-hailing service called Waymo One in the suburbs of Phoenix.

To some extent, this new trend of pilots aimed at learning the mechanics of autonomous ride-hailing lags behind Ford’s efforts to learn the ins and outs of autonomous delivery. Ford’s once-mocked partnerships with Domino’s and Postmates, which even included the use of fake autonomous vehicles, now appear to have been ahead of the curve.

A little bird

We hear a lot. But we’re not selfish. Let’s share.

Earlier this week, the California Public Utilities Commission held a workshop with autonomous vehicle companies. There was a way to listen in — and TechCrunch did — but it was hard to find and most folks likely missed it. Aurora, AutoX, Cruise, Pony.ai, Voyage, Waymo and Zoox were among the companies that participated in the day-long workshop. Advocates for peoples with disabilities and officials from various cities and county agencies were also there.

There’s a lot to unpack in this lengthy public meeting. I’ll highlight two nuggets of information here.

First, Aurora is applying for the CPUC permit that would allow it to participate in autonomous vehicle ride-hailing. Currently, only four companies (AutoX, Pony.ai, Waymo and Zoox) have CPUC permits. Permitted companies are not allowed to charge for rides.

Which is the perfect segue to one of the big underlying themes at the meeting: charging for rides. Every AV company had a representative that pleaded their case to the CPUC. The general theme was that charging for rides will help these companies prepare for commercialization.

The CPUC didn’t seem to have a lot of empathy for this particular plea. One commissioner noted that the purpose of the data requirements, which every participant must provide under the permit, is designed so the government can learn about the technology, and not for companies to learn about whether to commercialize.

“I’m not sure how to feel about these comments that if you can’t commercialize your service, you stop learning,” one CPUC commissioner said. “I don’t know what to take from that except to express a little bit of surprise because I think that companies can set out to learn as much as they want to and it depends on how they take on their testing.”

The testing we see to date is highly controlled, largely with pre-selected participants and they follow a very fixed route, the commissioner said, noting that government doesn’t place restrictions on the scope of testing. He then closed with this advice.

“If you wanted to open it up and be more of an open source of testing, you may find yourself learning very different lessons from that experience.”

A NASCAR thing

Earlier this month, NASCAR’s SVP for Racing Development John Probst told TechCrunch that it could introduce hybrid-powered cars as early as 2022. NASCAR is still sorting how a race hybrid would come together, but it would have a unique application. Rather than use hybrid technology to extend range, the race series plans to direct it toward improving performance.

This pursuit could end up affecting passenger vehicles. The race series will work directly with partner OEMs, such as Ford, to develop its hybrid program, according to NASCAR. While NASCAR stands for National Association of Stock Car Auto Racing, it’s been decades since anything on the series’ cars even remotely resembled a stock car you would buy at a dealership.

That will likely remain (mostly) the same, but with NASCAR going hybrid, technology on its competition vehicles could find application that transfers in some way to our daily drives. — Jake Bright

Notable reads: lidar, space vans and EVs

Here are a few other quick bullets, links and other tidbits to round out the week.

First in the reality-check-let’s-mark-our-calendars department, Tesla CEO Elon Musk said Wednesday that the company’s full self-driving mode, in a “feature-complete release,” could arrive as early as the end of this year. FSD, which currently costs $7,000 for the upgrade, provides higher levels of automation than other passenger vehicles on the market, and Musk has repeatedly insisted that this inching toward full self-driving is the best approach. Tesla vehicles are not fully autonomous today.

It’s important for the company to continue to improve the capabilities of FSD — and not just to keep customers happy. Tesla gets to recognize deferred revenue — nearly $500 million according to the company’s estimates in its third-quarter earnings report — tied to Autopilot and FSD features.

TechCrunch reporter Darrell Etherington got an up-close look at the Airstream Astrovan II, the vehicle that will be used for Boeing’s first CST-100 Starliner crew launch next year. Check out these astrovans.

Lidar company Sense Photonics appears to have made it past the “theoretically works” gates and is now on a path toward mass production.

The startup, which came out of stealth earlier this year and raised $26 million, is now offering its advanced flash lidar for pre-order.

Richard Branson’s Virgin Galactic will find out what the public markets think of its ambitious plan to make commercial space tourism a reality. The company’s shareholders have approved a merger, announced earlier this year, with Chamath Palihapitiya’s special Social Capital Hedosophia holding company. The merged company will debut Monday on the NYSE.

Morningstar issued a report recently making a few forecasts on all-electric vehicles, namely that EVs will account for one out of every five new cars sold globally by 2030.

Still, Morningstar notes it will be a bumpy ride. Remember, EVs were just 2% of global auto sales in 2018.

A few other takeaways:

  • EVs will reach cost and functional parity with gas and diesel-powered vehicles by 2025.
  • Rapid EV adoption will occur in the second half of the next decade. China and Europe will have the necessary charging infrastructure to support mass-market EV adoption, while the U.S. will have the infrastructure in place in some parts of the country.
  • By 2030, half of all new cars sold will be EV or hybrid. The 2020s will be the decade of the hybrid as these partially electric vehicles will reach 30% of new auto sales by 2030.
