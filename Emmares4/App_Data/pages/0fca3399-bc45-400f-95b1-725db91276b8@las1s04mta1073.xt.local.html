Surveillance

FISA TROUBLES — Criticism from President Donald Trump, DOJ and progressive Democrats on Wednesday sank prospects for renewing surveillance authorities that lapsed earlier this year, and it’s not clear when or if reauthorization legislation (H.R. 6172) will return. Trump threatened a veto and heaped other criticism on the bill, tying the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act to his grievances over the FBI’s Russia investigation.

Never mind that Trump signed legislation reauthorizing the powers in 2018, a fact that both Republicans and Democrats savaged him for on Wednesday. “It’s utter hypocrisy that the president has threatened to veto the bill for not being strong enough, while his own Justice Department urges opposition to reforms that directly address the Carter Page surveillance abuses,” said Neema Singh Guliani, senior legislative counsel for the ACLU. “It is also disappointing that Congress can’t come together to pass privacy reforms that Republicans and Democrats agree on — and advocated for long before Trump became president.”

REGULATORY CACOPHONY — Federal agencies that require states to meet cybersecurity standards need to coordinate better in the management of those standards , the Government Accountability Office warned on Wednesday. “The four federal agencies that GAO reviewed either fully or partially had policies for coordinating assessments with states, but none of them had policies for coordinating assessments with each other,” the watchdog agency said in its report. The FBI, IRS, Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services and Social Security Administration all impose some cybersecurity standards, but many of those standards conflict with each other. For example, nearly 80 percent of SSA’s requirements used different technical thresholds than similar requirements from other agencies. OMB “requires agencies to coordinate” but isn’t making sure they do so, GAO said.

State chief information officers told GAO that the agencies’ conflicting requirements made their jobs more difficult, with 15 CIOs citing a significant increase in the amount of calendar time and staff hours devoted to compliance. Twenty-two states cited either a great or very great increase in the cost of procuring equipment and software to comply with the clashing federal rules.

The National Association of State Chief Information Officers, which has consistently advocated removing regulatory conflicts, praised the new report. “We are hopeful that the federal agencies will heed the report’s recommendations and foster a much greater collaborative environment on these regulations,” NASCIO Executive Director Doug Robinson said in a statement.

STOP, DROP, ROLL — Partially in response to a rise in Covid-19 scams and partially in response to the people reporting $1.9 billion in scams to the FTC last year, Google today announced it was teaming up with the Cybercrime Support Network to launch the Scam Spotter program . It's focused on three principles: "Slow it down" (if they’re seeking urgent action, reconsider) "spot check" (verify the institution involved) and "Stop! Don't send" (reconsider things like requests to buy gift cards). “Just because COVID-19 has disrupted everyone’s life, it doesn’t mean the scammers have taken a break,” wrote Vint Cerf, vice president and chief internet evangelist at Google and one of the architects of the Internet. “In fact, scammers have exploited the pandemic with alarming speed.”

Google also said on Wednesday said it has seen a new phishing campaign using Covid-19 lures disguised as emails from the World Health Organization. “We’ve seen new activity from ‘hack-for-hire’ firms, many based in India, that have been creating Gmail accounts spoofing the WHO,” the company's Threat Analysis Group wrote in a blog post.

Google said most of the messages went to leaders at financial services, consulting and health care corporations in multiple countries, including the U.S. The lures “encourage individuals to sign up for direct notifications from the WHO to stay informed of COVID-19 related announcements, and link to attacker-hosted websites that bear a strong resemblance to the official WHO website.” Google also wrote that last month it sent 1,755 warnings to users whose accounts were targets of government-backed attackers.

UNTIMELY DMARC SHORTCOMINGS — More than 80 percent of 10 U.S. state governments and health departments have not implemented the strictest version of a standard to defend against email spoofing, Proofpoint said today. The company found that 44 percent of these entities do not have a published Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance (DMARC) record, and that 92 percent of state governments and 88 percent of health departments had not set their DMARC protections to the highest level.

VANDA(L) FOUND — Check Point said today that it tracked down the identity of a prolific hactivist who has defaced nearly 5,000 websites since 2013 and notified law enforcement in Brazil about the hacker, who goes by VandaTheGod. Of the 4,820 sites in 40 countries the hacker has defaced toward a personal goal of 5,000 defacements, 57 percent were in the U.S., largely targeting city, state and health care websites with anti-government and social justice messages, Check Point said. The company believes he is still at large. It reported him in October but found activity from him as recently as March.

Report Watch

— This year’s form-based attacks — where "scammers leverage file, content-sharing, or other productivity sites like docs.google.com or sway.office.com to convince victims to hand over their credentials" — disproportionately used Google file sharing and storage websites in 65 percent of cases, Barracuda found in a report out today . Microsoft brands were targeted in 13 percent of attacks.

— Nearly half of U.S. and U.K. employees are less likely to follow safe data practices when working from home, with 52 saying they can get away with riskier behavior from home and 48 percent citing the lack of IT oversight as why, according to a survey from Tessian out today. More than 9 out of 10 IT leaders trust staff to follow safe data practices at home, apparently in a misplaced sense of optimism.
