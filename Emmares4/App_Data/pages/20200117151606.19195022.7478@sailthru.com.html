CSX Corp., a railroad that ferries everything from cars to chemicals, and Fastenal Co., a distributor of factory-floor basics, unofficially kicked off the reporting period for fourth-quarter results this week with a flop. At CSX, sales fell 8% in the last three months of 2019, mostly due to a continued slide in automotive and coal volumes as well as intermodal freight, which is mostly consumer products that can be put on a truck or a train. The labor strike at General Motors Co. last year made that slump particularly acute, but CSX doesn’t see much hope for a turnaround: It predicted as much as a 2% slide in sales in 2020, worse than analysts had anticipated. Fastenal’s average daily sales volume grew just 3.7% in the fourth quarter, compared with 13.2% a year earlier and an already moderated pace of 6.1% in the third quarter.

“The general slowing in economic activity that we experienced in the second and third quarters of 2019 continued in the fourth quarter of 2019,” Fastenal said in its earnings release. “It took industrial activity a while to cool off and it will take a while to heat back up,” CSX CEO Jim Foote said on the company’s earnings call.

While the U.S.-China trade deal that President Donald Trump officially signed this week has sparked optimism for a swift rebound in capital spending, the reality is that many uncertainties remain. Some $360 billion of Chinese imports are still subject to U.S. tariffs, and that’s likely to continue at least through what is apt to be a volatile presidential election. There’s little in the deal to dispel fears that the two countries are entering a new cold war over influence and technology leadership.

China has pledged to buy $200 billion more in American goods over the next two years than it did in 2017, including nearly $80 billion in manufactured products. But details remain scant and skepticism abounds as to how iron-clad the commitments are. Chinese Vice Premier Liu He struck a reserved tone at a signing ceremony this week, noting that purchases will be based on “market demand in China.” An article in the Communist Party’s flagship People’s Daily laid out additional caveats including that prices for U.S. agricultural products must be competitive and meet China’s quality and safety standards, and that the U.S. is responsible for adjusting its export restrictions, according to a translation from Simon Rabinovitch at the Economist. 

“Setting aside some unpredictable event, I still think global growth is going to be sluggish but stabilized,” Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas President Robert Kaplan said in an interview with Bloomberg TV’s Michael McKee. “Manufacturing is going to be sluggish but stabilized.” The problem is, many industrial stocks are not priced for a sluggish but stabilized growth environment; they’re priced for a swift acceleration that seems unlikely to happen. Apart from the lingering trade uncertainties, the industrial slackening in 2019 was more of a slow burn than a marked, outright recession. While growth likely continued to deteriorate for most manufacturers in the fourth quarter, that decline may have bottomed, and and the overall slowdown in earnings was relatively shallow.

Fastenal, for example, had climbed 20% since Oct. 11, when Trump announced a preliminary trade agreement with China. The company also reported third-quarter results the same day that pointed to fresh evidence of a slowdown, but weren’t quite as bad as investors had feared. Those troubling data points got incrementally worse in the fourth quarter. Fastenal’s gross margins continued to slide, a fact that it blamed on an increase in less-profitable but faster-growing offerings such as factory-floor vending machines and on-site services. And yet Fastenal fell short of its (already reduced) target for 22,000 new vending machine signups in 2019. Operating margins, which have been comparatively steady, were weighed down by the weak sales volumes. Sluggish but stabilized growth would be better than a downturn, but about 25 times forward price-earnings ratio calls for a lot more than that.

ANOTHER BAD WEEK FOR BOEING
Perhaps we should make this a recurring section. Boeing Co.’s new CEO David Calhoun was in attendance when Trump signed the U.S.-China trade deal. “It’s not your fault,” the president said of the crisis that’s engulfed Boeing’s 737 Max jet. “You just got there.” Actually, Calhoun joined the board in 2009 and, along with Boeing’s other directors, very much bears responsibility for the decisions that brought the company to this point. That didn't stop the board from showering him with money, including a special $7 million bonus for the safe return of the Max and a regular $2.5 million bonus that pays at target in 2020 seemingly no matter what. The continued messaging missteps and disregard for PR optics is truly something to behold. In a statement after the trade signing ceremony, Calhoun said the company was “proud that Boeing airplanes will continue to be a part of this valued relationship.” Presumably, Boeing jets will factor into China’s purchasing commitments, although I have to think if there was anything remotely specific on this front, Boeing would be shouting this much-needed win from the rooftops. Instead, Xiamen Airlines Co., previously an exclusive Boeing customer, is inviting bids from leasing companies for Airbus SE’s A321neo jet. Airbus has also been in talks with China’s central plane-purchasing authority since 2019 about jets for the country’s economic plan for the five years beginning 2021, whereas the procurement firm hasn’t even been authorized to begin talks with Boeing yet, people familiar with the matter told Bloomberg News. Meanwhile, on the home front, all major U.S. Max operators have now yanked the plane from their schedules through June.

DEALS, ACTIVISTS AND CORPORATE GOVERNANCE
Woodward Inc. on Sunday agreed to acquire Hexcel Corp. in an all-stock transaction valued at about $7.5 billion including debt. The companies pitched the deal as a means to better compete in the push for more environmentally friendly air travel. They hardly could have picked better timing for that argument than the week that BlackRock Inc. CEO Larry Fink urged companies to proactively address the impact of climate change, lest his firm become “increasingly disposed” to vote against their management and directors. BlackRock is among the biggest, if not the biggest, shareholder of most companies, and that happens to be the case at both Woodward and Hexcel. However tangible you think this sustainability-oriented investing phenomenon is, though, it’s hard to separate the Woodward-Hexcel merger from the context of Boeing’s grounded 737 Max. This time two years ago, rumors were swirling that Boeing might buy Woodward in the pursuit of a $50 billion parts and services business. Woodward is right to seize the opportunity to gain negotiating clout and product breadth, especially with the odds rising that both Boeing and Airbus will have to accelerate the development of new narrow-body jets. This is the latest in a string of commercial aerospace tie-ups and more consolidation is likely to follow. Barclays analyst Julian Mitchell cites Eaton Corp. as a company that may feel it needs to answer this M&A flurry with a scale play of its own. He also highlighted the possible disadvantages of the lack of diversity in General Electric Co.’s aviation unit, which is focused predominantly on commercial engines. Given the appetite for dealmaking, analysts from Berenberg and Canaccord say it’s a possibility another bidder could try to break up the Woodward-Hexcel deal.

Wesco International Inc. has won the takeover battle for Anixter International Inc., the industrial distributor of electrical supplies. Private equity firm Clayton, Dubilier & Rice declined to raise its offer for Anixter, allowing the company to formally accept Wesco’s stock-and-cash bid of about $100 a share, or $4.5 billion including debt. Entities associated with Anixter Chairman Sam Zell have agreed to vote their approximately 10.8% stake in the company in favor of the Wesco deal. Interestingly, the new merger agreement includes a reverse termination fee of $190 million that Wesco would be required to pay to Anixter should the companies fail to get regulatory approval. The deal should be hugely accretive to Wesco’s earnings even at this elevated valuation, but juggling the integration process with a ballooned debt load will be a challenge. I don’t think we’ve heard the last of CD&R, either. Recall that the private equity firm had at one point offered to buy both companies. “CD&R believes the merger of Anixter and Wesco will be industry defining and wishes both organizations great success in the future as a combined enterprise,” according to one of the nicest news releases I have ever seen issued by a bidding-war loser.

Resideo Technologies Inc., the thermostat and home-security company that Honeywell International Inc. spun off in 2018, was “thrown together piecemeal” and consists of a “hodgepodge” of underperforming businesses, according to a lawsuit filed by funds run by Mario Gabelli. We talked last month about a similar lawsuit filed by Garrett Motion Inc. alleging Honeywell burdened the turbochargers spinoff with an “oppressive and unconscionable” indemnification agreement for legacy asbestos liabilities. The parent company’s only obligation to a spinoff is to not put it in a position where it’s insolvent, and as bad as Resideo and Garrett have performed as independent companies, neither appear to be on the verge of bankruptcy. But the Gabelli funds argue Resideo’s plunge is due to allegedly false statements about the former unit’s prospects. Honeywell told Bloomberg News the lawsuit has no merit, but regardless of the legal outcome, it is interesting to see shareholders fighting back against spinoffs and reinforces my view that they’ve lost some of their luster.

Speaking of peak breakup, XPO Logistics Inc. announced a strategic review that reportedly could see the company exit businesses representing 75% of its revenue. Certain European, North American and Asian-Pacific supply-chain operations and XPO’s European and North American transportation arms could be jettisoned, people familiar with the matter told Bloomberg News. That would leave XPO with its business that handles relatively small freight. XPO CEO Brad Jacobs told Bloomberg TV he’s exploring breakup options because the company is suffering from a “conglomerate discount,” but the market “understands pure plays.” It's unclear when a company that only provides transportation and logistics services became a “conglomerate” but sure, OK. The market clearly does love this move because the stock climbed 15% on Thursday on the news. But it’s hard not to view this as a waving of the white flag for a company that was built via consolidation but has struggled of late to get deals done. Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Lee Klaskow says the breakup may be a sign that management has squeezed all it can from the business in terms of operating improvements and technology investments.

Speaking of peak breakup (part 2), DuPont de Nemours Inc. is reportedly working with advisers to review options for its electronics and imaging unit, including a sale, spinoff or a Reverse Morris Trust. The business generated about $2.6 billion of sales through the first three quarters of 2019, about 16% of the company’s total revenue. Any deal would follow last year’s spinoffs of the Dow Inc. commodity-chemical business and the Corteva Inc. agricultural-products company and the $26 billion Reverse Morris Trust deal for its nutrition and biosciences division. It also comes amid reports that DuPont is looking to divest its transportation and industrial unit, which had sales of $3.8 billion in the first nine months of 2019. Gordon Haskett analyst John Inch points out that DuPont has to maintain an annual Ebitda of more than $2.5 billion as a backstop against potential PFAS liabilities, per its separation agreement with Corteva. So that means it likely can’t divest both the electronics and industrial businesses. Inch says selling the electronics unit could generate value faster because peers for that business trade at higher multiples. DuPont will also likely want to put the proceeds from all these divestitures to work and it’s cheaper to grow a remaining transportation and industrial business through M&A.
