The View is Clearer Now

So, we have clarity. Or at least a little more than we had before. In the U.K., the Conservatives will be in power for the next five years, probably with Boris Johnson as prime minister throughout, and the nation will leave the EU at the end of next month. And in Washington, the U.S. has found a way to avoid imposing additional tariffs on China this Sunday.

The two most imminent risks for world markets have now been resolved. There will be no return to political chaos in Britain this week, and the trade system won’t have to withstand a new round of tariffs.  Calm is back. Here’s an attempted guide for investors to what happens next:

The U.K.

The result is unambiguously positive for British assets, although currency effects may be troublesome for U.K. investors. The huge rise in the pound won’t flatter the foreign earnings of the multinationals that dominate the FTSE-100. 

On a trade-weighted basis, and in comparison with the euro, the pound is back to levels it hasn’t touched since June 24, 2016 — the day after the Brexit referendum. That, in my view, is remarkable.

The case in favor of sterling bulls is that a negotiated Brexit is now assured. The risk of another year of political uncertainty is over, along with the risk of a weak minority government led by a very ideological Labour party. Johnson has to negotiate a free trade agreement with the EU by the end of next year; but the argument is that his standing is now so enhanced, and the nature of the MPs he commands (many of them from industrial heartland seats who know that they have to deliver for their working-class electorate) is so changed that he won’t do anything that damages the British manufacturing sector. That means an extension is likely, to be followed by a deal that is reasonably favorable to the auto sector and other British exporters. Meanwhile, Johnson plainly favors fiscal stimulus. Investors, like British voters, agree that this is a good idea.

This bullish case is fair as far as it goes, but ignores plenty of risks. Services are more important to the British economy than manufacturing, and the EU is unlikely to want to do any favors to the City of London. The rise in sterling takes away one of the advantages that has tided the U.K. through the political chaos of the last few years — the competitiveness that comes with a weak currency.

Finally, Scotland overwhelmingly voted for Nationalists, who will want a referendum on independence. And in Northern Ireland, unionists for the first time ever don’t account for a majority of MPs. Britain should have a few years of political stability, but there are still plenty of risks on the horizon.

 

The U.S.

Canceling the Dec. 15 tariffs had become a market totem. Their announcement by tweet on Aug. 1 triggered the stock market’s summer swoon. This immediately reduces perceived risk, in turn reducing demand for the dollar, which has functioned as a haven for a while. A weaker dollar is exactly what President Trump has vocally wanted. On a trade-weighted basis the dollar is clearly below its 200-day moving average, bolstering both American competitiveness and the financial position of the many emerging markets that have taken on dollar-denominated debt. It also lightens pressure on the Fed to cut rates further, which will bolster confidence. 

Also helping the weakening of the dollar is the pound’s strength. A weaker dollar should help U.S. stocks by flattering the overseas profits of the companies in the S&P 500. 

Beyond that, there are plenty of risks. In the U.S. there is the small matter of a presidential election, with the possibility of a significant change of direction toward left-wing candidates like Senator Elizabeth Warren (and the possibility of four more years of President Trump, which some might find more alarming). On the trade front, no tariffs have yet been lifted once they have been imposed, and both China and the U.S. will struggle to find a way to back down.

 

The Rest of the World

The release of stress may have its greatest impact on the balance between the rest of the world and the U.S. The immediate response to the tariff news brought MSCI’s index of world stocks excluding America to its highest in 16 months. Unlike the U.S., it is still far below its January 2018 peak. There ought to be more upside:

Look how drastic the rest of the world’s underperformance has been. Since global stock markets peaked on Halloween 2007, they have underperformed the S&P 500 (and the MSCI equivalent index) by more than 60%. A weaker dollar, growing faith in the avoidance of trade conflict, and signs of improvement in the eurozone should all help the rest of the world to fight back. Valuation disparities should also help: American stocks look far too expensive, and much of the rest of the world looks cheap. True, this has looked like a good bet for a long time and it hasn’t happened, but this is another chance:

Specifically, the most obvious beneficiaries of trade peace in Asia are still sitting on severe drawdowns since the trade rhetoric began to amplify early last year. Chinese industrials, Japan, and particularly South Korea are suffering from battered sentiment. Providing trade peace holds, the only way, surely, is up. 

Now to get on with the first day of the rest of our lives.

 

An Owl Joins the Hawks and Doves

A reminder: Earlier on Thursday, back when Brexit and the U.S.-China trade war were still in the balance, Christine Lagarde followed Jay Powell in a brilliant one-two of central bank meetings that created no news whatsoever. At one point they had been set for great drama. Following the Federal Open Market Committee on Wednesday, Lagarde had her inaugural press conference at the helm of the European Central Bank. 

Rather than set the agenda, or announce a clear path for the future, she wisely opted to keep all her options open. This was her key soundbite: “Once and for all, I'm neither a dove nor a hawk, and my ambition is to be this owl, that is often associated with a little bit of wisdom.”

Lagarde is one of the least owlish people ever to head a central bank, but her addition to the banking menagerie is an inspired one. Owls are not only famous for their wisdom, but also for their ability to look in all directions at once. They can swivel their head through 270 degrees. That flexibility is mighty useful in a modern central banker.

For an illustration, behold the journey of the Federal Reserve since Powell took over as chairman at the beginning of last year. This chart, produced by Torsten Slok, chief U.S. economist of Deutsche Bank AG, shows the fed funds rate predicted by a majority of Fed governors for the end of next  year, meeting by meeting, along with the actual rate they set at each. The extent of their shift is quite breathtaking:

Even more breathtaking is the extent of the change in the economy. Headline inflation was 2.1% two years ago; now it is 2.05%. Headline unemployment was 4.1%; now it is 3.5%. And 10-year expected inflation has gone from 2.1% to 1.63% — an indication that the market disliked rising rates and thought them constrictive, but no great sea-change in the economy. In the final analysis, these have been two quiet years for the U.S. economy in which has continued to grow at a gently slowing rate without producing big inflation. And yet the Fed has gone through a massive change in its planned course. An owl’s ability to look in several different directions at the same time must have been invaluable.

There are signs of light for the beleaguered eurozone economy, but owls of course have another advantage that could help Lagarde. They see well in the dark. 

The Myth of Capitalism: The Transcript

We held a 90-minute live chat on the latest Bloomberg book club selection, Jonathan Tepper and Denise Hearn’s The Myth of Capitalism, on Wednesday. Those without access to the terminal can now read the transcript online here. 

Perhaps the most interesting idea to emerge was that government intervention is ideally like that of a good sports referee; applying a few simple and clear rules strictly and fairly. This is a model that used to be applied in the U.S., but no longer. This was a comment by Hearn: 

Thurman Arnold is our best historical analogue for the sport referee you’ve just described. He managed antitrust enforcement under FDR in the early 1940s and brought a historically high number of cases forward.

This also shows that objections to antitrust enforcement on the basis of opposition to government interference in the market is misplaced. Markets need rules and someone to enforce them. A government that is more active in enforcing a few simple rules and blocking more mergers isn’t necessarily behaving in an anti-market way. Indeed it is probably helping the free market to function. The problem with government intervention is when it becomes too prescriptive and allows too much discretion to regulators. This comment from Tepper is very relevant:

 Markets have never existed without rules or regulations, whether it was common law or legislative statute. Markets are like a game, and they depend on clear rules and a referee who can enforce them. No one would enter into trade without laws and the ability to enforce contracts. This is true from Roman property law to English common law to the Sherman and Clayton Acts of today. There has never been a garden of Eden with no laws and some perfect state.
