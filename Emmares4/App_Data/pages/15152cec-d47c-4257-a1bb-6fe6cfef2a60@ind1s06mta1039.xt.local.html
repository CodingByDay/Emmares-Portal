Old colds

Common colds and cold sores have afflicted us for at least 31,000 years, and perhaps far longer. Researchers found DNA from four species of herpesvirus and a human adenovirus in fragmented milk teeth that were excavated at Yana in north-east Siberia. Previously, the oldest direct evidence of an identifiable virus infecting a human was from just 7000 years ago. What’s more, a comparison of the ancient viral DNA with modern strains suggests their last common ancestor dates back around 700,000 years ago – which would mean viruses that cause colds predate our species, and also troubled our Neanderthal cousins.  Read more

Bovine TB test

A new approach to tackle bovine tuberculosis (bTB) in cattle is being deployed in field trials in England as the UK government shifts away from its reliance on controversially culling badgers to curb the disease. A modified BCG vaccine against TB has existed for a few years, but crucially, there is now a skin test that can identify proteins present in infected animals and distinguish them from vaccinated cows. Doing so is critical for removing infected animals from herds and, later, paving the way for animals to be exported for trade. Trials are under way on a farm in Hertfordshire to check it does not yield too many false negatives. If results later this year are promising, a second trial phase will see the test combined with vaccines.  Read more

Aperture: Surfing sharks

Laurent Ballesta
The grey reef sharks in this shot seem to be just hanging in the water in a behaviour never reported before in these animals. Taken by researcher Laurent Ballesta at Andromede Oceanology in France in the South Pacific Ocean, it shows the sharks swimming against water currents, but barely moving their tails. Ballesta’s team discovered that the sharks were floating using the upward movement from currents, effectively “surfing” and cutting their energy consumption by about 15 per cent.
