Driving the Day

THE LATEST ROUND IN CHINA VS. U.S. — The U.S. court offensive against China over tech and cyberspace marched forward unabated on Thursday with its biggest event in the barrage yet: racketeering and conspiracy to steal trade secret charges filed against Huawei. While not as cybersecurity-specific as the recent charges unveiled against Chinese intelligence officials earlier this week for the hack of Equifax, the move wasn't without related elements.

The indictment implicates the company in aiding Iranian surveillance, for instance. "HUAWEI's business in Iran did violate laws and regulations, including sanction-related requirements, and included the provision of goods and services to the Iranian government, including surveillance technology used to monitor, identify and detain protestors during anti-government demonstrations in Tehran, Iran, in or about 2009," it states. It also cites news reports in subsequent years about Huawei allegedly assisting Iran in surveillance of mobile and internet communications.

Further, the indictment accuses Huawei of misappropriating "operating system source code for internet routers, command line interface (a structure of textual commands used to communicate with routers) and operating system manuals" from an unnamed company that appeared to be Cisco.

The charges inspired mostly laudatory remarks from lawmakers, two of whom — Sens. Tom Cotton (R-Ark.) and Chris Van Hollen (D-Md.) — used the occasion to tout their legislation that would direct the president to block U.S. exports to Chinese telecom companies that violate export control laws or sanctions (S. 152).

SORE VOATZ — Mobile voting app maker Voatz is in the spotlight again after MIT researchers on Thursday revealed several security and privacy vulnerabilities in its software . "Given the severity of failings discussed in this paper, the lack of transparency, the risks to voter privacy, and the trivial nature of the attacks, we suggest that any near-future plans to use this app for high-stakes elections be abandoned," MIT researchers Michael Specter, James Koppel and Daniel Weitzner wrote in their report. But Voatz isn't taking the criticism lying down. The company savaged the researchers in a press call with reporters. "Their whole paper is riddled with holes," said Voatz CEO Nimit Sawhney, describing one of their core assumptions as "completely, completely flawed."

Voatz executives also doubled down on their claims that the researchers were secretly trying to "thwart the process of innovation and progress for better voting access" and "sow doubt in the security of our election infrastructure," but they didn't provide serious evidence when confronted about this accusation. They also refused to name the outside entities that they claim have audited their app (or release the audits themselves), citing the terms of the NDA that they wrote. Instead, the company tried to keep the focus on the research community: "These attacks that have been leveled against us this morning really are a continuum of attacks against this kind of technology that started nearly 18 years ago," said Senior Vice President Larry Moore.

Highly respected voting security researchers laced into Voatz over the MIT report's findings and other issues with the technology. J. Alex Halderman of the University of Michigan described a problem with the way Voatz's server was configured, and researcher Kevin Beaumont highlighted his previous discoveries about the company . "The problems with online voting aren't coding errors," said Matt Blaze of Georgetown University, "they're fundamental architectural weaknesses in the model."

REAL VIRUS, FAKE CURE — Cybercriminals are expanding attacks by stoking conspiracy theory-based fears around purported unreleased cures for the coronavirus, according to research from Proofpoint released Thursday. The researchers found attackers are shoring up their hacking arsenal to include credential theft, as well as malware that can steal personal information, including financial information.

In one sample email, the hacker claims government entities are withholding the cure because the virus is being used as a bioweapon. The email urges the victim to find more information on the "cure" by clicking a link, which takes the victim to a fake DocuSign website where the victim needs to enter credentials to obtain the information. Proofpoint researchers found counterfeit Office 365, Adobe and DocuSign sites designed to steal credentials linked to coronavirus-themed emails. Another type of email researchers found primarily targets manufacturing, retail and transportation companies that contains an attachment, which if clicked installs malware (specifically, remote access Trojan) that can give the attacker complete control over the compromised system.

There's been quite a bit of research of late on hackers exploiting coronavirus fears. Besides Proofpoint on Thursday, Cisco Talos and Trustwave released their own insights on hackers using coronavirus lures. " These lures tied to coronavirus are likely to only increase in volume and variety as the virus continues to spread and dominate the headlines," Talos predicted.

EU ZEROES IN ON NEW RULES FOR SPYWARE EXPORTS — The EU is closing in on agreeing on rules for export controls on dual-use goods, which will affect tech companies' exports of encryption and surveillance technologies. Negotiations on the reform, introduced back in 2016 by the Commission, are nearing their end.

Two issues are still blocking a deal, though: 1. How national governments should report the licenses they give out for dual-use technology (transparency); and 2. How Europe can impose restrictions on a longer list of items, including things like facial recognition technologies (an "autonomous" EU list). Officials will have two technical meetings, Feb. 20 and March 5, ahead of a crunch-time meeting the morning of March 26, according to a draft agenda seen by POLITICO Europe's Cyber Insights.

TALLINN INTELLIGENCE GOES HARD ON RUSSIA — Also from our friends at Cyber Insights: The Estonian Foreign Intelligence Service found that "in 2019, Russian cyber operations were revealed that have been going on undiscovered for years," it said in its annual public report that came out Wednesday.

"In the summer of 2019, the European Union External Action Service identified leaks in the information systems of its Moscow delegation, which were traced back to February 2017," the report reads. The Estonians aren't directly attributing the attack, but the placing (in the middle of an exposé on Russian hacking operations) is, well, telling.

Estonia argues that Russia's favorite hacking method is watering-hole attacks: State-sponsored hackers indirectly target diplomats by looking for websites they are likely to visit and tricking the target to fill in credentials on a mimicked version of that website. The ultimate goal is to hack people in diplomatic networks "that have a low level of cybersecurity and possess sensitive information of another country due to membership in an international organisation," the report said.
