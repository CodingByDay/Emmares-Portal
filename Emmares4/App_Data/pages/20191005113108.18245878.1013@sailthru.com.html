Flying cars vs self-driving cars

Kitty Hawk, the aviation startup backed by Google co-founder Larry Page, has been working on a secret project for nearly two years called Heaviside. I got the first look at the aircraft — on the ground and in flight — about a week ago.

Kitty Hawk CEO Sebastian Thrun was on stage Thursday at TechCrunch Disrupt SF, where we talked about Heaviside, as well as the broader flying car industry and other future-y topics.

There’s a lot to unpack in the interview, and I’ve highlighted a few quotes that stood out.

“You want to just be flown and the technology for flying autonomous flying is so much easier than for self driving cars. I actually believe you’re going to be done with self-flying vehicles long before we start in cars.”

Yep, Thrun, who was a central early figure in self-driving cars, believes self-flying vehicles will be solved before autonomous vehicles. He later explained that autonomous vehicles have more complex environments.

On cost:

“So there’s today and then there’s the tomorrow. Today, obviously, this is incredibly expensive, and to be funding it with Larry’s (Page) money and other money.

“From the cost perspective, there’s a vehicle itself and it weighs maybe a third or a quarter of a car. And it should, in principle, be not more expensive per pound than a car when mass manufactured. From the energy side, almost all the energy is being used for drag and not for lift, Lift is the extra price you pay for having not wheels and being on the ground. But drag is the energy you use to combat the air. Because we can go on a on a straight line, We believe we will be roughly a third of the energy cost per mile as a Tesla. And Tesla is very efficient.”

Tiny but mighty mobility: Bird's big round

Each week, The Station will dig into the tiny, but mighty world of mobility. That’s the scooters, bikes and other lightweight and small products that people are using to get around. This week, it’s all about Bird.

Bird CEO and founder Travis VanderZanden was on the Disrupt SF stage this past week. The big headline grabber was that the company announced that it had closed a $275 million Series D round led by CDPQ and Sequoia Capital.

But Megan Rose Dickey’s interview with VanderZanden provided other insights into the scooter company, particularly around unit economics. Bird takes what VanderZanden described as a “punitive view on unit economics.” For Bird, that means it looks at the fully loaded cost per ride, including the depreciation of the vehicle. Based on that measurement, Bird is positive on every ride now across the globe, VanderZanden said.

Those metrics change, however, when looking at the third-party scooter providers on the Bird platform. For that segment of its business, Bird operates more like a software as a service company and takes a revenue share on every single ride. The economics on this segment were described as “quite good.”

A couple of other takeaways:

  • More than 99% all rides are actually on the custom Bird scooters now
  • The company’s Bird One scooters are lasting about 15 months on average

Ride-hailing niches

The ride-hailing industry might be dominated by Uber, Lyft and Didi, but that’s not keeping startups from jumping in and targeting niche demographics.

Take Safr, a ride-hailing startup targeting women. The company is in the process of closing its Series A round and recently launched operations in downtown Orlando. Safr says it’s a women-focused ride-sharing service and emphasizes its background checks on drivers and the safety-first focus.

It’s too early to determine whether Safr will succeed in scaling. The startup does say it plans to expand to other Florida cities. What this does illustrate is that the ride-hailing industry isn’t entirely settled, even if it feels that way.

Other startups are popping up to target underserved people or specific groups such as kids, women, the elderly and the LGBTQIA community. For instance, Onward launched a few months ago in the San Francisco Bay Area to help seniors safely get from point A to point B. The company announced in July that it raised $1.5 million in seed funding from Royal Street Ventures, Matchstick Ventures and JPK Capital.

Dispatches from Africa

The big business of transportation is not isolated to Asia, Europe and the United States. The Station is tracking what’s happening in other markets as well, including Africa. TechCrunch reporter Jake Bright, who has expertise in motorcycles and the startup scene in Africa, will bring us occasional dispatches that will give you, the reader, a deeper understanding of the region.

This week, Bright notes that big players such as Uber have been competing on the continent in the four-wheel taxis space for years. Now motorcycles are garnering attention and investment.

Over the last two years, Africa’s $4 billion motorcycle taxi market has seen a flurry of VC investment and startup activity. In 2018, Uber and Bolt jumped into the motorcycle taxi business in Africa, offering moto-taxi drop-downs on their apps in several African countries.  

This year, Nigeria — Africa’s largest population and economy — became a hotbed for startups digitizing motorcycle taxi activity. Chinese-owned browser company Opera launched a ride-hail motorcycle app, ORide, connected to its mobile-payment service called OPay. Another Lagos based moto-taxi startup, GoKada, raised a $5.3 million Series A in May. And Nigerian moto-taxi and moto-delivery startup Max.NG, recently closed $7 million in a round backed by Japanese motorcycle manufacturer Yamaha. 

What’s notable about this last raise is that MAX.ng will use this fresh capital to pilot e-moto taxis and an accompanying renewable energy grid to charge them. This mirrors recent developments in Rwanda, where the government has announced plans to work with EV startups such as Ampersand, to phase out gas motorcycles in its motorcycle taxi sector. This could put the East African country ahead of the Europeans on phasing out ICE vehicles in at least one segment of its mobility grid. 

Notable reads

AAA released new research that found automatic emergency braking systems with pedestrian detection perform inconsistently, and proved to be completely ineffective at night.

The upshot: with 75% of pedestrian fatalities occurring after dark, this means new technology that promises to reduce these numbers might not have the desired effect.

A couple of other points (the report is provided below):

  • In a simulated scenario, these systems did not react at all, colliding with the adult pedestrian target every time.
  • The systems performed best in the instance of ab adult crossing in front of a vehicle traveling at 20 mph during the day. In this case, the systems avoided a collision 40% of the time. At the higher speed of 30 mph, most systems failed to avoid a collision with the simulated pedestrian target.
